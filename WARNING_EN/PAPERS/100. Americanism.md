# Americanism

## How Critical Race Theory Keeps the Useful Idiots Entertained

### I. The Spectacle

There is a fight happening in America. It is loud, furious, and everywhere. It fills newspapers, cable news, school board meetings, and social media feeds. It has consumed the attention of millions of people who believe themselves to be engaged in a life-or-death struggle for the soul of the nation.

On one side: those who declare that racism is the fundamental sin of America, that the country was built on genocide and slavery, that white supremacy is encoded in every institution, and that only a radical reckoning can save us.

On the other side: those who declare that this is a lie, an attack on the nation itself, a Marxist conspiracy to destroy the greatest country on earth, and that the real racists are the ones who will not stop talking about race.

They hate each other. They call each other traitors and fascists and communists and bigots. They organize, march, vote, and fundraise based on this hatred. They believe, with total sincerity, that they are fighting the most important battle of our time.

They are both wrong. And they are both useful.

This essay will argue that the intense public battle over Critical Race Theory is not a genuine confrontation with power but a carefully managed spectacle. It will argue that the deep structure of American society—fundamentalist capitalism, or what might be called Americanism—has engineered a perfect distraction. It will argue that race discourse serves the same function for the twenty-first century that bread and circuses served for Rome: it keeps the population entertained, divided, and above all, *looking in the wrong direction* while the real work of domination continues uninterrupted.

And it will argue this without respect, because American society has earned none.

### II. What Critical Race Theory Actually Is

Before we can understand how CRT functions as a distraction, we must understand what it actually is. The public debate has so thoroughly distorted the theory that most people arguing about it have never read a single page of its founding texts.

Critical Race Theory emerged in the 1970s and 1980s among legal scholars who observed something troubling: the civil rights movement had won formal legal equality, but racial inequality persisted. Slavery was gone. Jim Crow was gone. Yet Black Americans remained poorer, sicker, more imprisoned, and shorter-lived than white Americans. Why?

The scholars who developed CRT—Derrick Bell, Kimberlé Crenshaw, Richard Delgado, and others—proposed an answer. Racism, they argued, was not primarily about individual prejudice or explicit discrimination. It was structural. It was embedded in the ordinary operation of laws, institutions, and social practices. It was, in their phrase, "normal" rather than aberrant.

They developed several key concepts:

- **Interest convergence:** Progress for Black Americans occurs only when it also serves the interests of white elites. Brown v. Board of Education happened not just because segregation was immoral, but because the United States needed to improve its international image during the Cold War.

- **Intersectionality:** People have multiple, overlapping identities. A Black woman experiences discrimination differently from both a white woman and a Black man. The law, which tends to treat discrimination as occurring along a single axis, often fails to recognize this compound injury.

- **The social construction of race:** Race is not biological. It is a category created by society to justify hierarchy and distribute power. The definitions of who counts as "white" have changed over time to include or exclude groups as economic conditions required.

- **Critique of colorblindness:** In a society structured by racism, pretending not to see race is not neutrality. It is a way of preserving the existing hierarchy while claiming moral high ground.

These are academic arguments. They are debatable, like all academic arguments. They have been refined, criticized, and debated within universities for decades. They are not a program for revolution. They are not a conspiracy to destroy America. They are a lens for examining how law and society function.

That is not how they have been presented in public.

### III. The Transformation

Something happened to Critical Race Theory around 2020. It escaped the academy and entered the public sphere. And in that transition, it was transformed into something entirely different.

For the left, CRT became a catechism. It was simplified into a set of propositions that could be recited as articles of faith: America is fundamentally racist. All white people benefit from white supremacy. Every institution is complicit. The only path to salvation is constant confession and purging of internal prejudice. Books were written. Workshops were conducted. Corporate diversity officers were hired. Social media profiles were adorned with statements of commitment.

For the right, CRT became a demon. It was presented as a Marxist plot to indoctrinate children, destroy the nuclear family, and overthrow Western civilization. Legislation was introduced to ban it from schools. Politicians built careers on opposing it. Parents were mobilized to attend school board meetings and demand that their children not be taught to hate their country.

Both sides escalated. Both sides became more certain. Both sides poured enormous energy into the fight.

And neither side noticed that the fight itself was the point.

### IV. The Liberal Function: Self-Punishment as Virtue

Consider the liberal engagement with CRT. What does it actually accomplish?

A well-meaning white person reads several books about anti-racism. They attend workshops. They post statements on social media acknowledging their privilege. They have difficult conversations with family members at Thanksgiving. They feel that they are doing something important, something morally serious.

What have they actually changed?

They have not altered the ownership structure of the economy. They have not reduced wealth concentration. They have not restored union power. They have not made healthcare or education more affordable. They have not stopped the destruction of the planet. They have not reduced the power of corporations to move capital across borders in search of cheaper labor.

They have performed a ritual of purification. They have demonstrated their membership in the virtuous tribe. They have signaled to others like them that they are on the right side.

This is deeply satisfying. It provides a clear moral framework, a path to redemption, and a sense of progress. It feels like fighting the good fight.

But it is a fight that cannot threaten the fundamental structure of American society. Indeed, American society has learned to absorb and even profit from this energy. Corporations hire diversity officers and run advertising campaigns celebrating inclusion while fighting unions and outsourcing jobs. Universities issue statements condemning racism while relying on endowments invested in industries that destroy communities. Political leaders give speeches about justice while accepting donations from the same financial institutions that foreclose on Black and brown homeowners.

The liberal engagement with race discourse is a pressure valve. It allows moral energy to be discharged in ways that do not threaten the actual distribution of power. It is the secular equivalent of indulgences: pay your money, perform your penance, and continue with your life.

### V. The Conservative Function: Resentment as Mobilization

Now consider the conservative engagement. What does it accomplish?

A conservative hears that their children are being taught that America is fundamentally racist. They are told that their ancestors were oppressors, that their identity is a source of shame, that the country they love is irredeemably evil. They are outraged. They organize. They vote. They fight.

Who benefits from this outrage?

Certainly not the conservative themselves. Their rage is genuine, but it is directed at a target that cannot deliver what they actually need. No amount of banning CRT from schools will bring back the manufacturing job that moved to Mexico. No amount of fighting culture wars will lower the cost of healthcare or make college affordable for their children. No amount of defending the flag will stop the concentration of wealth that has made their communities unlivable.

But someone does benefit. The political class benefits from having an energized base. The media benefits from having a perpetual conflict to monetize. The economic elite benefits from having the public focused on cultural battles while the structural transformation of the economy continues without resistance.

The conservative is told that their enemy is the liberal, the academic, the cultural elite. They are given someone to hate. They are given a cause. They are given the feeling of fighting for something important.

And while they fight, the system that is actually destroying their communities—the same fundamentalist capitalism that has offshored their jobs, bankrupted their towns, poisoned their water, and addicted their children to opioids—continues to operate without scrutiny.

The conservative is also a useful idiot. Just a different flavor.

### VI. The System's Gain

Step back and look at the whole picture.

On one side, liberals consume anti-racist content, perform virtue, and feel morally serious. On the other side, conservatives consume anti-CRT content, perform outrage, and feel patriotically virtuous. Both sides believe they are fighting the most important battle of our time. Both sides pour energy into the conflict. Both sides hate each other with genuine passion.

And the system continues.

Who owns the means of production? Who decides where capital flows? Who profits from the destruction of communities, the extraction of resources, the exploitation of labor? Who writes the rules that concentrate wealth at the top while the bottom stagnates?

Not the liberal academics. Not the conservative politicians. Not the people shouting at each other on cable news.

The people who actually run things—the managerial class, the financial elite, the owners of capital—are delighted by this arrangement. They could not have designed a better distraction if they had tried.

The left focuses on race. The right focuses on the left's focus on race. Both ignore the economy. Both ignore the structural transformation that is making life worse for everyone except the very rich. Both ignore the fact that poor people of all colors are being segregated together into communities abandoned by capital.

This is not a conspiracy in the sense of a secret meeting where billionaires plotted to invent CRT as a distraction. It is something more sophisticated: an emergent property of a system that has evolved to protect itself. The system generates conflicts that absorb energy. It creates moral panics that direct attention away from its own operations. It turns potential solidarity into actual division.

And it does all of this without anyone needing to plan it. That is the genius of the thing.

### VII. The Deeper Ideology: Americanism

But we must go deeper. Because race discourse is not just a distraction from economics. It is a distraction from something even more fundamental: the nature of Americanism itself.

**What is Americanism?**

It is not patriotism, though it wears that mask. It is not democracy, though it uses that word. It is not freedom, though that is its constant refrain.

Americanism is an ideology that has made itself invisible. It is fundamentalist capitalism wrapped in a flag and accompanied by a song. It is the belief that the pursuit of wealth is the highest human purpose, that the market is the ultimate arbiter of value, that human beings are primarily consumers, and that any alternative way of organizing life is either impossible or dangerous.

This ideology does not present itself as an ideology. It presents as reality itself. To question it is not to disagree with a political position; it is to be impractical, unrealistic, unreasonable. There is no alternative. This is just how things are.

This is the mark of a truly successful supremacy. It does not need to enforce itself through overt violence, though violence is always available when needed. It enforces itself through invisibility. You cannot fight what you cannot see. You cannot resist what you do not recognize as a system.

**Americanism is like Nazism made of glass.**

The comparison is not about content. Americanism does not advocate genocide. It does not march in uniforms. It does not explicitly declare one race superior to others, though it tolerates and exploits racial hierarchy when useful.

The comparison is about form. Nazism was a society that became psychopathic. It organized itself around the domination of others. It could not tolerate difference. It had to eliminate or subordinate anything outside itself.

Americanism is the same form, polished and sanitized. It seeks to dominate not through concentration camps but through assimilation. It demands that everyone, everywhere, eventually come to believe in money the way it believes in money. It demands that all economies, all cultures, all ways of life integrate into the global consumer market or be rendered irrelevant. It is totalizing not because it kills those who resist, though it sometimes does, but because it leaves no space outside itself.

The "Final Solution" of Americanism is not genocide. It is the elimination of alternatives. It is the conversion of all human activity into market transactions. It is the reduction of human worth to economic value. It is the transformation of citizens into consumers, communities into markets, meaning into merchandise.

And it is invisible. You do not see it until you are crushed by it. The unemployed factory worker does not see the system that destroyed his town; he sees immigrants and liberals. The student suffocating under debt does not see the financialization of education; she sees a job market that does not want her. The community poisoned by industrial waste does not see the logic of externalized costs; it sees a corporation that broke the rules, as if the rules were not designed to allow exactly this.

When the victims finally speak, they are told they are the problem. They are lazy, entitled, unpatriotic, dangerous. The system accuses its victims of being the aggressors. This is what psychopathic societies do.

### VIII. The House and the Field

This brings us back to race and to the most uncomfortable analogy in the African American experience.

During slavery, there was a distinction between house slaves and field slaves. The house slaves worked in the master's residence. They had proximity to power, access to information, marginally better material conditions. Some came to identify with the master's interests. The field slaves worked under brutal conditions, experienced the raw violence of the system directly, and had no illusions about their position.

The master deliberately cultivated this division. He gave the house slaves small privileges. He encouraged them to feel superior to the field slaves. He used them as informants. He made sure that any potential solidarity across the house-field divide was poisoned by resentment and distrust.

Today, that dynamic has been racialized in reverse.

The house population of the twenty-first century is integrated across racial lines. You see Black and brown faces in the C-suite, in Congress, in the media, in the upper reaches of the managerial class. They have been admitted into the house. They have demonstrated ideological compliance with the fundamental tenets of Americanism. They believe in money the way the master believes in money. They manage capital, defend the system, and enjoy its rewards.

The field population is also integrated across racial lines. Poor whites, poor Blacks, poor Latinos, poor others—they are segregated together in communities abandoned by capital. They work the remaining jobs, when they can find them. They are the raw material of the system, the disposable labor, the surplus population. They are contained, managed, and ignored.

The house slave of today is not a traitor to their race. They are a convert to the dominant theology. Their presence in the house proves that the system is not "racist" in the simple sense. It proves that ideological compliance, not skin color, is the real ticket to entry. And it ensures that any potential solidarity across class lines—any alliance between poor whites and poor Blacks—is poisoned by the visible presence of a few who "made it."

The master is delighted. He has integrated his house staff while maintaining the field. He has proven his own virtue while preserving the structure that keeps him on top. He has turned potential enemies into defenders of the system.

And the field stays in the field.

### IX. The Question of Consciousness

None of this requires that the people involved are insincere. The liberals who fight for racial justice are genuinely committed. The conservatives who fight against CRT are genuinely outraged. The Black executives in the C-suite genuinely believe they have earned their position through merit. The poor whites in abandoned towns genuinely believe their enemy is the liberal elite, not the system that created both the liberal elite and the abandoned town.

This is what makes the system so effective. It does not need puppeteers. It needs only to create conditions in which sincere people, pursuing what they believe to be right, nevertheless serve purposes they do not intend.

The liberal anti-racist, by focusing on individual prejudice and institutional representation, leaves the economic engine untouched. The CRT opponent, by focusing on cultural warfare, leaves the same engine untouched. The house negro, by celebrating inclusion, validates the system's claim to have solved racism. The poor white, by resenting the house negro, remains divided from the poor Black who shares his material condition.

Everyone is sincere. Everyone is fighting for what they believe. And the system continues.

This is the deepest form of domination: the one that does not need to enforce itself because its subjects enforce it on each other.

### X. The Unaskable Questions

If this analysis is correct, then the real questions are not being asked.

Not: How do we make the C-suite more diverse?
But: Why does the C-suite exist? Why should some people control the labor of others? Why should capital concentrate at the top?

Not: How do we make police more accountable?
But: Why do we need police? What function does the carceral state serve in a society organized around private property and wealth extraction?

Not: How do we make education more equitable?
But: Why is education funded by local property taxes? Why is the purpose of education to produce workers for the economy rather than free human beings?

Not: How do we reduce poverty?
But: Why does poverty exist in a society with enough resources for everyone? What function does poverty serve in a system that requires a reserve army of labor and a population desperate enough to accept any work at any wage?

These questions are not asked because they cannot be asked within the framework of Americanism. To ask them is to question the religion. And questioning the religion is the one thing the system cannot tolerate.

So instead, we argue about race. We fight about symbols. We consume and are consumed by the spectacle. And the glass structure grows around us, invisible until we are crushed.

### XI. The Path Forward

What would it mean to step outside this trap?

It would mean, first, seeing. Recognizing that Americanism is not reality but a specific, contingent, and pathological way of organizing human life. It would mean understanding that the culture war is a managed spectacle, not a genuine confrontation with power. It would mean looking at the field—the poor of all colors, segregated together—and asking what solidarity might be possible if the distractions were set aside.

It would mean asking uncomfortable questions about one's own position. For the liberal: Is my anti-racism actually challenging power, or is it making me feel virtuous while the system continues? For the conservative: Is my patriotism actually defending my community, or is it being used to keep me fighting the wrong enemy? For the house negro: Is my inclusion real, or am I being used to legitimize a system that will discard me when I am no longer useful?

It would mean building something new. Not a reformed version of the same system, but a genuine alternative. A way of organizing human life that does not require endless growth. That does not reduce human value to economic value. That does not demand ideological conformity. That does not segregate the poor. That does not make victims into enemies.

This is not a program. It is a direction. It is the slow, difficult work of making the invisible visible, of naming the glass, of building spaces outside the totalizing logic of the market.

It is the work of people who have stopped being useful idiots and started being something else: conscious, clear-eyed, and unwilling to be entertained while the world burns.

### XII. No Respect

I said at the beginning that this essay would be written without respect, because American society has earned none.

This is what I mean:

A society that has made an idol of money, that worships growth while the planet dies, that reduces human beings to consumers and workers, that segregates the poor and calls it freedom, that distracts its people with culture wars while the rich extract everything, that cannot tolerate any genuine alternative to itself, that presents its own pathology as common sense—such a society deserves no respect.

It deserves to be named. It deserves to be understood. It deserves to be resisted.

The fight over Critical Race Theory is not the real fight. It is the entertainment they give you while the real fight happens somewhere else, in boardrooms and banking halls, in trade agreements and tax codes, in the slow, steady concentration of wealth and power that continues whether you are tweeting about white privilege or about CRT indoctrination.

They are laughing at you. Both sides of you. They are laughing because you are doing exactly what you are supposed to do. You are fighting each other while they take everything. You are arguing about the paint job while they drive the car to the bank.

Stop being useful.

Start seeing.
