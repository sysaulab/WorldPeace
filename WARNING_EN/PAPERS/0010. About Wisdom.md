# About Wisdom

*An essay for anyone who has ever wondered: “Are we doing this right, or are we just doing what we’re used to?”*

---

## Introduction: The Trap of “How We’ve Always Done It”

Every human group has its own way of doing things. Your family has unspoken rules about who speaks first at dinner. Your workplace has rituals about how decisions get made. Your country has laws, holidays, and stories that feel “normal” – not because they are perfect, but because you grew up inside them.

This is culture. Culture is not bad. It gives us identity, belonging, and shortcuts for getting along. But culture has a dangerous side: it makes us mistake *familiar* for *good*. We defend our group’s way because it is *our* way, not because we have looked at it honestly.

Think about it. When someone from another culture criticizes your country’s healthcare, your first feeling might be defensiveness. When a coworker suggests a different way to run meetings, you might roll your eyes. When your teenager questions a family tradition, you might feel disrespected. In each case, the problem is not that the criticism is wrong. The problem is that you are comparing the suggestion to *what you already know* – and what you already know feels right because you know it.

This is the trap: **using your own culture as the only reference point.** It makes honest self-evaluation nearly impossible. How can you see the flaws in your own house when you have never stepped outside?

Systemic wisdom offers a way out. It replaces “what we usually do” with a different question: *“What keeps a living system healthy?”* That question does not belong to any one culture. It belongs to biology, cybernetics, and the physics of feedback. It is a **neutral abstraction** – a mirror that works the same for a family in Brazil, a company in Japan, a village in Kenya, or a government in Canada.

This essay explains what systemic wisdom is, why using systems as your core reference point frees you from cultural bias, how to practice it in daily life, and what it looks like when ordinary people use it to improve their families, workplaces, and communities.

## Part One: What Is Systemic Wisdom?

Let us start with a story.

In a Toyota factory in Japan, any worker can pull a cord that stops the entire assembly line. Not because something is broken – but because something *might* be wrong. The worker sees a scratch on a door panel. They pull the cord. The line stops. A manager runs over. They talk. They fix the problem. Then they restart.

This is not a sign of failure. It is a sign of wisdom. Toyota learned that stopping the line for thirty seconds to fix a small problem is much cheaper than shipping a defective car and recalling it months later. More importantly, they learned that the person with the least power – the worker on the line – is often the one who sees the problem first. If you silence that worker, you lose your best sensor.

Systemic wisdom is the art of designing any system – whether a factory, a family, or a nation – so that it can **sense its own problems** and **adjust before disaster strikes**. It does not care about your flag, your religion, or your traditions. It only cares about three things:

1. **Feedback** – Do the consequences of your actions return to you quickly, so you can learn?
2. **Curiosity** – Do you actively look for better ways, or do you repeat the same old patterns?
3. **Respect for fragility** – Do you listen to the parts of the system that break first (the “canaries”), or do you ignore them until they die?

A system that has these three qualities is wise. It learns. It adapts. It survives.

A system that lacks them is foolish. It repeats its mistakes. It crushes its weakest parts. It eventually collapses – sometimes dramatically, sometimes quietly, but always predictably.

The beautiful thing is that these three qualities are **not cultural**. They are not Western or Eastern, religious or secular, capitalist or socialist. They are properties of any complex system – a rainforest, a human body, a small team, a global corporation. You can observe them without ever mentioning God, government, or your grandmother’s recipes.

That is what makes systemic wisdom a **neutral reference point**. It allows you to compare two different cultures not by saying “ours is better because it’s ours,” but by asking: “Which one has shorter feedback loops? Which one protects its canaries? Which one encourages curiosity?” Those questions can be answered with evidence, not with loyalty.

## Part Two: Why “Systems” Removes the Temptation of Cultural Bias

Let us be honest. Most arguments about how to run a society, a company, or a family are really arguments about *whose culture wins*. “We should do it this way because this is how we have always done it.” “That way is wrong because it comes from *them*.”

This is tribalism, not wisdom. And it is everywhere.

Consider a debate about school discipline. One side says: “Children need strict rules and punishment. That is how we were raised, and we turned out fine.” The other side says: “Children need understanding and dialogue. That is modern and enlightened.” Both sides are using their own cultural reference points – tradition versus progress, authority versus freedom. Neither side is asking the systemic question: *“What feedback loops exist in this school? Who is the canary? How do we know if our discipline methods are working?”*

A systemic approach would ignore the cultural labels. It would measure: How quickly do teachers learn about a student’s distress? Do students have a safe way to report problems? Does punishment actually change behavior, or does it just create resentment? Are the most vulnerable students (those with learning differences, trauma, or home problems) being heard or silenced? These are not liberal or conservative questions. They are engineering questions. They apply to any school anywhere.

Here is another example. In some cultures, it is rude to criticize a boss publicly. In others, it is expected. Which is better? A cultural relativist says: “Neither is better; they are just different.” A cultural chauvinist says: “My culture’s way is best.” A systemic wisdom practitioner says: *“What happens to feedback in each system?”*

- In the culture where you cannot criticize the boss, feedback loops are long. Problems hide. The boss may never learn they are making bad decisions. The canaries (junior employees who see the problems) are silenced. Over time, the organization becomes brittle. It may fail suddenly when an unexpected crisis hits.
- In the culture where criticism is expected, feedback loops are short. Problems surface quickly. The boss can adjust. The organization learns. But there is a catch: if criticism is harsh or personal, it can also create fear and destroy trust. So the system needs a second layer – *psychological safety* – which is itself a form of respect for fragility.

The systemic answer is not “culture A wins” or “culture B wins.” It is: *“Which specific mechanisms can we borrow from both to create short, safe feedback loops?”* That might mean using a private suggestion box (from the polite culture) combined with regular public after-action reviews (from the direct culture). The wise person does not choose a culture. The wise person borrows what works and adapts it to local conditions.

This is what it means to use a **common external abstraction** for comparison. Instead of saying “my family is better than your family,” you say: “Let us look at our families as systems. How fast do we learn from mistakes? How safe is it to tell the truth? Who suffers first when something goes wrong, and do we listen to them?” Those questions are the same for a family in Tokyo, a family in Nairobi, and a family in Ohio. They allow honest comparison without defensiveness.

## Part Three: How to Practice Systemic Wisdom in Daily Life

Systemic wisdom is not a philosophy for experts. It is a set of simple, practical habits that anyone can learn. Here is how to apply it.

### Step 1: Map Your System

Every group you belong to – your family, your team at work, your book club, your neighbourhood – is a system. Take a piece of paper and draw it. Who talks to whom? Who makes decisions? Who suffers when things go wrong? Who is never listened to?

You do not need a fancy diagram. Just notice the patterns. In your family, does the parent always decide what is for dinner, or do children have a say? In your team, are problems discussed openly, or does everyone smile and complain later in private? In your community, do the poorest residents have a voice, or are they invisible?

Mapping the system is the first act of wisdom because it forces you to stop assuming and start seeing.

### Step 2: Check Your Feedback Loops

Ask: How long does it take between an action and its consequence being felt by the person who acted?

- If you say something hurtful to a friend, do they tell you immediately, or do they suffer in silence and then disappear from your life six months later? Short loop = healthy. Long loop = dangerous.
- If your company makes a bad product, do customers complain directly to the engineers, or does the complaint get lost in a call center in another country? Short loop = learning. Long loop = repeated mistakes.
- If you spend money on a credit card, do you feel the pain of debt immediately, or does it arrive as a surprise bill at the end of the month? Short loop = responsible spending. Long loop = financial collapse.

To shorten feedback loops, create mechanisms: weekly check-ins, anonymous surveys, a “stop the line” rule where anyone can call a timeout. The goal is not to be harsh. The goal is to learn before the damage is irreversible.

### Step 3: Find the Canaries

In every system, some part is more fragile than the rest. In a coal mine, miners used to bring a caged canary. If the canary died, they knew poisonous gas was present – and they had time to escape. The canary was not special. It was just the first to break.

Who is the canary in your system?

- In a school, it might be the shy child who never raises their hand. When they start acting out or withdrawing, that is your signal that something is wrong with the way the class is run.
- In a workplace, it might be the new employee who asks “dumb” questions. When they stop asking, that means fear has taken over – and soon everyone will be hiding problems.
- In a marriage, it might be the partner who always gives in. When they stop talking altogether, the marriage is already dead.

Wisdom means: **do not crush the canary.** Do not silence the person who brings bad news. Do not punish the whistleblower. Instead, thank them. They just saved your system.

### Step 4: Cultivate Curiosity

The opposite of wisdom is certainty. Certainty says: “We already know the answer.” Curiosity says: “What if we tried something different?”

You can practice curiosity in tiny ways:

- When a meeting reaches a deadlock, ask: “What would we do if we had no budget?” or “What would a child suggest?”
- When a family argument repeats for the tenth time, ask: “What if we swapped roles for a day?”
- When you feel bored or stuck, ask: “What is one small experiment I could run this week to see if a new way works better?”

Curiosity is not about being clever. It is about admitting that you do not know everything. That is the beginning of wisdom.

### Step 5: Act, Then Sense, Then Adjust

Wisdom is not about getting it right the first time. It is about getting it *less wrong* over time. So take action – but make it a small action. Then watch what happens. Then adjust.

This is called the **feedback loop**. A wise person does not make a giant plan and then pray. A wise person runs a tiny experiment, sees the result, learns, and runs another experiment. This is how a baby learns to walk, how a scientist discovers a vaccine, and how a community rebuilds after a disaster.

## Part Four: Examples of Systemic Wisdom in Real Life

Let us make this concrete with four everyday examples.

### Example 1: A Family Dinner

The Johnson family has a problem. At dinner, the father dominates the conversation. The mother nods along. The teenage son scrolls on his phone. The young daughter is silent. The father thinks everything is fine because no one complains.

Systemic wisdom: The family maps the system and sees that feedback loops are long (no one tells the father he talks too much) and the canaries (the daughter) are silenced. They decide to try a new rule: at dinner, each person gets two minutes to speak without interruption. They also add a “token” – a small stone – that the speaker holds. When they finish, they pass the stone.

Result: The father hears his daughter for the first time in months. The daughter says she feels lonely. The father is hurt at first – but because the rule is neutral (everyone follows it, not just him), he does not feel attacked. Over weeks, the family learns to listen. The system heals.

### Example 2: A Small Business

A bakery has high staff turnover. The owner thinks young workers are lazy. He hires, they quit, he hires again.

Systemic wisdom: The owner maps the system. He asks the exiting workers for honest feedback (anonymously). He learns that the problem is not laziness – it is that workers are punished for making mistakes, so they hide problems, which leads to bigger mistakes, which leads to more punishment. The canaries (new workers who make the most mistakes) are being crushed.

The owner introduces a new rule: anyone who spots a mistake gets a small bonus. Mistakes are treated as data, not sins. The bakery also starts a five-minute daily huddle where anyone can raise a concern.

Result: Mistakes are caught early. Workers feel safe. Turnover drops. The owner learns that his workers were never the problem – his system was.

### Example 3: A School Classroom

A teacher is frustrated that students are not paying attention. She raises her voice. They pay attention for five minutes, then drift again.

Systemic wisdom: The teacher asks herself: who is the canary? It is the student in the back who never answers questions. She pulls him aside privately and asks: “What would make this class better for you?” He says: “I am bored because you talk too long. I learn by doing.”

The teacher experiments: instead of a 45‑minute lecture, she does 10 minutes of teaching, then 10 minutes of group work, then 10 minutes of teaching, then 10 minutes of practice. She also creates a “parking lot” – a whiteboard where students can write questions or suggestions anonymously.

Result: Engagement improves. The canary starts contributing. The teacher realises she was not a bad teacher; she was using a bad system.

### Example 4: A Friend Group

A group of four friends has a problem. One friend, Alex, often cancels plans at the last minute. The others complain about Alex behind his back but smile to his face. They are being “nice,” which (as we learned in another paper) is actually harmful because it suppresses feedback.

Systemic wisdom: One friend, Maria, decides to break the niceness. She waits for a calm moment and says: “Alex, I value our friendship. When you cancel at the last minute, I feel hurt because I set aside time for you. Can we talk about what is making that happen?”

Alex is surprised but not angry. He explains that he has been struggling with anxiety and sometimes cannot leave the house. The group had no idea. They agree on a new system: Alex will be honest when he is struggling, and the others will not pressure him. They also agree that if he cancels more than twice in a row, they will check in with care, not with blame.

The feedback loop is now short. The canary (the friend who felt hurt but stayed silent) is heard. The system adapts.

## Part Five: Why This Matters – The Honest Mirror

The deepest gift of systemic wisdom is that it allows you to look at yourself honestly without collapsing into shame or defensiveness.

When someone from another culture criticises your country, it is tempting to feel attacked. But if you use systems as your reference, you can say: “Tell me more about the feedback loops you see that I might be missing. Where are my canaries? Where is my curiosity dead?” That is not weakness. That is strength.

When your own conscience tells you that you have been unfair to a coworker, you can use the same lens: “What feedback did I ignore? Who was the canary I silenced?” Instead of spiralling into guilt, you can act: shorten the loop, apologise, change the mechanism.

Systemic wisdom is not about being perfect. It is about being **open to learning**. It replaces the question “Am I good?” with “Is my system healthy?” That shift is enormous. “Am I good?” leads to defensiveness, because admitting you are wrong threatens your identity. “Is my system healthy?” leads to curiosity, because you can change a system without losing your self‑worth.

The mirror of systems does not flatter or condemn. It simply shows you: here is where feedback flows. Here is where it is blocked. Here is your canary, silent or singing. What will you do next?

---

## Conclusion: The Culture‑Free Path to Wisdom

We began with a problem: using our own culture as the only reference point makes honest self‑evaluation almost impossible. We defend our family, our company, our country – not because they are wise, but because they are *ours*.

Systemic wisdom offers a way out. It gives us a set of questions that work anywhere, for any group, at any scale:

- How fast do we learn from our actions?
- Who suffers first, and do we listen?
- Do we explore new ways, or just repeat old ones?

These questions are not Western or Eastern, ancient or modern. They are properties of living systems. A beehive has feedback loops. A forest has canaries. A human body is curious (through the immune system’s constant testing of new threats). Nature has been practicing systemic wisdom for billions of years without a single textbook.

When you use systems as your reference, you are not betraying your culture. You are stepping outside it – not to reject it, but to see it clearly. You are holding up a neutral mirror. What you see might be beautiful. It might be broken. Most likely, it is a mix of both. But now you can see it. And seeing it is the first step toward making it better.

So try this tomorrow. In your next meeting, ask: “How will we know if this decision was wrong?” In your next family argument, ask: “Who is not being heard?” In your own private thoughts, ask: “What feedback am I ignoring because it would hurt to hear?”

That is systemic wisdom. It is free. It fits in your pocket. And it works whether you pray to God, worship nature, or believe in nothing at all. Because systems do not care what you believe. They only care whether you learn.

*The mirror is waiting. Look honestly. Then act.*
