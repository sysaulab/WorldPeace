# The Currency Note

We begin here, because it's the symbol we've already named as a virus.

The story we tell each other: This piece of paper, or the digits on a screen, is "money." It represents value. It is a claim on goods and services. It is the reward for my hard work, the measure of my worth, the ticket to security and freedom. I hold it, and I feel a small pulse of agency.

The story we hide from ourselves: I worked an hour of my finite life to earn this note. That hour is gone. The note is its ghost. I tell myself that saving it is prudence, but a part of me knows that I am hoarding the disembodied labor of strangers against a future I cannot control. I treat it as a permanent store of something, but its purchasing power is a slow leak, a quiet tax on my stored time. The number on the screen is never enough, and the gap between it and "enough" is where my fear lives.

## Beneath the symbol: the biophysical layer

Strip away the paper, the ink, the ledger entry. What is this note actually a claim on? Trace it backward. It was created as debt—a promise to do future work. That work requires energy: calories for the worker, diesel for the machine, sunlight captured by ancient photosynthesis and now burned as coal or gas to power the computer on which the digital version of this money moves. The paper itself is cotton and linen, grown in soil, watered by rain, harvested by machines that consume steel and rubber and fossil fuel. The ink contains pigments and resins, mined, refined, transported. The note is a small, portable warehouse of deferred ecological consequences. When I spend it, I am not "buying" something so much as releasing a tiny pulse of stored exergy to redirect materials toward me. The money itself is nothing but a permission slip written in the language of a dying planet. If all the energy infrastructure evaporated, this note would be a bookmark for a world that no longer exists.

## The Smartphone
The altar at which we daily kneel. A sleek black rectangle, warm in the hand, humming with possibility.

The story we tell each other: It's a tool of connection, a window to all human knowledge, a camera, a map, a music player, a wallet. It makes life easier, faster, richer. It's a miracle of human ingenuity, democratizing access to information.

The story we hide from ourselves: It is also a leash. I check it a hundred times a day, not because it calls me, but because I need its small dopamine drips—a like, a message, a headline that makes me feel informed or outraged or alive. I use it to escape the slow, awkward, uncertain texture of face-to-face presence. I scroll through curated lives and feel my own life diminish in comparison. I am the product, my attention harvested and sold to advertisers who bid in real-time auctions on the micro-moment between my boredom and my next tap. The device is an engine of envy, distraction, and low-grade anxiety. I love it and resent it, and I cannot put it down.

## Beneath the symbol: the biophysical layer

The smooth glass was once sand, melted in furnaces at temperatures exceeding 1,500°C. That heat came from natural gas or coal. The aluminum frame was bauxite, strip-mined from the earth, its refining leaving behind a residue of toxic red mud that poisons rivers. The "rare earth" elements in its speakers and vibration motor—neodymium, praseodymium—came from mines in Inner Mongolia or Kachin State, leached from ore with acids that left behind radioactive tailings and poisoned groundwater. The cobalt in the battery was likely dug by hand in the Congo by miners, some of them children, breathing dust that will shorten their lives. The processor contains tin, tungsten, tantalum, gold—conflict minerals that have funded wars and funded forced labor. Each microchip was etched in a factory so clean that a single speck of dust would ruin it, yet that cleanliness depends on vast quantities of ultrapure water and solvents that become hazardous waste. The device was assembled by workers on twelve-hour shifts in dormitory factories, shipped across oceans on container vessels burning heavy fuel oil, and will be used for two or three years before it is discarded. It cannot be easily repaired or recycled. It will end its life in an e-waste dump in Ghana or India, where children burn its plastic casing to extract copper wire, inhaling dioxins and furans. The "cloud" where my photos live is a concrete data center in rural Oregon or Ireland, humming with cooling fans, drawing enough electricity to power a small town. Every "like" I receive has a faint metabolic cost that I will never see itemized. The smartphone is not a window to the world; it is a mining operation disguised as a mirror.

## The Family Car

Parked in the driveway, gleaming under the streetlight. A symbol of freedom, adulthood, the open road.

The story we tell each other: It's a means of transport, an enabler of opportunity. It gets me to work, takes my children to school, allows us to visit family and explore the world. It's engineered for safety, efficiency, comfort. Its advertisements show it cresting a mountain pass, empty road, sun setting, cargo rack carrying a kayak. Freedom.

The story we hide from ourselves: It is also a monthly payment. A depreciating asset that costs thousands a year in fuel, insurance, maintenance, and registration. I sit alone in it, in traffic, staring at the bumper in front of me, listening to a podcast that half-occupies my mind while my body tenses with low-grade road rage. I bought it partly for the image it projects—practical, sporty, rugged, successful—and I wash it on weekends as a kind of ritual care, polishing the exoskeleton I present to the world. It is the second-largest financial outlay of my life, and I use it primarily to commute between a mortgage and a job, both of which are also symbols we will shortly deconstruct.

Beneath the symbol: the biophysical layer
The car weighs roughly 1,500 kilograms. Of that, about 1,200 kg is steel, iron, and aluminum—extracted from open-pit mines in Australia, Brazil, or the Pilbara, processed in blast furnaces that run on metallurgical coal, releasing about 1.8 tons of CO₂ per ton of steel. The plastic interior was once crude oil, refined and polymerized and injection-molded. The tires are synthetic rubber (more petroleum) mixed with carbon black (a product of incomplete combustion) and silica, wearing down with every rotation into fine particulate dust that enters lungs and waterways. The battery contains lithium (brine evaporation in the Atacama Desert, consuming water that indigenous communities depend on) or lead (smelted at high temperatures, exposing workers to neurotoxic fumes). The fuel in its tank is ancient sunlight: plankton and algae that fell to the bottom of Jurassic seas, cooked by geological pressure and heat for 150 million years. Each liter of gasoline releases about 2.3 kilograms of CO₂ when burned, and over its lifetime, this single car will release tens of thousands of kilograms of invisible gas into the atmosphere, where it will trap heat for centuries. The road it drives on is asphalt—bitumen, sand, and crushed stone—laid by machines that also burn diesel. The parking lot it rests in was once topsoil, now sealed under impermeable pavement, unable to absorb rain, so the water rushes to drains carrying motor oil and tire dust to the nearest creek. The "freedom" of the open road is a carefully choreographed expenditure of ancient stored sunlight, performed inside a metal box that insulates me from the scent, sound, and consequence of that expenditure. I am not traveling the earth; I am borrowing against its crust and incinerating its past to propel my present.

## The Single-Family Home

The largest symbol of all for many of us. The white picket fence is an icon, even if the fence is now vinyl.

The story we tell each other: A home is shelter, safety, an investment, a place to raise a family. It is the cornerstone of the "American Dream," the largest purchase most people will ever make, and a tangible store of wealth passed down through generations. It is where I rest, where I am most myself.

The story we hide from ourselves: It is also a thirty-year mortgage, an anchor of debt that binds me to my job, my city, my credit score. The rooms I fill with furniture and objects I barely use are rooms I must heat, cool, clean, and maintain. I tell myself the house is an investment, but the property taxes and interest payments mean it is also a slow extraction of my labor by the financial system. The empty guest bedroom, the formal living room no one sits in, the attic full of forgotten things—these are spatial manifestations of an appetite that exceeds need. The lawn must be mowed, a ritual of suburban aesthetics that consumes Saturday mornings and produces nothing but a visual signal of order and respectability.

## Beneath the symbol: the biophysical layer

The wooden frame was once a forest. Two-by-fours from Canadian spruce, logged by harvesters that run on diesel, trucked to mills that run on grid electricity (coal, gas, hydro), kiln-dried at high heat. The drywall is gypsum, mined from ancient evaporite deposits, mixed with water and paper, baked in ovens. The concrete foundation is limestone and clay heated in a rotary kiln at 1,450°C, a process that releases about 600 kg of CO₂ for every ton of cement produced. The copper wiring was born in an open-pit porphyry mine, crushed and leached and electrorefined. The insulation was fiberglass (melted sand) or spray foam (petrochemicals). The roof shingles were asphalt and mineral granules. The paint on the walls contains titanium dioxide, acrylic polymers, volatile organic compounds. To build this house, a hole was dug, topsoil scraped away, a habitat fragmented. To power and heat it, natural gas is burned in a furnace, or electricity is drawn from a grid fed by distant combustion. Water arrives through buried pipes from a reservoir or aquifer, and leaves through sewers to a treatment plant. The yard is a monoculture of non-native grass, kept alive with water and petrochemical fertilizers and pesticides that run off into streams. The house is a regulated cave, a climate-controlled bubble whose existence requires the constant, invisible consumption of materials and energy sourced from places its occupants will never see. The "wealth" it represents is not a pile of money; it is a pile of organized matter, temporarily arranged against entropy, continuously slipping back toward disorder unless new energy is poured in.

## The Self as Brand

The most intimate symbol of our age: the curated self, the personal profile, the digital avatar.

The story we tell each other: This is me. My posts, my photos, my opinions, my accomplishments. I am sharing my authentic self with the world, connecting with friends and communities, building a personal brand for career and creative opportunities. My profile is a garden I tend.

The story we hide from ourselves: This is not me. This is a selection of performative moments, filtered and captioned, chosen to elicit a response. The messy, boring, ugly, uncertain moments are omitted. I measure my worth in engagement metrics. I craft an identity for external validation, and then mistake that construct for my actual personhood. I perform an emotion, then photograph the performance, then post the photograph, then feel the emotion fade and wonder if it was ever real. I am curating a museum of my life while the life itself passes outside the frame.

## Beneath the symbol: the biophysical layer

The self as brand is not made of atoms, but it burns energy all the same. The data centers that store my profile and process my posts consume electricity on a massive scale. The undersea cables that carry my self-image across continents required copper, plastic, and ships to lay them. The smartphone in my hand, the router in my living room, the server farm in Ashburn, Virginia—all are metabolic extensions of my desire to be seen. Every hour I spend crafting my digital self is an hour my body is stationary, my hands idle, my eyes fixed on a screen rather than on the face of a human being in my physical proximity. The "self" I am building online is literally immaterial, but its maintenance draws down the material world at a rate I never feel. It is pure symbol, and its creation consumes substance while producing no nutrition, no shelter, no warmth—only a fleeting neurochemical signal that another human somewhere has tapped a heart-shaped icon.

The Biophysical Layer, Exposed
Now peel everything away.

Take the money, the phone, the car, the house, the digital self. Strip off the stories—public and hidden. What remains is not a collection of independent objects, but a single, unified process:

Energy flows. Materials cycle. Entropy accumulates.

Sunlight falls on leaves. Soil organisms break down rock and humus. Water moves through roots, stems, atmosphere, rivers, oceans. Humans eat plants and animals, converting chemical energy into thought, muscle work, warmth, and waste. From the earth's crust, we pull concentrated ores and ancient biomass. With heat, we transform them into tools and machines and buildings. The heat eventually dissipates into space. The materials eventually wear out, corrode, fracture, and scatter. All the symbols we worship are temporary, local reductions of entropy, purchased by generating far greater entropy somewhere else—the mine, the smokestack, the landfill, the atmosphere's thickening blanket of waste gases.

The money note? A lien on a small fraction of these flows.
The smartphone? A highly ordered cluster of elements, held together for a few years against entropy's pull, then discarded.
The car? A mobile combustion chamber, oxidizing hydrocarbons into motion and gas.
The house? A shell of organized matter that slows the flow of heat, water, and air, maintained by continuous energy input.
The self as brand? A pattern of electrical signals stored in silicon, requiring a planetary infrastructure to persist.

None of it is permanent. None of it is self-sufficient. All of it rests on a biophysical foundation that does not care about our symbols, our stories, our likes, or our portfolios. The foundation simply is: a thin film of life on a wet rock, orbiting a middle-aged star, receiving a finite flux of energy, governed by the laws of thermodynamics. We can tell ourselves any story we want about what our symbols mean. But meaning does not change metabolism. The sun rises, the plants photosynthesize, the soil breathes, and our symbols will endure only as long as the energy holds out and the materials remain extractable.

To see the biophysical layer clearly, even for a moment, is to understand that every transaction is a transformation of matter and energy, and every symbol is a temporary eddy in a river that began long before us and will flow long after.
