# Homo Indigenus

### Forging a Post-Possession Paradigm: A Theory of Socio-Ecological Resurgence from *Homo Indigenus*

**Author:** Sylvain Saucier

### Abstract

The dominant Western socioeconomic model, characterized by *Homo economicus*, has precipitated a planetary-scale crisis termed here the "Great Perturbation." This paper examines the ontological schism between this extractive, possession-based paradigm and the relational worldview of *Homo indigenus*. We argue that emergent responses, such as *Homo ecologicus*, while well-intentioned, fail to address the root cause of the crisis: the axiomatic belief in private property and the commodification of nature. This failure is not merely theoretical; it is visible in the lived trajectories of those who have been most thoroughly processed by the machinery of *Homo economicus*—including those whose conversion was secured through the quiet channels of imperial contracting, perception management, and the epistemological colonization of the interior. This paper proposes a pathway to genuine sustainability through the adoption of an Indigenous ontological framework. We introduce a threshold mechanism—the voluntary relinquishment of all private property—as a practical and symbolic process for transcending the paradigm of possession. This act is theorized not as a loss, but as a transformative shedding of an illusion, enabling a fundamental reorientation from ownership to belonging. We conclude that the future of a viable human-Earth relationship is predicated not on managing the planet, but on becoming indigenous to it.

### 1.0 Introduction

For the past five centuries, a systemic pathology has propagated across the globe. Termed the "Great Perturbation," this condition originated with colonialism—a process characterized by the violent dispossession of peoples from their ancestral lands and the subsequent extraction of life and resources for capital accumulation. This is not a concluded historical period but a persistent and evolving mindset that treats the biosphere as a warehouse of inert resources. The agent of this pathology is *Homo economicus*, a constructed human archetype for whom the world is devoid of intrinsic value, its worth solely determined by its utility in a market economy. To *Homo economicus*, a forest is not a living community but a calculation of board feet; a river is not a living entity but a source of hydroelectric power or a sink for waste. This abstraction of value has superseded the intrinsic value of life itself, leading to the current socio-ecological crisis.

The expansion of *Homo economicus* is not achieved by market forces alone. It requires a sustained war on *Homo indigenus*—a deliberate, multi-generational effort to dismantle indigenous ways of knowing, relating, and being, and to replace them with a universalized, rationalist, market-compatible self. This war has mutated across epochs: from the sword and the mission, to the residential school and the structural adjustment program, to the NGO proposal and the perception management operation. In its current phase, it operates less through direct territorial control and more through the colonization of the interior—the shaping of fundamental categories through which a population knows friend from foe, justice from aggression, and self-interest from subjugation. This is epistemological warfare, and its front lines run directly through the human psyche.

In response to the visible degradation caused by this model, a derivative archetype has emerged: *Homo ecologicus*. This individual, while acknowledging the severity of the crisis, remains cognitively and materially bound to the paradigm of possession. *Homo ecologicus* seeks to "manage" the earth, to "offset" ecological damage, and to purchase "sustainability" through market mechanisms. We contend that this approach constitutes a performative apology that fails to dismantle the core driver of the crisis—the belief in ownership—and thus remains trapped within the very system it purports to correct. More dangerously, *Homo ecologicus* often performs indigeneity while reinforcing the structures that destroyed it: purchasing ancestral wisdom through global supply chains, optimizing the gut microbiome with imported superfoods, and adopting the aesthetics of rootedness while remaining structurally *economicus* in form.

This paper presents an alternative, drawing from the persistent and resilient worldview of *Homo indigenus*. This is not a romanticized relic of the past, but a living, evolving framework for human-Earth relations. The central tenet of this worldview is the absence of the concept of private property. The land is not an object to be owned, but a subject to be related to—a mother, a provider, a community to which humans belong. Our security and wealth derive not from individual accumulation, but from the strength of kinship networks and the health of the shared ecosystem.

We posit that a genuine solution to the Great Perturbation requires not a modification of the possession-based paradigm, but a fundamental ontological shift. This paper describes a proposed mechanism for facilitating this shift: a voluntary, non-transactional threshold of property relinquishment. This mechanism serves a dual purpose: it protects Indigenous communities from opportunistic co-option and provides a pathway for sincere individuals to undergo the transformative process necessary to become relatives, rather than owners, of the land.

### 2.0 Methods: The Transformative Threshold

This work is based on participatory action research and the synthesis of Indigenous epistemological frameworks passed down through countless generations. The proposed intervention is a socio-cultural mechanism designed to facilitate the transition from the *Economicus*/*Ecologicus* paradigms to an *Indigenus* paradigm. This is not a quantitative method but a qualitative, transformative process.

**The Intervention:** An open invitation is extended to all individuals ("proponents") seeking to join an *Indigenus* community. This invitation is conditional upon a single, non-negotiable act: the complete and voluntary divestiture of all private property, titles, and possessions. This includes all land, material goods, financial assets, and intellectual property.

**Data Collection and Validation:** The sincerity of the proponent is not measured through surveys or interviews, but through their willingness to complete this act. The act itself serves as the primary data point, a behavioral indicator of a shift in ontological commitment. The opportunist, still invested in the paradigm of possession, will by definition be unable to complete the threshold. The successful proponent, having shed their former identity, becomes a participant-observer in the construction of the new community. Their subsequent integration and testimony provide the longitudinal data for the study.

### 3.0 Results: The Anticipated Outcomes of Ontological Shift

Based on the historical resilience of *Indigenus* societies and the proposed mechanism, we project the following outcomes for those who successfully cross the threshold:

- **Shedding of the Illusion of Ownership:** The act of divestiture is theorized to be profoundly liberating. The proponent empirically demonstrates to themselves and the community that their identity was not contingent upon their possessions. They experience firsthand that the anxiety of accumulation and the fear of loss are artifacts of a constructed reality, not inherent human conditions.
- **Emergence of Relational Value:** With the abstraction of property removed, the proponent becomes receptive to the intrinsic and relational value of the world. A tree is no longer a potential timber asset or a carbon credit, but a being providing shade, oxygen, and habitat—a relative. Their worth is no longer calculated by net worth, but by their contribution to the community's well-being.
- **Integration into Kinship Networks:** The individual transitions from a state of isolated autonomy to one of embedded interdependence. Security is no longer found in fences and private stores, but in the reciprocal relationships of the community. This fosters a socio-economic model based on sharing and mutual aid, which has proven to be highly resilient over millennia.
- **Protection of Communal Integrity:** The threshold functions as an effective shield against those seeking to exploit or co-opt the community. It ensures that only individuals who have fundamentally rejected the *Economicus* paradigm can participate, preserving the cultural and ecological integrity of the group.

### 4.0 Case Study: The Contractor as Conversion Machine—From *Indigenus* to *Economicus* to *Ecologicus*

To understand the depth of the Great Perturbation and the insufficiency of the *Ecologicus* response, we must examine the mechanisms by which *Homo economicus* is produced and maintained. The following composite profile, drawn from documented trajectories of individuals processed through imperial contracting pipelines, illustrates the ontological conversion at work.

#### 4.1 The Pipeline

Between 2003 and 2021, thousands of nationals from post-Soviet and Global South states served as private military contractors for the United States and its allies in Iraq and Afghanistan. This labor pool—drawn from countries with strong martial traditions and lower wage expectations—was integrated into the operational and ideological apparatus of American expeditionary warfare. They guarded diplomatic installations, secured convoys, and worked alongside perception management units that framed occupation as liberation. This was not merely an economic arrangement; it was an immersive environment of epistemological transfer.

#### 4.2 The Conversion Process

A contractor entering this system underwent a multi-layered conversion:

- **Deracination:** She was removed from her native ecosystem and placed in a desert war zone, operating for a foreign power. Place became irrelevant; the mission was everywhere. The specific soils, foods, and relations of her homeland became memory, not daily reality.
- **Expert Epistemology:** Intelligence briefings, operational doctrine, and command hierarchies replaced folk knowledge, ancestral intuition, and local ways of knowing. She learned to trust the expert system, not the body's inherited wisdom.
- **Market Identity:** She became a self-branded unit of security provision, paid in dollars. Her survival and prosperity depended on her individual market value, not on communal reciprocity or kinship networks.
- **Narrative Universalism:** The language of "women's rights," "democracy," and "stability" was presented as universally valid endpoints, not as culturally specific European exports with a colonial genealogy. She became a missionary of this narrative, trained to frame any desired outcome as a universal good and to treat resistance as ignorance to be managed.

Crucially, this conversion did not require a conscious decision to "convert." It was an adaptive survival response in a high-stress, highly ideological environment. By adopting the Coalition's worldview, the contractor reduced the cognitive dissonance between witnessing the destruction wrought by the occupation and her own role within it. The adoption of imperial ideology was a psychological survival mechanism.

#### 4.3 The Emergence of *Homo Ecologicus* as a Mask

Upon returning to her home country—in many cases a nation now on the front line of great-power confrontation—the former contractor did not remain a pure *Homo economicus*. She had witnessed too much destruction to find peace in raw extraction. Instead, she gravitated toward *Homo ecologicus*. She pursued optimal nutrition, ancestral diets, and ecological consciousness. She spoke of healing the gut, reconnecting with nature, and eating purely.

But the structure of her pursuit remained unchanged:

- The diet was not inherited from her actual ancestors or her local soil. It was researched, optimized, and performance-tracked through expert-led wellness frameworks.
- Connection to the earth was purchased through global supply chains—superfoods shipped from continents away, not the foods grown in her grandmother's garden.
- The project remained individual: *her* healing, *her* optimization, *her* moral purity. The communal, intergenerational, place-bound web of *Homo indigenus* was absent.
- The old colonial binary—civilized/savage—was replaced with a new one: *awake/asleep*, *healed/toxic*, *vibrant/inflamed*. The convert looked upon the unconverted with quiet supremacy.

This is *Homo economicus* performing indigeneity. The market provides the aesthetics of belonging without the bonds of obligation. The soul remains colonized, but the colonizer now wears the clothes of the shaman.

#### 4.4 The Inability to Digest Pluralism

A key diagnostic feature of *Homo ecologicus* is the inability to tolerate genuine pluralism. When confronted with the suggestion that humans have always eaten from all parts of the earth—that there is no one universal diet, no single path to purity—the *Ecologicus* subject retreats. The response is a variation on "as long as I can do it": a withdrawal into sealed personal righteousness that mirrors the bunker mentality of the imperial missions she once served. This is the fortress of the self, the last redoubt when the universal claim fails to convert the other.

The presence of *Homo indigenus*—the one who simply eats what the local earth provides, without moralizing, without optimizing, without a mission—is a profound threat to this constructed identity. It exposes the performance. It reveals that the superfood regimen is not a return to wholeness but a continuation of colonization by other means.

### 5.0 Discussion

The proposed model directly challenges the foundational assumptions of Western sustainability discourse. *Homo ecologicus*, represented by concepts like "natural capital," "ecosystem services," and "carbon markets," attempts to solve a crisis of commodification through further commodification. This approach, we argue, is inherently contradictory. It seeks to place a price tag on the priceless, thereby reinforcing the very abstraction that caused the disconnection. The case study above demonstrates that this contradiction is not merely philosophical; it is lived. Individuals who have been most thoroughly processed by the machinery of empire often become the most ardent performers of ecological virtue, precisely because performance allows them to maintain the structures of possession while acquiring the moral comfort of apparent awakening.

The *Indigenus* model, by contrast, does not seek to "manage" the planet but to *rejoin* it. The threshold of property relinquishment is the critical mechanism for this re-joining. It is a rite of passage that marks the death of the possessive self and the birth of the relational self. It is not a fee for entry, but a shedding of the very concept of entry fees, transactions, and ownership. For the former contractor, this would mean relinquishing not only material possessions but the entire ideological architecture—the expert-dependency, the moral hierarchy, the narrative universalism—that has held her psyche together since her first bunker.

Critics may argue that this model is impractical on a global scale. However, the Great Perturbation itself is the result of the global application of an impractical and destructive model. The *Indigenus* proposal does not claim to be a quick, policy-based fix. It is a foundational reorientation. It posits that genuine sustainability cannot be achieved through top-down management or consumer choices, but only through a mass, grassroots transformation in how humans perceive their relationship with the Earth and each other. The threshold is the mechanism for initiating this transformation one individual, one community at a time.

The quiet refusal—the simple act of eating what the local earth provides without turning it into a salvation project—may be among the most potent forms of resistance to the ongoing colonization of the interior. It requires no mission, no expert, and no performance. It is the humble, daily practice of being here, in a body, on a land, in relation. That practice is the seed from which *Homo indigenus* regrows, not as a lifestyle brand, but as a way of life.

### 6.0 Conclusion

The sickness of *Homo economicus* has brought the planet to a critical threshold. The guilt of *Homo ecologicus* is an insufficient remedy. A viable future requires a return to the oldest and most resilient path: the Indigenous path. This path is not defined by blood or ancestry, but by a fundamental commitment to a worldview where belonging supersedes possession.

The mechanisms of conversion that produced the contractor, the perception manager, and the wellness missionary are formidable but not final. The human capacity for relation, for embeddedness, for knowing the land as kin rather than resource, persists beneath the layers of ideological inscription. The threshold of divestiture is offered as a surgical cut through those layers—a way to shed the constructed self and recover what was always there.

We, the *Homo Indigenus*, extend an invitation to all who are prepared to shed the skin of ownership and embrace a life of genuine kinship. The mechanism is simple and profound: give everything away, and in doing so, gain everything that matters—a place in the community of life. The world built on abstraction is dying. The world rooted in relationship is ready to be born. The future is indigenous.
