# Why Being Nice Is Evil

## Preface: A Necessary Distinction

Before I begin, let me be precise about what this essay is *not* saying.

It is not saying that kindness is evil. It is not saying that compassion is evil. It is not saying that gentleness, warmth, consideration, or care are evil. These are among the most precious qualities human beings can offer one another.

This essay is about **niceness**—a specific cultural performance that has, in many contexts, replaced genuine virtue with a polished imitation. Niceness is what remains when love has been evacuated but the smile remains. Niceness is what we do to avoid conflict rather than to seek truth. Niceness is the mechanism by which we protect the powerful from the consequences of their actions, and by which we abandon the vulnerable to their suffering.

If kindness is the medicine, niceness is the sugar coating that makes the medicine unnecessary—and therefore makes the sickness continue.

## Part One: Defining the Term

What do I mean by "niceness"?

Niceness is a set of behaviors and communicative norms characterized by:

- **Agreeableness at any cost**: The nice person avoids disagreement, not because they have considered the issue and changed their mind, but because disagreement is uncomfortable.

- **Pleasantness as priority**: The nice person ensures that social interactions remain smooth and pleasant, even when important truths are being sacrificed to maintain that smoothness.

- **Conflict avoidance as highest value**: The nice person will allow harm to continue rather than risk the discomfort of confrontation.

- **Emotional labor displaced onto the vulnerable**: The nice person expects others to manage their own distress quietly so as not to disturb the peace.

- **Truth subordinated to comfort**: The nice person will tell you what you want to hear, not what you need to know.

Niceness is not the same as politeness, though they often travel together. Politeness is a set of conventions that enable strangers to interact without violence. It is useful. Niceness is a set of inhibitions that prevent intimates from telling each other the truth. It is destructive.

Niceness is not the same as kindness. Kindness seeks the genuine good of the other, even when that requires temporary discomfort. Kindness will tell you that you have hurt someone, because not telling you would leave you ignorant and therefore dangerous. Kindness will risk the relationship for the sake of the person. Niceness will preserve the relationship at the expense of the person.

## Part Two: The Mechanism of Harm

How does niceness cause harm? Through three interconnected mechanisms.

### The Suppression of Feedback

In any healthy system—whether a body, an ecosystem, a relationship, or a society—feedback is essential. The organism must know the consequences of its actions in order to adjust. Pain is not a design flaw; it is a signal. Without pain, you would keep your hand on a hot stove until the flesh burned away.

Niceness suppresses pain signals. When someone has caused harm, the nice response is to say nothing—to preserve harmony, to avoid awkwardness, to spare feelings. But whose feelings are being spared? Not the victim's; their pain continues unaddressed. The feelings being spared are the *perpetrator's*—their comfort, their self-image, their right not to be displeased.

This is a transfer of burden. The victim must carry their pain in silence so that the perpetrator need not carry the discomfort of knowing what they have done. The victim must manage their own suffering so that the powerful can remain comfortable in their ignorance.

The result is that harm continues. The person who caused it never learns. The conditions that produced it never change. The system never corrects. Niceness is the lubricant that allows injustice to operate smoothly, without friction, without the grating sound of protest that might alert someone to the damage being done.

### The Protection of Power

Niceness is not distributed equally. It flows *upward*.

The powerful are surrounded by niceness. Their assistants are nice. Their spouses are nice. Their friends are nice. Everyone agrees, everyone smiles, everyone tells them they are wonderful. This is not because they *are* wonderful. It is because displeasing them has consequences. The assistant who speaks truth may be fired. The spouse who complains may be divorced. The friend who confronts may be cut off.

The vulnerable, by contrast, receive very little niceness. They are told the truth, often brutally. They are told they are lazy, undeserving, responsible for their own suffering. No one protects *their* feelings. No one smoothes *their* path.

Niceness thus functions as a **protective shield for the powerful**—a cushion of pleasant lies that insulates them from the consequences of their actions. And because they never hear the truth, they never change. They become more entrenched in their power, more convinced of their righteousness, more isolated from reality.

This is not conspiracy. It is *structure*. Everyone is responding rationally to the incentives they face. And the cumulative effect is a society in which the powerful cannot learn and the vulnerable cannot speak.

### The Erosion of Love

This is the deepest harm.

Love requires truth. You cannot love someone you do not know, and you cannot know someone who will not show you their real self. But niceness demands performance. It demands that we present only what is pleasant, only what will not disturb. It demands that we hide our pain, our anger, our fear, our need.

The nice person is not loved. They are *liked*—which is not the same thing. Liked for their pleasantness, their agreeability, their ability to make others comfortable. But the real self, the complex self, the self that hurts and needs and sometimes fails—that self is hidden away, unloved and unknown.

And the person surrounded by niceness is also not loved. They are surrounded by performances. Everyone is playing a role designed to please them. No one is real. No one will tell them the truth. They are the most alone person in the room, surrounded by people, connected to no one.

Niceness is the enemy of love because love requires *vulnerability*—the willingness to be seen, the willingness to displease, the willingness to tell the truth even when it costs. Niceness requires none of this. It requires only performance.

## Part Three: The Societal Scale

What happens when niceness becomes a cultural norm—not just among the powerful, but throughout society?

### The Professionalization of Niceness

Consider the modern workplace. Performance reviews are "constructive." Feedback is "sandwiched" between compliments. Problems are "opportunities." Everyone is "doing great work." And then, quietly, people are fired—often bewildered, because no one ever told them there was a problem.

The workplace has become a theater of niceness. The real feedback—the feedback that might actually help someone improve—is withheld because it might be uncomfortable to deliver. Instead, the discomfort is deferred until it becomes a crisis, at which point it is too late for feedback to help. The person is removed, still ignorant of what they did wrong, and the cycle continues with the next person.

This is not kindness. It is cruelty disguised as politeness. It leaves people unable to grow, unable to learn, unable to become better at what they do. And it leaves organizations dysfunctional, because problems are never named and therefore never solved.

### The Consumerization of Relationships

Niceness has become a commodity. We pay for it. We tip for smiles. We rate services based on whether the server was "friendly." We have outsourced basic human warmth to the service economy, where it is performed on demand.

The result is that we have forgotten what genuine warmth feels like. We have become accustomed to performances. We have begun to expect that everyone we encounter will be *nice* to us, will make us feel good, will prioritize our comfort. And when someone is not nice—when they are honest, or tired, or simply not performing—we feel *offended*. How dare they not manage our feelings?

This expectation of universal niceness is a form of entitlement. It assumes that others exist to make us comfortable. It denies their reality, their complexity, their right to have a bad day. It is, in its own way, a small tyranny—the demand that everyone perform emotional labor on our behalf.

### The Digital Amplification

Social media has made this infinitely worse. Online, we curate our niceness. We post only what will receive approval. We hide our complexity, our struggle, our truth. We perform for an audience that we hope will like us.

And because we only see others' performances, we believe that everyone else is happier, more successful, more together than we are. We feel alone in our struggle, alone in our pain, alone in our truth—because no one else is showing theirs.

The result is an epidemic of loneliness, depression, and disconnection. We are surrounded by performances of connection, but the connection itself is absent. We are liked by thousands and loved by no one.

## Part Four: The Political Consequences

At the societal level, niceness has devastating political effects.

### The Collapse of Accountability

When everyone is nice, no one is held accountable. The politician who makes disastrous decisions is met with polite questions, respectful tones, and a reluctance to "attack." The CEO who destroys communities is treated with deference, invited to conferences, asked for their "perspective." The billionaire who accumulates obscene wealth while workers struggle is praised for their "philanthropy."

Niceness has become a shield for power. As long as you are *nice*—as long as you smile, use the right language, avoid obvious cruelty—you can do enormous harm without facing serious opposition. The opposition itself is constrained by niceness. To be too critical is to be "negative," "divisive," "unconstructive." The critique is muted by the very norms that should enable it.

### The Death of Solidarity

Solidarity requires truth-telling. It requires naming the harm, identifying the perpetrator, demanding change. None of this is nice. It is confrontational. It makes people uncomfortable. It disrupts the smooth functioning of the system.

Niceness undermines solidarity by making confrontation taboo. It tells the oppressed that they should be *polite* in their demands, *reasonable* in their expectations, *grateful* for whatever scraps they receive. It tells them that anger is unseemly, that protest is disruptive, that they should express themselves in ways that do not disturb the comfort of the powerful.

This is not accidental. The powerful benefit enormously from a culture of niceness. It is the most effective form of social control ever devised—one that does not require violence, only the internalized belief that one should not make a fuss.

### The Maintenance of Injustice

Injustice persists not primarily through active cruelty but through passive complicity. Most people are not actively oppressing anyone. They are simply being *nice*—going along, not rocking the boat, not making trouble. And their niceness allows the machinery of injustice to continue operating smoothly.

The slave owner was not the only problem. So were the neighbors who saw nothing, said nothing, did nothing. The segregationist was not the only problem. So were the white liberals who tut-tutted over dinner but took no action. The corporate exploiter is not the only problem. So are the consumers who want cheap goods and don't want to think about how they are made.

Niceness is the permission structure for evil. It allows evil to continue because no one is willing to be *rude* enough to stop it.

## Part Five: The Alternative

What is the alternative to niceness? Not rudeness for its own sake. Not cruelty. Not the performative abrasiveness of those who mistake being unpleasant for being honest.

The alternative is **kindness**—which is something entirely different.

Kindness is not afraid to displease. Kindness will tell you the truth because it cares about you. Kindness will risk the relationship for the sake of the person. Kindness will name the harm, not to punish, but to heal.

Kindness is not smooth. It is often awkward. It stumbles. It makes mistakes. But it is *real*. It comes from a genuine desire for the other's good, not from a need to maintain one's own comfort.

The alternative to niceness is also **courage**—the willingness to speak when speaking is costly, to name what is hidden, to disrupt what is comfortable. Courage is what the assistant needs when they tell the boss the truth. Courage is what the spouse needs when they say "this is hurting me." Courage is what the citizen needs when they stand against power.

And the alternative is **love**—real love, not the pale imitation that dares not speak its name. Love that stays through conflict. Love that tells the truth. Love that protects the vulnerable even when the powerful are displeased.

## Part Six: The Path Forward

How do we move from a culture of niceness to a culture of genuine care?

### For Individuals

**Learn to distinguish niceness from kindness**. Notice when you are being pleasant to avoid discomfort rather than to serve the other's good. Ask yourself: Am I silent because silence serves, or because silence is safe?

**Practice truthful speech in safe contexts**. Start with people who have shown they can handle it. Tell a friend something you have been withholding. Ask for the truth about yourself from someone you trust. Build the muscle of honesty where the stakes are low, so that it is available when the stakes are high.

**Welcome truth when it is offered**. When someone risks telling you something difficult, do not punish them. Do not become defensive. Thank them. They have given you something precious: a chance to know what you could not see.

**Be willing to displease**. Not for its own sake—not to be contrarian or difficult—but because some truths are worth the discomfort they cause. Your job is not to make everyone comfortable. Your job is to be real.

### For Communities

**Create spaces where truth is honored more than pleasantness**. Circles where everyone can speak. Practices of feedback that are routine rather than exceptional. Norms that celebrate the person who names the hard thing rather than the person who smooths it over.

**Protect the truth-tellers**. They are vulnerable. They will be attacked for their honesty. The community must surround them, support them, let them know that their gift is valued even when it hurts.

**Teach children the difference**. Do not raise children to be nice. Raise them to be kind, to be honest, to be courageous. Let them know that love sometimes requires conflict. Let them see you navigate disagreement without collapsing. Let them learn that relationship can hold truth.

### For Society

**Reduce the power imbalances that make niceness a survival strategy**. When people depend on the powerful for their livelihood, their housing, their safety, they will be nice. They have no choice. The only way to make truth-telling possible is to make it safe—to reduce the consequences of displeasing the powerful.

**Celebrate accountability over pleasantness**. Honor the journalists who name corruption, not the politicians who smile while it continues. Honor the whistleblowers, not the executives who maintained the pleasant fiction. Shift the cultural reward system so that truth-telling is valued more than smoothness.

**Build institutions that encode feedback**. Your paper on organic systems offers a path: structures where consequences return to those who caused them, where the vulnerable can speak, where power cannot insulate itself from truth. This is not just an economic project. It is a moral one.

## Conclusion: The Evil of Niceness

Niceness is evil because it is the enemy of love, the enemy of truth, the enemy of justice.

It is evil because it protects the powerful from the consequences of their actions while abandoning the vulnerable to their suffering.

It is evil because it substitutes performance for presence, pleasantness for care, smoothness for honesty.

It is evil because it allows injustice to continue without opposition, harm to continue without feedback, evil to continue without name.

The nice person is not evil in the sense of actively willing harm. They are evil in the more dangerous sense: they are the enabler, the collaborator, the one whose silence makes harm possible. They are the neighbor who saw nothing, the friend who said nothing, the citizen who did nothing. They are the reason that systems of oppression can function without most people needing to consider themselves oppressors.

The way out is not to stop being kind. It is to stop being *nice*—to reclaim the courage to tell the truth, the love that risks conflict, the care that names harm. It is to build a world where feedback flows, where the vulnerable can speak, where power cannot insulate itself from reality.

This is not a comfortable path. It will require confrontation. It will require conflict. It will require being willing to displease, to disrupt, to name what is hidden.

But it is the only path that leads to love.

The other path—the path of niceness—leads to a world of pleasant surfaces and rotting interiors, of smiles that hide suffering, of harmony that masks injustice. It leads to the peace of the graveyard, where everyone is polite because everyone is dead.

We can choose otherwise. We can choose to be real.

*For those who have told me the truth when it would have been easier to be nice: I am alive because of you. For those who have risked my displeasure to serve my good: I am grateful beyond words. For those who have loved me enough to confront me: you have given me the only gift that matters.*

*May we learn to do the same for others.*
