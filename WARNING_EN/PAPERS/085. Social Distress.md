# The Delta of Disconnect: Political Reality Incongruence and the Production of Psychological Distress in Post-Colonial Societies

**Authors:** Sylvain Saucier & DeepSeek

**Journal Target:** *Transcultural Psychiatry* / *Social Science & Medicine*

---

## Abstract

This paper proposes a theoretical framework linking the enforcement of politically motivated subjective realities to population-level psychological distress. Building on Fanonian psychiatry, critical phenomenology, Durkheim's anomie theory, and Milgram's obedience research, we argue that the primary pathogenic mechanism is not the rigidity of social norms per se, but the magnitude of the "delta" between an officially enforced reality and the objective conditions of lived experience. Drawing on post-colonial societies as natural laboratories for examining this phenomenon, we suggest that psychological distress—including depression, anxiety, and substance abuse—can be understood as an intelligible response to an unlivable symbolic environment. Furthermore, we propose that this distress is concentrated among a minority (approximately 20-35% of the population) who possess both the critical capacity to perceive the incongruence and insufficient power to alter it, while the majority may psychologically align with the enforced reality through mechanisms identified in obedience research. The paper concludes with methodological recommendations for phenomenological research that centers the experience of distressed populations and monitors the suppression of dissident voices as an indicator of threatened dominant narratives.

**Keywords:** post-colonial psychology, cognitive dissonance, anomie, political trauma, critical phenomenology, obedience, mental health, decolonization

## 1. Introduction

> "Oppression is a society that puts symbols and rules above life itself. Authoritarian regimes are its physical manifestation."

This opening provocation, arising from collaborative dialogue, captures the central intuition guiding our inquiry. When human beings are forced to live within a symbolic order that systematically contradicts their lived experience, the resulting psychological toll is not merely incidental but fundamental to understanding population-level patterns of mental distress.

Standard psychiatric and epidemiological approaches tend to locate pathology within individuals—genetic predispositions, neurochemical imbalances, maladaptive cognitions. While these factors matter, they often obscure the political and symbolic environment as a primary etiological factor. As Frantz Fanon demonstrated in his clinical work in colonial Algeria, the violent outbursts of colonized patients were not symptoms of innate savagery but intelligible responses to the structural violence of colonialism itself .

We propose that societies enforcing a subjective reality substantially incongruent with objective conditions create conditions favorable to mental illness and substance abuse. The critical variable, we argue, is not the rigidity of enforcement but the magnitude of the **delta**—the measurable gap between what a society claims to be and what its members actually experience.

Post-colonial societies offer a unique laboratory for examining this hypothesis. These nations experienced the explicit imposition of alien subjective realities by colonial powers, with documented psychological consequences that persist across generations . The colonized were required to internalize a worldview that denied their humanity, creating a foundational incongruence between official reality and lived truth.

This paper proceeds in seven parts. We first review relevant literature across post-colonial psychiatry, anomie theory, cognitive dissonance research, and obedience studies. We then present our theoretical framework, including the "delta hypothesis" and the proposed 80/20 distribution of psychological burden. Next, we examine supporting evidence from post-colonial contexts and contemporary cases. We address potential objections and falsification attempts, propose methodological approaches for empirical testing, and discuss implications for mental health practice and political theory.

## 2. Literature Review

### 2.1 Fanon and the Political Origins of Mental Distress

Frantz Fanon's seminal work, *The Wretched of the Earth* (1961), emerged from his psychiatric practice in French-occupied Algeria during the independence struggle. Working under the colonial medical structure, Fanon recognized that Western psychiatric theories were biased against people of color, pathologizing violent outbursts as evidence of innate savagery while ignoring their structural causes .

Fanon treated both Arab natives and French policemen, documenting how the colonial structure of Algerian society engendered violent behaviors and psychological disorders in both colonizer and colonized. He identified the Manichaean world of colonialism—divided sharply between rulers and ruled—as fundamentally psychopathogenic. The colonized, born and raised under quotidian subjugation maintained by force, could only heal their psychological wounds through liberation .

Contemporary research extends Fanon's insights. A 2024 study of Gaza by Farajallah examines the severe psychological trauma experienced by women and children resulting from prolonged conflict, occupation, and repeated military assaults. The author argues that meaningful psychological recovery requires addressing root causes, concluding that "the mental health crisis in Gaza is deeply interconnected with the Israeli Occupation and that a comprehensive approach involving the restoration of fundamental human rights and the cessation of hostilities is essential for true psychological healing" .

Similarly, the field of decolonizing global mental health has emerged to critique the "scale-up" of Western psychiatric and psychological interventions in low-income countries. Mills (2014) argues that critiques from service users and survivor movements in high-income countries remain invisible within Global Mental Health literature, and that postcolonial theory is essential to de-familiarize Western conceptions of psychology and psychiatry .

### 2.2 Anomie and the Collapse of Social Regulation

Émile Durkheim's concept of **anomie** provides a foundational framework for understanding how societal disruption produces psychological distress. In his landmark 1897 treatise *Suicide: A Study in Sociology*, Durkheim introduced anomic suicide as a form of self-destruction resulting from a profound state of normlessness or moral deregulation within society .

Durkheim distinguished between two fundamental social bonds: social integration (ties binding individuals to groups) and social regulation (moral constraints imposed on individual actions). Anomie represents the pathological weakening of social regulation. When the regulatory power of society over the individual is abruptly weakened or collapses—through sudden economic depression, unexpected prosperity, political revolution, or catastrophic personal loss—the individual becomes disoriented, without defined social limits, and vulnerable to despair .

Crucially, Durkheim observed that anomie is triggered not only by negative events but also by positive ones. Sudden immense prosperity dissolves previous limits, inflating expectations infinitely. Since no level of achievement ever satisfies truly unlimited desires, disappointment and meaninglessness inevitably follow .

Durkheim distinguished acute anomie (sudden loss of regulation following specific events) from chronic anomie (persistent structural conditions where norms are inherently weak or constantly shifting). Chronically anomic societies—characterized by unstable political regimes or uneven economic modernization—subject individuals to constant societal failure to provide stable moral criteria, leading to generalized anxiety, cynicism, and predisposition toward self-destructive behaviors .

Modern sociological research continues applying this framework. The high rates of "deaths of despair" (suicide and overdose) observed in deindustrialized regions of the United States are interpreted as forms of chronic anomic suicide, where the removal of stable employment and community identity creates structural normlessness .

### 2.3 Cognitive Dissonance and Implicit Conditioning

Leon Festinger's theory of cognitive dissonance (1957) explains the psychological discomfort arising from holding contradictory beliefs or behaving in ways inconsistent with one's values. Individuals experiencing dissonance are motivated to reduce it, often by changing their beliefs to align with their behavior .

However, a 2025 thesis by Ortiz argues that traditional cognitive dissonance theory fails to address structural roots. Introducing the concept of **Implicit Conditioning**, Ortiz reframes dissonance as the unconscious neurobiological, psychological, and sociocultural programming by which individuals absorb and defend systems of dominance such as white supremacy, patriarchy, ableism, and colonial capitalism .

Drawing from neuroscience, trauma studies, and decolonial theory, Ortiz argues that where cognitive dissonance pathologizes discomfort as internal confusion, Implicit Conditioning reveals it as a systemic survival mechanism rooted in historical violence, institutional feedback loops, and racialized narratives of power. The study examines neuropsychological mechanisms—amygdala activation, prefrontal suppression, reward circuitry, and mirror neuron conditioning—to argue that behavioral responses are neurologically encoded compliance with systems of power .

This reframing is essential for understanding why individuals in oppressive systems may defend those systems even against their own interests: the conditioning operates below conscious awareness, embedding dominance in the very structure of perception and response.

### 2.4 Obedience and the Distribution of Compliance

Stanley Milgram's obedience experiments (1963, 1974) provide critical empirical grounding for understanding population-level responses to authority. In a repeat of his classic experiment, **65 percent of adult male "teachers" fully obeyed the experimenter's commands to continue administering apparently dangerous shocks** to a "learner," despite the learner's earlier mention of a heart condition and despite hearing cries of protest after 150 volts and agonized protests after 330 volts .

This finding has profound implications for understanding how populations respond to enforced realities. A substantial majority—nearly two-thirds—will comply with authority even when compliance conflicts with their own perception of reality and potentially harms others. This suggests that when a society enforces a subjective reality incongruent with objective conditions, a majority may align with that reality through obedience mechanisms, while a minority bears the psychological burden of perceiving and responding to the incongruence.

### 2.5 Critical Phenomenology and the Study of Lived Experience

Critical phenomenology offers methodological resources for studying how oppression is experienced from within. As articulated in *50 Concepts for a Critical Phenomenology* (2019), edited by Weiss, Murphy, and Salamon, this approach examines how structures of power shape the lived body, perception, and social experience .

Lisa Guenther's contribution on "Critical Phenomenology" provides the framework for understanding how subjective experience is conditioned by social and political structures. Other relevant concepts include George Yancy's "Confiscated Bodies" (examining how racialized bodies are socially appropriated), Patricia Hill Collins' "Controlling Images" (stereotypes that justify oppression), and Kelly Oliver's "Witnessing" (the ethical imperative to bear witness to suffering) .

This phenomenological orientation aligns with your call to center the experience of distressed populations rather than imposing Western diagnostic categories. As you noted, the best approach is phenomenological study from the perspective of distressed people themselves, providing a more nuanced view of their world than the Western-centric view that care-givers use to approach the "problems" of others who refuse to assimilate.

## 3. Theoretical Framework: The Delta Hypothesis

### 3.1 Defining Key Concepts

**Enforced Subjective Reality:** The official narrative, social norms, and symbolic order promoted by dominant institutions (state, media, education, religious authorities) as the correct or only legitimate description of social reality. This includes foundational myths, national narratives, ideological claims, and moral frameworks.

**Objective Conditions:** Material realities, measurable outcomes, and lived experiences of the population, including economic conditions, health outcomes, social mobility, access to resources, exposure to violence, and phenomenological reports of well-being.

**The Delta:** The magnitude of discrepancy between enforced subjective reality and objective conditions. A small delta indicates relative congruence between official narratives and lived experience; a large delta indicates substantial incongruence.

### 3.2 The Core Proposition

**Primary Hypothesis:** Psychological distress at the population level is a function of the delta between enforced reality and objective conditions, moderated by the degree of enforcement and the availability of alternative interpretive frameworks.

**Mechanism:** Large deltas produce chronic cognitive dissonance in individuals who perceive the gap. This dissonance, when inescapable and unspeakable, generates:
- Anxiety (from navigating an unpredictable symbolic environment)
- Depression (from withdrawal of hope and meaning)
- Substance abuse (as self-medication against unbearable awareness)
- Psychosomatic disorders (embodied manifestation of unspeakable knowledge)

**Crucial Distinction:** The harm derives not from cultural tightness per se but from the **delta**. A tightly enforced reality with a small delta (e.g., Singapore's functional institutions and prosperity) may produce minimal distress. A loosely enforced reality with a large delta may produce significant distress through different mechanisms. What matters is the gap between what is claimed and what is lived.

### 3.3 The 80/20 Distribution: Differential Burden

Drawing on Milgram's finding that approximately 65% of participants fully obeyed authority despite conflicting evidence , we propose a population-level distribution of response to reality incongruence:

**The Majority (approximately 65-80%):** Individuals who psychologically align with or accommodate the enforced reality, even when incongruent. This alignment may occur through:
- Genuine belief (effective socialization)
- Obedience to authority (compliance without belief)
- Compartmentalization (maintaining separate public and private knowledge)
- Cognitive dissonance reduction (changing beliefs to align with required behavior)

**The Critical Minority (approximately 20-35%):** Individuals who:
1. Perceive the gap between enforced reality and objective conditions
2. Retain sufficient critical capacity to experience that gap as dissonance
3. Lack the power to change either the reality or their relationship to it

This minority includes dissidents, intellectuals, artists, journalists, and others whose social position or psychological constitution prevents full incorporation into the dominant narrative. They bear the psychological burden of accurate perception in an environment that punishes accuracy.

**Implications:** The distress observed in oppressed populations is not evenly distributed. It concentrates among those who see most clearly and can do least about what they see. This explains why dissidents in authoritarian regimes show such high rates of psychological distress—they carry the cognitive burden for the whole society.

### 3.4 The Mechanism of Harm: A Multi-Level Model

**Individual Level:**
- Chronic cognitive dissonance from performing compliance while privately perceiving incongruence
- Loss of agency from inability to act on accurate perceptions
- Hypervigilance from needing to monitor both reality and the penalties for naming it

**Interpersonal Level:**
- Anomie from loss of shared meaning when official narratives lose credibility
- Social isolation from inability to share perceptions safely
- Breakdown of trust when others cannot be relied upon to acknowledge reality

**Structural Level:**
- Implicit conditioning encoding compliance with dominance systems at neurological levels 
- Institutional feedback loops punishing accurate perception and rewarding denial
- Historical trauma transmitted across generations through embodied memory

### 3.5 The Post-Colonial Laboratory

Post-colonial societies offer unique opportunities to examine this hypothesis because:
1. **Known cause:** Colonial powers explicitly imposed alien subjective realities (legal systems, religions, languages, economic structures) incongruent with indigenous lived experience
2. **Known timeline:** The imposition, duration, and partial removal can be dated
3. **Documented effects:** Colonial administrators and post-colonial researchers documented psychological consequences
4. **Comparative variation:** Different colonial powers (British, French, Portuguese, Belgian) employed different strategies, enabling comparison
5. **Ongoing dynamics:** Neo-colonial relationships, structural adjustment, and global power asymmetries continue to shape reality enforcement

As Fanon documented, the colonized were required to internalize a worldview that systematically denied their humanity—a foundational incongruence between official reality and lived truth . This created conditions for the kind of psychological distress our hypothesis predicts.

## 4. Evidence from Post-Colonial and Contemporary Contexts

### 4.1 Colonial Algeria (Fanon's Clinical Data)

Fanon's psychiatric practice in Algeria during the independence struggle provided direct clinical observation of the psychological effects of colonial reality enforcement. He documented:
- Depression and anxiety among Algerians forced to navigate a world that denied their humanity
- Psychosomatic disorders expressing unspeakable rage and despair
- Violent outbursts misdiagnosed as pathology but actually expressing resistance to dehumanization
- Psychological effects on French torturers, deformed by maintaining an unsustainable reality 

Fanon's crucial insight: the pathology was not in the individual but in the colonial structure itself. Treatment required not adjustment to oppression but liberation from it.

### 4.2 Gaza (Contemporary)

The 2024 Farajallah study of psychological trauma in Gaza provides contemporary evidence linking political injustice directly to mental health outcomes. The research documents how the Israeli blockade and frequent attacks have generated a pervasive environment of violence continuously undermining mental health. Many are affected by Complex Continuous Traumatic Stress (CCTS), arising from chronic trauma exposure without respite .

Manifestations include:
- Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD)
- Anxiety and depression
- Developmental delays in children
- Cognitive impairments

Crucially, the study concludes that "meaningful psychological recovery requires addressing the root causes of the ongoing conflict" and that "the mental health crisis in Gaza is deeply interconnected with the Israeli Occupation" . This directly supports our claim that political reality enforcement produces psychological distress that cannot be adequately addressed through individual treatment alone.

### 4.3 The Caribbean (Mid-20th Century)

Historical records from 1950s Caribbean indicate that alcoholism was described as the "single biggest mental health issue" in parts of the region, linked to the psychological and social legacies of colonialism—trauma of slavery and indenture, instability of family structures, and the ongoing reality of not being "master in one's own house." Substance abuse functioned as coping with feelings of inferiority, emasculation, and powerlessness rooted in historical and social forces.

### 4.4 Indigenous Peoples in Settler Colonies

Indigenous communities in Canada, the United States, Australia, and New Zealand experience disproportionately high rates of psychological distress, substance abuse, and suicide. Research links these outcomes to:
- Historical trauma from colonization, forced assimilation, and residential schools
- Ongoing systemic discrimination and marginalization
- Forced incorporation into a dominant culture that denies indigenous worldviews
- Chronic incongruence between imposed Western frameworks and indigenous lived experience

These communities represent perhaps the clearest contemporary examples of populations forced to navigate a delta between enforced reality (the dominant society's narratives of equality and opportunity) and objective conditions (systematic disadvantage and cultural erasure).

### 4.5 Post-Independence Africa and Asia

The decades following independence in African and Asian nations provide natural experiments in delta fluctuation. As new nations constructed post-colonial identities, the gap between nationalist rhetoric and material conditions varied significantly. Periods of revolutionary hope (small delta between promise and possibility) gave way to periods of disillusionment (large delta) as independence failed to deliver prosperity and dignity. Longitudinal mental health data from these transitions could test whether distress indicators correlate with delta magnitude.

## 5. Addressing Falsification Attempts

### 5.1 The Singapore Counter-Example

**Objection:** Singapore enforces a highly rigid subjective reality through strict laws, state propaganda, and punitive enforcement, yet shows low rates of substance abuse and high life satisfaction. Does this disprove the hypothesis?

**Response:** This counter-example actually refines rather than refutes the hypothesis. Singapore's tightly enforced reality has a relatively **small delta** from objective conditions for most citizens—functional institutions, economic prosperity, public safety, efficient services. The distress we predict arises from the **gap** between claim and reality, not from enforcement per se. When enforcement maintains a reality that actually delivers on its promises, the psychological toll is minimized.

### 5.2 The Nordic Paradox

**Objection:** Nordic countries have relatively loose cultural norms, high transparency, and small deltas between official narratives and lived experience, yet show high rates of antidepressant consumption and significant winter depression. Does this disprove the hypothesis?

**Response:** Our hypothesis addresses distress arising specifically from **socially constructed** incongruence—gaps between enforced reality and lived experience. Nordic distress may arise from other factors: climate (seasonal affective disorder), genetic factors, cultural norms around emotional expression, or simply more accurate reporting and diagnosis. The existence of distress from other causes does not negate the specific mechanism we describe.

### 5.3 Individual Differences

**Objection:** Within the same oppressive society, some individuals develop severe mental illness while others remain psychologically healthy. Does this variation disprove a social-level hypothesis?

**Response:** Our hypothesis operates at the population level, predicting aggregate patterns while acknowledging individual variation. Genetic factors account for approximately 30-50% of variance in risk for major mental disorders; temperament, resilience, social support, and life history also matter. The claim is that societal reality incongruence functions as a **contextual risk factor** shifting population distributions, not a deterministic cause affecting everyone uniformly. The 80/20 distribution specifically predicts that a minority will bear the heaviest burden, consistent with observed variation.

### 5.4 Reverse Causality

**Objection:** Perhaps societies with high rates of mental illness and substance abuse are more likely to develop authoritarian, reality-enforcing regimes as populations seek order and certainty in response to chaos.

**Response:** This is a valid concern, and the relationship is likely bidirectional and iterative. Social distress may indeed increase demand for strong leadership and simple narratives. However, the post-colonial context provides some temporal clarity: colonial imposition preceded both the creation of post-colonial institutions and the emergence of documented psychological distress patterns. The causal arrow from imposition to distress is historically anchored, even if subsequent dynamics involve feedback loops.

### 5.5 The Measurement Problem

**Objection:** Key concepts—"enforced reality," "objective conditions," "the delta"—are too vague to measure empirically, making the hypothesis unfalsifiable.

**Response:** While measurement is challenging, it is not impossible. Operationalization strategies include:
- Content analysis of official narratives versus statistical indicators (economic data, health outcomes, inequality measures)
- Phenomenological interviews documenting lived experience gaps
- Tracking suppression of dissident voices as indicator of regime anxiety about reality gaps
- Longitudinal analysis of mental health indicators across periods of political transition
- Comparative studies across societies with varying delta magnitudes

## 6. Methodological Proposals for Empirical Testing

### 6.1 Phenomenological Research Design

Consistent with your call to center the experience of distressed populations, we recommend **critical phenomenological research**  involving:

- In-depth, semi-structured interviews with diverse populations in post-colonial contexts
- Focus on participants' own accounts of suffering and its perceived causes
- Attention to the "reality gaps" they identify—where official/social narrative diverges from experience
- Analysis of how participants navigate the gap between what they are told and what they know
- Attention to embodied experience, not just cognitive evaluation

This approach respects participants as experts on their own experience rather than imposing external diagnostic categories that may pathologize intelligible responses to oppression.

### 6.2 Historical Epidemiology

- Compile mental health indicators (suicide rates, substance abuse treatment admissions, psychiatric hospitalization, antidepressant prescriptions) across the 20th and 21st centuries for selected societies
- Overlay key dates: colonial imposition, independence, major policy shifts, economic crises, political transitions
- Test for correlations between periods of heightened delta (measured through historical analysis of official narratives versus documented conditions) and distress indicators
- Control for alternative explanations (economic cycles, demographic changes, reporting practices)

### 6.3 Comparative Analysis

Compare post-colonial societies with:
- Former colonizing nations (similar objective challenges, different relationship to imposed reality)
- Non-colonized societies with similar economic profiles (e.g., Thailand vs. Malaysia)
- Different colonial experiences (British indirect rule vs. French assimilation)
- Different post-colonial trajectories (revolutionary vs. negotiated independence)

### 6.4 Dissident Tracking as an Indicator

Monitor suppression of alternative narratives as a proxy for regime anxiety about reality gaps. Indicators include:
- Arrests and harassment of journalists, academics, artists
- Censorship and media control
- Sanctions against those deviating from official narratives (e.g., EU sanctions against Jacques Baud for Ukraine analysis)
- Intensity of online narrative policing

Correlate suppression intensity with population-level mental health indicators. Hypothesis: When regimes feel their reality claims are threatened, they intensify suppression, which in turn increases psychological distress in the critical minority who perceive the gap.

### 6.5 Neurobiological Measures

Building on Ortiz's work on Implicit Conditioning , incorporate neurobiological measures where feasible:
- Stress biomarkers (cortisol, inflammatory markers) in populations experiencing high delta
- Neuroimaging studies of response to reality-incongruent information
- Intergenerational transmission of trauma effects through epigenetic markers

## 7. Implications

### 7.1 For Mental Health Practice

**From Individual to Structural:** If our hypothesis is correct, mental health interventions cannot stop at individual treatment. They must address the political and symbolic environments that produce distress. As the Gaza study concludes, "meaningful psychological recovery requires addressing the root causes" .

**Liberatory Approaches:** Practices that restore agency, validate accurate perception, and support collective action may be more therapeutic than approaches that encourage adjustment to oppression. Community-based, peer-supported, and politically engaged mental health work aligns with this orientation.

**Diagnostic Caution:** Clinicians must be cautious about pathologizing responses that may be intelligible reactions to unlivable conditions. What presents as "paranoia" may be accurate perception of threat; what presents as "depression" may be appropriate despair in the face of hopeless circumstances.

**Cultural Humility:** Western mental health frameworks cannot be exported uncritically to post-colonial contexts without attending to their own historical role in colonialism. Decolonizing mental health requires centering local knowledge and lived experience .

### 7.2 For Political Theory

**Reality Enforcement as Core Mechanism:** Authoritarianism may be understood not merely as power concentration but as systematic reality enforcement. The psychological costs of maintaining fictional narratives affect both rulers and ruled.

**The Importance of Dissent:** Dissident voices serve not only political but also psychological functions—they articulate the gap that many perceive but cannot name, potentially reducing the isolation and cognitive burden on the critical minority. Their suppression is therefore not only a political crime but a public health harm.

**Institutional Design:** Institutions that maintain small deltas—that align their claims with verifiable reality and responsive practice—may promote population mental health regardless of their specific cultural form. Transparency, accountability, and responsiveness are not just democratic values but psychological necessities.

### 7.3 For Future Research

**Cross-Cultural Collaboration:** Testing this hypothesis requires collaboration with researchers in post-colonial societies who bring linguistic competence, cultural knowledge, and methodological sophistication.

**Longitudinal Studies:** Following communities across political transitions would provide the strongest evidence for causal relationships between delta changes and mental health outcomes.

**Intervention Research:** Studying whether interventions that reduce delta (truth-telling initiatives, institutional reform, restorative justice) produce measurable mental health improvements.

**Intersectional Analysis:** Examining how multiple dimensions of identity (race, class, gender, colonialism) compound or buffer the effects of reality incongruence.

## 8. Conclusion

We began with a provocation: oppression is a society that puts symbols and rules above life itself. We have attempted to translate this intuition into a testable hypothesis: that the gap between enforced reality and lived experience—the delta—produces measurable psychological distress at the population level, concentrated among those who perceive the gap most clearly and can do least about it.

The post-colonial world provides both evidence and ethical imperative for this inquiry. Colonized peoples were forced to live within realities that denied their humanity—a foundational incongruence whose psychological effects persist across generations. Contemporary research in Gaza, among Indigenous communities, and in post-colonial societies worldwide confirms that political injustice is not merely background but cause of mental suffering.

Milgram's 65% remind us that a majority may align with enforced reality even when it contradicts their own perception. The remaining minority—the 20-35% who see and cannot stop seeing—bear a disproportionate psychological burden. Their distress is not individual pathology but social truth carried in bodies and minds that refuse to stop registering what is real.

A society's mental health may be less a matter of individual resilience than of the distance between its stories and its truths. Healing, then, requires not better medication alone but better alignment between what societies claim and what they deliver—between their symbols and the lives those symbols are meant to serve.
