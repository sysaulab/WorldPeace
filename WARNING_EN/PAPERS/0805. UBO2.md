## 1. Executive Summary

**Universal Basic Outcome & Ownership (UBO2)** is a two-tier socio-economic architecture that guarantees every person a dignified material floor within planetary boundaries, while grounding that guarantee in locally owned and governed community assets.

The **planetary layer** defines an ecologically safe global surplus, distributes it equally to every person as a dividend, and mandates that a set of core outcomes—housing, food, health, and education—be unconditionally available to all. The **local layer** consists of voluntary, geographically rooted Community Ownership Funds that residents form to pool dividends, hold productive assets, and deliver the Outcome Core directly. A legally required “give-half” rule ensures that a portion of all distributed surplus flows through a web of peer-to-peer recognition, building social cohesion and surfacing hidden value.

UBO2 synthesises the principled universalism of a global basic outcome with the institutional concreteness of local collective ownership. It is neither a state handout nor a market commodity; it is a property right in the common wealth of the Earth and the community.

## 2. The Two Lineages

**Universal Basic Outcome (Outcome-UBO)** started from the insight that cash alone cannot guarantee a dignified life when markets for essential goods fail. It proposed that a portion of society’s productive capacity be ring-fenced to provide housing, food, healthcare, and education as direct outcomes, while an ecologically bounded surplus is distributed equally to all, with a mandatory 50% recognition gift to others. The framework was planetary in ambition, universal in scope, and governed by a hard ecological constraint: surplus extraction must remain minimal until biodiversity recovers.

**Universal Basic Ownership (Ownership-UBO)** began from the opposite direction: the collapse of post-industrial communities and the failure of means-tested welfare. It proposed a neighbourhood-based fund that owns land, energy, housing, and enterprises. Residents become members through a buy-in or work-in, receive a dividend, and govern the fund democratically. A bulk transfer from the state consolidates welfare budgets into a single, dignity-preserving block grant. The same give-half rule turns passive income into an active social signal.

The synthesis recognises that these two models are not competitors but complementary layers of a single system. The Outcome-UBO provides the *universal floor and ecological discipline*; the Ownership-UBO provides the *institutional vehicle for local delivery, agency, and wealth-building*. Together, they form **UBO2**: a nested architecture of global rights and local ownership.

## 3. Core Architecture of UBO2

UBO2 rests on five interlocking principles:

1.  **Planetary Boundaries First.** The total material throughput of the human economy is capped at a level that permits biodiversity recovery. All dividends and provisioning flow from a surplus calculated within that cap.
2.  **Universal Outcome Entitlement.** Every person, by virtue of existence, has a right to adequate housing, nutrition, healthcare, and lifelong education. This is a claim on real resources, not a cash voucher vulnerable to inflation or market failure.
3.  **Equal Dividend from the Commons.** The ecologically safe surplus is divided equally among all humans, respecting the principle that the Earth’s regenerative capacity is a common inheritance.
4.  **Local Ownership Vehicles.** Individuals and communities voluntarily pool their dividends and labour into democratically governed funds that acquire and manage the assets needed to deliver the Outcome Core and generate further local surplus.
5.  **Recognition-Based Distribution.** A structural requirement that 50% of all dividend income be given to others as an act of recognition creates an economy of gratitude that weaves the social fabric, rewards prosocial contribution, and prevents calcification.

## 4. The Planetary Layer: Ecological Surplus and the Outcome Guarantee

### 4.1 The Ecological Governor

A global or regional body, informed by Earth system science, determines the maximum sustainable material throughput of the economy. Key indicators include biodiversity abundance, habitat integrity, freshwater availability, and atmospheric carbon concentration. The rule is strict:

> *Surplus extraction for human use beyond the Outcome Core shall be held at minimal levels until biodiversity indicators show sustained recovery. Thereafter, the surplus cap may be gradually relaxed, but never beyond a level that risks ecological degradation.*

This governor replaces GDP growth as the primary macroeconomic constraint.

### 4.2 The Outcome Core

A defined bundle of essential goods and services—shelter, nutritionally complete food, comprehensive healthcare, and lifelong education—is declared a universal entitlement. The total resources required to provision this Core for the global population are deducted from the sustainable surplus before any dividend is calculated.

### 4.3 The Global Dividend

The remaining ecologically safe surplus is divided equally among all humans and distributed as a regular dividend. This dividend is unconditional, non-withdrawable by any state, and constitutes the individual’s share of the planetary commons. Its distribution is handled through a federation of national and regional trust institutions.

## 5. The Local Layer: Community Ownership Funds

The global dividend and the Outcome Core require an institutional form that can deliver goods and services, hold assets, and channel recognition. That form is the **Community Ownership Fund**—a legally constituted, geographically anchored, member-governed entity.

### 5.1 Legal Structure and Asset Lock

Each Fund is a Community Benefit Society, multi-stakeholder cooperative, or equivalent legal form with a perpetual asset lock. Assets held by the Fund—land, housing, energy infrastructure, food production, care enterprises, digital networks—cannot be privatised or extracted for individual gain. They are held in trust for current and future residents.

### 5.2 Formation and Asset Aggregation

Funds form voluntarily. A founding group of residents in a defined neighbourhood, town, or rural district adopts a charter and begins aggregating assets through:

- **Voluntary contribution:** Residents contribute property, land, or capital to the Fund in exchange for lifetime membership and, optionally, a higher initial dividend tranche that vests over time.
- **Bulk transfer from the state:** Public land, vacant housing, and underutilised infrastructure within the Fund’s territory are transferred at nominal value.
- **Purchase and buy-back:** The Fund, in partnership with a local financial institution, purchases strategic assets from willing sellers, particularly absentee-owned rental properties, vacant commercial space, or failing enterprises.

### 5.3 Relationship to the Global Dividend

Residents may choose to direct a portion of their global dividend into their local Fund as an additional capital contribution, in exchange for membership or enhanced dividend rights. Alternatively, the Fund may receive a block allocation of dividends from its members, pooling them to finance Outcome Core provisioning and asset acquisition. The dividend thus becomes both an individual income stream and a source of collective investment.

## 6. The Flow of Value: From Global Dividend to Local Provisioning

The system operates as a continuous value cycle:

1.  **Ecological surplus is calculated** at the planetary level and distributed as a per-capita global dividend.
2.  **Each person receives their dividend**, a portion of which they may commit to their local Community Ownership Fund as a membership contribution or capital injection.
3.  **The Fund delivers the Outcome Core**—housing, food, healthcare, education—to all residents within its territory, whether they are members or not. This delivery is financed by a combination of:
    - A consolidated **bulk transfer from the state** (the repurposed welfare, health, and development budgets),
    - The **committed global dividends** of members,
    - **Revenue generated by Fund-owned enterprises** (energy sales, food production, rental income, services).
4.  **After covering Core obligations and reinvestment**, the Fund distributes any local surplus to its members as a **local dividend**. The global dividend remains a separate, unconditional stream.
5.  **Members apply the Give-Half rule** to their combined dividend income (global + local), allocating 50% to other members or community entities as recognition tips.

This structure ensures that the universal entitlement is met locally, while surplus circulates through both global equity and local democratic control.

## 7. Membership, the Give-Half Protocol, and the Social Graph

### 7.1 Membership Tiers

- **Non-members:** Receive the Outcome Core (food and shelter) unconditionally. They receive their global dividend but have no governance rights in the Fund and do not participate in the local recognition protocol. They are permanent guests, cared for but without an ownership stake.
- **Members:** Have bought in (with capital or committed dividends) or worked in (completing a defined period of labour in Fund enterprises). Members receive the local dividend, vote on Fund governance, and participate fully in the Give-Half protocol. They are co-owners.

### 7.2 The Recognition Pathway to Membership

As developed in earlier work, a non-member who receives substantial recognition tips from members—for care work, community support, kindness, or any prosocial contribution—may accumulate those tips and use them to buy into membership. This creates a “recognised in” pathway that does not require standard employment or a work-in period. The community itself, through the distributed act of tipping, validates the contribution.

### 7.3 The Give-Half Rule

Every member must allocate 50% of their combined dividend income (global + local) to another member or a recognised community entity before the remaining 50% becomes spendable. The allocation is:

- **Voluntary in recipient choice** (the giver decides who to recognise),
- **Recorded in a transparent recognition ledger** (with an optional public note),
- **Not subject to approval** (no one judges the validity of the reason),
- **Not taxable** (it is a gift, not income for the giver).

### 7.4 Social Dynamics

The Give-Half protocol creates a living social graph. It surfaces hidden contributors, identifies socially isolated individuals for community support, and generates a parallel esteem economy that rewards prosocial behaviour. Collusion (swapping halves symmetrically) is permitted but socially self-limiting: colluders remain invisible in the graph and accrue no social capital. Over time, the intrinsic rewards of genuine recognition and the desire for community visibility re-socialise most participants.

## 8. The State as Enabler: Bulk Transfer and Regulatory Compact

### 8.1 The Bulk Transfer Compact

Higher levels of government (national or regional) negotiate a compact with each Community Ownership Fund. The state consolidates all existing means-tested welfare, public health, housing benefit, and local development budgets attributable to the Fund’s territory into a single annual block grant. This grant is:

- **Unconditional in use** (the Fund decides how to meet the Outcome Core),
- **Conditional on the Fund meeting the Core guarantee** and conducting an annual, non-intrusive population survey,
- **Capped and predictable**, allowing the state to achieve budgetary savings and the Fund to plan long-term.

### 8.2 The Annual Survey

The sole reporting mechanism back to the state is an annual headcount survey. It records the number of residents and their membership status. No individual income, employment, disability, or household data is collected. This dismantles the bureaucratic apparatus of welfare policing and restores dignity.

### 8.3 Regulatory Sandbox

Initial Funds will require exemptions from standard welfare regulations, minimum wage laws (for cooperative member-work), and local taxation rules. A regulatory sandbox framework, granted for a defined pilot period with rigorous evaluation, allows the model to operate without immediate conflict with existing statutes.

### 8.4 Compartmentalisation of Failure

Each Fund is legally and financially independent. Failure of one Fund—due to mismanagement, demographic collapse, or internal conflict—does not cascade. The bulk transfer acts as a floor, sustaining basic provisioning while the community restructures or neighbouring Funds assist. This containment stands in stark contrast to the systemic risk of a nationally administered UBI.

## 9. Governance Stack: Global, National, and Local

UBO2 operates as a nested governance system:

| Level | Function | Institution |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| **Global** | Ecological surplus calculation; dividend issuance; boundary-setting | A federation of regional Earth System Trusts, governed by scientific bodies and citizen assemblies |
| **National/Regional** | Bulk transfer negotiation; regulatory sandbox; legal framework for Funds; distribution of global dividend to individuals | National Dividend Authority and a Devolution Compact Office |
| **Local** | Asset ownership; Outcome Core delivery; local dividend distribution; Give-Half protocol; membership governance | Community Ownership Fund (one-member-one-vote) |

The global level sets the ecological boundary and guarantees the universal dividend. The national level enables and funds the transition. The local level owns, governs, and lives the daily reality of the system.

## 10. Implementation Roadmap

### Phase 1: Foundational Pilots (Years 1–3)
- Identify 3–5 willing communities across different contexts (post-industrial, rural, urban periphery).
- Negotiate regulatory sandbox status and a 5-year bulk transfer pilot with national/regional governments.
- Form the first Community Ownership Funds; aggregate initial assets; begin Outcome Core provisioning.
- Launch the Give-Half protocol with a simple digital or paper-based recognition ledger.

### Phase 2: Integration and Learning (Years 3–5)
- Pilot communities begin receiving a simulated global dividend (funded by a combination of state contribution and philanthropic seed capital), testing the integration of universal dividend with local Funds.
- Establish a lightweight National Dividend Authority to model ecological surplus calculations and prepare for live implementation.
- Evaluate outcomes: health, wellbeing, employment, social cohesion, fiscal efficiency, ecological impact.

### Phase 3: Scaling the Local Layer (Years 5–10)
- Open the model to any community that adopts the standard charter and meets governance requirements.
- Federate existing Funds for mutual credit, knowledge sharing, and political voice.
- Scale the bulk transfer compact as evidence of savings and improved outcomes accumulates.

### Phase 4: Activating the Planetary Layer (Years 10+)
- With a network of Funds proving the model, negotiate the international treaty framework for a binding ecological governor and a universal global dividend.
- Transition from simulated to live global dividend, funded by a combination of carbon fees, resource royalties, and financial transaction levies calibrated to the ecological cap.

## 11. Conclusion: A New Social Contract at Scale

UBO2 is a bet that the deepest human needs—for security, dignity, belonging, and purpose—can be met without destroying the living systems on which we depend. It marries the universalism of a global outcome guarantee with the agency and concreteness of local ownership. It replaces the conditional, atomising logic of welfare with a property right in the commons, and it embeds the economy in a web of recognition that makes visible what markets and bureaucracies cannot see.

The architecture is designed for emergence. Communities can begin now, with the tools of cooperative law, community land trusts, and local currencies, building the local layer from the ground up while the planetary layer is negotiated over the longer arc of political change. Each Fund that succeeds becomes a proof point. Each recognition tip cast is a quiet refutation of the idea that we are merely isolated consumers.

The future is owned. The question is whether we will own it together.
