
# Projecting the Moral Field into Human Action: A Constructive‑Destructive Spectrum Derived from Systems Ethics

**Authors**: Sylvain Saucier  
**Date**: March 2026 (revised)

## Abstract

This paper presents a unified moral framework derived from systems theory—centered on **feedback**, **curiosity**, and **respect**—and demonstrates how it can be applied to any human movement, political ideology, or cultural tradition. By mapping movements onto a three‑dimensional moral space and retaining the cube’s eight attractors while also collapsing it into a composite index, we derive a linear spectrum from **destructive** (system‑eroding) to **constructive** (system‑healing). This spectrum cuts across religious and ideological identities, revealing that moral alignment is not determined by a tradition’s label but by how well its practices preserve feedback, cultivate curiosity, and protect complexity. We apply this lens to the Israeli‑Palestinian conflict as a case study, showing that the framework provides a common language for cross‑partisan critique and systemic peacebuilding. The paper deliberately avoids translating the three principles into any religious vocabulary; local actors and theologians are best positioned to undertake their own mappings. The framework is offered as a culturally agnostic tool for those ready to move beyond symbolic fights toward systemic health.

**Keywords**: systems ethics, moral spectrum, constructive‑destructive spectrum, feedback, curiosity, respect, complexity, peacebuilding

## 1. Introduction

Contemporary moral and political conflicts are often framed as clashes of civilizations, religions, or irreconcilable values. This framing obscures a deeper truth: **the conditions for a living system to learn, adapt, and persist** are universal, and violations of those conditions produce the same pathologies—domination, dogmatism, and destruction—regardless of cultural origin.

This paper introduces a unified moral lens grounded in systems theory and operationalized through three principles: **feedback**, **curiosity**, and **respect**. These principles are not culturally arbitrary; they describe the necessary conditions for any complex system to remain healthy. Their normative force arises from the observation that systems violating them inevitably degrade, while those embodying them flourish.

We map human movements (political, social, ideological) onto a three‑dimensional moral space defined by these axes. We then identify the eight attractors (corners of the cube) as archetypal moral configurations. Finally, we collapse the cube into a linear composite index ranging from **destructive** (−6) to **constructive** (+6). This two‑level approach (cube + spectrum) provides both analytical depth and a simple, communicable metric.

The framework is applied here to the Israeli‑Palestinian conflict as an illustrative case study. However, the framework is generic and can be used by any community or analyst to assess any movement or set of practices.

## 2. The Moral Field: Three Universal Principles

### 2.1 Definition of Principles

Moral action (from a systems perspective) is that which:

1. **Preserves or restores feedback** – ensuring that actors sense the consequences of their actions, especially on the vulnerable. Without feedback, a system becomes blind and self‑destructive.
2. **Cultivates curiosity** – actively seeking new configurations that better serve the whole. Without curiosity, a system cannot adapt to changing circumstances.
3. **Protects complexity** – safeguarding diversity, vulnerability, and interdependence, as complexity is the source of resilience. Without respect for complexity, a system collapses into brittle uniformity.

These principles are **descriptive** (they characterize healthy systems) and **prescriptive** (to the extent one values system persistence and flourishing).

### 2.2 Cultural Agnosticism

The framework does not claim that any particular cultural or religious tradition already embodies these principles. It offers a **generic ethical language** that local actors—including theologians, community leaders, and activists—may choose to translate into their own vocabularies or reject altogether. This paper makes no such translations. A separate section explains this deliberate choice.

## 3. Mapping Movements onto the Moral Field

### 3.1 Scoring Methodology

We evaluate movements based on:

- Official doctrines and authoritative statements.
- Institutional practices (treatment of dissent, minorities, outsiders).
- Historical behavior, especially toward the vulnerable.
- Self‑understanding: does the movement prioritize systemic health over symbolic victories?

Each movement receives a score from **−2** (severely destructive) to **+2** (highly constructive) on each of the three axes. Scores are interpretive but grounded in documented evidence. For the case study below, scores reflect the authors’ assessment; others may differ.

### 3.3 The Cube Attractors (Eight Corners)

Before collapsing into a linear spectrum, we identify the eight moral attractors at the cube’s corners. Each represents a pure (ideal‑typical) configuration.

| (Feedback, Curiosity, Respect) | Attractor Name | Description |
|-------------------------------|----------------|-------------|
| (−, −, −) | **Barbarism** | Total system destruction |
| (+, −, −) | **Zealot** | Dogmatic harm‑awareness, no curiosity or respect |
| (−, +, −) | **Puppeteer** | Amoral novelty‑seeking, blind to consequences |
| (−, −, +) | **Preserver** | Protective but rigid and blind |
| (+, +, −) | **Unmaker** | Reckless transformation, no respect for existing complexity |
| (+, −, +) | **Guardian** | Rigid compassion, high feedback & respect, no curiosity |
| (−, +, +) | **Dreamer** | Naive care, high curiosity & respect, no feedback |
| (+, +, +) | **Wisdom** | Integrated systemic health |

These attractors help diagnose specific failure modes. For example, a movement scoring (+2, −2, +2) is a *Guardian*—protective and sensitive to harm but unable to adapt. A movement scoring (−2, +2, +2) is a *Dreamer*—caring and open but blind to actual consequences.

## 4. Interpreting the World with a Unified Moral Lens

### 4.1 The Israeli‑Palestinian Conflict Re‑Examined

When the moral field is applied to the Israeli‑Palestinian conflict, the conflict is revealed not as a clash of nations or religions, but as a **systemic pathology** involving multiple destructive attractors. The lowest‑scoring movements (A, B, C, D) share a structure of broken feedback, killed curiosity, and destroyed complexity—despite having opposing political goals. They fight for **symbols** (land, holy sites, sovereignty, martyrdom) over **principles** (feedback, curiosity, respect). From a systems perspective, that choice is definitionally destructive, regardless of which side makes it.

Conversely, the highest‑scoring movements (M, N, and to a lesser extent I, J, K, L) share a commitment to making harm visible, seeking new configurations, and protecting the vulnerable—again across the divide. This lens dissolves the binary of “pro‑X vs. pro‑Y” and replaces it with a moral axis: **constructive vs. destructive**. It allows external observers to support critical human rights groups within the dominant group and liberationist movements within the marginalized group simultaneously, because both are working to restore feedback and protect complexity.

The framework does not ask whether a movement’s goals are just or its grievances legitimate. It asks only: does the movement’s actual behavior preserve feedback, cultivate curiosity, and protect complexity? A movement fighting for a just cause with destructive methods remains destructive. Conversely, a movement fighting for an unjust cause with constructive methods is, by this metric, more morally aligned. This is counterintuitive to many, but it follows from a systems view: destroying the system’s capacity to learn and adapt ultimately undermines any cause, just or unjust.

### 4.2 Generalizability Beyond the Case Study

The framework is generic. Any analyst can apply it to any context: political movements within a single country, corporate behavior, international alliances, or even personal relationships. The three axes remain the same; only the scoring evidence changes.

For example, in the United States:
- A movement that suppresses dissent, rejects evidence, and attacks vulnerable minorities would score low (destructive).
- A movement that amplifies marginalized voices, encourages internal debate, and protects complex ecosystems (social or natural) would score high (constructive).

The real moral battle is not between pre‑labeled groups but between **life‑affirming and life‑destroying attractors** within all groups.

## 5. On the Deliberate Absence of Theological Language

The authors have chosen not to translate the three principles (feedback, curiosity, respect) into the specific vocabularies of Judaism, Christianity, Islam, or any other religious tradition. This is a **deliberate methodological decision**, not an omission.

**Reasons**:

1. **Respect for local interpretation**: Religious traditions contain rich internal ethical resources. Outsiders imposing a translation risks distortion, oversimplification, and intellectual imperialism. Believers and their theologians are best positioned to ask, “Does our tradition value feedback? How? Where has it failed?”
2. **Neutral common language**: By remaining secular in articulation, the framework can serve as a shared language between religious and non‑religious actors, and between different religious traditions, without requiring any participant to accept another’s theology.
3. **Gradual movement, not revolution**: The framework is offered to those *ready* to move beyond symbolic fights. Theological fundamentalists may reject it entirely. That is fine. The framework does not claim to persuade everyone; it provides a tool for those already seeking systemic health.
4. **Avoiding false authority**: The authors are not theologians. Claiming that “curiosity is actually *ijtihad*” would be a substantive theological claim requiring defense. This paper makes no such claims.

**Invitation**: Readers from religious backgrounds are welcome to undertake their own mappings, using their tradition’s concepts if they find it helpful. But the paper itself does not do that work. The framework is complete as stated.

## 6. Implications for Peacebuilding and Ethics

### 6.1 Intra‑Movement Critique

The framework gives any group a mirror. A movement can ask itself: Do we sense the consequences of our actions on the vulnerable? Do we encourage dissent and new ideas? Do we protect diversity and interdependence? Low scores indicate pathology, regardless of the movement’s stated ideals.

### 6.2 Cross‑Partisan Alliances

Movements with high composite scores—even from opposing political sides—share a fundamental orientation. They can recognize each other as allies in restoring systemic health. Concrete examples include joint Israeli‑Palestinian peace organizations (e.g., Combatants for Peace, Women Wage Peace) and cross‑ideological human rights coalitions.

### 6.3 Policy and Intervention

External actors (governments, international organizations, funders) can use the spectrum to condition support: resources should favor movements that demonstrate feedback, curiosity, and respect. This is not a neutral stance but an ethical one—choosing to invest in systemic health rather than fueling systemic pathology.

### 6.4 The Peace That Does Not Erase

The unified lens clarifies the kind of peace we seek: not the peace of the graveyard (where difference is eliminated), but the peace of the living system—dynamic, diverse, with feedback flowing, curiosity alive, and the vulnerable protected.

## 7. Delicacy and Responsibility

Applying a moral framework to real‑world movements is delicate. Scores are not final judgments but invitations to deeper engagement. They are designed to be contested, refined, and improved through dialogue. No movement is monolithic; internal diversity exists. The goal is to illuminate patterns, not to condemn or canonize.

Moreover, the framework should be applied with historical and power‑sensitive awareness. A movement that scores low due to trauma or self‑defense may require different intervention than one that scores low from dominance. The cube attractors (e.g., Guardian vs. Zealot) help here, but final judgment always requires local knowledge.

## 8. Conclusion

We have presented a unified moral framework derived from systems theory—feedback, curiosity, and respect—and operationalized it as a three‑dimensional space with eight attractors (from Barbarism to Wisdom) and a linear composite spectrum from Destructive to Constructive. This framework is culturally agnostic, deliberately avoids theological translation, and can be applied to any human movement or set of practices.

Using the Israeli‑Palestinian conflict as a case study, we showed that the real moral divide is not between nations or religions but between constructive and destructive attractors that cut across all sides. Fighting for symbols over principles is definitionally destructive. The path to peace lies in strengthening constructive movements—those that restore feedback, cultivate curiosity, and protect complexity—regardless of their nominal affiliation.

The work ahead is to use this lens to build bridges: between critical human rights groups and liberationist movements, between reformists and ecumenical networks, between all who refuse to let the attractors of destruction have the final word. In doing so, we move toward the peace of the living system—a peace of justice, complexity, and the flourishing of all.
