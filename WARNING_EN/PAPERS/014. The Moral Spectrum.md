## Abstract

This paper presents a unified moral framework derived from systems theory—centered on **feedback**, **curiosity**, and **respect**—and demonstrates how it can be projected into the symbolic and theological universe of the Abrahamic traditions (Judaism, Christianity, Islam). By mapping key theological and political movements onto a three‑dimensional moral space and collapsing it into a composite index, we derive a linear spectrum from “animalistic” (system‑destroying) to “divine‑seeking” (system‑healing). This spectrum cuts across religious identities, revealing that moral alignment is not determined by tradition but by the depth to which a movement draws on its own ethical resources. We apply this unified lens to interpret the Israeli‑Palestinian conflict and other contemporary moral crises, showing that the framework provides a common language for intra‑faith critique, inter‑faith alliance, and systemic peacebuilding. The paper concludes that projecting the moral field into the Abrahamic universe allows us to see beyond ideological polarizations and toward a peace that honors complexity.

**Keywords**: systems ethics, Abrahamic traditions, moral spectrum, Israeli‑Palestinian conflict, feedback, curiosity, respect, composite index, peacebuilding

## 1. Introduction

The Abrahamic traditions have shaped the moral imagination of half of humanity. Yet when their adherents clash—as in the Israeli‑Palestinian conflict, sectarian violence in the Middle East, or culture wars in the West—the conflict is often framed as a clash of civilizations or an irreconcilable opposition of values. This framing obscures a deeper truth: **the ethical core of each Abrahamic tradition converges on the same systemic conditions for life**, while the corruption of those traditions produces the same pathologies of domination, idolatry, and destruction.

This paper introduces a unified moral lens grounded in systems theory and operationalized through three principles: **feedback**, **curiosity**, and **respect**. We show that these principles are already encoded in the sacred texts, legal traditions, and spiritual practices of Judaism, Christianity, and Islam. By projecting this moral field into the symbolic universe of the Abrahamic traditions, we construct a common metric for assessing the moral commitment of diverse movements—from religious Zionism to liberation theology, from political Islam to ecumenical Christianity. We then collapse the three‑dimensional space into a composite index, yielding a linear spectrum that ranks movements from system‑destroying (animalistic) to system‑healing (divine‑seeking). This spectrum reveals that the deepest moral resources within each tradition align across religious boundaries, while the most destructive forces also share a common structure of broken feedback, killed curiosity, and destroyed complexity.

The goal is not to homogenize the traditions or to impose a secular metric on sacred categories. Rather, it is to enable **intra‑faith critique** from within each tradition’s own vocabulary, **inter‑faith alliance** across traditions, and a **systemic approach to peace** that addresses the root pathologies of the conflict.

## 2. The Moral Field: Principles and Their Abrahamic Expressions

### 2.1 Three Universal Principles

The “Morality of Systems” framework (2026) defines moral action as that which:

1. **Preserves or restores feedback** – ensuring that actors sense the consequences of their actions, especially on the vulnerable.
2. **Cultivates curiosity** – actively seeking new configurations that better serve the whole.
3. **Protects complexity** – safeguarding diversity and vulnerability, as complexity is the source of resilience.

These principles are not culturally arbitrary; they describe the necessary conditions for any living system to learn, adapt, and persist. Their normative force arises from the observation that systems violating them inevitably degrade, while those embodying them flourish.

### 2.2 Projecting onto Abrahamic Traditions

Each tradition contains concepts that map directly onto these principles. Table 1 summarizes the projection.

**Table 1: Abrahamic Operationalizations of the Three Principles**

| Principle | Judaic Dimension | Christian Dimension | Islamic Dimension |
|-----------|------------------|---------------------|-------------------|
| **Feedback** | *Zecher yetziat mitzrayim* (memory of Egypt); prophetic critique; *teshuvah* (repentance) | *Metanoia* (repentance); confession; *kenosis* (self‑emptying) | *Hisba* (commanding right and forbidding wrong); *mizan* (balance) |
| **Curiosity** | *Machloket l’shem shamayim* (argument for heaven); *shivim panim laTorah* (seventy faces of Torah) | *Kenosis* as receptivity; *xenia* (hospitality); theological exploration | *Ijtihad* (disciplined reinterpretation); *tafakkur* (reflection) |
| **Respect** | *Ger* (stranger) as protected class; *tikkun olam*; *bal tashchit* (do not destroy) | *Mystical body of Christ* (unity through difference); preferential option for the poor | *Khalifa* (stewardship); *amanah* (trust); protection of *mustad‘afun* (the vulnerable) |

These mappings are not exhaustive but provide a shared vocabulary for ethical assessment.

We evaluate movements based on:
- Official doctrines and authoritative interpretations.
- Institutional practices (treatment of dissent, minorities, the other).
- Historical behavior, especially toward the vulnerable.
- Self‑understanding: does the movement explicitly invoke its tradition’s ethical resources?

Each movement receives a score from –2 (system‑destroying) to +2 (system‑healing) on each of the three axes. Scores are interpretive but grounded in documented evidence.

## 4. Interpreting the World with a Unified Moral Lens

### 4.1 The Israeli‑Palestinian Conflict Re‑Examined

When the moral field is projected onto the Abrahamic universe, the Israeli‑Palestinian conflict is revealed not as a clash of religions or nations, but as a **systemic pathology** involving all three traditions simultaneously. The lowest‑scoring movements (Israeli right, Christian Zionism, religious Zionism, political Islam) share a structure of broken feedback, killed curiosity, and destroyed complexity—despite their opposing political goals. Conversely, the highest‑scoring movements (post‑Zionist critical movements, liberation theology, reformist Islam, ecumenical Christianity) share a commitment to making harm visible, seeking new configurations, and protecting the vulnerable, again across the divide.

This lens dissolves the binary of “pro‑Israel” vs. “pro‑Palestinian” and replaces it with a moral axis: **pro‑system‑health vs. pro‑system‑destruction**. It allows us to support Israeli human rights groups and Palestinian civil society simultaneously, because both are working to restore feedback and protect complexity.

### 4.2 Beyond the Middle East

The framework is generalizable. In the United States, the same spectrum can map movements within each tradition:

- **Christian nationalism** (low feedback, low curiosity, low respect) aligns with Christian Zionism.
- **Black liberation theology** (high feedback, high curiosity, high respect) aligns with liberation theology.
- **Progressive Jewish movements** (high scores) align with Reform and post‑Zionist ethics.
- **Islamist extremism** aligns with political Islam; **Sufi and reformist currents** align with the healthy pole.

The unified lens thus provides a common language for diagnosing moral health across contexts, revealing that the real battle is not between religions but between the life‑affirming and life‑destroying tendencies within each.

## 5. Implications for Peacebuilding and Ethics

### 5.1 Intra‑Faith Critique

The framework gives each tradition a mirror. Jewish movements can be evaluated by how well they embody *ger* and prophetic feedback; Islamic movements by *hisba* and *khalifa*; Christian movements by *metanoia* and *kenosis*. This allows believers to reclaim their tradition’s deepest ethical resources against those who would use the tradition for domination.

### 5.2 Inter‑Faith Alliances

Movements with high composite scores—even from opposing political sides—share a fundamental orientation. They can recognize each other as allies in the work of restoring systemic health. Concrete examples include joint Israeli‑Palestinian peace organizations (e.g., Combatants for Peace, Women Wage Peace) that draw on both Jewish and Islamic ethics, and interfaith networks that support Palestinian liberation theology alongside progressive Judaism.

### 5.3 Policy and Intervention

External actors (governments, international organizations) can use the spectrum to condition support: resources should favor movements that demonstrate feedback, curiosity, and respect. This is not a neutral stance but an ethical one—choosing to invest in systemic health rather than fueling systemic pathology.

### 5.4 The Peace That Does Not Erase

The unified lens clarifies the kind of peace we seek: not the peace of the graveyard (where difference is eliminated), but the peace of the living system—dynamic, diverse, with feedback flowing, curiosity alive, and the vulnerable protected. This peace is possible when each tradition goes deep enough to discover that the other is not a threat but a teacher.

## 6. Delicacy and Responsibility

Projecting a moral field into the sacred universes of Abrahamic traditions is delicate. It risks being perceived as reductionist or imperial. We emphasize that the framework does not claim to replace theology; it offers a **meta‑ethical translation** that allows traditions to speak to each other and to hold themselves accountable. The scores and rankings are not final judgments but invitations to deeper engagement. They are designed to be contested, refined, and improved through dialogue.

Moreover, the framework must be applied with humility. No movement is monolithic; internal diversity exists. The scores reflect dominant tendencies, not every individual. The goal is to illuminate patterns, not to condemn or canonize.

## 7. Conclusion

We have shown that the moral field derived from systems theory can be projected into the Abrahamic universe, revealing a unified spectrum of moral commitment that cuts across religious boundaries. By operationalizing feedback, curiosity, and respect through each tradition’s own concepts, we can map movements from system‑destroying to system‑healing. This unified lens allows us to interpret the Israeli‑Palestinian conflict—and other moral crises—not as a clash of civilizations but as a struggle between life‑affirming and life‑destroying forces within all civilizations.

The work ahead is to use this lens to build bridges: between critical Jewish movements and reformist Islam, between liberation theology and progressive Judaism, between all who refuse to let the animalistic self have the final word. In doing so, we move toward the peace that the Abrahamic traditions have always promised—a peace of justice, complexity, and the flourishing of all creation.
