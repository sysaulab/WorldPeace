# A Worldview‑Neutral Framework for Just Action: Bridging Islamic Jihad Ethics and Secular Just War Theory

**Abstract**  
This paper proposes a generic, worldview‑neutral framework for evaluating when and how physical force—including armed conflict—may be justified. The framework distills ethical principles from classical Islamic jurisprudence on *jihad* and from secular just‑war theory, presenting them as a common lens applicable across religious and ideological traditions. It rests on a foundational axiom: *life takes precedence over symbols, rules, and institutions*. The framework then articulates a sequential logic: exhaustion of non‑violent means, genuine offer of exit for those holding harmful ideologies, containment as a last resort, strict constraints on the use of force, transparency and accountability in justification, and post‑action responsibility. Historical cases (World War II strategic bombing, the 1999 Kosovo intervention, the 2003 Iraq War) are examined to illustrate the framework’s utility in exposing gaps between stated justifications and actual conduct. The paper argues that such a shared ethical grammar can help counteract the self‑serving narratives of victors and the ideological polarisation that enables unjust violence.

---

## 1. Introduction

Contemporary debates about the ethics of war are often trapped within competing ideological “flavours”—liberal interventionism, realist power politics, religious justifications, and anti‑imperial critiques—each speaking its own language and dismissing others. Nowhere is this more evident than in discussions of *jihad*, a term that has been reduced either to a synonym for terrorism or to a purely spiritual struggle, both simplifications that erase centuries of nuanced ethical reasoning.

This paper attempts to step outside these polarised framings by constructing a *worldview‑neutral* framework for reflecting on the use of force. The framework is not intended as a new doctrine, but as a lens—a set of structured questions that any ethical tradition, whether religious or secular, can use to examine its own justifications for violence. It draws on two rich, yet often isolated, streams of thought: classical Islamic jurisprudence on armed conflict (the “lesser jihad”) and the Western just‑war tradition. By showing that these two streams converge on a common set of principles, the paper suggests that a shared ethical grammar is possible, one that might help cut through the self‑serving narratives that too often accompany the use of force.

The framework rests on a foundational axiom that emerged from the authors’ earlier dialogical work: *life takes precedence over symbols, rules, and institutions*. When the preservation of an ideological symbol or the rigid application of a rule becomes more important than protecting human life, oppression has already begun. From this axiom, the framework develops a sequence of responses, constraints, and responsibilities that can be applied to any proposal to use physical force, whether by a state, a non‑state actor, or an international coalition.

## 2. A Generic Framework for Reflecting on Just Action

The framework is organised around seven interlocking principles. They are presented here as a generic lens, deliberately free of theological or ideological commitments, but each can be grounded in diverse ethical traditions.

### 2.1 Foundational Axiom: Life over Symbols  
No ideological construct—religious, political, or cultural—may be preserved at the cost of avoidable human harm. This axiom serves as a check against forms of oppression that sacrifice individuals for the sake of an abstraction. It echoes the Islamic legal principle of *maqāṣid al‑sharī‘ah* (higher objectives of the law), particularly the protection of life (*ḥifẓ al‑nafs*) (Al‑Raysuni 1992; Opwis 2017), and the just‑war requirement that the cause must be proportionate and aimed at a just peace (Walzer 1977).

### 2.2 Exhaustion of Non‑Violent Means  
All reasonable non‑violent approaches must be genuinely attempted and shown to be insufficient before any physical action is taken. These approaches include diplomacy, dialogue, mediation, education, and—where applicable—economic measures, provided those measures are themselves not causing unjust harm. This reflects the classical Islamic condition that war is only permissible after *da‘wah* (invitation to peaceful resolution) and when the enemy does not incline to peace (Qur’an 8:61). It also corresponds to the *jus ad bellum* criterion of *last resort* (Walzer 1977, 84).

### 2.3 Offer of Exit  
When the danger stems from an ideology that demonstrably produces more harm than good, those who hold it must be given a genuine opportunity to abandon it without punishment. This requires distinguishing between leadership actively perpetuating harm and those who are merely associated or coerced. The principle is rooted in the Qur’anic command “There is no compulsion in religion” (Qur’an 2:256) and in the classical practice of offering safe passage (*amān*) to enemies who surrendered or sought protection (Abou El Fadl 2001, 162‑64).

### 2.4 Containment as Final Resort  
Only when non‑violent means are exhausted *and* the harmful ideology cannot be abandoned by those who actively wield it to inflict harm does containment become justified. Containment may range from political and economic isolation to the physical removal of the capacity to cause harm. The goal is the protection of life and the restoration of conditions under which non‑violent coexistence can again become possible. This aligns with the Islamic concept of *hirabah*—the state’s duty to stop those who spread “corruption on earth” (*fasād fī al‑arḍ*) when peaceful means fail (Abou El Fadl 2001, 249‑53).

### 2.5 Constraints During Physical Action  
- **Proportionality:** Force used must be the minimum necessary to achieve containment and must not exceed the harm it seeks to prevent.  
- **Discrimination / No Collective Punishment:** Non‑combatants, civilians, and those not actively perpetuating harm are to be protected. Deliberate targeting of civilians or civilian infrastructure as a means of pressure or reprisal is prohibited.  
- **Right Intention:** Action must be aimed at a just and sustainable peace, not at conquest, economic gain, revenge, or ideological supremacy.

These constraints are explicitly codified in the Islamic rules of engagement, such as the Prophet’s injunction not to kill women, children, monks, or non‑combatants, and not to destroy crops or buildings (Bukhārī, *Ṣaḥīḥ*, kitāb al‑jihād; Kelsay 2007, 77‑80). They are also central to the *jus in bello* principles of the just‑war tradition (Walzer 1977, 151‑59) and to international humanitarian law (Geneva Conventions 1949; Additional Protocol I 1977).

### 2.6 Transparency and Accountability  
The cause for action must be based on verifiable evidence subject to independent scrutiny. If the stated justification is later proven false, the action is retroactively unjust. This principle challenges the tendency of states to invoke self‑serving or fabricated justifications, a problem that Islamic jurists addressed by requiring legitimate authority (*walī al‑amr*) and a just cause that could be publicly examined (Kelsay 2007, 49‑52).

### 2.7 Post‑Action Responsibility  
After containment, there is an obligation to: (a) protect vulnerable populations; (b) re‑open pathways for non‑violent resolution of remaining grievances; (c) reintegrate those willing to abandon harmful ideology without retributive persecution; and (d) repair, where possible, the harm inflicted, including rebuilding civilian infrastructure and restoring self‑determination. This corresponds to *jus post bellum* in just‑war theory (Orend 2002) and the Islamic concept of *‘ahd* (covenant) and *amān* (security) that governed post‑conflict relations (Abou El Fadl 2001, 196‑200).

## 3. Islamic Ethical Foundations of the Framework

The framework is not derived from a single tradition, but it finds strong resonance in classical Islamic jurisprudence on armed conflict.

### 3.1 Qur’anic Foundations  
The Qur’an permits armed self‑defence and the protection of the oppressed, but always within strict bounds. Surah 22:39‑40 grants permission to fight to those who have been wrongfully expelled from their homes, “for had Allah not repelled some people by means of others, monasteries, churches, synagogues, and mosques, wherein the name of Allah is mentioned, would have been destroyed.” The famous verse “Fight in the way of Allah those who fight you but do not transgress. Indeed, Allah does not like transgressors” (2:190) establishes the principle of non‑transgression. The command to cease fighting if the enemy inclines to peace (8:61) enshrines the priority of peace.

### 3.2 Prophetic Rules of Engagement  
The Prophet Muhammad’s instructions to military commanders codified a detailed *jus in bello*. According to traditions recorded in *Ṣaḥīḥ al‑Bukhārī* and *Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim*, he ordered: “Do not kill women, children, the elderly, or monks. Do not cut down trees, destroy crops, or kill livestock. Do not break treaties or betray those who seek protection.” These prohibitions were reiterated by the first caliphs (Abū Bakr’s famous ten rules) and became the basis of classical legal manuals (Al‑Shaybānī, *Siyar*; cf. Kelsay 2007, 77‑80).

### 3.3 The Distinction Between Greater and Lesser Jihad  
A well‑known tradition (often graded weak but widely cited in ethical discourse) has the Prophet returning from battle and saying, “We have returned from the lesser jihad to the greater jihad”—the struggle against one’s own ego (al‑Khatīb al‑Baghdādī, *Tārīkh Baghdād*; for discussion see Cook 2005, 34‑38). This distinction reinforced that armed struggle is not the core of the faith; internal moral development is.

### 3.4 Classical Jurisprudence and *Maqāṣid*  
Classical jurists like al‑Shāfiʿī, al‑Māwardī, and al‑Ghazālī elaborated conditions for just war, emphasising legitimate authority, just cause, and proper conduct. Later, the *maqāṣid* tradition (Al‑Shāṭibī, 14th century) elevated the protection of life, religion, intellect, lineage, and property as the objectives of the law, providing a framework to evaluate whether specific rules (including those about war) were fulfilling their purpose (Opwis 2017; Raysuni 1992).

### 3.5 Modern Reinterpretations  
Contemporary Muslim scholars such as Khaled Abou El Fadl (2001), Abdullahi An‑Na’im (2008), and John Kelsay (2007) have argued that the classical rules on *jihad* must be re‑examined in light of modern international law and the changed context of sovereign states. Many conclude that the principles of non‑aggression, protection of civilians, and peacemaking are as binding today as ever, while classical concepts of expansionist jihad are obsolete.

## 4. Secular Just‑War Parallels

The secular just‑war tradition, rooted in Augustine, refined by Aquinas, and articulated in contemporary terms by Michael Walzer (1977) and others, provides a complementary framework.

### 4.1 *Jus ad Bellum* (Conditions for Going to War)  
The traditional criteria are: just cause (e.g., self‑defence against aggression, protection of the innocent), right authority (legitimate government), right intention (aiming for peace), last resort, proportionality (the good achieved must outweigh the harms), and reasonable hope of success. These mirror the framework’s principles of non‑violent means first, containment as last resort, and right intention.

### 4.2 *Jus in Bello* (Conduct Within War)  
The two main principles—distinction (non‑combatant immunity) and proportionality (force must not be excessive in relation to military objective)—are directly reflected in our constraints. They are codified in the Geneva Conventions (1949) and Additional Protocols (1977), which form the core of international humanitarian law.

### 4.3 *Jus post Bellum* (Post‑War Justice)  
Emerging scholarship on justice after war (Orend 2002; Bass 2004) stresses obligations to protect civilians, punish atrocities, rebuild institutions, and ensure reconciliation. This aligns with the framework’s post‑action responsibility.

### 4.4 Responsibility to Protect (R2P)  
The 2005 UN World Summit endorsed the principle that states have a responsibility to protect their populations from genocide, war crimes, ethnic cleansing, and crimes against humanity, and that the international community has a responsibility to intervene when states fail. R2P represents a contemporary secular articulation of the idea that containment of massive harm may sometimes be justified as a last resort (ICISS 2001).

## 5. Testing the Framework: Historical Cases

To illustrate the framework’s analytical power, we briefly apply it to three contested cases, deliberately avoiding the self‑serving narratives of victors.

### 5.1 World War II – Allied Strategic Bombing  
**Context:** Nazi Germany’s aggression and genocide constituted a clear just cause. Non‑violent means had been exhausted.  
**Framework Application:** The war as a whole met the threshold for containment. However, the Allied area bombing of cities (Hamburg, Dresden, Tokyo) and the atomic bombings violated *discrimination* and *proportionality*. These were deliberate collective punishments that targeted civilians, not a last resort. The framework highlights that a just overall cause does not justify unjust conduct, and it exposes the victors’ tendency to minimise or rationalise these violations.

### 5.2 Kosovo (NATO, 1999)  
**Context:** Serbian ethnic cleansing was a grave harm. Diplomacy was attempted but the UN Security Council did not authorise force.  
**Framework Application:** The case illustrates contested criteria: did NATO genuinely exhaust non‑violent means? Was there a genuine exit offered to Serbia? The conduct raised discrimination concerns (e.g., bombing of a civilian convoy, the Chinese embassy). Post‑action responsibility was mixed. The framework does not deliver a simple verdict but forces clarity on where the ethical hinges lie.

### 5.3 Iraq War (2003)  
**Context:** The US‑led invasion was justified by claims of weapons of mass destruction and links to terrorism, later proven false.  
**Framework Application:** The invasion fails on nearly every criterion. Non‑violent means (inspections) were not exhausted; the justification was false; no genuine exit was offered; conduct included widespread violations of discrimination (Fallujah, Abu Ghraib); post‑action responsibility was catastrophic. The framework underscores that a war launched under false pretense and without last resort is unjust regardless of stated intentions.

## 6. The Problem of Victor’s Justice and Self‑Deception

A recurring theme in ethical reflection on war is the tendency of the powerful to construct self‑justifying narratives. As Chris Hedges (2002) argues, the myth of “righteous war” allows societies to commit atrocities while maintaining a clean conscience. The framework explicitly counters this by requiring transparency and accountability, and by evaluating conduct independently of overall cause.

The principle that life takes precedence over symbols is particularly subversive of victor’s justice. It forces even those who believe they are fighting for a noble cause to confront whether they have sacrificed the very values they claim to defend.

---

## 7. Conclusion

The generic framework presented here—life over symbols, exhaustion of non‑violent means, offer of exit, containment as final resort, constraints, accountability, and post‑action responsibility—provides a shared ethical grammar that can be used by Muslims drawing on *jihad* ethics, secular just‑war theorists, and anyone else seeking to reflect on the use of force without being trapped by ideological flavours.

The convergence between classical Islamic jurisprudence and just‑war principles suggests that, when ethical reasoning is conducted carefully, different traditions can arrive at common insights. Such common ground is not only intellectually satisfying but practically important: in a world where violence is often justified by appeal to competing worldviews, a shared lens can help expose self‑serving justifications and hold all parties to a common standard.

The framework is not a destination but a tool—a set of structured questions that can be used to test any claim that force is necessary. Used honestly, it may help prevent the kind of self‑deception that enables atrocity, and perhaps bring us a step closer to a world where the question “how do we use force without becoming what we oppose?” is asked before, not after, the bombs fall.
