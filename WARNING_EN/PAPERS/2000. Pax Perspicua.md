# Pax Perspicua: Peace Through Transparency

**A Policy Proposal**  
*March 2026*

## Executive Summary

This paper proposes the **Pax Perspicua** framework—a voluntary association of nations built on three interconnected pillars: (1) mandatory military restraint, with all forces confined to national territory; (2) radical transparency through an Open Intelligence Network (OIN) where all state-generated intelligence is published to a permanent public repository; and (3) automatic, five-year trade sanctions against any member that commits aggression or conceals information.

Drawing on peer-reviewed research in international relations, intelligence studies, and digital governance, we demonstrate that such a framework is not merely utopian but rests on solid empirical foundations. The capitalist peace literature confirms that economic interdependence raises the opportunity cost of conflict. Recent advances in open-source intelligence (OSINT) show that 70-90% of classified analytic value can now be generated from public sources at 2% of the cost. And emerging research on Digital Public Infrastructures provides governance models for the transparent, decentralized systems our framework requires.

Pax Perspicua offers a path beyond the bloc politics of a post-BRICS world—a truly plurilateral system where peace is enforced not by military deterrence but by the collective, automatic withdrawal of economic cooperation.

## 1. Introduction: The Case for a New Framework

The international order stands at a crossroads. The post-1945 institutions—the United Nations, the Bretton Woods system, the alliances of the Cold War—were designed for a world of bipolar confrontation and then unipolar dominance. That world is gone. We now face a multipolar landscape of rising powers, regional rivalries, and erosion of trust in traditional institutions.

Yet the fundamental challenge remains unchanged from Kant's time: how can sovereign states achieve lasting peace without surrendering their sovereignty? The liberal peace tradition, from Kant through Oneal and Russett, has long argued that democracy, economic interdependence, and international institutions can create a "zone of peace". But these mechanisms have proven partial and fragile. Democracies fight non-democracies. Economic interdependence can be weaponized. Institutions lack enforcement power.

Pax Perspicua proposes a radical simplification: **peace through transparency, enforced by trade.**

Rather than relying on trust, shared values, or military alliances, we propose a system where every member's military posture is visible to all, where no state can harbor aggressive intentions in secret, and where the cost of aggression is immediate and automatic exclusion from the world's most prosperous market.

This is not a utopian fantasy. It is an extrapolation of existing research and demonstrated capabilities.

## 2. Theoretical Foundations: What Peer-Reviewed Research Tells Us

### 2.1 The Capitalist Peace: Economic Interdependence as Conflict Deterrent

The "capitalist peace" thesis extends the liberal peace argument by focusing specifically on economic ties. In a seminal study published in the *Journal of Peace Research*, Powell and Chacha (2016) demonstrate that economic interdependence raises the opportunity costs of domestic political disturbances—including coups d'état—for both the targeted state and its financial partners.

Their quantitative analysis of global data from 1952 to 2007 found that:

> "As a state becomes more economically interdependent with the rest of the world, the opportunity costs of domestic political disturbances are raised for both the targeted state and its financial partners. These costs – potential economic losses and a damaged economic reputation – influence belligerents in the state to use constitutional means to resolve their disputes while providing stronger incentives to foreign economic partners to influence the calculus of these belligerents." 

This finding directly supports the core mechanism of Pax Perspicua: **automatic trade sanctions create real, calculable costs that rational actors seek to avoid.**

Gelpi and Grieco (2003) extend this logic, arguing that the impact of trade and democracy may be contingent upon one another:

> "Economic interdependence may reduce the risk of war between democracies but exacerbate the risk of such conflicts between non-democracies... the combined influence of democracy and interdependence may create a powerful web of constraint that reinforces the zone of peace among increasingly interdependent democracies." 

Pax Perspicua effectively **hard-codes** this "web of constraint" through mandatory transparency and automatic sanctions, making it binding even for non-democracies that choose to join.

### 2.2 The OSINT Revolution: Transparency is Now Technologically Feasible

Perhaps the most significant objection to radical transparency has always been feasibility. How could we possibly verify compliance without an intrusive, expensive inspection regime? The answer lies in the open-source intelligence (OSINT) revolution of the past decade.

As documented extensively in *Small Wars Journal* (2025), the democratization of intelligence capabilities has fundamentally transformed what is possible:

> "Commercial satellite imagery represents the most visible capability shift. Once the exclusive means of states with space programs, companies like Maxar, Planet, and BlackSky provide sub-meter resolution imagery... The cost asymmetry is staggering. Accessing imagery would have required a national satellite program 20 years ago, but now costs a few thousand dollars monthly." 

The resource asymmetry is even more striking:

> "RAND studies and congressional testimony show that small OSINT cells can generate 70–90 percent of the analytic value of classified collection while operating at roughly two percent of the cost of a comparable government program." 

This means that **the collective monitoring capacity of the Open Intelligence Network would be immense at minimal cost.** Every member state's intelligence agencies would contribute their classified assessments to the public record, but even if they attempted to cheat, the ecosystem of journalists, researchers, and civil society organizations using commercial tools would catch them.

The MH17 case is instructive. When Russia denied involvement in shooting down the civilian airliner, a small team of online investigators at Bellingcat systematically disproved their claims using only publicly available satellite imagery, social media, and digital forensics:

> "Their investigation, conducted without classified intelligence access, subpoenaed records, or state resources, proved more comprehensive and transparent than many official accounts. Independent analysts clearly demonstrated that they could compete with state powers in intelligence." 

Academic research confirms this capability. A 2025 working paper from arXiv presents CONTACT, a framework for territorial control prediction using large language models trained on open-source news articles. European University Viadrina students have successfully used OSINT methods to verify destruction of civilian infrastructure in Ukraine, map border pushbacks, and analyze disinformation campaigns.

### 2.3 The Limits and Possibilities of Transparency

Of course, transparency is not a panacea. Northwestern political scientist Isabella Bellezza's research on "preemptive disclosure" during international crises finds important limitations:

> "Among the US public, preemptive disclosure effectively shifts perceptions but does little to shift support for costly countermeasures." 

This suggests that transparency alone may not mobilize public opinion to support military intervention. But Pax Perspicua does not rely on public opinion or military intervention. It relies on **automatic, pre-committed economic sanctions.** The mechanism is not "persuading voters to support war" but "triggering a binding treaty obligation." Bellezza's findings actually support our framework: transparency shifts perceptions (establishing the factual basis for sanctions), but we do not need it to do more.

A more nuanced challenge comes from the peace mediation literature. The PRIO-led TRIPEN project (2023-2024) examines how Peace Facilitation Organizations handle transparency in peace negotiations, finding that:

> "Secrecy can sometimes aid mediation, allowing parties to explore compromises without public posturing." 

This is a valid concern. Would the OIN's radical transparency make peace negotiations impossible by exposing every exploratory offer? The solution is the **two-tier architecture** we propose: final agreements and state positions are public, but confidential mediation channels can be protected through delayed publication (e.g., releasing records after 20 years, or after the death of involved parties). This preserves the historical record while enabling the privacy necessary for diplomacy.

### 2.4 Digital Public Infrastructure: The Governance Model We Need

The technical architecture of the Open Intelligence Network finds strong support in emerging research on Digital Public Infrastructures (DPIs). A comprehensive 2025 study published in the *Proceedings of the Digital Government Society* defines DPIs as:

> "Socio-technical systems with modular and layered architectures, governed through public-private collective action to support value creation and multi-actor interaction at scale." 

The study identifies three critical characteristics of successful DPIs:

1. **Governance architecture and functional modularity**: The system must be designed to accommodate diverse participants and use cases.
2. **Stakeholder orchestration**: Effective governance requires coordinating public and private actors.
3. **Policy context and public rationale**: The infrastructure must serve clearly defined public interest objectives. 

The Agora—our proposed permanent public repository—embodies all three characteristics. It is modular (accepting uploads from states, journalists, scientists, and citizens), requires stakeholder coordination (between member governments, the Secretariat, and civil society), and serves the public interest of preventing conflict through transparency.

A related 2025 review in *Land* journal examines the integration of spatial data infrastructures with IoT, AI, and geospatial technologies for smart governance, concluding that such integration enables:

> "Real-time data-driven decision-making... improved urban resilience, public service delivery, environmental monitoring and citizen engagement." 

However, the authors also note challenges: "interoperability, data protection, institutional barriers and unequal access to technologies."  These are precisely the challenges Pax Perspicua must address through open standards, ethical frameworks, and capacity-building assistance for smaller members.

### 2.5 The Open Intelligence Concept

Israeli intelligence methodologist Ofer Guterman (2024) has proposed an integrative conceptual framework for "open intelligence" that closely parallels our OIN. His framework includes five interactive components:

1. Intelligence dealing with national security issues that concern global civilian threats (climate change, public health)
2. Intelligence-civilian partnerships in collection, analysis, and operations
3. Embedding intelligence organizations within the civilian STEM ecosystem
4. Obligation to share information and assessments with the public in an era of fake news
5. Human-capital strategies symbiotic with the public sector and private labor market 

Guterman acknowledges the inherent challenges: developing a more sophisticated secrecy culture, managing sources and methods risk, internalizing the loss of monopoly on intelligence, and managing tension with political decision-makers. These are the same challenges Pax Perspicua must navigate.

## 3. The Pax Perspicua Framework: Detailed Proposal

### 3.1 Core Pillars

**Pillar I: Military Restraint**

All member states shall maintain 100% of their military personnel, conventional weapons, naval vessels, military aircraft, and offensive cyber capabilities within their sovereign territory and territorial waters. Military forces shall not cross another member's border for any purpose without explicit, public invitation.

**Pillar II: The Open Intelligence Network (OIN)**

All member states shall participate in the Open Intelligence Network. All intelligence products—raw and analyzed—pertaining to the security, governance, or economy of any member state shall be deposited in a permanent public repository (the Agora). This includes geospatial intelligence, signals intelligence, human intelligence reports, financial intelligence, and domestic security assessments.

**Pillar III: Automatic Sanctions**

Any verified act of aggression or material breach of transparency obligations triggers an automatic, comprehensive trade embargo by all members against the violating state. The minimum duration is five years from the cessation of hostilities and restoration of compliance. Re-admittance requires full audit and probationary period.

### 3.2 Optional Protocols

**Protocol A: The Open Law Framework (Lex Agora)**

A decentralized legal protocol for dispute resolution, using randomly selected citizen panels and smart contract infrastructure. Participation is optional for states and citizens.

**Protocol B: The Common Currency (The Concord)**

A digital reserve currency backed by the zone's combined GDP, available to members meeting additional fiscal transparency rules.

**Protocol C: The Infrastructure Union**

Joint funding and development of cross-border rail, energy, and digital networks.

### 3.3 Public Utilities (The Agora Pillars)

The Agora serves as the permanent repository for:

- **State Intelligence**: All member intelligence agencies upload
- **Journalism**: Accredited media upload raw materials and published work (with source protection through delayed release)
- **Science**: All publicly funded research, including raw data and negative results
- **Law**: All court rulings, filings, and legal precedents
- **Health**: Real-time epidemiological data
- **Environment**: Continuous monitoring data from sensors
- **Commerce**: Beneficial ownership registers and supply chain disclosures

## 4. Addressing Objections: A Scholarly Rebuttal

### 4.1 "Nations will never surrender their secrets."

The research suggests otherwise. Guterman documents that intelligence organizations are already "undergoing significant changes" toward "unprecedented openness to the civilian sphere". The OSINT revolution means that many "secrets" are already publicly available to those with the skills to find them. The question is not whether to be transparent, but whether to participate in a system that **rewards** transparency with trade benefits.

### 4.2 "The 5-year sanction is too harsh; nations will fear joining."

The capitalist peace literature shows that the opportunity costs of instability are already high. Pax Perspicua simply makes them explicit and predictable. The 5-year period is designed to be long enough to deter aggression but short enough to allow rehabilitation. It is a feature, not a bug, that the cost of violation is severe—that is what makes the commitment credible.

### 4.3 "What about external aggression from non-members?"

This is the most serious objection. Pax Perspicua does not provide military defense. Its defense is economic: any non-member attacking a member is permanently locked out of the world's most prosperous market. For most modern economies, this cost is prohibitive. For those willing to pay it, the member states retain their national militaries for territorial defense (confined to their own soil). The framework does not abolish self-defense; it merely prohibits **offensive** action against fellow members.

### 4.4 "The transparency requirements violate privacy."

The framework includes robust privacy protections:

- Personal medical data is excluded (only aggregated public health data is included)
- Journalists' confidential sources are protected through delayed release
- Juvenile records and sensitive victim information can be sealed
- The principle is: **transparency for institutions and power-holders; privacy for individuals**

### 4.5 "This would require a world government."

It requires precisely the opposite. The CEZ Secretariat is minimal—it merely administers and verifies. The Lex Agora is decentralized by design. Members retain full sovereignty over domestic affairs. The system is **plurilateral**—nations join voluntarily, can leave (at significant cost), and opt into protocols as they choose.

## 5. Conclusion: A Research-Grounded Path Forward

Pax Perspicua is not a utopian fantasy. It is a concrete proposal grounded in:

- **Empirical research** on economic interdependence and conflict 
- **Demonstrated technological capabilities** in open-source intelligence 
- **Emerging governance models** for Digital Public Infrastructures 
- **Conceptual frameworks** for open intelligence 

The remaining challenges—managing the transition, ensuring equitable access to analytical tools, protecting legitimate privacy, handling the burden of constant scrutiny—are real but surmountable. They are problems of design and implementation, not proof of impossibility.

In a post-BRICS world of competing blocs and eroding trust, Pax Perspicua offers something genuinely new: a system where peace is enforced not by military deterrence but by the collective, automatic withdrawal of economic cooperation. It is a system where the only competition that remains is the competition to be the most prosperous, the most transparent, the most trustworthy.

**That is a competition worth winning.**
