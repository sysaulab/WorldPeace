# Historical Patterns

## A Gentle Inquiry into the Fusion of Power and Apocalyptic Purpose in Western Expansion

### A Crossroads of Disciplines: Mapping the Fields This Paper Engages

Before presenting the revised framework, we must situate this investigation within the academic landscape it traverses. Like the previous papers, this one does not belong to a single discipline but stands at the intersection of multiple fields. Its central contribution is not the discovery of new historical facts but the articulation of a **pattern-based lens** for re-examining familiar history—a lens that reveals repeating structures across scales and centuries.

#### 1. The Macro-Historical Framework: Jiang Xueqin and Structural Pattern Analysis

The paper's methodological orientation finds a significant parallel in the work of Sino-Canadian historian and geopolitical theorist Jiang Xueqin. Jiang, a Yale-educated scholar based in Beijing, has developed a framework he calls "Predictive History" through his popular YouTube channel of the same name. His approach explicitly applies "structural historical analysis, game theory, and concepts inspired by Isaac Asimov's fictional psychohistory" to interpret and predict major geopolitical developments.

Jiang's method rests on a fundamental premise that aligns with this paper's orientation: **recurring historical patterns, when properly understood, reveal the underlying structures that drive human events across different eras and contexts**. He argues that "both historical patterns and game theory can be used to accurately predict future events" because the same structural dynamics tend to produce similar outcomes under similar conditions.

What makes Jiang's framework particularly relevant to this inquiry is his attention to the deep, macro-historical forces that shape civilizations. His lecture series "Civilization" traces "the narrative and ideological evolution of human societies from prehistory to the contemporary period, with particular emphasis on the unifying and mobilizing power of transcendent religious and civilizational belief systems". This focus on how belief systems mobilize populations aligns directly with this paper's examination of apocalyptic and quasi-religious narratives as drivers of expansion.

Jiang's analysis of contemporary geopolitics draws heavily on historical parallels. In his widely discussed 2024 lecture, he compared a potential US-Iran conflict to the Athenian Sicilian Expedition during the Peloponnesian War—"a massive, ambitious military campaign launched by Athens that ultimately ended in total catastrophe". This methodological move—using deep historical patterns to illuminate present dynamics—exemplifies the pattern-based approach this paper advocates.

**This paper draws on Jiang's framework** to situate its own pattern-based analysis within a broader scholarly conversation about macro-historical structures. Jiang's work demonstrates that pattern recognition, when done rigorously, can generate testable hypotheses about geopolitical outcomes—two of his three major 2024 predictions (Trump's return to power and a US-Iran conflict) have already been borne out.

#### 2. The Steppe Hypothesis: Geographical Migration as Pattern Generator

Your extension of the framework—that "the European pattern was shaped by a continuous stream of steppes people invading Europe, based on geographical migration"—engages a substantial body of scholarship on the role of Eurasian steppe nomads in shaping European civilization. This is a macro-historical pattern operating at an even deeper temporal scale than the Crusades-to-present arc this paper primarily examines.

The geographical determinism implicit in this hypothesis finds theoretical grounding in Jiang's work, which acknowledges "geographical determinism" as one of his analytical frameworks. The argument would be that Europe's historical trajectory cannot be understood without accounting for the repeated influx of steppe peoples—Scythians, Huns, Avars, Magyars, Mongols, and others—whose migrations shaped European political geography, military technology, and cultural development over millennia.

This pattern operates at what might be called the **tectonic scale** of history. Just as geological forces shape the terrain on which human events unfold, the centuries-long pressure of steppe migrations created conditions that predisposed European societies toward certain responses—fortification, militarization, and eventually, outward expansion as a release valve for internal pressures. The crusading impulse that this paper identifies as a template for later expansion may itself be understood as a response, in part, to the deep historical memory of invasion from the East.

**This paper acknowledges this deeper pattern** while focusing on the more recent fusion of power and meaning that characterized Western expansion. The steppe hypothesis reminds us that the patterns we observe at one scale are often themselves expressions of patterns operating at larger scales—a recursive insight that Jiang's framework, with its emphasis on structural analysis across time, is well-equipped to accommodate.

#### 3. Historiography and the Revisionist Tradition

The paper engages most directly with the revisionist tradition in American historiography. Scholars have long moved beyond the celebratory narratives of nineteenth-century historians who "glorified expansionism as a heroic struggle, a romantic adventure, a benevolent mission, and a Darwinian right or inevitability". Postcolonial and revisionist historians have instead "asserted a more critical lens," revealing that "viewed from the perspective of the conquered and colonized, the United States has been in a continual imperial mode since its colonial status, pushing ever westward."

Robert J. Miller's scholarship on the Doctrine of Discovery provides crucial legal-historical grounding for this revisionist perspective. Miller demonstrates that **Manifest Destiny arose directly from the legal principles and elements of the Doctrine of Discovery** established in fifteenth-century papal bulls. These documents authorized Christian nations to claim non-Christian lands and subjugate their inhabitants, creating principles that became Christian international law and were subsequently adopted into American colonial and state law, incorporated into the U.S. Constitution, and affirmed by the Supreme Court in *Johnson v. M'Intosh* (1823).

John Christopher Pinheiro's work on the Mexican-American War further substantiates the pattern. Pinheiro documents how "many Americans argued that Roman Catholicism was incompatible with civil and religious liberty" and pointed to "Mexico's political troubles as an example of this incompatibility in action." This pre-war framing "helped to establish a preconception of Mexicans as a cognitively inferior, superstitious, and lazy race," while evangelical Protestants "saw the war as a Providential opportunity to evangelize Catholic Mexico with a 'pure' gospel."

**This paper builds upon these historiographical foundations** by extracting the underlying pattern: the fusion of elite material ambition with popular apocalyptic narrative. It then asks whether this same pattern can be observed operating at different scales and in different contexts—from the Crusades to contemporary geopolitics.

#### 4. The Crusades and the Medieval Origins of Western Expansion

The paper's framing of the Crusades as a "template" finds support in specialized scholarship on medieval expansion and its afterlife in later centuries. The crusading ideal proved remarkably adaptable: when Jerusalem was lost, the framework found new targets—heretics in southern France, pagans in the Baltic, political opponents of the papacy in Italy.

This adaptability is crucial for the pattern-based view. The crusade was not a single event but a **meme complex**—a cluster of ideas (holy war, remission of sins, cosmic struggle) that could attach itself to diverse material projects. The same structure that sent knights to Jerusalem could later send conquistadors to the Americas and settlers across the continent.

**This paper draws on crusade historiography** not for new archival discoveries but for the pattern it reveals: a religious framework flexible enough to legitimate diverse projects of expansion while providing participants with cosmic meaning.

#### 5. The Frankfurt School and the Critique of Enlightenment

Your bibliography includes Adorno and Horkheimer's *Dialectic of Enlightenment*, and this philosophical tradition is central to the paper's deeper argument. The Frankfurt School theorists argued that Enlightenment reason, intended to liberate humanity from myth, had itself become a new form of mythology—a tool of domination rather than liberation.

This framework directly parallels the paper's argument about the fusion of material interest and cosmic meaning. For Adorno and Horkheimer, the Enlightenment's promise of freedom through reason was betrayed when reason became instrumental—a tool for controlling nature and other humans rather than for understanding. The result was a new form of irrationalism disguised as rationality.

**This paper extends this critique** by showing how the fusion operates not only in Enlightenment philosophy but in the concrete historical projects of Western expansion. The "dialectic of enlightenment" is not an abstract philosophical problem but a lived historical pattern visible in the Crusades, colonialism, Manifest Destiny, and modern interventions.

#### 6. Political Psychology and the Contagion of Leadership

Your extension of the framework—that a psychopathic leader will induce psychopathic values and behaviors in a population—engages a growing literature in political psychology and social contagion theory. This is where the pattern-based view becomes truly powerful: it suggests that the same dynamics that operate at the scale of centuries also operate at the scale of decades, years, and even individual lives.

The literature on authoritarianism has long documented how populations can internalize the values of authoritarian leaders. Theodor Adorno's *The Authoritarian Personality* (1950) was an early attempt to understand how psychological dispositions make populations susceptible to fascist appeals. More recent work has examined how leaders shape not only policies but the moral and emotional climate of entire societies.

The pattern-based view suggests that **Nazism is better explained through this lens than through exceptionalist accounts** that treat it as a unique historical aberration. The fusion of elite ambition (industrialists, military officers, party officials) with popular apocalyptic narrative (the myth of Aryan destiny, the cosmic struggle against Bolshevism and international Jewry) follows precisely the same structure as the Crusades, colonialism, and Manifest Destiny.

This is not to minimize the specific horrors of the Holocaust, nor to suggest that all expansions are morally equivalent. It is to recognize that the underlying pattern—the engine of power fused with meaning—operates across contexts. When a psychopathic leader ascends to power, they do not merely impose their pathology on a passive population. They activate existing cultural narratives, channel material anxieties, and provide a framework that makes psychopathic behavior seem not only acceptable but righteous.

#### 7. Network Theory and Pattern Recognition Across Scales

The pattern-based view finds methodological support in contemporary network theory and complexity science. Recent work on information cascades and social contagion examines how beliefs and behaviors propagate through populations, and how the structure of social networks shapes what spreads.

The key insight for our purposes is that **patterns can repeat across scales without requiring direct historical causation**. The Crusades did not cause Manifest Destiny. But the same underlying dynamics—elite ambition seeking legitimation, populations seeking meaning, the fusion of the two into an engine of expansion—can operate independently in different times and places because they reflect fundamental features of human social organization.

This is what the paper means by "patterns who can repeat and appear at many scales." It is not claiming that later expansions were consciously modeled on earlier ones, though sometimes they were. It is claiming that the same structural dynamics tend to produce similar outcomes across different historical contexts.

**This paper draws on network theory** not for mathematical modeling but for its conceptual vocabulary: cascades, contagion, scale invariance, emergent behavior. These concepts provide a language for describing how patterns propagate and repeat without requiring conspiracy or direct influence.

#### 8. The Taboo Zone: Pattern Recognition as Suspect

We must acknowledge, as with the previous papers, the academic taboos this work challenges. Pattern-based historical analysis occupies an uncomfortable position in the academy. It risks being accused of:

- **Reductionism:** collapsing complex historical events into a single explanatory framework.
- **Conspiracy theory:** suggesting hidden connections across time and space.
- **Presentism:** reading contemporary concerns into the past.
- **Moral equivalency:** comparing events that are not morally comparable.

These risks are real. The paper must navigate them carefully. But the taboo against pattern recognition also has costs. It can prevent us from seeing genuine structural continuities that operate across centuries. It can trap us in exceptionalist narratives that treat each historical event as sui generis, obscuring the dynamics they share.

Jiang Xueqin's work has faced similar critiques. Some commentators have questioned whether his predictions "rely on selective historical analogies, speculative game-theoretic reasoning, and untestable assumptions". Yet the accuracy of two of his three major 2024 predictions has forced a reconsideration of his methodology. The pattern-based approach, when applied rigorously, can generate testable claims about the future—a powerful argument for its validity.

**This paper accepts these risks** because the patterns it identifies are too persistent and too consequential to ignore. The fusion of elite ambition and popular apocalyptic narrative has shaped Western expansion for a millennium. To refuse to see it because seeing it is uncomfortable is to remain willfully blind.

### Introduction: An Invitation to Look Anew

Every society tells itself a story about its past. These stories are not false, but they are inevitably partial. They emphasize certain events and downplay others; they highlight noble intentions and sometimes obscure inconvenient consequences. This is not malice. It is how collective memory functions. It provides cohesion, meaning, and a sense of moral direction.

This paper is an invitation to consider an additional layer to the Western story. It is not an attempt to replace the existing narrative, but to supplement it with a lens that reveals certain recurring patterns—patterns that connect the Crusader marching toward Jerusalem, the Conquistador sailing for the New World, the pioneer venturing west under Manifest Destiny, the Nazi marching under the swastika, and the modern soldier deployed in the name of spreading freedom.

The lens is this: **Western expansion has been consistently driven by a fusion between the material ambitions of powerful groups and the apocalyptic or quasi-religious yearnings of broader populations.** These two forces, the cynical and the sincere, have repeatedly converged to create an engine of enormous power—one capable of justifying immense violence, sustaining itself across centuries, and adapting its language to suit each new era.

To examine this is not to accuse every participant of bad faith. On the contrary, it is to take sincere belief seriously, and to ask the difficult question: how does sincere belief become entangled with systems of power that it would otherwise abhor? And what might it mean to recognize this entanglement?

### Part I: The Engine Defined

Before tracing the engine through history, we must understand its components.

**The First Component: Pragmatic Ambition**

Every society has its elites—individuals and groups who accumulate disproportionate influence over resources, institutions, and decision-making. Their motivations are generally not mysterious. They seek to preserve and expand their position. This can mean securing access to valuable trade routes, acquiring land and resources, establishing military advantage, or controlling markets. In the medieval period, this meant popes seeking authority over kings and Italian city-states seeking control of Mediterranean trade. In the colonial era, it meant monarchs filling treasuries and companies seeking sugar and cotton. In the modern era, it means securing oil, maintaining global military basing, and ensuring favorable conditions for multinational corporations.

This component is not inherently evil. It is a description of how power tends to operate. But it is incomplete without the second component.

**The Second Component: The Search for Cosmic Meaning**

Human beings are not merely economic actors. We are meaning-makers. We crave narratives that place our lives within a larger story, that give our struggles purpose and our sacrifices significance. The most powerful of these narratives have often been religious, but they can also take secularized forms: the mission to spread democracy, the duty to defend civilization, the destiny to tame a wilderness, the destiny of a master race.

When these two components operate separately, their effects are limited. Ambitious elites without a compelling narrative can only rule through brute force, which is expensive and unstable. A population with powerful beliefs but no organized power can sustain a movement, but may struggle to shape large-scale events. The danger—and the engine—emerges when the two fuse.

**The Fusion**

When elite ambition aligns with popular apocalyptic or quasi-religious narrative, each amplifies the other. The elite gain legitimacy, popular enthusiasm, and a motivated workforce or soldiery. The population gains a sense of cosmic purpose, a conviction that their actions serve not just material interests but divine or moral destiny. The fusion produces a force capable of immense destruction, precisely because it combines the cold calculation of power with the white-hot energy of faith.

Crucially, this fusion does not require conscious conspiracy. It often emerges organically, as elites learn to speak the language of the people, and as religious leaders interpret events in ways that align with the interests of the powerful. The result is a system that perpetuates itself without anyone needing to be fully aware of its mechanics.

**The Deep Temporal Context: Steppe Invasions and European Formation**

To understand why European societies developed this particular engine of expansion, we must look deeper—to the millennia of steppe invasions that shaped the continent's genetic, cultural, and political landscape. From the Scythians in the first millennium BCE to the Huns in the fourth and fifth centuries, the Avars and Magyars in subsequent centuries, and the Mongol invasions of the thirteenth century, Europe was repeatedly subjected to waves of mounted warriors from the Eurasian steppe.

This continuous pressure had several lasting effects:

- **Militarization:** European societies developed fortified structures and military technologies (the castle, heavy cavalry, eventually gunpowder) partially in response to steppe threats.
- **Cultural memory:** The figure of the "barbarian" from the East became embedded in European consciousness, later to be projected onto other peoples during expansion.
- **Release valve dynamics:** When internal pressures built up—surplus warrior populations, religious fervor, economic ambition—the memory of invasion from the East could be inverted into a mandate for invasion toward the East (the Crusades) or, when the Atlantic opened, toward the West.

The steppe hypothesis suggests that the engine this paper describes did not emerge from a vacuum. It was forged in response to millennia of pressure from the East—a pressure that shaped European societies in ways that predisposed them toward expansion when conditions allowed.

### Part II: The Prototype—The Crusades as Template

The Crusades provide our clearest view of this engine in its formative stage.

Consider the situation in late 11th-century Europe. The papal reform movement had strengthened the papacy, but it still struggled for authority against secular rulers and the Eastern Church. The feudal system produced a class of younger sons of nobles with no land and no prospects. Italian maritime republics like Venice and Genoa sought advantage in Mediterranean trade. Into this environment came Pope Urban II's call at Clermont in 1095.

The stated purpose was religious: to liberate Jerusalem from Muslim rule and to aid Eastern Christians. The promise was apocalyptic: participants would receive remission of sins, a spiritual reward that transcended earthly concerns. The enemy was framed as cosmic: not merely political opponents, but defilers of holy places, enemies of God.

Yet the material outcomes were unmistakable. The Crusades extended papal influence. They provided an outlet for restless warriors, channeling their violence outward rather than inward. They opened trade routes that enriched European merchants. They established Crusader states that functioned as new feudal domains for ambitious nobles.

Were the Crusaders merely cynical adventurers using religion as a cover? Some may have been. But the historical record suggests that most participants were sincere in their faith. They believed they were undertaking a holy mission. The tragedy—and the pattern—is that their sincere belief served interests far beyond their own salvation.

The Crusades also reveal the engine's adaptability. When Jerusalem was lost, the framework did not collapse. It simply found new targets: heretics in southern France, pagans in the Baltic, political opponents of the papacy in Italy. The crusading ideal became a flexible meme, capable of legitimating diverse projects of expansion and domination.

### Part III: The Expansion—Colonialism and the New World

When European powers turned their attention across the Atlantic, the crusading framework traveled with them, though its language evolved.

The material ambitions are well-documented. Spain sought gold and silver. Portugal sought a route to the spice trade. England and France sought territory and resources. Joint-stock companies emerged to finance and profit from colonial ventures. These were not hidden motives; they were openly declared.

Yet the enterprise could not have succeeded on greed alone. It required a legitimating narrative. This came from the same fusion we have observed.

The Doctrine of Discovery, articulated in papal bulls of the 15th century—Pope Nicholas V's *Romanus Pontifex* (1455) and Pope Alexander VI's *Inter Caetera* (1493)—provided the theological framework: Christian nations had the right to claim lands not ruled by Christians, and to subjugate their inhabitants for the purpose of conversion. These documents established what became Christian international law, creating principles that would shape European colonization for centuries.

The Puritan colonies of New England offer a particularly pure expression of the fusion. John Winthrop's vision of a "City upon a Hill" explicitly invoked biblical imagery. The colonists saw themselves as a new Israel, fleeing bondage in Europe to establish a holy commonwealth in the wilderness. Their expansion was not conquest but fulfillment of covenant. The native inhabitants were not nations with their own rights and histories, but either potential converts or demonic impediments to be cleared away.

The same framework justified the enslavement of Africans. A twisted theology developed: Africans were descendants of Ham, cursed to servitude; their enslavement, followed by forced conversion, was presented as rescuing them from paganism. The material interests of plantation owners and slave traders fused with a narrative that gave cosmic meaning to atrocity.

### Part IV: The Culmination—Manifest Destiny and the Nineteenth Century

The 19th century gave this fusion its most explicit American expression: Manifest Destiny.

The phrase itself, coined in 1845, captured both components perfectly. "Manifest" suggested obviousness, self-evidence, truth that required no argument. "Destiny" suggested inevitability, divine ordination, a future already written. The expansion of the United States across the continent was framed not as conquest but as fulfillment.

**The Legal Foundation:** As Robert J. Miller's scholarship demonstrates, Manifest Destiny arose directly from the legal principles of the Doctrine of Discovery. This international law was adopted into American colonial and state law, incorporated into the U.S. Constitution, and affirmed by the Supreme Court in *Johnson v. M'Intosh* (1823). The framework was not merely rhetorical; it was embedded in American law.

**The Religious Dimension:** John Christopher Pinheiro's research on the Mexican-American War reveals how anti-Catholicism and crusading language shaped American perceptions. Many Americans argued that "Roman Catholicism was incompatible with civil and religious liberty" and pointed to "Mexico's political troubles as an example of this incompatibility in action." Evangelical Protestants "saw the war as a Providential opportunity to evangelize Catholic Mexico with a 'pure' gospel."

The cost to indigenous peoples was catastrophic. Yet it was framed within the same narrative. Native Americans were either obstacles to progress or people to be "civilized." The Indian Removal Act was presented as humanitarian—separating native peoples from the corrupting influence of white settlement. The Trail of Tears was justified as necessary for the greater good.

### Part V: The Pathology—Nazism as Pattern

The pattern-based view finds one of its clearest expressions in Nazi Germany. Here, the fusion of elite ambition and popular apocalyptic narrative operated with terrifying efficiency.

**The Material Component:** German industrialists, military officers, and party officials had concrete material interests: rearmament, territorial expansion, access to resources, elimination of political opposition. The SS became an economic empire, operating businesses and exploiting slave labor. These were not irrational actors pursuing abstract ideology; they were rational actors pursuing power and profit.

**The Narrative Component:** The Nazi movement offered a cosmic narrative: the Aryan race as the bearer of civilization, locked in an apocalyptic struggle with Jewish Bolshevism, destined to expand into living space in the East. This was not merely propaganda for mass consumption. Leading Nazis sincerely believed elements of this mythology. Himmler's SS was organized as a quasi-religious order, complete with rituals, ceremonies, and an eschatological vision.

**The Fusion:** Elite interests and popular belief amplified each other. Industrialists profited from rearmament while believing they were serving the nation. Soldiers marched east convinced they were defending civilization against subhuman enemies. Ordinary Germans participated in atrocities while believing they were fulfilling their duty to a higher cause.

**The Psychopathic Leader Hypothesis:** Adolf Hitler exhibited classic psychopathic traits: lack of empathy, grandiosity, manipulativeness, pathological lying. But the horror of Nazism cannot be explained by Hitler's psychology alone. The fusion occurred because Hitler's pathology resonated with existing cultural narratives (German nationalism, antisemitism, anti-communism) and material conditions (economic crisis, resentment of Versailles). The population did not become clinically psychopathic, but they adopted psychopathic values—the devaluation of outgroups, the suspension of moral constraints, the willingness to commit atrocities.

This is the pattern at work. Nazism was not a unique historical aberration. It was an expression of the same engine that drove the Crusades, colonialism, and Manifest Destiny—adapted to German cultural materials and twentieth-century conditions.

### Part VI: The Adaptation—The Modern Era

After World War II, explicit religious language became less acceptable in formal statecraft. Yet the underlying structure of the fusion adapted, finding new language for old patterns.

The Cold War provided the first great modern framework. The enemy was communism, framed not merely as a political opponent but as an "evil empire," an atheistic force threatening civilization itself. The language was secularized—"freedom" and "democracy" replaced "Christendom" and "the faith"—but the structure was identical. The West had a cosmic mission: to defend the free world, to contain evil, to ultimately triumph in a struggle of light against darkness.

The material interests were substantial. The military-industrial complex grew enormously. Defense contracts flowed to corporations. Strategic bases were established around the world. Access to oil in the Middle East became a paramount concern.

The War on Terror, launched after September 11, 2001, represents the purest modern expression of the fusion. The enemy was not a state but an abstract evil: "terror" itself. This framing allowed for endless war, as terror could never be fully defeated. It justified interventions across the globe, suspension of civil liberties at home, and a permanent state of emergency.

The language was explicitly Manichaean. President George W. Bush spoke of an "axis of evil." He framed the conflict as a struggle between good and evil, between civilization and barbarism. "Either you are with us, or you are with the terrorists." This was the language of crusade, updated for a secular age.

Yet the material outcomes were concrete. The Iraq War, justified through claims about weapons of mass destruction that proved false, secured American military presence in an oil-rich region and awarded lucrative contracts to defense and reconstruction firms.

### Part VII: The Contemporary Edge—Israel and Eschatology

Nowhere is this fusion more visible today than in the intersection of American foreign policy toward Israel and the eschatological beliefs of some Christian fundamentalists.

The material and strategic interests are clear. Israel has long been viewed by American strategists as a crucial ally in a volatile region. It provides intelligence cooperation, military basing access, and a counterweight to hostile regional powers.

Yet this alliance also receives fervent support from a different quarter: evangelical Christians who believe that the gathering of Jews in the Holy Land is a necessary precondition for the End Times and the Second Coming of Christ. For these believers, support for Israel is not a matter of geopolitics but of prophecy. It is a sacred duty.

Recent scholarship documents this fusion. Motti Inbari and Kirill Bumin's research on Christian Zionism finds "significant theological and eschatological differences among evangelical pastors which are likely to have direct implications on their current foreign policy views, and especially on their support for the State of Israel." Stephen Sizer's work identifies "seven basic theological tenets that distinguish the various strands within the Christian Zionist movement," including beliefs that have direct "political consequences."

The convergence is striking. Strategists and believers find themselves advocating for the same policies, for entirely different reasons. Each reinforces the other. The believers provide grassroots energy and voting power. The strategists provide access and influence. The result is a policy that can survive even when it conflicts with other American interests or values.

### Part VIII: Seeing Gently—Compassion and Complexity

At this point, a reader might feel uncomfortable. The framework can seem to reduce history to a sordid tale of manipulation and delusion. It can feel like an attack on sincere faith, on genuine patriotism, on the noble intentions of countless individuals who have sought to do good.

This discomfort must be taken seriously. If the lens cannot accommodate compassion, it is not a lens but a weapon.

So let us be clear about what this framework does *not* claim.

It does not claim that every participant in these historical processes was cynical or malevolent. Most were not. The Crusader who marched east, the colonist who crossed the Atlantic, the pioneer who ventured west, the German soldier who fought for the Reich, the American soldier who served in Iraq—these were ordinary human beings, seeking meaning, security, and purpose. They loved their families, mourned their dead, and tried, in their own way, to live good lives.

It does not claim that religious or moral belief is merely a mask for power. Belief is real. It shapes action. It gives life meaning. To suggest otherwise is to misunderstand what it means to be human.

It does not claim that all outcomes of Western expansion have been negative. The tradition that produced the Crusades also produced universities, hospitals, and works of art. The civilization that engaged in colonialism also developed concepts of human rights, democracy, and universal dignity. We are not required to choose between celebration and condemnation.

What the framework does claim is this: **Sincere belief and material power have repeatedly become entangled in ways that serve the interests of the powerful while providing cosmic meaning to the population, and this entanglement has produced consequences that neither group fully intended or fully perceived.**

The tragedy is not that people believe. The tragedy is that their belief can be woven into systems of violence and exploitation without their knowledge or consent.

### Part IX: The Gentle Purpose—Seeing as Liberation

If this framework is not about blame, what is its purpose? Why offer this lens at all?

The answer is that **seeing clearly is itself a form of liberation.** Systems of power maintain themselves partly by becoming invisible. They become the water in which we swim, the air we breathe. We do not question them because we do not see them. The fusion of elite interest and popular narrative is most powerful when it is most hidden.

To see the pattern is to begin to question. Not to reject everything, but to ask: In what ways might my sincere beliefs be serving purposes I have not considered? In what ways might the narratives I absorb from my culture be channeling my energy toward ends I would not endorse if I saw them clearly? In what ways might I be participating in systems that I would oppose if I understood them?

These are not comfortable questions. But they are the questions of maturity. A child accepts the stories they are told. An adult learns to examine them. A society that cannot examine its own stories is a society that cannot grow.

The gentle purpose of this lens is to invite that examination. Not to force it, not to shame those who decline it, but to offer it as a tool for those who seek greater clarity.

Jiang Xueqin's work demonstrates that this kind of seeing has practical value. By recognizing patterns, we can anticipate outcomes. His framework, which combines "recurring historical patterns, geopolitical incentives, and game theory," has generated predictions that have proven remarkably accurate. This is not prophecy; it is structural analysis applied rigorously.

### Conclusion: The Continuous Crusade and the Possibility of Awakening

We have traced a pattern across a millennium. From the Crusader marching to Jerusalem, to the Conquistador sailing for the New World, to the Puritan building a city on a hill, to the pioneer fulfilling Manifest Destiny, to the Nazi marching under the swastika, to the modern soldier fighting to spread freedom, to the believer supporting Israel to hasten the End Times—through all these, we have seen the same engine at work.

It is the engine of power fused with meaning, of elite ambition channeling popular faith, of sincere belief serving purposes beyond itself. It is the continuous crusade.

The pattern-based view reveals that these are not isolated phenomena. They are expressions of a single, adaptive structure—one that operates across centuries, across cultures, across scales. The same dynamics that drove the Crusades drove Manifest Destiny and Nazism and the War on Terror. Not because later actors consciously imitated earlier ones, but because the underlying structure of power and meaning tends to produce similar outcomes in similar conditions.

The deep temporal context of steppe invasions reminds us that even this engine has preconditions—historical forces that shaped European societies in ways that made them expansion-prone. The pattern at one scale is nested within patterns at larger scales.

To name it is not to end it. Patterns this deep do not dissolve simply because they are recognized. But recognition is a beginning. It is the moment when we stop being merely carried by the current and begin to see the current itself. It is the moment when we can ask, for the first time: Do I want to go where this current is taking me? Is this really the story I want to be part of?

These questions are not comfortable. They are not easy. But they are the questions of moral awakening. And if the continuous crusade has taught us anything, it is that such awakening is always possible—and always, for those who benefit from the current, deeply threatening.

The purpose of this gentle inquiry has been to make that awakening a little more possible, a little less frightening, and a little more available to all of us who swim in waters we did not create and do not fully see.

### An Invitation to Collaboration

This paper has offered a pattern-based lens for re-examining Western expansion from the Crusades to the present. It suggests that the same underlying structure—the fusion of elite ambition with popular apocalyptic narrative—has operated across centuries and scales, producing similar outcomes in different contexts.

The framework remains conceptual. It requires testing, refinement, and application across multiple domains. We invite collaboration from:

- **Historians** to test the framework against archival evidence, identifying cases where the fusion operated and cases where it did not.
- **Political scientists** to examine how the pattern manifests in contemporary geopolitics and foreign policy.
- **Sociologists** to study how apocalyptic narratives spread through populations and how they interact with elite interests.
- **Psychologists** to investigate the mechanisms by which populations internalize the values of psychopathic leaders.
- **Network theorists** to model how the fusion propagates through social systems and how it might be interrupted.
- **Theologians and religious scholars** to bring their understanding of apocalyptic traditions to bear on the pattern's religious dimensions.
- **Macro-historians and pattern theorists** working in the tradition of Jiang Xueqin to refine our understanding of how deep structural patterns shape human events across scales and centuries.

The pattern is real. It matters. It shapes our world in ways we are only beginning to understand. Understanding it better—with rigor, compassion, and intellectual humility—is a task worthy of our best efforts.
