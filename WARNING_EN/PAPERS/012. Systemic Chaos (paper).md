# Systemic Chaos: True Randomness from Deterministic Concurrency

## The Seedy Generator

## Abstract

It is a foundational principle of computer science that deterministic processes cannot produce true randomness. A deterministic machine executing deterministic code should, by definition, produce deterministic output. This paper presents evidence that this principle, while true for isolated sequential processes, does not hold for concurrent systems with cyclic topology.

We demonstrate a minimal software implementation—three threads with a circular dependency, executing asynchronously with no synchronization primitives—that produces output passing rigorous statistical tests for randomness. Two layers of testing are presented: direct output tested with PractRand (passed despite the generator's slow rate), and extrapolated testing where Seedy seeds a fast PRNG (QX64) to generate vast amounts of data, which also passes PractRand and TestU01. This confirms that the entropy harvested is sufficient for high-quality randomness.

We term this phenomenon **systemic chaos**: emergent unpredictability arising not from stochastic components but from the interaction topology of deterministic subsystems under concurrent execution. We propose that **memory contention under concurrent execution is the logical energy** injected into the system—when threads saturate the memory subsystem, their interleaving becomes unpredictable, amplifying microscopic variations into macroscopic chaos. The system behaves as a **logical unstable resonator**, where timing variations circulate and compound through the cyclic dependency.

We further propose the **energy threshold hypothesis**: such systems exhibit a phase transition from predictable to chaotic behavior when the memory subsystem becomes saturated by concurrent access. Below saturation, the system remains predictable; at saturation, chaos emerges.

If validated, this work has implications for random number generation, complex systems theory, and our understanding of computation itself. It suggests that determinism at the component level does not entail determinism at the system level when topology and concurrency align.

**Keywords:** true random number generator, concurrency, deterministic chaos, systemic chaos, energy threshold, memory contention, emergent behavior

## 1. Introduction: The Paradox

### 1.1 The Dogma

Computer science rests on a bedrock claim: a deterministic machine produces deterministic output. Given the same initial state and the same inputs, a program will always produce the same result. This is the foundation of verification, testing, and our very understanding of what computation *is*.

From this follows a corollary: true randomness cannot be generated in software. Software random number generators are *pseudorandom*—deterministic algorithms whose output *appears* random but is entirely determined by their initial seed. True randomness requires hardware entropy sources: thermal noise, radioactive decay, quantum effects, or other physical processes with fundamental indeterminacy.

This distinction is taught as absolute. It is embedded in every cryptography textbook, every random number generator API, every discussion of system security.

### 1.2 The Counter-Intuitive Hypothesis

But consider the three-body problem in physics. Three masses moving under mutual gravity, each following deterministic Newtonian laws, produce trajectories that are, for practical purposes, unpredictable. The system is deterministic at the component level—every force, every acceleration, every position is mathematically determined—yet the *compound* behavior defies long-term prediction.

What if a computational system could approximate this topology? What if cyclic dependency, combined with the inherent asynchrony of concurrent execution, could similarly amplify microscopic indeterminacies into macroscopic unpredictability?

This paper reports on an experiment that began from this intuition.

### 1.3 What This Paper Reports

We present:

1. **The Seedy generator**: a minimal software implementation using three threads with cyclic dependency and no synchronization, running on standard multicore hardware

2. **Preliminary empirical results**: direct testing with PractRand (passed despite low data rate) and extrapolated testing using Seedy to seed a fast PRNG (QX64) to generate vast amounts of data (also passed PractRand and TestU01)

3. **A conceptual framework**: memory contention under concurrent execution as the logical energy that drives systemic chaos; the system as a logical unstable resonator

4. **The energy threshold hypothesis**: a testable prediction that such systems exhibit a phase transition from predictable to chaotic behavior when the memory subsystem becomes saturated

5. **Implications and open questions**: what this means for randomness generation and the philosophy of computation

This is a preliminary report. Controlled experiments remain to be conducted. But the phenomenon is real, reproducible, and demands explanation.

## 2. Genesis of the Idea

### 2.1 The Three-Body Problem as Inspiration

The intuition arose from considering a classic problem in physics. Poincaré showed in 1890 that even a simple system—three bodies under mutual gravity—could exhibit dynamics so complex that infinite precision would be required for long-term prediction [1]. The system is deterministic; the unpredictability is *emergent*.

What creates this unpredictability? Three factors seem essential:

- **Deterministic components** (each body follows Newton's laws)
- **Cyclic interdependency** (each body's future depends on the others, which depend on it)
- **Sensitive dependence** (microscopic differences amplify through interactions)

### 2.2 The Bird and the Insight

On New Year's Eve, 2022, returning from Yellowknife and listening to a cryptography podcast, a bird flying outside the window caught my attention. The turbulence behind its wings sparked a thought: unpredictability in nature often arises from loose, concurrent interactions. Could this be replicated in software? The image that formed was of a system that was interdependent and loosely coupled—where uncoordinated race conditions would create exactly that kind of loose coupling, each thread's input intersecting with another's output through shared memory.

### 2.3 From Physics to Computation

A modern multicore processor executing concurrent threads is, in some respects, analogous. It contains multiple deterministic processing units (cores) that interact through shared memory. They execute asynchronously—their relative timing is not fixed.

The key insight was this: if we could create a computational topology that mirrored the cyclic dependency of the three-body problem, might the system's inherent asynchrony play the role of the "sensitive dependence" that amplifies into macroscopic unpredictability?

### 2.4 The Minimal Case

The simplest possible cyclic topology is a ring of three elements, each depending on the previous:

```
Thread 0: reads Node 0, writes Node 1
Thread 1: reads Node 1, writes Node 2
Thread 2: reads Node 2, writes Node 0
```

Each thread performs a deterministic transformation on the value it reads. No thread waits for another. No synchronization. No locks. Just three loops, running as fast as the scheduler allows, continuously reading and writing shared memory.

If the system were perfectly synchronized, this would produce a periodic sequence. But perfect synchronization is impossible on real hardware. Cores execute independently. The operating system scheduler introduces arbitrary delays. Cache coherence creates variable latencies.

The question was: would these variations be amplified by the cyclic topology into macroscopic randomness?

### 2.5 First Implementation

The first implementation took under an hour to write. Three POSIX threads, three 64-bit integers in shared memory, a simple mixing function. No attempts to "inject" randomness. No deliberate use of system entropy sources.

The output was captured and piped to the PractRand statistical test suite.

It passed.

And kept passing.

The impossible, it seemed, was happening: a deterministic software system was producing output indistinguishable from true randomness.

## 3. The Seedy Generator

### 3.1 Architecture: The Accountant Analogy

To understand the generator's inner workings, consider an office with three accountants and three filing cabinets. The accountants work continuously, following strict rules. The cabinets connect them in a ring:

- **Accountant A (AA)** reads from cabinet 3 (C3) and writes to cabinet 1 (C1)
- **Accountant B (AB)** reads from cabinet 1 (C1) and writes to cabinet 2 (C2)
- **Accountant C (AC)** reads from cabinet 2 (C2) and writes to cabinet 3 (C3)

The flow forms a closed loop:

```
AA -> C1 -> AB -> C2 -> AC -> C3
  \\____________ <- ____________/
```

Each accountant's job for generating a single 64-bit number is:

1. Write the number 1 on a sheet that remains on their desk.
2. Do the following 64 times:
    1. Look at the number in their designated input cabinet to see if it is odd or even.
    2. Based on that, pick the first or second number from the next pair on a predetermined list.
    3. Multiply the picked number with the current number on their sheet.
    4. Look again at the input cabinet and combine that number with their sheet using binary XOR.
    5. Take the result and add it to the number in their output cabinet, replacing what was there.
3. Finally, combine the number on their sheet with the output cabinet using binary XOR.

### 3.2 Why This Creates Systemic Chaos

Each accountant's individual work is entirely predictable. But when three accountants work simultaneously, their interactions become wildly unpredictable because they interfere with each other's access to the cabinets. The order in which they arrive at each cabinet—who reads first, who writes first—affects every subsequent number for everyone.

Since each accountant's future depends on the others' outputs, which in turn depend on their own prior states, the system forms a closed loop of mutual influence. No single actor can know its own future with certainty because no one coordinates their actions. A supervisor (the monitoring thread) makes a point *not* to coordinate anything. Instead, an alarm periodically triggers the supervisor to read all three cabinets, combine them with XOR, and output the result.

Each of the 64 rounds per accountant creates another opportunity for divergence. By repeating the process 64 times to compute a single 64-bit integer, the generator ensures the final number is computed with sufficient accumulated uncertainty to guarantee complete decorrelation from the initial state.

When the threads cannot run simultaneously (on a single-core machine), the effect is diminished. The system becomes *pseudo-chaotic*—the work of each accountant is predictable, and only the moments when they stop and start again contribute to deviation. This is why true concurrency is essential.

### 3.3 The Code

In C, the accountants become POSIX threads, and the filing cabinets become shared memory locations:

```c
// Shared state (the filing cabinets)
uint64_t node0, node1, node2;

// Accountant A: reads node0, writes node1
void* thread_a(void* arg) {
    while (1) {
        uint64_t val = node0;
        val = seed_modify_64(val);
        node1 = val;
    }
}

// Accountant B: reads node1, writes node2
void* thread_b(void* arg) {
    while (1) {
        uint64_t val = node1;
        val = seed_modify_64(val);
        node2 = val;
    }
}

// Accountant C: reads node2, writes node0
void* thread_c(void* arg) {
    while (1) {
        uint64_t val = node2;
        val = seed_modify_64(val);
        node0 = val;
    }
}

// The transformation function (each accountant's "rules")
uint64_t seed_modify_64(uint64_t x) {
    x ^= x >> 12;
    x ^= x << 25;
    x ^= x >> 27;
    return x * 0x2545F4914F6CDD1DULL;
}

// The supervisor: samples all three cabinets periodically
uint64_t sample_output() {
    return node0 ^ node1 ^ node2;
}
```

### 3.4 Key Design Choices

Several choices are essential:

- **No synchronization**: No mutexes, no atomics, no memory barriers. Race conditions are not bugs—they are the mechanism.
- **Cyclic dependency**: Each thread writes a node that another thread reads. The graph contains a cycle of length three.
- **Asynchronous execution**: Threads run independently, scheduled across available cores. Their relative timing is uncontrolled.
- **Deterministic transformation**: The `seed_modify_64` function is pure and deterministic. Each thread's work is predictable in isolation.
- **No injected randomness**: The code contains no calls to hardware RNGs, system entropy sources, or deliberate noise sources.

### 3.5 What the System Does Not Contain

By the standard definitions of computer science, this system should produce deterministic output. It does not rely on:

- Hardware random number generator instructions (no `rdrand`, `rdseed`)
- Operating system entropy sources (`/dev/urandom`, `getrandom`)
- Deliberate use of environmental noise
- Non-deterministic system calls
- Uninitialized memory

### 3.6 Platform Requirements

The generator requires:
- Multiple physical cores capable of true concurrency
- A compiler that does not optimize away shared memory accesses (volatile qualifier or equivalent)

## 4. Empirical Results

### 4.1 Testing Methodology

The Seedy generator was tested using two complementary approaches:

1. **Direct testing**: The raw output of the generator was piped directly into the PractRand statistical test suite [2], known for its sensitivity to even subtle biases. This tests the generator's native output, though its slow rate (limited by thread scheduling and sleep timers) makes direct testing time‑consuming.

2. **Extrapolated testing**: To evaluate the quality of the entropy at much larger scales, Seedy was used as a seed source for a fast, deterministic stretching algorithm, QX64. QX64 fills a large internal table (4 × 65536 64‑bit words) with output from Seedy, then generates an output stream by XORing four table entries indexed by a simple linear function. Because QX64's output is merely a deterministic combination of the seed material, any statistical anomalies in the final stream would reveal insufficient entropy in the seed. Passing extended tests with QX64 therefore confirms that Seedy provides high‑quality entropy.

The QX64 code is minimal and transparent:

```c
typedef struct QX64_s 
{
    void* feeder;                // Seedy generator
    uint64_t step;
    uint64_t iter;
    uint64_t pool[4][65536];     // 2 MiB table filled by Seedy
} QX64;

uint64_t qx64_next(QX64* q)
{
    if( q->step == 0 )
    {
        // Fill the pool using Seedy
        ((void(*)(uint8_t* b, uint64_t n))q->feeder)((uint8_t*)&q->pool, sizeof(q->pool));
        q->step++;
    }
    // Generate output by XORing four pool entries
    uint64_t pos64 = q->step * q->iter;
    uint16_t* pos16 = (uint16_t*)&pos64;
    uint64_t next = q->pool[0][pos16[0]] ^
                    q->pool[1][pos16[1]] ^
                    q->pool[2][pos16[2]] ^
                    q->pool[3][pos16[3]];
    q->step++;
    return next;
}
```

This design ensures that any bias in Seedy's output would be amplified and detectable in the QX64 stream.

### 4.2 Direct Test Results

When tested directly, Seedy's output passed PractRand without any anomalies for all lengths examined (up to 2^38 bytes). This result is notable because PractRand is extremely sensitive, and Seedy's low data rate would normally make it more likely to flag timing‑related artifacts. The fact that it passed confirms that the generator's internal dynamics produce high‑quality unpredictability from the very first sample—no warm‑up period is required, although a safety margin is kept in production code. On macOS, sampling intervals as low as 5 µs still showed immediate decorrelation from the initial known state.

### 4.3 Extrapolated Test Results

Using Seedy to seed QX64, we generated a 2 MiB pool and then produced a continuous stream totaling 2^50 bytes. This stream was tested with both PractRand and TestU01 (Small Crush). The results, summarized below, show that QX64 fed by Seedy passes all tests, confirming that Seedy's entropy is sufficient to drive a fast PRNG without introducing detectable artifacts.

| Test                | M1 (8 cores) | i5 (2 cores/4 threads) | G4 (1 core) |
|---------------------|--------------|------------------------|-------------|
| PractRand (direct)  | PASS         | PASS                   | FAIL        |
| TestU01 (direct)    | PASS         | PASS                   | FAIL        |
| QX64 + PractRand    | PASS (94%)   | PASS (93%)             | PASS (95%)  |
| QX64 + TestU01 S.C. | PASS (97%)   | PASS (96%)             | PASS (99%)  |

*Note: Percentages indicate the proportion of fast preliminary tests passed; all full tests passed with no anomalies.*

The G4, a single‑core PowerPC machine, cannot provide true concurrency; its threads are time‑sliced, so the systemic chaos effect is severely diminished. Direct testing of Seedy on the G4 failed, as expected. However, when its output was used to seed QX64, the resulting stream still passed the extended tests—a surprising result suggesting that even degraded systemic chaos contains enough entropy for many practical purposes when stretched by a deterministic algorithm.

On multicore machines (M1 and i5), both direct and extrapolated tests passed, confirming that true concurrency is the critical factor.

### 4.4 Hardware Independence

Seedy was tested on x86 (Intel i5, strongly ordered memory model) and ARM (Apple M1, weakly ordered). Despite these fundamental architectural differences, the results were consistent: both platforms passed all statistical tests. This indicates that the phenomenon is not an artifact of a specific CPU's memory consistency model, but rather a general property of cyclic concurrency on modern multicore systems.

### 4.5 Summary of Findings

- **Immediate decorrelation**: The generator produces statistically random output from the first sample onward.
- **Concurrency requirement**: True parallelism (multiple physical cores) is necessary for full systemic chaos; single‑core execution yields degraded randomness.
- **Extrapolation works**: Even the degraded output from a single‑core machine, when used to seed a simple deterministic stretcher, can pass stringent tests—suggesting that systemic chaos is a robust entropy source.
- **Hardware independent**: The effect holds across different CPU architectures and memory models.

These results, though preliminary, provide strong evidence that systemic chaos is real, reproducible, and potentially useful for random number generation.

## 5. Toward an Explanation: Memory Contention as Logical Energy

### 5.1 The Core Insight

The Seedy generator works because **concurrent execution saturates the memory subsystem, creating contention that amplifies microscopic timing variations into macroscopic unpredictability**.

Consider what happens when multiple threads continuously read and write shared memory locations with no synchronization:

- Each memory access must be serialized by the memory controller. When multiple cores issue accesses simultaneously, they contend for limited bandwidth.
- The exact order in which competing accesses are serviced depends on nanoscale timing: the state of caches, the relative phase of core clocks, the electrical characteristics of the bus.
- These timing differences are not deterministic at the software level—they arise from the physical implementation of the hardware.

In Seedy, the cyclic dependency ensures that the outcome of every memory operation affects future values, which in turn affect future access patterns. The system becomes a **logical unstable resonator**: timing variations circulate around the cycle, each pass amplifying the uncertainty.

### 5.2 Saturation as the Energy Threshold

The memory subsystem has a finite capacity to handle concurrent accesses. Below that capacity—when threads are not truly simultaneous, or when the memory system can keep up without contention—access order remains relatively stable. The system behaves predictably.

But when the threads push the memory subsystem to its limit—when they "hammer the poor thing" with continuous, unsynchronized reads and writes—contention becomes the dominant factor. The exact order of operations becomes exquisitely sensitive to conditions that are themselves affected by previous operations. The system crosses a threshold into chaos.

This is why true concurrency matters: only when threads run simultaneously on physically distinct cores can they generate enough pressure to saturate the memory subsystem. On a single-core machine, memory accesses are naturally serialized; there is no contention, and chaos does not emerge.

### 5.3 The Energy Spectrum

The degree of memory saturation determines the system's behavior:

- **No saturation (sequential execution)** → **No chaos**: The system is perfectly predictable. The cyclic dependency collapses to a fixed order; the output is deterministic.

- **Partial saturation (limited concurrency)** → **Crappy chaos**: The system shows some unpredictability, but constraints (coarse-grained scheduling, shared-core contention) limit the amplification of variations. Statistical tests may show anomalies.

- **Full saturation (truly parallel, unsynchronized)** → **Full chaos**: Threads execute simultaneously on physically distinct cores, saturating the memory subsystem. Contention amplifies microscopic variations through the cyclic topology. Output becomes statistically random.

### 5.4 A Thought Experiment

If a memory subsystem could be built that outperformed the cores—that could service all concurrent accesses without ever creating contention—then Seedy might become deterministic even with true concurrency. The logical energy would be dissipated without amplification. This hypothetical underscores that it is not concurrency alone, but the *saturation of a limited resource*, that drives systemic chaos.

### 5.5 Concurrency as a Control Parameter

If memory saturation is the energy source, then controlling concurrency controls the energy input. By varying the number of threads, their core affinity, or the memory access pattern, we should be able to move the system along the spectrum:

- Pin all threads to the same core → no contention → predictable
- Spread threads across cores but reduce access frequency → partial contention → degraded randomness
- Spread threads across cores with maximum access frequency → full saturation → full chaos

This is a testable prediction.

## 6. The Energy Threshold Hypothesis

### 6.1 A Testable Prediction

From the above, we derive a hypothesis:

**Energy Threshold Hypothesis:** For a system of deterministic components with cyclic interdependency, there exists a critical level of memory contention—a saturation point—below which the dynamics remain predictable and above which systemic chaos emerges.

"Memory contention" here means the degree to which concurrent accesses compete for limited memory subsystem resources (bandwidth, cache lines, bus arbitration).

### 6.2 Predictions

If the hypothesis is correct, we predict:

1. **Core binding**: Running all threads on a single core (eliminating contention) will cause the Seedy generator's output to fail statistical tests.

2. **Hyperthread degradation**: Running on hyperthreads (which share execution units and cache) will produce lower contention and thus lower-quality randomness than running on physical cores.

3. **Access frequency reduction**: Introducing delays between memory accesses (e.g., with `nanosleep`) will reduce contention and degrade randomness.

4. **Synchronization injection**: Adding any synchronization primitive (mutex, atomic, memory barrier) will serialize accesses and reduce contention, degrading randomness.

5. **Phase transition**: The transition from predictable to chaotic should occur at the point where the memory subsystem becomes saturated—a threshold that may be measurable via performance counters (e.g., cache miss rates, bus contention events).

These predictions can be tested experimentally. The G4 results already provide initial confirmation: when true concurrency is impossible, direct output fails.

## 7. Related Work

### 7.1 Classical Chaos Theory

Poincaré's work on the three-body problem [1] and Lorenz's discovery of sensitive dependence [3] established that deterministic systems can produce unpredictable behavior. This work differs in focusing on discrete concurrent systems rather than continuous equations, and on chaos arising from *resource contention* rather than nonlinearity alone.

### 7.2 Concurrency and Nondeterminism

Computer science has long recognized that concurrency introduces nondeterminism [4]. Race conditions are typically viewed as bugs to be eliminated. This work suggests they can be a resource to be harvested—and that their effectiveness depends on saturating the memory subsystem.

### 7.3 Random Number Generation

True random number generators typically rely on dedicated hardware [5,6,7]. The Seedy generator is unusual in using general-purpose hardware and software to achieve true randomness, harvesting entropy from memory contention under concurrent execution.

## 8. Discussion

### 8.1 Implications for Random Number Generation

If validated, the Seedy generator suggests a new class of random number generators: **contention-based entropy sources** that rely on cyclic topology and memory saturation rather than dedicated hardware.

Advantages:
- Runs on standard multicore hardware
- Software-only deployment (though hardware-dependent)
- High throughput (when used as a seed for a fast PRNG)
- Minimal code footprint
- Provides original entropy, not just pseudorandomness

Disadvantages:
- Hardware-dependent behavior must be characterized per platform
- OS and scheduler interactions need careful management
- Requires true concurrency (multiple physical cores) to achieve saturation
- Not formally provable like cryptographic PRNGs

For applications requiring high-quality randomness—cryptography, scientific simulation, gambling—a hybrid approach might be appropriate: use Seedy as an entropy source to seed a cryptographic PRNG.

### 8.2 Implications for Complex Systems Theory

The Seedy generator provides a concrete, manipulable instance of emergent complexity driven by resource contention. Unlike abstract mathematical models, this system can be run, measured, and experimented with on ordinary hardware.

Key theoretical insights:

- **Topology matters**: The same components arranged acyclically behave predictably; arranged cyclically, they become chaotic. The structure of interactions is as important as the components themselves.

- **Contention enables amplification**: Without contention, timing variations would average out or remain microscopic. Contention allows them to grow.

- **Saturation drives the transition**: The system has a phase transition controlled by the degree of memory saturation—a concrete example of order-chaos transitions in a computational medium.

### 8.3 Implications for the Philosophy of Computation

The Seedy generator challenges a comfortable assumption: that deterministic components guarantee deterministic systems.

This is true for sequential processes. It is not true for concurrent systems with cyclic topology running on hardware with limited resources. The *contention* for those resources reasserts the physicality of computation: programs are not purely logical abstractions; they run on machines with finite bandwidth, and that finitude can give rise to genuine unpredictability.

We are accustomed to thinking of software as pure logic. But software executes on hardware where resources are shared, and sharing creates contention, and contention amplifies the microscopic into the macroscopic. The boundary between software and hardware, between logical and physical, becomes permeable.

### 8.4 Limitations

- **Preliminary results**: Not yet peer-reviewed; controlled experiments remain to be conducted
- **Uncontrolled environment**: OS scheduling, background processes, and power management all affect behavior
- **No systematic contention experiments**: Predictions about core binding, hyperthreads, and access frequency remain untested
- **Platform dependence**: Results may vary across processors; characterization is incomplete
- **No mathematical model**: Unlike classical chaos theory, we lack equations that describe the dynamics

### 8.5 Future Work

Immediate next steps:

1. **Controlled contention experiments**: Systematic variation of core binding, access frequency, and synchronization to map the phase transition
2. **Platform characterization**: Testing across a wide range of processors to understand how memory architecture affects the threshold
3. **Topology exploration**: Varying the number of nodes and connection patterns to find minimal and optimal configurations
4. **Mathematical modeling**: Developing formal models (e.g., queueing networks with feedback) that capture contention-amplified dynamics
5. **Applications**: Designing and evaluating a production-quality RNG based on these principles
6. **Long-term testing**: Extending statistical tests to 2^60 bytes and beyond

## 9. Conclusion

We began with a counter-intuitive intuition: that a deterministic software system, appropriately structured, might produce true randomness. That intuition, sparked by a bird's flight on New Year's Eve, led to a minimal implementation—three threads, three shared variables, a cyclic dependency—that produces output passing stringent statistical tests, both directly and when used as a seed for a fast PRNG.

We propose that **memory contention under concurrent execution is the logical energy** that drives this system into chaos. When threads saturate the memory subsystem, their interleaving becomes unpredictable, and the cyclic topology turns the system into a **logical unstable resonator** where timing variations circulate and compound. The energy threshold hypothesis makes testable predictions about how controlling contention should control randomness.

The phenomenon is real. It has been observed on x86 and ARM, on machines old and new, and it survives the most stringent tests we can apply. It challenges comfortable assumptions about determinism and randomness, software and hardware, logic and physics.

The deeper lesson may be this: determinism at the component level does not entail determinism at the system level when topology and finite resources align. Unpredictability is not always a bug to be eliminated. Sometimes it is a property to be cultivated—a resource to be harvested from the fertile chaos of contention.

The three threads in a ring, hammering the memory system, teach us what the three bodies in the heavens taught Poincaré: that even perfect determinism, when woven into a closed loop of mutual influence and forced to compete for limited resources, can give birth to genuine unpredictability. The universe may be deterministic. But it is not, for that reason, predictable.
