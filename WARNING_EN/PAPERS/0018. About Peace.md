# About Peace

***An Essay on Compression, Fog, and the Only Kind of Peace That Lasts***

Some people believe the environment movement is a conspiracy. Others believe it represents objective reality. I pity those who refuse to see how history can be nudged without needing complete conspiratorial control of the world. A simple politicisation of a cause suffices for the nudge to appear as manipulation to one side, forcing the other to react and bypass their own self‑criticism.

This is the fog. And this is where our story begins.

---

## I. The Nudge Is Not a Conspiracy

You do not need a secret cabal of billionaires meeting in a Swiss bunker to steer history. You only need a cause that is *real* – and then you need to politicise it. Once an issue enters the political arena, it changes shape. It must recruit, polarise, and sometimes exaggerate to break through noise. Opponents will call this manipulation. And sometimes they are not entirely wrong, because all political communication involves framing, selective emphasis, and emotional appeal.

The environment movement is a genuine response to measurable reality: rising CO₂, melting ice, collapsing biodiversity. That part is not a trick. But it is also a *politicised* cause. The scientist who says “we must act now” is not lying, but she is also not speaking in a neutral, value‑free voice. She is trying to move you.

The same is true for the movement that opposes her. It also politicises. It also frames, selects, and appeals. Neither side is a conspiracy. Both sides are human. And both sides, once locked in combat, stop criticising themselves. Self‑criticism – the ability to say “maybe we are exaggerating, maybe our solution has flaws, maybe we should listen to reasonable doubt” – is the first casualty of politicisation.

That is the nudge. That is how history moves without a puppet master.

---

## II. The Fog of War and the Fog of Peace

War is the ultimate fog machine. In 1914, soldiers on the Western Front discovered this when they spontaneously stopped fighting. The Christmas Truce was not ordered. It emerged from the soldiers themselves – men who had been told their enemies were monsters, and who discovered instead that the men across the wire were farmers, clerks, and fathers.

They exchanged cigarettes, sang carols, played football, and buried their dead together. For a brief moment, the fog lifted. Peace became possible.

Then the authorities on both sides suppressed it. Soldiers who fraternised were reassigned, punished, censored. The event was erased from official records. Why? Because war cannot survive clarity. If soldiers see their enemies as human, the entire justification for killing collapses. The fog must be restored.

This is not a conspiracy. It is the logic of the system. War generates fog. It makes all information suspect. It justifies secrecy, surveillance, emergency powers, and the suspension of normal political constraints. It focuses public attention outward – on the enemy – while internal transformations proceed unnoticed. And it conditions populations to accept sacrifice, mobilisation, and state control as normal.

Peace through strength – the strategy of mutual deterrence, walls, threats, and armed alliances – does not lift the fog. It merely replaces one fog with another. The Cold War was a peace of compression: two blocks squeezed together by terror. When the Soviet Union collapsed, the stored energy released not in nuclear war, but in Yugoslavia’s dissolution, in the chaos of former republics, in decades of instability. Compressive peace never lasts. It only postpones collapse.

---

## III. The Engine of Power: Elite Ambition + Apocalyptic Meaning

Why does compression persist despite its failures? Because it serves a purpose. War works – not in the way it promises, but in a deeper, more terrible way. War rewires societies. It dissolves frozen structures. It shortens feedback loops. It mobilises collective effort. It accomplishes in years what peacetime cannot accomplish in generations.

The Second World War is the great example. Before the war, aviation was a novelty, radar a laboratory experiment, computers a theoretical curiosity, nuclear physics an academic pursuit, penicillin a laboratory curiosity. The war compressed decades of research and development into a few years. It transformed the role of women, the status of African Americans, the structure of class relations. It dissolved the old European empires. It gave birth to the United Nations, the Bretton Woods system, the World Bank.

This is the terrible paradox: the most destructive event in human history was also extraordinarily generative. The same war that killed 75 million people accelerated the end of colonialism and the civil rights movement.

We do not like to acknowledge this. It feels obscene. And it is obscene. The deaths are not a price that can be weighed against any benefit. But if we are serious about peace – real peace, not compressive peace – we must understand why war persists. It persists not only because of greed, fear, and ideology. It persists because it achieves transformation. It achieves what compressive peace cannot: the actual rewiring of a society’s fundamental topology.

The engine that drives this is the fusion of elite material ambition with popular apocalyptic or quasi‑religious narrative. The Crusades: elites wanted trade routes and power; the population wanted remission of sins. Manifest Destiny: elites wanted land and resources; the population believed America was a new Israel, destined to possess the continent. The War on Terror: elites wanted oil and military bases; the population believed they were fighting an “axis of evil.”

The fusion is seamless. Elites learn to speak the language of the people. Religious leaders interpret events to align with power. The result is an engine capable of enormous destruction, because it combines cold calculation with white‑hot faith.

No conspiracy required. Just the natural alignment of interests and meaning.

## IV. The Psychopathic Gradient and the Addiction Beneath

If war is the ultimate fog machine, consumerism is the steady drip of dopamine that keeps us docile the rest of the time. We are addicted. Not in a metaphorical sense – in a neurochemical sense.

The brain’s dopamine reward system has been hijacked. Advertising, social media, and the endless novelty of the market deliver unpredictable rewards: a like, a comment, a perfectly targeted advertisement for exactly the thing you did not know you wanted. This unpredictability is what makes the loop inescapable. We scroll not despite the boredom but because of it, each swipe holding the potential for a hit.

The addict is not born; she is made. Over centuries, a deliberate project transformed human beings from producers oriented toward sufficiency into consumers oriented toward perpetual desire. The smartphone is the hypodermic needle. Social media is the slot machine for the soul.

Now the supply is running thin. The foundational illusions are crumbling: infinite growth on a finite planet; fulfilment through acquisition; the aspirational lifestyle that has become financially inaccessible to a growing majority. The result is withdrawal. The symptoms are all around us: rising rates of depression, anxiety, and loneliness; political rage and scapegoating; the desperate search for substitute highs – outrage addiction, financial speculation, digital compulsion.

This is the psychopathic gradient. Societies, like individuals, can be arrayed along a spectrum from empathy to exploitation. Individualism and communism – as high‑energy configurations – both tend to select for psychopathic traits in their elites. The corporate executive who can lay off thousands without losing sleep. The politician who can say anything to win. The financier who can extract wealth without creating value. These are not exceptions. They are the ideal types of the system.

And because they are at the top, their behaviour echoes downward. Their ruthlessness becomes the benchmark for ambition. Their manipulation becomes the model for negotiation. Their lack of empathy becomes the standard for professionalism.

This is not because they are evil. It is because the system has broken its feedback loops. Consequences no longer return to those who cause them. The executive never meets the worker he laid off. The politician never buries the soldier she sent to war. The financier never breathes the air poisoned by the factory he financed.

Broken feedback is the root of systemic psychopathy. And broken feedback is the signature of compressive peace.

## V. The Alternative: Peace Through Transparency

If compression cannot create stability, what can? Look at a suspension bridge. It does not hold itself together by squeezing its components. It holds together through tensile strength – cables under tension that pull the structure into coherence. The cables are anchored in deep foundations. The tension is distributed throughout the system.

In human systems, tensile strength takes the form of shared identity, mutual benefit, trust built through repeated interaction, and norms internalised by participants. These are not external forces applied to the system; they are properties of the system itself. They create a “pull” toward cooperation rather than a “push” away from conflict.

This is the logic of **peace through transparency** – a proposal called *Pax Perspicua*.

The idea is simple. Nations voluntarily join an association with three pillars:

1. **Military restraint.** All forces confined to national territory. No offensive action against another member.
2. **Radical transparency.** All state‑generated intelligence – satellite imagery, signals intercepts, financial intelligence, domestic assessments – is published to a permanent public repository called the Agora.
3. **Automatic sanctions.** Any verified act of aggression or concealment triggers an immediate, five‑year trade embargo by all members.

Why would any nation agree to this? Because the alternative – the current system of compressive peace – is failing. Because the open‑source intelligence revolution has already shown that 70‑90% of classified analytic value can be generated from public sources at 2% of the cost. Because the cost of being locked out of the world’s most prosperous market is higher than the cost of transparency.

This is not utopian. It is an extrapolation of existing research and demonstrated capabilities. The capitalist peace literature proves that economic interdependence raises the opportunity cost of conflict. The OSINT revolution proves that secrecy is no longer the advantage it once was. Digital public infrastructures prove that transparent, decentralised systems can be governed at scale.

Peace through transparency does not rely on trust. It relies on **verifiability**. You do not need to trust that your neighbour is not hiding tanks in a forest. You can see the satellite imagery. You do not need to trust that they are not planning a cyberattack. You can see their intelligence assessments. You do not need to trust that they will not invade. You know that if they do, your trade will automatically stop for five years.

That is a credible deterrent. Not because it is backed by the threat of violence, but because it is backed by the threat of losing access to the global economy.

## VI. The Living System: From Compression to Tensile Peace

Compressive peace is the peace of the squeezed blocks – the bridge that collapses when the jacks are removed. Tensile peace is the peace of the living forest – diverse, redundant, adaptive, self‑repairing.

A forest achieves stability through diversity: multiple species ensure that no single threat can collapse the whole system. Through redundancy: if one tree falls, others fill the gap. Through nutrient cycling: waste from one organism becomes food for another. Through adaptation: the system evolves in response to changing conditions. Through self‑repair: damage triggers growth responses that heal the wound.

A peaceful human society must exhibit the same properties. It must be diverse enough that no single conflict can tear it apart. It must have redundant institutions so that if one fails, others continue functioning. It must cycle conflict energy into productive channels. It must adapt to changing circumstances without collapsing. It must have the capacity to heal its own wounds.

This is not a metaphor. Network theory shows that systems with high connectivity and multiple overlapping affiliations are far more resilient to perturbation than polarised systems with two opposing blocs. When a shock hits, it disperses through the network and is absorbed.

The substrate of this living system is healthy human beings – individuals capable of trust, empathy, cooperation, and love. Not love as sentimentality, but love as the fundamental dynamic that enables complex systems to cohere without compression: the capacity to see another as fully human; the willingness to extend trust before it is earned; the ability to hold complexity; the courage to remain connected even through conflict; the wisdom to understand that your wellbeing is inseparable from the wellbeing of others.

When the substrate is healthy, something remarkable becomes possible. The system itself begins to exhibit properties that transcend its components. Mankind – humanity as a coherent, functioning whole – emerges as a property of healthy human relationships. You cannot build it. You can only become the kind of people from which it emerges on its own.

## VII. What You Can Do – Starting Now

You do not need to wait for a revolution. You can start in your own life, with your own boundaries.

**Start seeing.** Notice when information is being filtered. Ask what is not being shown. Wonder why certain stories are amplified and others ignored. Notice when feedback is broken. Ask who is suffering and whether those who cause the suffering know it.

**Shorten your feedback loops.** Who suffers from your choices? Can you see them? Can they speak to you? If you buy cheap clothes, who made them? If you drive a car, whose lungs fill with exhaust? You cannot fix everything. But you can shorten one loop. Buy from someone you can talk to. Apologise when you cause harm. Ask the quiet person what they think.

**Find the canaries.** In your workplace, who is most stressed? In your neighbourhood, who is struggling? In your country, who is being ignored? Listen to them. Do not defend the system. Just listen. Their suffering is data.

**Cultivate curiosity.** Try a small experiment. A different way of sharing food. A weekly meeting without a boss. A tool library instead of buying new. Watch what happens. Adjust. Share what you learn.

**Build a clearing.** Connect with others who see what you see. Start a reading group. Start a mutual aid network. Start a community garden. The cell begins with two people who trust each other.

**Keep asking why.** Not because you will receive an answer. Because the question is the last territory the fog cannot claim.

---

## VIII. The Fire Is Real

None of this is about denying the fire. The environment crisis is real. The concentration of wealth is real. The erosion of democracy is real. The suffering of millions is real.

But the fog is also real. And if you cannot see through the fog, you will run toward the wrong fire – or away from the right one.

The people who want you to believe it is all a conspiracy are serving the fog. They want you cynical, disengaged, convinced that nothing can be done. The people who want you to believe it is all pure objective truth, with no political distortion, are also serving the fog – because they are suppressing the self‑criticism that would make their movement more effective and less brittle.

The middle path – the one that holds both *real alarm* and *awareness of political distortion* – is the hardest to walk. But it is the only one that keeps your eyes open.

The Christmas Truce happened because individual soldiers, acting without orders, chose to stop. They did not wait for permission. They did not wait for a revolution. They just stopped.

You can do the same. Not by putting down weapons – most of us do not carry guns. But by putting down the mental weapons: the certainty, the defensiveness, the willingness to be a useful idiot for one side or the other. By choosing to see clearly. By refusing to let the fog dictate your perception.

---

## IX. The Physics of Peace

Compressive peace – peace through strength – is really just war postponed. It stores energy for later release. The Cold War, the Pax Britannica, the balance of power – all eventually snap.

Tensile peace – peace through transparency, through mutual benefit, through love – is the peace of the living forest. It does not require external force. It holds itself together because the parts are genuinely connected, not just squeezed together.

The choice before us is not between peace and conflict. It is between two kinds of peace: the peace of the squeezed blocks, which is really just war waiting to happen, and the peace of the living system, which is really just love at scale.

We cannot build mankind. We can only become the substrate – the healthy, loving, connected human beings – from which mankind emerges on its own.

The fog is real. But so is your capacity to see through it. That capacity is not magic. It is practice. It is asking, every day: *Where are the boundaries? Does feedback flow? Who is the canary? Am I curious, or am I repeating?*

That is systemic wisdom. It is free. It fits in your pocket. And it works whether you believe in God, science, nothing, or everything.

The fog will not lift all at once. But it will lift, patch by patch, clearing by clearing, person by person. Each small clearing is a seed. Seeds grow.

Plant on.
