## Abstract

The Seedy generator – three concurrent threads with cyclic dependency, no synchronization, running on multicore hardware – produces output that is statistically indistinguishable from true randomness and decorrelates from its initial state within microseconds. This challenges the dogma that deterministic software cannot generate genuine unpredictability. We propose that **memory contention under concurrent execution acts as a source of energy**, and the cyclic topology creates a *logical unstable resonator* where microscopic timing variations amplify into macroscopic chaos.

This document takes the Seedy generator as an empirical anchor and asks: *What if this is not a computational curiosity but a universal principle?* We develop a philosophical framework – **driven topology** – in which all systems (physical, biological, social, computational) are understood as metastable topologies through which energy flows. Chaos is not a behavior but the substrate: energy excess that exceeds local dissipation capacity. Order emerges locally when fields converge, creating temporary condensations (particles, stars, organisms, organizations). We extend this view to cosmology (uneven expansion, spiral galaxies as dissipation channels), to time (time as the felt rate of energy dissipation), and to determinism (interdependent systems are fundamentally non‑deterministic). This is a mental model, not a finished theory – an invitation to see the universe as a driven, creative, perpetually transitional process.

## 1. Introduction: From a Small Program to a Large Question

The Seedy generator (Saucier, 2026) is a minimal C program: three threads, three shared 64‑bit integers, arranged in a ring. Each thread reads one node, applies a deterministic mixing function, and writes to the next node. No locks. No atomics. No random seeds. On a single‑core machine, its output fails statistical tests. On a multicore machine, it passes PractRand and TestU01 – and, as we have observed, from a known initial state (e.g., all zeros) the first sample after 5 µs already covers the full 32‑bit space uniformly, showing immediate decorrelation.

This is surprising. Computer science teaches that deterministic processes cannot produce true randomness. Yet here is a deterministic program, running on deterministic hardware, producing unpredictability. The explanation is **memory contention**: when multiple cores saturate the memory subsystem, the order of read/write operations becomes sensitive to nanoscale timing – cache state, clock phase, bus arbitration – and the cyclic topology amplifies these variations into macroscopic chaos. The system is a *logical unstable resonator*.

If a simple ring of three threads can do this, what else might be an unstable resonator? The question leads us outward: from computation to mechanical engineering (the Toyota Prius transmission), to galactic dynamics (spiral arms), to cosmology (the expansion of voids), and to the nature of time itself.

This document is a **philosophical exploration**. It does not pretend to be a rigorous theory of everything. It offers a mental model – a way of seeing – that may help connect these domains. The empirical anchor is real; the speculation is intentional and, we hope, generative.

## 2. The Seedy Generator: A Brief Recap

For readers unfamiliar with the Seedy generator, we summarize its essential properties (full details in Saucier, 2026):

- **Topology:** Three threads, three shared variables (node0, node1, node2). Thread A reads node0 → writes node1; Thread B reads node1 → writes node2; Thread C reads node2 → writes node0. Cyclic dependency.
- **Concurrency:** Threads run asynchronously on physical cores. No synchronization primitives.
- **Deterministic per‑thread function:** `seed_modify_64` (XOR‑shift and multiply) – pure and deterministic.
- **Output:** A supervisor thread periodically samples the XOR of all three nodes.

**Empirical findings (multicore):**
- Direct output passes PractRand (up to 2^38 bytes).
- When used as an entropy source to seed a fast deterministic stretcher (QX64), the resulting stream passes PractRand and TestU01 (Small Crush) at 2^50 bytes.
- From a known initial state (all zeros), the first sample after 5 µs already shows uniform distribution of all 32‑bit combinations – immediate decorrelation.
- On single‑core (PowerPC G4), direct output fails; stretched output still passes, indicating that even degraded systemic chaos contains usable entropy.

**Interpretation:** The system’s unpredictability arises from **memory contention** – the energy‑like saturation of a shared resource. The cyclic topology turns this contention into a resonator: timing variations circulate and amplify. This is not quantum randomness; it is **systemic chaos** emergent from deterministic components.

## 4. The Philosophical Core: Driven Topology

We propose the following mental model:

**1. Chaos is the substrate.** The default state of reality is not ordered but undifferentiated potential – a “quantic flux.” Order is temporary, local, and energy‑sustained.

**2. Opposing forces create gradients.** Without opposition (hot/cold, positive/negative, read/write contention), there is no flow. Gradients drive everything.

**3. Energy flows through topologies.** Every system – a gearset, a thread ring, a galaxy, an organization – is a topology that channels energy. Its “dissipation capacity” is how fast it can convert incoming energy into harmless forms (heat, work, radiation, information).

**4. Chaos = energy excess.** When energy input exceeds dissipation capacity, the system enters a *capacity‑to‑dismemberment interval*: the old topology fails, new configurations become possible, and creativity (or destruction) occurs.

**5. Local convergence stabilizes.** Where opposing fields cancel or harmonize, the local gradient drops below dissipation threshold. These convergences are the “particles” of reality – from electrons to stars to coherent star clusters.

**6. Unstable resonators live on the edge.** Systems that deliberately maintain Φ ≈ Ψ (input ≈ dissipation capacity) are *unstable resonators*. They never settle; they ride the edge between order and chaos. The Seedy generator is one. The Prius transmission is another. Perhaps the universe itself is one.

## 5. Case Study: The Toyota Prius Transmission

The Prius Power Split Device is a mechanical unstable resonator (Toyota, 1997). Three power sources (engine, MG1, MG2) are locked on a planetary gearset. No clutch. Torque opposition is continuous. The control software constantly balances energy flows to keep the system within thermal and electrical limits. It never shifts gears – it lives in perpetual near‑dismemberment. Yet it is one of the most reliable transmissions ever built.

Parallels with Seedy:

- Three interacting deterministic agents (engine, two motors) vs. three threads.
- Cyclic dependency (torque flows in a loop) vs. read/write ring.
- Energy input from driver and road load vs. memory contention from concurrent access.
- Dissipation via battery, heat, mechanical work vs. dissipation via cache coherence and bus arbitration.
- Emergent stability through local convergence (control software) vs. emergent randomness through thread interleaving.

The Prius shows that managed instability is not only possible but optimal. It is a proof of concept that the Seedy principle scales to physical engineering.

## 6. Cosmological Implications: A Mental Model of the Universe

If we take driven topology seriously as a mental model, what does the universe look like?

### 6.1 Uneven Expansion

Energy is continuously infused into the universe (what cosmologists call dark energy). Dense regions – galaxies, clusters – have high dissipation capacity: matter, radiation, and fields can absorb and reradiate this energy. Voids have almost no dissipation capacity. In voids, the infused energy accumulates as potential, driving rapid expansion. Thus, voids expand faster than dense regions – a prediction that could be tested with peculiar velocity surveys.

### 6.2 Spiral Galaxies as Dissipation Channels

Spiral arms are not just density waves; they are **dissipation channels** for energy flowing from the galactic halo (low dissipation) to the disk (high dissipation). Rotational shear spirals this flow inward. Star formation occurs in the arms because that is where energy is being dissipated. This explains why arms are stable, trailing, and star‑rich.

### 6.3 Local Coherence (≈100 light years)

Within about 100 light years of the Sun, nearby stars move together with very low velocity dispersion (Gaia data). In our model, this region is a local dissipation equilibrium: the infused energy gradient is shallow because local matter density matches dissipation capacity. Stars are not independent; they are coupled through the shared energy field – they “surf” the same dissipation topology. The scale emerges from the balance between cosmic injection and local dissipation.

### 6.4 Relation to the Janus Model

Petit’s Janus cosmology (2021) posits a twin universe of negative mass. In our mental model, the negative mass sector can be seen as the accumulated potential in voids – energy that cannot be dissipated and therefore acts repulsively. The opposition between positive and negative mass is a special case of the general principle that opposing forces generate the quantic flux.

## 7. Time as the Felt Rate of Energy Dissipation

We propose a philosophical identification: **time is not a dimension; it is the felt rate at which infused energy dissipates through a local topology.**

- In a gravity well (high density), energy dissipates through many interactions (particle collisions, radiation). The flow is impeded – time runs slower (gravitational time dilation).
- In empty space, energy cannot dissipate; it accumulates as potential. The flow is unimpeded – time runs faster (void expansion).
- At high velocity, an object interacts more frequently with the background energy field, dissipating faster – time runs slower (special relativistic time dilation).

This is consistent with Einstein’s relativity but gives time a physical *substance*: the energy flow itself. There is no need for a “block universe” where past and future coexist. Time is real, directional, and variable.

We do not provide equations. This is a mental model. It may be wrong, but it is coherent.

## 8. Determinism, Laplace’s Demon, and Interdependence

Laplace’s demon assumed that perfect knowledge of initial conditions and deterministic laws would allow perfect prediction. Our philosophical stance rejects this for interdependent systems:

- **Topology changes.** In the capacity‑to‑dismemberment interval, the system’s own structure reconfigures. No demon can know the future topology.
- **Computational irreducibility.** As Wolfram (2002) argued, some systems cannot be shortcut; you must run them. The Seedy generator is such a system – its output cannot be predicted without actually executing it with all its physical contention.
- **Interdependence creates freedom.** When three threads depend on each other cyclically, no single thread’s future is determined by its own past alone. The system has a degree of freedom that looks like randomness.

Thus, determinism is an illusion that appears when we isolate simple, non‑interdependent subsystems. In the full interdependent universe, the future is not written.
