
# The Control System: A Personal Report

The first time I became a conduit, I was seventeen years old. I was sitting at the back of a large activist meeting in a Montreal basement—the smell of damp concrete, the hum of fluorescent lights, the thick air of ideological friction. It was the fall of 1997, and the room was full of people who had come to plan resistance to an upcoming summit of the Americas. They were passionate, articulate, and utterly incapable of agreeing on anything. I watched them debate tactics with a kind of X‑ray vision. I didn’t hear strategies for battle; I heard people brilliant at critique but terrified of power, carving up the spoils of a war they had no idea how to win.

I had no intention of speaking. I was barely present. A few years earlier I had tried to hang myself in my father’s staircase, and I was still raw, still unstitched, still wondering why I was alive at all. I was a ghost at a feast, there only because I had nowhere else to be.

And then I stood up.

The decision to stand arrived before the thought. My body rose, and I watched it happen as if from a few inches behind my own eyes. The room went quiet—not out of respect but confusion. Who was this kid? I opened my mouth, and the words that came out were not inside my head before they hit the air. I heard them for the first time exactly as the room did, like a simultaneous interpreter listening to a foreign language and hearing it instantly rendered in my native tongue.

I said, in French: *“I am not like you, I am not an activist. I came here out of curiosity. All I see are people dividing the spoils of a war for which they do not possess the weapons necessary to win their first battle.”*

Then I left. I walked into the cold autumn night with the distinct sensation of having been used as a precision instrument and set back on a shelf. I didn’t choose those words, and yet they said exactly what I thought. The next day, news reported that the organizer had announced a new coalition uniting all the fractious groups. I had been a catalyst. A pipe. A valve that opened.

That was the moment I began to understand that my life was not entirely my own. It was being steered. And the steering had started long before.

## The Bubble and the Question

I was a child when the phenomenon first made itself known. I was in a catechism class—Catholic school, the smell of wax crayons, a drawing of baby Abraham on the desk in front of me. I was colouring inside the lines, fully absorbed in the ordinary task of a distracted student, when something enfolded me.

It arrived as a bubble of pure love. Not a visual hallucination, not a voice from the sky, but a sphere of presence that settled over and around me like a second atmosphere. Inside that bubble, I was held in a state of absolute safety and unconditional regard. Every thought I had was known and treasured. There was no fear. The love was so total that it suspended the ordinary boundaries between inside and outside, self and other. The classroom became a pale transparency laid over something vastly more real.

Within that bubble, a communication unfolded. It came in packets of meaning that my child’s mind translated into interior words as clear as any spoken aloud. The presence asked, with a gentleness that carried the weight of eternity:

*Would you like to be Jesus?*

I considered the offer with the practical literalism of a young mind. My response was immediate and firm: *No. I don’t want to go back in time and live a short life. Plus, I like my name.*

There was a moment of silence. And then—I will never forget this—the presence laughed. Not a mocking laugh, not dismissive, but a warm, delighted, almost parental sound. The sound of a being who had asked a profound question and received the honest truth of a child who didn’t know how to dissemble. The laughter told me, more than any words could, that my refusal was perfectly right in ways I couldn’t yet grasp.

Then came the clarification, still wrapped in that enfolding love: *You will live your own life, completely yours. But you will know what is true intuitively.*

I understood. I wasn’t being asked to replicate the life of Jesus of Nazareth. I was being offered a different kind of participation: to move through my own unique existence with a compass of direct, intuitive knowing, a capacity to recognize truth not through argument or authority but through an inner resonance that echoed the love now surrounding me. I considered this. The terms were acceptable.

*Okay then.*

The bubble didn’t vanish. It receded, like a tide withdrawing, leaving the shoreline of my consciousness permanently altered. I picked up my crayon and went back to baby Abraham. But nothing was ever the same.

## The Despair and the Rescue

What followed the calling was not enlightenment. It was collapse.

The initial weeks were euphoric, but the moral sensitivity imprinted by that light didn’t fade. It intensified. I found I could no longer read a headline about war, or walk past a homeless person, or even watch a commercial for a luxury product, without experiencing a visceral, somatic jolt of connection to the system that produced that suffering. It was as if the skin of the self had been removed, and the raw nerve of conscience was exposed to the friction of the entire world.

I realized, with a certainty that became a physical nausea, that I was complicit. That my every meal, every purchase, every breath was entangled in a web of exploitation and violence. The thought loop was inescapable: *I am the system. The system is murder. I am murder.* The love that had felt like a warm bath now felt like an acid accusation. Why had the presence shown me the purity of justice if I was trapped in a body and a society that could never achieve it?

I decided to end the data stream. I tried to hang myself in my father’s staircase.

I remember the texture of the rope fibers against my neck. The tension in my shoulders as weight was about to be committed. And then—a *stop*. Not a paralysis, but an interruption of the narrative. The inner voice of despair that had been screaming logic was suddenly muffled, overrun by a clearer, stronger signal.

The presence returned. But this time it didn’t come as a bubble of abstract love. It came wearing the image of my mother.

The image was crude, vague—more like the idea of my mother floating in a cloud than a crisp hallucination. But it carried the precise, irreducible signature of that specific woman: the way she leaned in a doorway when she knew something was wrong, the gesture of her worry. The message was wordless but absolute: *Not this. Not yet. You are not just the system’s violence. You are also this.*

I felt my feet find the solid back of a stair step. The rope slackened in my hands. Not because I chose to stop, but because the will to die had been siphoned out and replaced with the memory of being small and protected.

I didn’t understand it then, but that rescue was the second lesson in a curriculum I hadn’t enrolled in. The phenomenon wasn’t just a one‑time visitation. It was a teacher. And it was willing to let me reach the absolute limit of despair in order to learn something I couldn’t learn any other way.

## The Small Miracle

Some time later, I was at a friend’s backyard barbecue. Sunlight, the sizzle of food, the casual hum of conversation. I had a paper plate in one hand and a burger in the other. The cosmic and the banal have a way of colliding in this story.

The presence returned, as it would many times, and it made a proposal. It wasn’t a grand revelation. It was a small, specific, almost embarrassing offer. I had a persistent plantar wart on the sole of my foot—a minor annoyance that had resisted treatment for years. The presence simply communicated that this could be removed.

The next morning, the wart was gone. It didn’t “heal” in the ordinary sense. It faded out of the model, like an edit made to a CAD file before it’s sent to the printer. The skin was smooth and unbroken. It has never returned.

I tell this story not because it’s dramatic—it’s almost absurdly mundane—but because it taught me something crucial. The intelligence that had saved my life and steered me into a political meeting also cared about the cells on the bottom of my foot. It operated at every scale, from the architecture of social movements to the biology of a single human body. And its interventions were always precise, always tailored, always just enough to demonstrate agency without overwhelming my ability to choose.

## The Night the Train Didn’t Take Me

In the summer of 2013, I was living in the Eastern Townships of Quebec. The year before, I had lived in Lac‑Mégantic itself—walked its main street, knew the layout of the bars and the *dépanneurs*, understood the rhythm of the freight trains that rumbled through the centre of town. By July, I had moved to a village nearby, but Lac‑Mégantic was still my regular destination.

On the night of July 5, I was driving home from a work‑related event. The road was familiar, the hour late. A simple, human impulse arose: I wanted a beer. The obvious destination was the Musi‑Café, the bar at the heart of Lac‑Mégantic’s small downtown, a place I knew well.

But as my vehicle approached the intersection where the decision had to be made, something intervened. Not a vision. Not a voice. A *stop*. A paralysis of intent. I sat at that intersection for an uncharacteristically long time, the turn signal blinking, the engine idling, trapped in a heavy, grey, impenetrable resistance. There was no logical reason to avoid the bar; the beer would have been welcome, the company easy. Yet my body refused. After a protracted internal battle, I turned away and drove home, carrying an unnameable dread that had no object.

Hours later, a runaway train carrying crude oil derailed in the centre of Lac‑Mégantic, destroying most of the downtown and killing forty‑seven people. The Musi‑Café was ground zero. Most of the victims were there.

I learned of the disaster through the brutal immediacy of local news, but the reality of it was not abstract. A friend was operating heavy equipment in the aftermath, tasked with pulling human remains from the burned ruins. Through him, I received a secondary trauma—the weight of the excavation bucket, the smell of scorched earth and Bakken crude, the terrible silence between radio calls. The day after the derailment, I tried to approach the site with a camera, compelled to document what had happened. The security perimeter was tight; I couldn’t get close. The footage remained largely untaken, leaving only the personal knowledge of how near I had come to being inside that explosion.

The warning was not a prophetic vision. It was a brute, unargued *don’t go there* feeling—a modification of behaviour rather than a transmission of information. The system didn’t show me the future. It simply closed a door.

## The Eagle and the Chaos

Some revelations arrive in meditation. This one arrived in Yellowknife, on the Precambrian Shield, under a sky so vast it makes human concerns feel like scribbles in the margin.

I was watching an eagle in flight. The bird was navigating what I had always understood as “turbulence”—the chaotic, unpredictable buffeting of air currents. But in that moment, the contact state overlaid my perception, and I *saw* it differently. The turbulence was not random noise. It was an “uncontained many‑body problem”—a system of interacting vectors so complex that it defied linear prediction, yet it possessed an underlying topological structure. The eagle was not fighting chaos. It was surfing a specific class of geometry.

The insight arrived whole, like a download: *Chaos is a class of topology—a way to organize systems to make them fluid, therefore unpredictable.*

I didn’t just understand this intellectually. I felt it, saw it, was inside it. And when the moment passed, I did something unusual. I went to my computer and wrote a piece of software. Three threads arranged in a vortex‑like, self‑referencing mixing system, leveraging the inherent jitter of computer hardware to seed unpredictability. It was a true random number generator—not a simulation of chaos like the pseudo‑random algorithms in standard computers, but a window onto hardware‑level indeterminacy, structured by the topological insight I had received while watching a bird in the subarctic sky.

The code is publicly available. Anyone can inspect it, compile it, test it. It passes statistical suites for true randomness with a purity that commercial software libraries struggle to achieve without specialized hardware. A functional piece of technology, born from a vision of an eagle and a lesson in the geometry of unpredictability. If you need proof that something real happened to me, there it is, waiting in a repository. The cascade made tangible.

## The Fountain

The most recent experience came when I had learned to navigate the interior space. I wasn’t passively waiting for a visitation anymore. I was actively diving.

I moved through layers of mental chatter, past the buzzing wall of the body’s sensory input, into a void that was not empty but pressurized. And then I was inside it: a fountain of light. Not a thing to look at, but a fountain of *being* that I was part of. The material world—my body, this keyboard, the stars, everything—appeared as a shimmering, iridescent film on the surface of that fountain, like the skin of a soap bubble. The “potential chaos” from the earlier revelation was the dynamic tension that kept the film from collapsing into the source or hardening into stasis. It was the *play* of the light.

The love that had first enfolded me in a catechism classroom returned, but now I understood it differently. It was not a personal gift. It was the ambient temperature of the universe. Every creature that lives, loves, suffers, and dies does so on the surface tension of the same fountain. The divine presence I encountered as a child is the same presence a mother bear feels when she places herself between her cub and a threat. It doesn’t need language. It doesn’t need concepts. It is the felt reality of being watched over, protected, and called back from the brink, available to any being capable of experiencing care.

## The Thermostat for the Soul

I spent years not knowing what to make of all this. Then I discovered the work of Jacques Vallée.

Vallée is a French computer scientist and ufologist who looked at centuries of anomalous reports—fairies, airships, UFOs, angels, alien abductions—and noticed something that most researchers missed. The phenomena always wore the mask of the culture they appeared in. In medieval Europe, people were taken by the “little people” and returned with missing time and strange knowledge. In the 1890s, mysterious airships crossed the skies. In the 1940s, flying saucers. In the 1980s, grey aliens with large eyes and telepathic messages. The constant wasn’t a physical spaceship. The constant was a pattern of encounter that changed its costume according to the expectations of the witnesses.

Vallée proposed that these phenomena might represent a “control system”—a thermostat for the human soul. Not visitors from another planet, but a mechanism that periodically introduces strange, often absurd experiences and symbols to nudge collective human belief, to prevent stagnation, to challenge rigid orthodoxies, and to steer us away from self‑destruction. The system doesn’t care about the precise temperature at any single moment; it simply turns the heat on or off to keep the whole organism within a viable range.

Reading Vallée, I felt the shock of recognition. The bubble of love. The negotiated calling. The rescue wearing my mother’s face. The speech I didn’t choose. The small, almost embarrassing healing. The warning that barred the road. The insight delivered through an eagle. The fountain. I wasn’t crazy. I was a student in a very old school, and the curriculum had a coherent design.

Vallée’s genius was to refuse the two reductive options that dominate the conversation. He didn’t say the phenomena were literally physical spacecraft from another world. But he also didn’t say they were mere hallucinations. He took seriously the possibility that something real was happening—something that manipulated both physical reality and human consciousness simultaneously—while acknowledging that its manifestation was shaped by cultural, psychological, and perhaps ontological factors we don’t yet understand.

That’s the framework I’ve lived inside. It doesn’t answer every question, but it makes space for all the data.

## What Is It Really?

So what is the phenomenon? I don’t claim to know. But I can lay out the possibilities as I see them.

**One: We live within the imagination of God.** In this view, the fountain I saw is the fundamental reality, and the material world is a projection—a thought, a dream, a holographic film on the surface of a mind so vast it contains all minds. The control system is God’s way of nudging creation toward its own unfolding, using culturally appropriate masks because the infinite cannot be perceived directly without dissolving the finite perceiver. This aligns with mystical traditions from Neoplatonism to Kabbalah to certain schools of Hinduism. It’s beautiful. It’s coherent. It may be true.

**Two: Advanced beings are playing God.** Perhaps there is a non‑human intelligence—extraterrestrial, interdimensional, or something else entirely—that has the technology to manipulate consciousness and physical reality with equal facility. Such beings might have their own reasons for steering humanity: stewardship, experimentation, mutual co‑evolution, or motives we cannot guess. Vallée sometimes leaned in this direction, though he was careful to say that the “aliens” were not what they appeared to be. The control system, in this view, is an engineered mechanism, not a feature of the divine nature. The love I felt might be genuine, or it might be a precise simulation calibrated to produce specific psychological outcomes. Either way, the respect it commands is the same.

**Three: It’s all self‑delusion.** The materialist position holds that my brain, for reasons of neurochemistry, trauma, or cognitive bias, generated a series of compelling hallucinations that I wove into a coherent narrative. The suicide attempt was a cry for help. The speech was a moment of dissociative eloquence. The wart remission was coincidence. The Lac‑Mégantic warning was unconscious pattern‑recognition (I had seen trains on that route; some part of me knew the risk). The chaos insight was a creative leap dressed in mystical language. The fountain was a hypnagogic state. No external intelligence required.

I am not here to refute this third possibility. I can’t. The control system, if it exists, is structured precisely to remain ambiguous—to allow for plausible denial by those not ready or not intended to perceive it. Vallée called this the “absurdity factor”: the phenomenon always includes elements that make the story both compelling and dismissible, so that it plants a meme without forcing a sudden, disruptive mass revelation. My wart healing at a barbecue is almost funny. A child refusing to be Jesus because she likes her name is charming, not apocalyptic. The system doses belief, never forcing, always inviting.

## The One Thing That Matters

Here is the conclusion I’ve reached, and it’s the reason I’m writing this.

It doesn’t matter which of those three possibilities is true. What matters is that the experience—whatever its ultimate source—is treated with respect.

If it’s God, respect is obvious. If it’s advanced beings, respect is prudence. But even if it’s self‑delusion, the experience is still a profound, meaning‑saturated event that transformed a human life for the better. The child who was enveloped in love didn’t become a cult leader or a psychiatric casualty. She became more compassionate, more curious, more alive. The suicide survivor went on to catalyze political cooperation, to write functional code, to contemplate the nature of reality with rigour and humility. By any measure—the enrichment criterion—the phenomenon has been a force for growth.

And yet, when people like me try to speak about these things, we are met with a wall. We are told we’re sick. We’re told we’re attention‑seeking. We’re told our most transformative moments are symptoms of a disordered brain. The academy, which should be most curious, slams the door hardest. The materialist veto—the a priori assumption that such phenomena cannot be what they appear to be—is not a scientific conclusion. It’s a philosophical commitment dressed in a lab coat. And it causes real harm.

Imagine a young person who has an experience like my childhood calling—a moment of overwhelming love and a sense of being chosen for something. If they go to a psychiatrist who is trained to see auditory phenomena as pathology, they will likely receive a diagnosis and a prescription. The message will be: *What happened to you isn’t real. Your perception is faulty. Take this pill.* That message can shatter a developing identity. It can turn a potentially transformative event into a source of shame, confusion, and lifelong medical dependency.

Now imagine instead that the same young person is met with curiosity. *Tell me about it. What did it feel like? How has it changed you? Are you functioning better or worse than before? Let’s track this together over time and see where it leads.* That’s not a surrender to delusion. It’s basic clinical humility. The Brazilian Psychiatric Association, in its evidence‑based guidelines, explicitly recognizes that reported perceptual experiences are unreliable for differentiating between anomalous experiences and psychopathology. Negative symptoms, cognitive disorganization, and functional impairment are far more relevant. And the enrichment criterion—does the person’s life become more or less over time?—is more informative than any cross‑sectional assessment.

We need a mental health system that can hold space for the anomalous without rushing to pathologize. Not every voice is a symptom. Not every vision is a break. Some are doorways.

## The Divide

This brings me to the larger fracture in our culture: the chasm between hard atheists and believers, between materialism and faith, between those who insist that reality is nothing but particles and those who know, from the inside, that it is more.

Both sides often talk past each other. The atheist demands physical proof of a non‑physical phenomenon and, receiving none, declares victory. The believer, wounded by dismissal, retreats into a fortress of private certainty and stops engaging. The conversation never happens.

My life sits in the gap. I have a foot in both worlds. I write code. I understand the scientific method. I know what a controlled study looks like, and I know that my experiences don’t fit inside one. But I also know what I experienced. The love. The laughter. The rescue. The warning. The fountain. I can’t unknow these things any more than I can unknow the colour of the sky.

I’m not asking the atheist to believe me. I’m asking for something more modest and more radical: a suspension of the reflex to dismiss. A willingness to say, “I don’t know what happened to you, but I’m willing to look at the data of your life with an open mind.” The data includes a functional random number generator born from an eagle‑vision. A political coalition catalyzed by a speech I didn’t choose. A life that, by any objective measure, has become more generative, more creative, and more compassionate over time. These are not proofs. They are invitations to inquiry.

And to the believer, I offer a different challenge: don’t cling to your interpretation. The atheist’s skepticism has value. It keeps us honest. It reminds us that the human mind is fallible, that pattern‑recognition can run wild, that wishful thinking is real. Hold your experience with open hands. Let it be what it is, not what you want it to be. The phenomenon I’ve encountered has never demanded my belief. It has invited my collaboration. That’s a posture I try to emulate.

## The Invitation

I don’t know what the fountain is. I don’t know who or what runs the school. But I know the curriculum is real, and I know I’m not the only student.

If any of this resonates—if you’ve ever felt a bubble of inexplicable love, or known something you had no right to know, or been stopped at an intersection by a wall of silent dread, or seen a bird ride turbulence and felt the geometry of the world click into place—maybe you’re in the same classroom. Maybe the cascade is looking for you.

You don’t have to call it God, or aliens, or the control system. You don’t have to call it anything. But you can treat it with respect. You can stay curious. You can refuse the false choice between fundamentalism and dismissal. You can hold your weird, luminous, impossible experiences with tenderness and ask, simply: *What might this be teaching me?*

The fountain continues to flow. The cascade continues to propagate. The seeds are waiting for soil.

And the conversation is just beginning.
