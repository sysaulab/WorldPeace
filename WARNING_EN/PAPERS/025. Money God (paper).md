# The Money God

## The Spiritual Conquest of the World and the Architecture of Enslavement

### Abstract

This paper argues that colonialism and its contemporary form, neo-colonialism, are best understood not primarily as political or economic projects, but as a sustained spiritual crusade to install money as the dominant god in human consciousness. Drawing a contrast between the indigenous worldview of "belonging to the land" and the Western worldview of "dominating the land," this analysis reframes colonization as the forced propagation of a money-based reality. It further argues that this project succeeds by creating "internal pathways" within individuals—patterns of thought, desire, and fear—that transform human beings into predictable "economic automatas" whose worth is tied to accumulation. True decolonization, therefore, cannot be achieved through political independence or cultural revival alone. It requires the internal exorcism of the money god, the restoration of inherent worth, and the embrace of circular economies and voluntary material humility as expressions of a healed relationship with land and life.

### Introduction: The Unasked Question

Modern discourse treats colonialism as a historical period of territorial expansion, resource extraction, and cultural domination. Neo-colonialism is similarly framed as economic control through debt, trade agreements, and corporate power. These analyses are not wrong, but they are incomplete. They describe the mechanisms without examining the theology.

This paper proposes that we have been asking the wrong questions. We ask: *How did colonizers extract resources? How do corporations maintain control? How can nations achieve economic independence?* We have not asked the foundational question: *What god did colonization worship, and what does conversion to that god do to the human soul?*

The thread of conversation that precedes this paper arrived at a startling conclusion: **The colonization project was the propagation of money as a deity, with cultural elements serving as incidental delivery mechanisms.** If this is true, then the entire framework of decolonization must be reimagined. It is not merely a political project but an exorcism.

### Part I: Two Worldviews, Two Gods

#### 1.1 The Worldview of Belonging

In indigenous cosmologies worldwide, a common thread appears with remarkable consistency: the land is not property but relative. The human does not stand above the land but within it. This is not romanticism but ontology—a understanding of what it means to exist.

To belong to the land means:

- **Identity is placed.** You are not a disembodied consciousness floating across geography. You are of this river, these mountains, this valley. The land holds your ancestors and will hold your grandchildren.
- **Reciprocity is law.** You take only what you need, and you give back. The concept of waste is not merely inefficient but sacrilegious. The land gives life; you sustain the land.
- **Worth is inherent.** Your value does not need to be earned or proven. It is given by virtue of your existence within the web of kinship that includes animals, plants, rivers, and stars.
- **Economy is subset.** Economic activity serves the community and the relationship with land. It is a tool for sustaining belonging, not an end in itself.

In this worldview, the question is never "How much can I accumulate?" but "Are the relationships healthy?"

#### 1.2 The Worldview of Domination

The modern Western worldview, emerging from specific readings of religious texts, amplified by Enlightenment rationalism and supercharged by industrial capitalism, offers a radically different understanding.

To dominate the land means:

- **Identity is achieved.** You are not born into worth; you must prove it. The land is a stage for your accomplishments, a resource for your projects.
- **Control is freedom.** Freedom is defined as the absence of constraint—the ability to bend the world to your will. The ultimate expression of freedom is mastery over nature.
- **Worth is measured.** Value is quantitative. A mountain is "board feet of timber." A river is "acre-feet of water." A human is "human capital." The universal metric is money.
- **Economy is everything.** All spheres of life—education, healthcare, relationships, even spirituality—are subsumed into economic logic. They must justify themselves by their contribution to growth.

In this worldview, the question is always "How much is it worth?" and "How can I get more?"

#### 1.3 The Incompatibility

These worldviews cannot coexist peacefully because they worship different gods. The god of belonging requires gratitude, limits, and relationship. The god of domination requires expansion, control, and accumulation. One says: *You are enough.* The other says: *You will never have enough.*

When these worldviews meet, one must convert or be destroyed. There is no compromise between a god who says "the land is your relative" and a god who says "the land is your resource."

### Part II: Colonization as Spiritual Conquest

#### 2.1 Reframing the Colonial Project

If we accept that the core conflict is theological, then the history of colonization reads differently. The familiar elements remain—the ships, the guns, the treaties, the diseases—but they are recontextualized as tools of conversion.

The goal of colonization was not merely to extract wealth. Extraction was the worship service. The goal was to bring the entire world under the dominion of the money god—to make every river, every forest, every mountain, and every human being a participant in the great liturgy of accumulation.

#### 2.2 The Technologies of Conversion

**The Doctrine of Discovery (1452-1493):** These papal bulls declared that non-Christian lands were empty (*terra nullius*) and available for claim by Christian sovereigns. This was not merely a legal fiction but a theological declaration: the relationship of belonging was spiritually invalid. The land's true owners—the indigenous peoples—were rendered invisible because they did not worship the right god. The land was declared available for its true purpose: to become property.

**Private Property and Enclosure:** The introduction of individual, alienable land ownership was perhaps the most potent weapon. It performed a transubstantiation: living relative became dead commodity. Land that had been held in trust for future generations became an asset to be bought, sold, and leveraged. This is not economics; it is sacrament. The indigenous person who could not comprehend selling their mother was now told that their mother was not their mother at all, but real estate.

**Resource Extraction:** The mines of Potosí, the plantations of the Caribbean, the rubber camps of the Congo—these were not merely enterprises but temples. The wealth extracted was the offering. The endless labor was the prayer. The landscape, once sacred grove, became worksite.

**Cultural Erasure:** The prohibition of languages, the suppression of ceremonies, the removal of children to residential schools—these were the systematic destruction of the old operating system. Language carries the stories of belonging. Ritual encodes the relationship with land. Break these, and the mind becomes a blank slate upon which the new gospel can be written.

#### 2.3 The Incidental Nature of Culture

This reframing reveals that the specific cultural packaging of the colonizer—Spanish Catholicism, British Protestantism, French secularism—was incidental to the underlying project. These were the delivery vehicles, not the cargo.

The cargo was always the same: **the transformation of all life, all land, and all relationships into measurable, ownable, and exploitable units.** The "civilizing mission" was the mission to make the world safe for the money god.

### Part III: The Internal Pathways—From Freedom to Slavery

#### 3.1 The Architecture of Predictability

If colonization succeeded in installing the money god externally—in laws, in economies, in borders—neo-colonialism succeeds by installing it internally. This is the deeper conquest. The colonized do not need to be ruled by foreign armies if they rule themselves with foreign gods.

The money god creates what we might call **internal pathways**—grooves worn into the psyche that make human behavior predictable and therefore controllable. This is the slavery your frame identifies: not the slavery of chains, but the slavery of conditioned response.

#### 3.2 The Pathway of Measurement

Money provides a universal, objective-seeming scale of value. It quantifies the unquantifiable.

- **The Internal Groove:** The mind learns to translate all value into this scale. A sunset is less valuable than billable hours. A child's laughter is a distraction from productivity. Friendship is assessed by its "networking" potential.
- **The Predictability:** The person whose worth is tied to a number will consistently choose actions that increase the number. They will fear anything that threatens it. Their choices are not free; they are calculated.

#### 3.3 The Pathway of Scarcity

The money god is worshiped through accumulation, but accumulation can never satisfy because the god's primary commandment is *more*.

- **The Internal Groove:** A constant, low-level anxiety. Enough is never enough because the goalposts move. The new salary, the bigger house, the better car—each achievement is a plateau, not a peak. The fear of falling behind, of becoming worthless, is the engine.
- **The Predictability:** The person driven by scarcity is easy to manipulate. Threaten loss, promise gain, and they will comply. They hoard. They compete. They prioritize their own security above all else.

#### 3.4 The Pathway of Reward (The Dopamine Loop)

Money and its acquisitions trigger the brain's reward system. Earning and spending provide dopamine hits.

- **The Internal Groove:** Work, get paid, feel good. Crave feeling, return to work. The labor is not chosen for its meaning but because it has been conditioned as the path to reward.
- **The Predictability:** This is operant conditioning. The person becomes the rat pressing the lever. Their behavior is response to stimulus, not free choice.

#### 3.5 The Economic Automata

The result is the figure you named: the **economic automaton**. A being who believes they are free because they can choose any path, as long as it leads to more money. Their identity is their portfolio. Their worth is their net worth. They have traded the terrifying freedom of being human for the comforting predictability of being an economic unit.

This is not liberation. This is the internal colonization complete. The empire no longer needs to send administrators; the colonized administer themselves.

### Part IV: The God-Centered Alternative—The Architecture of Freedom

#### 4.1 The Contrast

Against this, you placed the person who puts God first. This is not sectarian religion but a principle: the orientation of the self around a center that is not measurable, not scarce, and not transactional.

#### 4.2 The Pathway of Inherent Worth

If one is created in the image of God—or possesses inherent dignity by virtue of existence—worth is not earned.

- **The Internal Pathway:** The frantic need to prove value through accumulation quiets. Identity is secure, not because of what one has done, but because of whose one is.
- **The Freedom:** One can act based on other values—justice, mercy, love, beauty—without calculating the effect on net worth. One can be "unproductive" in economic terms because life has a different productivity.

#### 4.3 The Pathway of Sufficiency

If God is first, the universe is not a hostile place of scarcity but a creation held in being by love.

- **The Internal Pathway:** The deep groove of anxiety begins to fill with trust. "Consider the lilies of the field" is a direct assault on the economic automaton's core driver. It invites release from compulsive securing.
- **The Freedom:** One is freed from the terror of losing everything, because everything is not located in the bank account. This enables radical generosity, risk-taking in service of calling, and a peace the market cannot give or take.

#### 4.4 The Pathway of Love

The market logic is transactional: give this, get that. Love is gratuitous: give because giving is good.

- **The Internal Pathway:** Actions are not dictated by stimulus-response but shaped by internal dialogue with a living God or highest principle. This call can lead to actions that make no economic sense.
- **The Freedom:** The person who follows Love is unpredictable to systems. They cannot be bribed, because they already have what they value. They cannot be threatened, because they do not fear loss of what can be taken. They are, in the world's eyes, dangerous. They are free.

#### 4.5 The Unpredictable Human

This is the crucial point: **The person oriented around God is fundamentally unpredictable to the systems of control.** Their behavior cannot be modeled by economic algorithms because their motivations are not economic. They are the being the money god cannot assimilate.

### Part V: Decolonization as Exorcism

#### 5.1 The Incomplete Project

Political decolonization—the removal of colonial administrators, the establishment of independent governments—leaves the money god enthroned. The flag changes; the worship service continues. The economy still demands growth. The people still measure their worth in currency. The land is still resource.

This is not decolonization. It is colonization with local management.

#### 5.2 The True Target

If colonization was the installation of the money god, then decolonization must be the **exorcism of the money god from the human mind and heart.** It must operate on the level of the internal pathways.

This means:

- **Exorcising scarcity:** Restoring a cosmology of enough. The land provides. The community shares. There is enough because we live within limits and care for each other.
- **Exorcising the worth-wealth equation:** Grounding identity in something unshakable—being a child of God, a strand in the web of life, a bearer of ancestral legacy.
- **Exorcising domination:** Relearning to be *with* the land, not just *on* it. Letting the land teach its rhythms and limits.

#### 5.3 Circular Economies as Spiritual Practice

You named circular economies as a goal. From this frame, they are not merely technical solutions but **spiritual disciplines.**

A circular economy—where waste is food, resources are endlessly reused, nothing is thrown away—is a direct assault on the money god's doctrine of perpetual growth. It says: enough. It says: balance. It says: we belong to a system larger than ourselves and must live within its limits.

To build a circular economy is to practice a different worship. It is to enact, in material form, the truth that we are participants in a living world, not masters of a dead one.

#### 5.4 Voluntary Material Humility as Liberation

Even more radical is the concept you named: **voluntary material humility.** This is the act of saying: "My worth is not in my things. I need less. I am enough."

To the money god, this is unforgivable. It refuses to participate in the worship service. It undermines the entire system of validation. It whispers the terrifying question: *If my worth isn't in my wealth, then what have I been doing with my life?*

But to the human soul, it is liberation. It is the breaking of the internal pathway of accumulation. It is the rediscovery that enough is sufficient, that being is enough, that belonging is enough.

### Part VI: The Two Economies

The contrast can be summarized in the following table:

| **The Economy of the Money God** | **The Economy of Belonging** |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Linear:** Take - Make - Waste | **Circular:** Borrow - Use - Return - Regenerate |
| **Driven by:** Scarcity and Fear | **Driven by:** Sufficiency and Trust |
| **Measure of Success:** Accumulation (More) | **Measure of Health:** Balance (Enough) |
| **Human Role:** Consumer / Producer | **Human Role:** Participant / Relative |
| **Relation to Land:** Owner / Dominator | **Relation to Land:** Steward / Kinsman |
| **Psychological State:** Anxiety, Never Enough | **Psychological State:** Gratitude, Enough |
| **Spiritual Goal:** Prove your worth through accumulation | **Spiritual Goal:** Live your worth through belonging |
| **Freedom:** Choice within a controlled system (predictable) | **Freedom:** Choice informed by love (unpredictable) |

### Conclusion: Choosing Our Story

The thread that generated this paper began with a simple observation: modern Western societies risk becoming mere economies with just enough social component to keep people happy. It ended with a radical reframing of colonization, neo-colonialism, and the very nature of human freedom.

What emerged was a coherent story:

Colonization was the global propagation of the money god. Its success is measured not in the territories it conquered but in the minds it converted. We now live in a world where the money god's commandments—*grow, accumulate, compete, consume*—are experienced not as external demands but as internal truths. We have become economic automatas, predictable units in a global system, our internal pathways grooved by measurement, scarcity, and reward.

But the old gods are not dead. The worldview of belonging persists, in indigenous communities that have resisted, in ecological movements that intuit a different relationship, in spiritual traditions that insist on inherent worth, and in the quiet places of the human heart that still remember what it feels like to be enough.

Decolonization, then, is the work of helping people see the idol for what it is. It is the patient, difficult work of overwriting the pathways of scarcity with pathways of enough. It is the creation of spaces—physical, mental, spiritual—where the money god is not worshiped. It is the practice of circular economies and voluntary material humility not as policy prescriptions but as acts of exorcism.

The question before us is not *How do we reform the economy?* but *What god will we serve?*

One god offers comfort, predictability, and the slavery of conditioned response. It promises freedom and delivers a gilded cage. The other god offers the terrifying freedom of love, the unpredictability of the soul that answers to a higher call, and the deep peace of belonging to a world that does not need to be dominated because it is already home.

The choice between these gods is the choice between two kinds of life. And it is made not in parliaments or boardrooms, but in the quiet places where each human being decides what they will worship—and therefore, what they will become.

*"No one can serve two masters. Either you will hate the one and love the other, or you will be devoted to the one and despise the other. You cannot serve both God and money."*

— Matthew 6:24

*The question is not whether you will serve. Everyone serves something. The question is only what.*
