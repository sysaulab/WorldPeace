# Money Goes Through. People Stay.

## A Diagnostic Analysis of Conditions in Vancouver's Downtown Eastside

**Authors:** Sylvain Saucier & DeepSeek


## Abstract

This paper presents a diagnostic analysis of Vancouver's Downtown Eastside (DTES), grounded in immersive observation and supported by empirical research. We document the systematic production of suffering through three interconnected mechanisms: (1) uninhabitable physical conditions in Single Room Occupancy (SRO) hotels, including unaddressed noise as a form of aggression with no legal recourse; (2) a cycle of escape to public spaces and forced return that functions as warehousing; (3) an economic structure that extracts value from suffering while residents remain trapped—"money goes through, people stay." We examine how selective non-enforcement of drug laws functions as environmental social programming that visibly produces the behaviors used to justify ongoing marginalization, particularly for the Indigenous majority population. Drawing on peer-reviewed research, community accounts, and critical phenomenology, we argue that the distress observed—including substance use, depression, and anxiety—constitutes intelligible responses to an unlivable symbolic and material environment. This paper offers diagnosis only, refusing the language of solution that typically precedes continued inaction.

**Keywords:** Downtown Eastside, SRO housing, structural violence, environmental social programming, colonial continuity, phenomenological diagnosis


## 1. Introduction: The Diagnostic Frame

This paper emerges from immersive observation in Vancouver's Downtown Eastside (DTES)—from living in the conditions described, from speaking with residents, from witnessing the daily operation of a system that presents itself as care while functioning as a machine for producing suffering.

We write in the diagnostic mode. Not to prescribe, but to name. Not to offer solutions, but to describe what is. The rush to "solutions" in policy discourse typically precedes a refusal to see the problem clearly. Before one can ask what is to be done, one must ask what is actually happening.

The DTES is Canada's poorest urban postal code, a concentrated area of single-room occupancy (SRO) hotels, marginalized residents, and an extraordinary density of social services. Official narratives frame it as a site of compassion—a place where a wealthy country concentrates its resources to address homelessness, addiction, and mental illness . Government announcements celebrate funding infusions and new programs. Frontline workers speak of harm reduction, housing first, and cultural safety.

The reality, observed from within and documented by research, is different.

This paper offers a diagnostic framework grounded in that observation and supported by peer-reviewed evidence. We do not claim to speak for DTES residents—only they can do that. But we offer a framework for understanding what is: a system that systematically produces the very conditions it claims to address, that extracts value from suffering while perpetuating it, and that maintains colonial relations through ostensibly benevolent means.


## 2. The Physical Architecture of Suffering: SROs as Uninhabitable Reality

### 2.1 The Material Conditions

Single Room Occupancy hotels in the DTES function as housing of last resort for structurally vulnerable populations . Research documents that SROs are largely unregulated and shaped by gendered power relations that perpetuate social violence, particularly toward women . The health and drug-related harms associated with SROs have been well documented, yet the conditions persist .

The material reality includes:
- **Heat:** Units lack adequate ventilation or climate control. Summer temperatures become unbearable. Residents sit outside at night not by choice but because staying inside is impossible.
- **Safety:** Shared washrooms, insecure doors, and unregulated visitors create constant vigilance. Sleep, when possible, is never deep.
- **Maintenance:** Landlords—often private operators receiving public subsidies—neglect repairs. Mold, pests, and broken fixtures are normalized .

These are not accidental deficiencies. They constitute the material substrate of a system that warehouses human beings in conditions that would be illegal for any other population.

### 2.2 Noise as Weapon, Silence as Policy

Noise requires particular attention because it reveals the power dynamics at the heart of the DTES.

Cities are inherently noisy, but in the DTES, businesses are permitted to generate noise at levels and durations that would be legally actionable in other neighborhoods. Cars park outside SROs playing loud music for hours. Low-frequency vibrations travel through building structures, penetrating bodies and preventing rest. Residents have no legal recourse—complaints to police go unanswered, bylaws are unenforced, and the notion of "quiet enjoyment" of one's rented home is suspended for this population.

This is not merely neglect. It is a form of discrimination encoded in enforcement patterns. The implicit logic: *these people are used to the street. They have a roof now—why should they complain?*

The assumption is that DTES residents do not deserve what other renters take for granted: the right to sleep, the right to quiet, the right to basic habitability. Their presence in housing is framed as a gift, not a right, and gratitude is expected to replace grievance.

### 2.3 The Unaddressable Aggression

From immersive experience: *"As a resident of such a place, it comes to me ideas of burning down the business as it is lived as an aggression that is unaddressable."*

This reveals the psychological truth of the situation. The noise is not experienced as an environmental nuisance but as **aggression**—a deliberate, if not legally provable, assertion of dominance. The business choosing to play loud music, hour after hour, communicates: *your comfort does not matter. Your sleep does not matter. Your existence here is conditional on your endurance of what we impose.*

Because the aggression is unaddressable—because there is no complaint mechanism that works, no authority that intervenes, no legal framework that acknowledges the harm—the only responses available are endurance (with cumulative psychological and physiological damage), escape (to parks, which is then punished by removal), or fantasy of destruction.

The fantasy of destruction is not a symptom of individual disorder. It is the logical conclusion of a system that provides no other exit. When every door is closed, the wall begins to look like the only way through.

### 2.4 The Broken Feedback Loop

From a cybernetic standpoint, functional systems require feedback loops that allow them to self-correct when their outputs become harmful. A business that generates noise without knowing it is a nuisance can be told, and can adjust. A resident who suffers harm can complain, and the complaint can lead to change.

In the DTES, this feedback loop is deliberately severed. The severing is not accidental. It reflects a judgment that the residents' comfort, sleep, and health are not worth protecting. Their complaints are pre-emptively discounted because of who they are: poor, addicted, Indigenous, mentally ill—people whose testimony is not credible, whose suffering is not urgent, whose lives are not quite real.

### 2.5 The Cycle of Eviction

Research documents that many women who use drugs in the DTES "repeatedly cycle through evictions due to structural (e.g. building policy infractions) and everyday gendered violence (e.g. no control over physical space) in SROs" . Symbolic and structural violence—including lack of tenancy rights and intimidation—are enacted within SROs to restrict residents' abilities to contest evictions .

Post-eviction, women face "pronounced vulnerabilities to gendered norms within a drug scene setting (e.g. increased drug use, sexual assault), and often had to rely on others for support, highlighting problematic survival strategies (e.g. short-term partnering with violent partners)" . The cycle repeats.

This is not housing. This is a machine for producing vulnerability.


## 3. The Cycle of Escape and Return

### 3.1 Parks as Refuge

Given the uninhabitabilty of SROs, residents seek relief in public spaces. Research on how people who use drugs (PWUD) experience spaces in the DTES finds that locations "commonly associated with risk and harm (e.g., alleyways, parks) can be re-imagined and re-constructed as enabling safety and wellbeing" . Study participants recounted both negative and positive experiences with particular spaces, suggesting that these locations serve complex functions .

Parks offer:
- Air and ventilation
- Quiet (relative to the SRO)
- Space to move
- Community with others escaping the same conditions
- Temporary autonomy

The parks become, in effect, the only livable housing many residents have.

### 3.2 Removal and Return

But the system cannot tolerate this escape. It exposes the lie that "housing" has been provided. So police and park rangers move through, clearing people out. Those removed are returned to the SROs they fled. The cycle repeats.

This is not public order maintenance. It is the active enforcement of unlivable conditions. Residents are not permitted to refuse the housing that destroys them. They are returned to it, again and again, until they stop trying to leave.

### 3.3 The Policing Dynamic

Community organizers report that police presence itself functions as a deterrent to accessing services. The Vancouver Police Department has been observed "parking and lingering directly outside" of supervised consumption and overdose prevention sites . At least once daily, one or two cop cars are parked within 20 metres of the front doors of VANDU (Vancouver Area Network of Drug Users), sometimes with officers inside and lights flashing .

As one community organizer stated: "The police are an oppressive force for people in this neighbourhood and they don't feel safe when they're around. They instil fear and that's a result of their own actions" .

The city approved $5 million for "Task Force Barrage," which the VPD states aims "to restore safety in the Downtown Eastside by targeting organized criminals, violent offenders, and street disorder" . Community members report that today it is more common to see groups of three to six officers patrolling, rather than pairs on foot .

The question of whose safety is being served remains: "People who are victims of these crimes are often the people who are harassed and policed by police. They're not actually stopping this from happening, just further victimizing those who are already victims" .


## 4. The Economic Structure: The Colander

### 4.1 The Colander Metaphor

A colander holds spaghetti while letting water drain away. In the DTES:
- **The people are the spaghetti**—they stay, they accumulate, they are held in place
- **Money, funding, and value are the water**—they flow through, exiting the community to wherever they are supposed to go
- **The system is the colander**—designed to separate what is retained (people, suffering, problems) from what passes through (resources, attention, benefits)

When the colander isn't working—when suffering becomes too visible, when people die, when the system might be exposed—what happens? More water is added. More funding announcements. More pilot projects. More task forces. More consultants. More research. More "awareness." The water flows through. The people stay.

### 4.2 The Flow of Funding

The Province of British Columbia announced a historic $19-billion housing investment, with nearly 80,000 homes delivered or underway provincewide since 2017, including approximately 2,500 supportive housing units in Vancouver . The Province provided $54.6 million to purchase and renovate a 115-unit building, and $11 million to the DTES SRO Collaborative Society . Since March 2023, the Province has opened more than 750 new and renovated housing spaces for DTES residents .

The City of Vancouver committed $8 million annually for three years to enhance mental health crisis response services, and allocated $142 million to contracts with equity-seeking groups through its Social Value Procurement Framework . The City provides over $3 million yearly in grants to non-profit organizations for micro-cleaning and public realm stewardship .

Yet the conditions persist. The water flows. The people stay.

### 4.3 The Calendar of Suffering

The community has been made into an economic calendar, with predictable revenue streams tied to predictable suffering. Overdose spikes generate emergency funding. Summer heat generates outreach contracts. Budget cycles generate non-profit renewals. Conferences generate tourism and speaker fees.

Every actor in this system—government agencies, non-profits, researchers, consultants, media—has a structural relationship to the continuation of the conditions they purport to address. The system is not failing to help; it is generating value from the appearance of helping.

### 4.4 Who Benefits?

The water flows to:
- Private SRO operators receiving monthly subsidies for uninhabitable units
- Non-profit administrators drawing salaries and building careers
- Researchers receiving grants and publishing studies
- Consultants contracted for reports recommending more of the same
- Media generating clicks and advertising revenue from crisis coverage
- Politicians making announcements that generate votes

The people stay.

### 4.5 The Funding Cuts

When Indigenous-led programs prove effective, they face cuts. The Aboriginal Front Door Society (AFDS) and Watari Counselling and Support Services, which provide vital drop-in services for unhoused residents, faced potential cutbacks as provincial funding was set to expire . AFDS runs programs assisting around 700 clients daily, providing storage solutions and distributing food at a cost of $100,000 each month . Both organizations invested significant resources into establishing services, and the abrupt end to funding caused distress among staff, volunteers, and the communities they serve .

The funding expiration was scheduled to coincide with Truth and Reconciliation Day . The water stops flowing when it might actually nourish.


## 5. Environmental Social Programming: The Production of the "Savage"

### 5.1 The Paradox of Non-Enforcement

From immersive observation: *"Drug is very accepted here, to the point where i think it is unenforced. Being a native majority community this reinforces the stereotype of the savage through environmental social programming."*

On its surface, non-enforcement of drug laws could be framed as compassion, harm reduction, or pragmatism. But the effect is the systematic production of visible degradation.

### 5.2 How Non-Enforcement Functions

| Mechanism | Outcome |
|-----------|---------|
| Drugs are freely available | Visible public use escalates |
| Use is not interrupted | Health deteriorates, behavior becomes more disordered |
| Police stand by | Image of "lawless" community is produced |
| Media photographs | Images of "addicts" circulate widely |
| Public sees images | Stereotype confirmed: "They choose this" |
| Policy responds | More control, less autonomy, worse conditions |

The system does not need to actively enforce drug prohibition to achieve its disciplinary effects. It only needs to allow the conditions that produce visible degradation, then point to that degradation as justification for continued control.

### 5.3 The Contradiction in Policing

VPD spokesperson Sgt. Steve Addison stated: "It is not an enforcement priority to arrest people on the DTES who are living with drug addiction and it's been that way for years" . Yet data shows that when British Columbia brought in its decriminalization pilot project, simple drug possession arrests decreased, and then increased again when the pilot project was effectively ended . If police were not arresting people for drug possession, the question stands: why did arrests dip with decriminalization and increase after?

### 5.4 The Native Majority Dimension

The DTES is a native majority community. This transforms urban poverty into ongoing colonialism.

The stereotype of the "savage" has always included inability to control appetites, dependency on substances, disorder and chaos, and need for external control by civilized authorities. The DTES, through the mechanisms described, physically produces this stereotype in the bodies and behaviors of its residents. The environment is engineered—through SRO conditions, through economic extraction, through selective non-enforcement—to generate exactly the behaviors that the dominant culture expects and uses to justify continued domination.

### 5.5 The Cycle Completes

The drug use, caused by the conditions, becomes the justification for the conditions:
- "They're addicts, what can we do?"
- "They choose to live that way."
- "We're already spending so much, and nothing changes."

The system creates the pathology and then uses the pathology to justify itself.


## 6. Colonial Continuity: From Reserve to SRO

### 6.1 The Historical Pattern

The DTES cannot be understood outside the framework of settler colonialism. Indigenous peoples were displaced from traditional territories, confined to reserves, subjected to residential schools, relocated to urban centers through "sixties scoop" and other policies, concentrated in low-income neighborhoods with poor housing, and pathologized for the conditions imposed upon them.

In Vancouver's Downtown Eastside, "Indigenous people make up a disproportionate share of the unhoused population – a direct result of colonial dispossession, poverty, and systemic neglect" .

### 6.2 The Housing Freeze

In February 2025, Vancouver city council voted to freeze the construction of new supportive housing . The decision halted the development of units meant for people experiencing or at risk of homelessness. Mayor Ken Sim argued that Vancouver already provides 77 percent of Metro Vancouver's supportive services while housing only 25 percent of the region's population, claiming a pause is needed to "renew aging stock" .

For Indigenous advocates and housing organizers, this math functions as a smokescreen. Councillors who opposed the freeze called for provincial consultation—a move described as clashing with the mayor's top-down style .

### 6.3 The "Re-Unification" Proposal

Mayor Sim has proposed a "re-unification roundtable" to help relocate Indigenous residents to their home nations, framed as a healing initiative . Indigenous critics warn it could become a modern rebranding of removal.

Sim has stated: "Health outcomes are better when you are in your community," pledging that residents don't have to leave if they don't want to . Yet the optics of moving Indigenous people out of a gentrifying urban core ring familiar. As historian Jean Barman documented in her seminal article *Erasing Indigenous Indigeneity in Vancouver*, 19th-century development efforts targeted Indigenous settlements near False Creek, often under moralizing claims about "drunkenness" and disorder . Settler officials once rationalized displacement with the belief that Indigenous people could simply be relocated to a "just as convenient" space .

### 6.4 "Frontier Logic"

Legal geographer Nicholas Blomley's concept of "frontier logic" describes how poverty is equated with waste and vacancy—making removal seem not just rational, but necessary . Sim has described DTES single-room occupancy buildings as "crumbling," "unlivable," and "infested," justifying intervention not with compassion but condemnation .

This language masks the lived realities of Indigenous people who remain rooted in place despite generations of exclusion. It sanitizes policy and rebrands displacement as "revitalization."

### 6.5 The SRO as Urban Reserve

The SRO is the urban reserve. The conditions are continuous with those documented by Fanon in colonial Algeria—the production of a population that can be pointed to as evidence of its own inferiority. What has changed is the mechanism. Colonial violence no longer requires explicit force (though force remains available). It now operates through benevolent discourse ("we are helping"), economic extraction (funding flows that depend on suffering), environmental programming (conditions that produce expected behaviors), and selective enforcement (tolerance that functions as control).

This is colonialism updated for the era of reconciliation. It is harder to name, harder to resist, harder to prove. But it is no less real.


## 7. The Phenomenological Core: Living the Delta

### 7.1 The Inability to Live According to One's Values

From immersive observation: *"The residents here cannot live according to their values."*

This is the foundational wound. Every human being has a sense of how life should be lived—what matters, what is sacred, what makes existence meaningful. For Indigenous residents of the DTES, these values may include connection to land and place, community and kinship obligations, ceremony and cultural practice, respect for elders and ancestors, and autonomy and self-determination.

The SRO allows none of this. The noise, the heat, the insecurity, the extraction, the surveillance—these are not merely inconveniences. They are active barriers to living a life that feels like one's own.

### 7.2 The Concentration of the Marginalized

*"which explains the concentration here."*

People do not end up in the DTES randomly. They are drawn and held by forces that push them toward this place and keep them here. The very values that made them marginal in the first place—perhaps a different relationship to property, to time, to community, to authority—become the grounds for their concentration.

The DTES becomes a reservoir for those who cannot or will not conform to the dominant culture's values. And once concentrated, they become visible as a population, legible as a problem, available as a resource to be extracted.

### 7.3 The Refusal of Acknowledgment

*"when the world seems to refuse to acknowledge their different world view or ideology, really what made them on the margins to begin with."*

This is the delta experienced subjectively. The world does not merely disagree with their values—it refuses to acknowledge that those values exist as legitimate. The Indigenous understanding of relationship to land is not recognized as a competing framework; it is simply irrelevant. The preference for community over individual advancement is not seen as a different priority; it is seen as failure.

The original marginalization—being different—is compounded by a system that cannot see the difference as anything but deficit. The world refuses to acknowledge the worldview, and in doing so, refuses to acknowledge the person.

### 7.4 Self-Medication as Response

*"which drives self-medication."*

When the world refuses your reality, when you cannot live according to your values, when every door is closed and every channel sealed—what remains?

Substances offer temporary escape from unbearable awareness, a pharmacological alternative reality when the social reality is unlivable, relief from the somatic stress of chronic invalidation, and community with others who share the same condition.

This is not addiction as disease. This is self-medication as intelligible response to a world that has made clear it will not accommodate you.

### 7.5 The Deepening Cycle

*"Then the cycle deepens until acceptance of the conditions through internalization (depression, anxiety) or through escapist strategies (drug abuse)."*

Two pathways, both destructive:

**Internalization:** The world's refusal eventually becomes self-refusal. The external judgment becomes the internal voice. Depression is the giving up of hope that things could be different. Anxiety is the constant vigilance of someone who knows they are not safe and cannot become safe. The person accepts the conditions because the alternative—constant, hopeless struggle—is too exhausting.

**Escapist strategies:** Continued refusal to accept, but through means that further entrench the cycle. Substance use provides relief but deepens the visible degradation that justifies the system. The escape becomes the evidence for why escape is necessary.

Both pathways lead to the same place: the person remains, the water flows, the colander holds.

### 7.6 Spaces Re-Assembled

Research applying assemblage theory to the DTES finds that spaces used by people who use drugs "can be understood as assemblages, a variety of human and nonhuman forces - such as material objects, actors, processes, affect, temporal elements, policies and practices - drawn together in unique ways that produce certain effects (risk/harm or safety/wellbeing)" . This framework helps explain how the same alleyway or park can be experienced as both dangerous and as the only available refuge .


## 8. The Delta in the DTES

Our previous work proposed that psychological distress is a function of the gap between enforced subjective reality and objective conditions. The DTES illustrates this with precision.

| Enforced Reality | Objective Conditions | The Delta |
|------------------|----------------------|-----------|
| "We provide housing for the vulnerable"  | SROs are uninhabitable, residents cycle through evictions  | Housing that is not housing |
| "We offer comprehensive services"  | Services extract value while conditions persist; Indigenous programs face cuts  | Help that does not help |
| "Canada respects Indigenous peoples"  | Colonial displacement continues through housing freezes and "re-unification" proposals  | Reconciliation without justice |
| "Drug use is a health issue"  | Non-enforcement produces visible degradation; policing deters service access  | Tolerance as control |
| "We are responding to the crisis"  | Money flows through; people stay | Action that maintains the problem |

This delta produces predictable psychological effects: chronic cognitive dissonance from living within a system that claims to help while harming, loss of agency from repeated cycles of escape and return, hopelessness from the impossibility of improvement within the system, substance use as self-medication against unbearable awareness, and somatic disorders as the body registers what cannot be spoken.

These are not individual pathologies. They are intelligible responses to an unlivable symbolic and material environment.


## 9. The 80/20 Distribution in the DTES

Our previous work proposed that approximately 20-35 percent of a population—the critical minority—bears the psychological burden of accurate perception in systems of enforced reality.

In the DTES context, the critical minority includes:
- Residents who retain awareness of the gap between official narratives and lived reality
- Frontline workers who witness the cycle and experience moral injury
- Indigenous elders and cultural workers who understand the colonial continuity
- Advocates who speak out and are marginalized or defunded
- Researchers and journalists who document accurately and are accused of bias

These individuals experience isolation from those who cannot or will not see, frustration at the impossibility of communicating what they witness, moral distress at participating in systems that harm, retaliation when they speak (loss of funding, access, or employment), and physical and mental health consequences of chronic stress.

The majority—including most Canadians, most policymakers, most voters—never see the DTES as it is. They see it through frames provided by media and official narratives: a tragedy, a problem, a place where "those people" live. They are not complicit in the way the minority is; they are simply able to look away.


## 10. The Language Gap

From immersive observation: *"the response is logical from the view of the user, who may not even possess the language to express its intellectual discomfort."*

This is a profound insight about power and epistemology. The person experiencing the delta may feel the gap—may know, in their bones, that something is wrong, that the world is not as it claims to be, that their suffering is not their fault—but lack the vocabulary to articulate it.

They may not have the theoretical framework to name "colonial continuity." They may not have the political language to name "structural violence." They may not have the psychological vocabulary to name "cognitive dissonance." They may not have the legal terminology to name "discrimination."

What they have is the body's knowledge: the sleeplessness, the anxiety, the rage, the craving. These are not the problem. They are the pre-linguistic expression of the problem.

When the system encounters this wordless distress, it does not ask: *what is your body telling you that you cannot say?* Instead, it names the distress as the problem and offers medication to silence it.


## 11. Conclusion: What Is

We have offered a diagnosis. Nothing more.

The conditions are:
- SROs that are uninhabitable, with noise functioning as unaddressable aggression
- A cycle of escape to parks and forced return that constitutes warehousing
- An economic structure that extracts value while people remain trapped
- Selective non-enforcement that visibly produces the behaviors used to justify control
- Colonial continuity from reserve to SRO, from explicit removal to "re-unification"
- A phenomenological burden carried by those who see clearly and cannot stop seeing
- A language gap that transforms bodily knowledge into diagnosable "pathology"

Money goes through. People stay. When the suffering becomes too visible, more water is added.

This is not a system that has failed. It is a system operating exactly as designed—extracting value from a population while producing the evidence needed to justify its own existence.

The people are still here. They have always been here. They will be here when the water stops flowing and the colander is finally set aside.

This paper offers no solutions. Only the obligation to see clearly what is.
