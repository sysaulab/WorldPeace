## Strategic Futures of North America: A Macro-Historical Analysis of Continental Destabilization, Conflict, and Transformation

**Draft Paper**
**Date: March 6, 2026**

### Abstract

This paper explores potential strategic futures for North America by synthesizing geopolitical analysis, economic trends, and socio-political indicators through a macro-historical lens. Drawing on frameworks of ideological subversion, shock doctrine, and predictive history, it examines the continent's trajectory amid U.S. strategic reorientation toward hemispheric dominance, deepening internal polarization, and Canada's existential dilemma. The analysis projects two distinct pathways toward the potential disabling of the military-industrial complex (MIC): one driven by external intervention following U.S. overreach, and another catalyzed by internal civil unrest. The latter scenario—domestic upheaval forcing systemic change—is examined in depth as a plausible alternative to foreign-imposed transformation. The paper concludes that North America stands at a inflection point where the convergence of economic fragmentation, climate-induced stress, and political delegitimization could precipitate either continental collapse or fundamental restructuring.

### 1. Introduction: The Macro-Historical Context

The year 2026 marks a pivotal juncture in North American history. The post-1945 order, characterized by U.S. global leadership and deep continental integration, is undergoing rapid dissolution. Drawing on Jiang Xueqin's framework of "Predictive History"—which identifies historical patterns and geopolitical incentives to anticipate futures—we observe a convergence of forces suggesting systemic transformation .

The United States has explicitly pivoted from defending a "rules-based international order" to securing "the homeland and primacy in the Western Hemisphere," as articulated in its January 2026 Defense Strategy . This "Monroe Doctrine 2.0" represents both a strategic contraction and a regional assertion of dominance, with direct implications for its neighbors. Simultaneously, American society exhibits unprecedented "regime cleavage"—a political struggle no longer about winning elections but about the fundamental definition of nationhood . With 89% of Americans expecting high political conflict and trust in institutions at historic lows, the conditions for systemic crisis are rapidly maturing .

Canada confronts an existential dilemma: a long-standing ally increasingly frames it as a strategic competitor, with U.S. officials openly encouraging Canadian fragmentation . The expiration of the New START treaty in February 2026 has removed the last legal constraints on U.S. and Russian nuclear arsenals, reintroducing the possibility of escalation unthinkable a decade ago . Within this volatile context, this paper examines two critical variables: the fate of the military-industrial complex (MIC) and the potential for civil unrest to reshape continental outcomes.

### 2. Key Drivers Shaping North American Futures

#### 2.1 U.S. Strategic Reorientation: Hemispheric Assertiveness and Alliance Realignment

The 2026 U.S. Defense Strategy signals a profound shift. Its core tenets include:
- **Prioritization of Homeland Security:** Redirecting resources toward "Iron Dome"-style defenses and border militarization .
- **Industrial Base Revitalization:** Mandating that by 2030, all critical weapons components be manufactured domestically, severing overseas dependencies .
- **Cost Transference to Allies:** Demanding NATO allies assume primary responsibility for European defense and requiring Israel and Gulf states to counter Iran independently .
- **Unilateral Intervention:** Demonstrated by January 2026 military action in Venezuela, signaling willingness to forcibly reshape neighboring regimes .

This posture creates immediate tension with Canada and Mexico. The USMCA faces a pivotal July 2026 review, with threats of U.S. withdrawal looming . U.S. officials have met with Alberta separatists, and Treasury Secretary Bessent publicly praised Alberta's "independent spirit," indicating a strategy of leveraging internal Canadian divisions .

#### 2.2 Economic Fragmentation and Continental Decoupling

Trade policy uncertainty constitutes the primary economic risk. Canadian growth remains anaemic at 1.4%, heavily dependent on government spending . Key vulnerabilities include:
- **Tariff Persistence:** Section 232 and IEEPA tariffs remain, contributing to inflation and complicating cross-border supply chains .
- **U.S.-China Tensions:** President Trump's threat of 100% tariffs on Canadian goods if Ottawa pursues a trade deal with Beijing exemplifies the weaponization of economic dependency .
- **Investment Uncertainty:** Business investment in both countries is constrained by unpredictable policy, with Canada's manufacturing sector among the G7's weakest performers .

#### 2.3 Socio-Political Polarization and the "Language of Humiliation"

American society is experiencing affective polarization—citizens not merely disagreeing but actively despising opponents as existential threats . Key indicators include:
- **Identity Dehumanization:** Political discourse increasingly labels entire groups as "vermin" or "garbage," feeding the "Rage Bait Cycle" designed to mobilize bases through outrage .
- **Cultural Conflict:** Events like the 2026 Super Bowl halftime show have become proxy battles over national identity, with "All-American" counter-programming reflecting deep cultural fissures .
- **Institutional Delegitimization:** Trust in both major parties among young voters has collapsed, with 65% of 18-25 year-olds expressing dissatisfaction with traditional political institutions .

#### 2.4 Climate Stress as Threat Multiplier

Environmental pressures compound these vulnerabilities. Recent research identifies **western North America as an emerging agricultural drought hotspot**, with soil moisture deficits expected to intensify regardless of precipitation patterns . Concurrently, British Columbia's forest ecosystems are projected to undergo significant transformation, with some habitats disappearing entirely and novel climates emerging . These shifts will strain food systems, increase resource competition, and potentially trigger migration pressures.

### 3. The Military-Industrial Complex Under Pressure

A central variable in any strategic forecast is the fate of the military-industrial complex. Two distinct pathways toward its potential disabling can be identified:

#### 3.1 Pathway One: External Intervention (The "Gaza Liberation" Hypothesis)

This scenario, previously discussed, posits that U.S. overreach in prolonged conflicts (e.g., Iran) could deplete military capabilities, enabling external actors—potentially China—to exploit vulnerabilities. Indicators supporting this pathway include:
- **Depleted Interceptor Stocks:** U.S. inventories have fallen to critically low levels .
- **Chinese Positioning:** Congressional concerns about China selling advanced missiles to Iran reflect growing U.S.-China confrontation .
- **Export Control Vulnerabilities:** Reports of China acquiring advanced U.S. chips for AI development despite export controls suggest systemic enforcement failures .

#### 3.2 Pathway Two: Internal Confrontation (The Civil Unrest Scenario)

An alternative pathway—and the focus of this paper's scenario analysis—involves domestic upheaval forcing fundamental change. Evidence of this potential includes:

**a. The January 2026 Executive Order**
On January 7, 2026, President Trump issued an Executive Order "Prioritizing the Warfighter in Defense Contracting," directly targeting defense industry financial practices . Key provisions:
- Prohibiting stock buybacks and dividends for underperforming contractors
- Tying executive compensation to production metrics rather than financial returns
- Authorizing the Secretary of War to cap executive salaries (Trump suggested a $5 million cap)
- Allowing cessation of government advocacy for contractors' foreign sales

This represents unprecedented executive pressure on the MIC, potentially signaling either genuine reform or a restructuring of control. It demonstrates that internal mechanisms exist—if political will aligns—to constrain defense industry power .

**b. Escalating Civil Unrest**
Indicators suggest the administration may be preparing for, or even encouraging, domestic confrontation:
- **Strategic Chaos:** Observers note the administration's pattern of creating and sustaining turmoil to position itself as the only stabilizing force .
- **Electoral Manipulation Concerns:** Discussions of federalizing elections, aggressive gerrymandering, and voter roll purges raise questions about electoral legitimacy .
- **Martial Law Contemplation:** Former Trump lawyer Ceta Mitchell's prediction that emergency powers would be exercised, combined with James Carville's warning to remain on "high alert," suggests preparedness for authoritarian escalation .

**c. Legal Mechanisms for Domestic Military Deployment**
The Insurrection Act provides statutory authority for deploying active-duty military or federalizing the National Guard to suppress civil disorder. It has been invoked approximately 30 times in U.S. history, most recently during the 1992 Los Angeles riots . Martial law—invoked only once nationally during the Civil War—would involve wholesale suspension of civil liberties .

### 4. Scenario Analysis: Civil Unrest Forcing MIC Transformation

This scenario explores how domestic upheaval could achieve what external pressure might not: fundamental restructuring of the military-industrial complex.

#### 4.1 Phase One: Crisis Catalyst (Late 2026)

The immediate trigger could be contested midterm elections. With 89% of Americans expecting high political conflict and deep polarization over electoral processes, a disputed outcome—particularly if accompanied by allegations of voter suppression or foreign interference—could mobilize mass protests simultaneously in multiple cities .

Concurrently, agricultural drought impacts materialize. Food price spikes and regional shortages, compounded by supply chain disruptions from ongoing trade conflicts, create economic distress in rural and urban communities alike .

#### 4.2 Phase Two: Government Response and Escalation

Facing widespread protests, the administration invokes the Insurrection Act, deploying active-duty military to major cities. This action—unprecedented in scale and duration—triggers three critical developments:

**a. Military Resistance**
Junior officers and enlisted personnel, drawn from communities now facing occupation, face conflicting loyalties. Instances of refusal to deploy or fraternization with protesters emerge. The Executive Order's focus on production metrics rather than troop welfare exacerbates morale problems .

**b. Elite Defection**
Defense contractors, facing simultaneous pressures—executive compensation caps, prohibition of buybacks, and now reputational damage from military deployments against civilians—begin lobbying for restraint. The MIC, traditionally unified behind military expansion, fractures between contractors dependent on domestic contracts and those with significant international sales vulnerable to the administration's "cease advocacy" threat .

**c. International Isolation**
Allies, already strained by U.S. demands for increased defense spending and unilateral interventions in Venezuela, condemn domestic military deployments. Canada, facing its own pressures, closes the border, citing humanitarian concerns—a move that would devastate integrated supply chains but might be necessitated by domestic Canadian politics .

#### 4.3 Phase Three: Systemic Confrontation

Three potential outcomes emerge:

**Outcome A: Authoritarian Consolidation**
The administration successfully suppresses dissent, using martial law to suspend elections indefinitely. The MIC, recognizing its dependence on government contracts, aligns with the regime. Executive Order provisions are selectively enforced against contractors perceived as disloyal, while loyalists receive exemptions. The "post-American" society is deferred indefinitely.

**Outcome B: MIC-Led Restoration**
Fearing reputational and financial collapse, leading defense contractors join with Congressional leaders to invoke the 25th Amendment or otherwise remove the president. The new administration immediately revokes domestic deployments, restores civilian control, and implements—ironically—the Executive Order's provisions as permanent reform, codifying constraints on defense industry financial practices. The MIC, having purged its most compromised elements, emerges leaner but more politically constrained.

**Outcome C: Continental Fragmentation**
The crisis accelerates centrifugal forces. Alberta, observing U.S. instability and encouraged by prior U.S. outreach, declares independence . Quebec follows. A rump Canada, centered on Ontario and the Maritimes, aligns with a similarly fragmented U.S. West Coast (California, Oregon, Washington) to form a "Pacific Progressive Alliance." The remaining U.S. rump, dominated by the military and MIC, becomes a garrison state centered on the Southeast and Plains. The military-industrial complex is not disabled but regionalized, with each successor entity maintaining its own defense industrial base.

### 5. Implications and Synthesis

The civil unrest scenario reveals several critical insights:

**First, internal pressure can achieve what external intervention cannot.** While foreign adversaries may exploit U.S. vulnerabilities, domestic mobilization directly challenges the legitimacy of the system itself. The MIC, designed to project power externally, is ill-equipped to suppress domestic populations indefinitely without fracturing.

**Second, the Executive Order of January 2026 creates a double-edged sword.** By linking executive compensation to production metrics and restricting buybacks, it has already begun restructuring incentives. In a crisis, these provisions provide legal cover for systemic reform—or for punishing contractors deemed insufficiently loyal .

**Third, Canada's fate remains deeply intertwined with U.S. outcomes.** Whether as a "preserved" post-nationalist society, a fragmented collection of successor states, or an unwilling participant in U.S. military adventures, Canada cannot escape the gravitational pull of its southern neighbor. The "studied ambiguity" of current policy may prove insufficient as crisis deepens .

**Fourth, the nuclear dimension cannot be ignored.** With New START expired and both sides possessing upload capacity, any extended conventional conflict risks escalation. The "Burevestnik" and "Avangard" systems, while unverified in operational status, represent capabilities that could fundamentally alter escalation dynamics if deployed .

### 6. Conclusion: Living in the Interregnum

The Italian philosopher Antonio Gramsci famously wrote: "The crisis consists precisely in the fact that the old is dying and the new cannot be born; in this interregnum a great variety of morbid symptoms appear." North America in 2026 embodies this interregnum. The old order—U.S. global leadership, continental integration, stable democratic norms—is visibly dying. The new order remains unwritten.

Our analysis suggests that civil unrest, far from being merely a symptom of decline, may constitute the primary mechanism through which fundamental transformation occurs. The military-industrial complex, the institutional embodiment of American global power, faces unprecedented internal pressure. Whether it adapts, fractures, or entrenches will determine the shape of the post-American continent.

For Canada, the strategic imperative is clear: diversify partnerships, reduce dependency, and prepare for a future in which the United States is no longer a reliable partner but an unpredictable, potentially hostile, neighbor. The "post-nationalist" model Carney articulates may prove not merely aspirational but essential for survival .

For individuals contemplating relocation to the woods, the instinct is understandable. In an interregnum, self-reliance and distance from anticipated targets represent rational adaptations. Yet as Bezmenov taught us, the ultimate victory of subversion is not external conquest but internal paralysis. The greatest defense against uncertainty remains clarity of analysis, refusal to accept manufactured narratives, and preparation—both practical and psychological—for whatever comes next.
