# Historical PAtterns

***A Gentle Inquiry into the Fusion of Power and Apocalyptic Purpose in Western Expansion***

**Abstract**
*This paper proposes a conceptual lens for re-examining Western expansion from the medieval Crusades to the present day. It suggests that what are often treated as discrete historical phenomena—the Crusades, colonialism, the era of Manifest Destiny, and modern geopolitical interventions—may be understood as expressions of a single, adaptive engine. This engine is powered by the convergence of two forces: the pragmatic pursuit of material and strategic advantage by elite groups, and the profound human need for cosmic meaning, often expressed through apocalyptic or quasi-religious narratives. The purpose of this inquiry is not to assign blame or to reduce complex history to a conspiracy, but to offer a tool for seeing more clearly the patterns that shape our world, and to do so with humility and compassion for all those caught within them, including ourselves.*

## Introduction: An Invitation to Look Anew

Every society tells itself a story about its past. These stories are not false, but they are inevitably partial. They emphasize certain events and downplay others; they highlight noble intentions and sometimes obscure inconvenient consequences. This is not malice. It is how collective memory functions. It provides cohesion, meaning, and a sense of moral direction.

This paper is an invitation to consider an additional layer to the Western story. It is not an attempt to replace the existing narrative, but to supplement it with a lens that reveals certain recurring patterns—patterns that connect the Crusader marching toward Jerusalem, the Conquistador sailing for the New World, the pioneer venturing west under Manifest Destiny, and the modern soldier deployed in the name of spreading freedom.

The lens is this: **Western expansion has been consistently driven by a fusion between the material ambitions of powerful groups and the apocalyptic or quasi-religious yearnings of broader populations.** These two forces, the cynical and the sincere, have repeatedly converged to create a engine of enormous power—one capable of justifying immense violence, sustaining itself across centuries, and adapting its language to suit each new era.

To examine this is not to accuse every participant of bad faith. On the contrary, it is to take sincere belief seriously, and to ask the difficult question: how does sincere belief become entangled with systems of power that it would otherwise abhor? And what might it mean to recognize this entanglement?

## Part I: The Engine Defined

Before tracing the engine through history, we must understand its components.

**The First Component: Pragmatic Ambition**

Every society has its elites—individuals and groups who accumulate disproportionate influence over resources, institutions, and decision-making. Their motivations are generally not mysterious. They seek to preserve and expand their position. This can mean securing access to valuable trade routes, acquiring land and resources, establishing military advantage, or controlling markets. In the medieval period, this meant popes seeking authority over kings and Italian city-states seeking control of Mediterranean trade. In the colonial era, it meant monarchs filling treasuries and companies seeking sugar and cotton. In the modern era, it means securing oil, maintaining global military basing, and ensuring favorable conditions for multinational corporations.

This component is not inherently evil. It is a description of how power tends to operate. But it is incomplete without the second component.

**The Second Component: The Search for Cosmic Meaning**

Human beings are not merely economic actors. We are meaning-makers. We crave narratives that place our lives within a larger story, that give our struggles purpose and our sacrifices significance. The most powerful of these narratives have often been religious, but they can also take secularized forms: the mission to spread democracy, the duty to defend civilization, the destiny to tame a wilderness.

When these two components operate separately, their effects are limited. Ambitious elites without a compelling narrative can only rule through brute force, which is expensive and unstable. A population with powerful beliefs but no organized power can sustain a movement, but may struggle to shape large-scale events. The danger—and the engine—emerges when the two fuse.

**The Fusion**

When elite ambition aligns with popular apocalyptic or quasi-religious narrative, each amplifies the other. The elite gain legitimacy, popular enthusiasm, and a motivated workforce or soldiery. The population gains a sense of cosmic purpose, a conviction that their actions serve not just material interests but divine or moral destiny. The fusion produces a force capable of immense destruction, precisely because it combines the cold calculation of power with the white-hot energy of faith.

Crucially, this fusion does not require conscious conspiracy. It often emerges organically, as elites learn to speak the language of the people, and as religious leaders interpret events in ways that align with the interests of the powerful. The result is a system that perpetuates itself without anyone needing to be fully aware of its mechanics.

## Part II: The Prototype—The Crusades as Template

The Crusades provide our clearest view of this engine in its formative stage.

Consider the situation in late 11th-century Europe. The papal reform movement had strengthened the papacy, but it still struggled for authority against secular rulers and the Eastern Church. The feudal system produced a class of younger sons of nobles with no land and no prospects. Italian maritime republics like Venice and Genoa sought advantage in Mediterranean trade. Into this environment came Pope Urban II's call at Clermont in 1095.

The stated purpose was religious: to liberate Jerusalem from Muslim rule and to aid Eastern Christians. The promise was apocalyptic: participants would receive remission of sins, a spiritual reward that transcended earthly concerns. The enemy was framed as cosmic: not merely political opponents, but defilers of holy places, enemies of God.

Yet the material outcomes were unmistakable. The Crusades extended papal influence. They provided an outlet for restless warriors, channeling their violence outward rather than inward. They opened trade routes that enriched European merchants. They established Crusader states that functioned as new feudal domains for ambitious nobles.

Were the Crusaders merely cynical adventurers using religion as a cover? Some may have been. But the historical record suggests that most participants were sincere in their faith. They believed they were undertaking a holy mission. The tragedy—and the pattern—is that their sincere belief served interests far beyond their own salvation.

The Crusades also reveal the engine's adaptability. When Jerusalem was lost, the framework did not collapse. It simply found new targets: heretics in southern France, pagans in the Baltic, political opponents of the papacy in Italy. The crusading ideal became a flexible tool, capable of legitimating diverse projects of expansion and domination.

## Part III: The Expansion—Colonialism and the New World

When European powers turned their attention across the Atlantic, the crusading framework traveled with them, though its language evolved.

The material ambitions are well-documented. Spain sought gold and silver. Portugal sought a route to the spice trade. England and France sought territory and resources. Joint-stock companies emerged to finance and profit from colonial ventures. These were not hidden motives; they were openly declared.

Yet the enterprise could not have succeeded on greed alone. It required a legitimating narrative. This came from the same fusion we have observed.

The Doctrine of Discovery, articulated in papal bulls of the 15th century, provided the theological framework: Christian nations had the right to claim lands not ruled by Christians, and to subjugate their inhabitants for the purpose of conversion. This was not merely cynical justification; it was sincerely believed by many. The indigenous peoples of the Americas were seen through existing categories: they were the "heathen," the "infidel," the obstacle to God's plan.

The Puritan colonies of New England offer a particularly pure expression of the fusion. John Winthrop's vision of a "City upon a Hill" explicitly invoked biblical imagery. The colonists saw themselves as a new Israel, fleeing bondage in Europe to establish a holy commonwealth in the wilderness. Their expansion was not conquest but fulfillment of covenant. The native inhabitants were not nations with their own rights and histories, but either potential converts or demonic impediments to be cleared away.

The same framework justified the enslavement of Africans. A twisted theology developed: Africans were descendants of Ham, cursed to servitude; their enslavement, followed by forced conversion, was presented as rescuing them from paganism. The material interests of plantation owners and slave traders fused with a narrative that gave cosmic meaning to atrocity.

This is not to say that every colonist was a crusader in armor, or that every slave trader cited scripture. But the broader culture absorbed these justifications. They became part of the moral atmosphere, the taken-for-granted assumptions that made exploitation seem natural and even righteous.

## Part IV: The Culmination—Manifest Destiny and the Nineteenth Century

The 19th century gave this fusion its most explicit American expression: Manifest Destiny.

The phrase itself, coined in 1845, captured both components perfectly. "Manifest" suggested obviousness, self-evidence, truth that required no argument. "Destiny" suggested inevitability, divine ordination, a future already written. The expansion of the United States across the continent was framed not as conquest but as fulfillment.

The material interests were substantial: land for cotton cultivation, access to Pacific ports, mineral wealth in California and beyond. Railroads required vast territories and government support. Land speculators stood to profit enormously. These interests actively promoted expansion.

But the language was cosmic. Newspaper editorials spoke of "the finger of Providence." Politicians invoked America's special mission to spread democracy and Christianity across the continent. Artists depicted westward expansion as a procession of light pushing back darkness. The Mexican-American War, which secured vast territories, was presented not as an aggressive land grab but as the inevitable unfolding of American destiny.

The cost to indigenous peoples was catastrophic. Yet it was framed within the same narrative. Native Americans were either obstacles to progress or people to be "civilized." The Indian Removal Act was presented as humanitarian—separating native peoples from the corrupting influence of white settlement. The Trail of Tears was justified as necessary for the greater good.

Again, we must distinguish between system and individual. Many settlers sincerely believed they were fulfilling a divine mission. Many soldiers fought with genuine patriotism. The tragedy is that their sincere beliefs were woven into a system of dispossession and violence.

## Part V: The Adaptation—The Modern Era

After World War II, explicit religious language became less acceptable in formal statecraft. Yet the underlying structure of the fusion adapted, finding new language for old patterns.

The Cold War provided the first great modern framework. The enemy was communism, framed not merely as a political opponent but as an "evil empire," an atheistic force threatening civilization itself. The language was secularized—"freedom" and "democracy" replaced "Christendom" and "the faith"—but the structure was identical. The West had a cosmic mission: to defend the free world, to contain evil, to ultimately triumph in a struggle of light against darkness.

The material interests were substantial. The military-industrial complex grew enormously. Defense contracts flowed to corporations. Strategic bases were established around the world. Access to oil in the Middle East became a paramount concern. These interests shaped policy, often in ways that contradicted the stated ideals of freedom and democracy.

The War on Terror, launched after September 11, 2001, represents the purest modern expression of the fusion. The enemy was not a state but an abstract evil: "terror" itself. This framing allowed for endless war, as terror could never be fully defeated. It justified interventions across the globe, suspension of civil liberties at home, and a permanent state of emergency.

The language was explicitly Manichaean. President George W. Bush spoke of an "axis of evil." He framed the conflict as a struggle between good and evil, between civilization and barbarism. "Either you are with us, or you are with the terrorists," he declared. This was the language of crusade, updated for a secular age.

Yet the material outcomes were concrete. The Iraq War, justified through claims about weapons of mass destruction that proved false, secured American military presence in a oil-rich region and awarded lucrative contracts to defense and reconstruction firms. The Afghanistan War, begun to eliminate Al-Qaeda, became a two-decade occupation that served strategic interests in Central Asia.

Again, this is not to say that every soldier who served in these wars was a dupe, or that every policymaker was cynical. Many sincerely believed they were defending their country and spreading freedom. The tragedy is the same: sincere belief, channeled by systems of power, served purposes far beyond what individual actors intended.

## Part VI: The Contemporary Edge—Israel and Eschatology

Nowhere is this fusion more visible today than in the intersection of American foreign policy toward Israel and the eschatological beliefs of some Christian fundamentalists.

The material and strategic interests are clear. Israel has long been viewed by American strategists as a crucial ally in a volatile region. It provides intelligence cooperation, military basing access, and a counterweight to hostile regional powers. The alliance has been supported by successive administrations of both parties for these strategic reasons.

Yet this alliance also receives fervent support from a different quarter: evangelical Christians who believe that the gathering of Jews in the Holy Land is a necessary precondition for the End Times and the Second Coming of Christ. For these believers, support for Israel is not a matter of geopolitics but of prophecy. It is a sacred duty.

The convergence is striking. Cynical strategists and sincere believers find themselves advocating for the same policies, for entirely different reasons. Each reinforces the other. The believers provide grassroots energy and voting power. The strategists provide access and influence. The result is a policy that can survive even when it conflicts with other American interests or values.

This is not to say that all supporters of Israel are either cynical strategists or apocalyptic believers. Many support Israel for other reasons: historical guilt over the Holocaust, admiration for Israeli democracy, or genuine belief in Israel's right to exist. But the presence of this particular fusion—elite strategic interest combined with popular apocalyptic fervor—suggests that the ancient engine is still running.

## Seeing Gently—Compassion and Complexity

At this point, a reader might feel uncomfortable. The framework can seem to reduce history to a sordid tale of manipulation and delusion. It can feel like an attack on sincere faith, on genuine patriotism, on the noble intentions of countless individuals who have sought to do good.

This discomfort must be taken seriously. If the lens cannot accommodate compassion, it is not a lens but a weapon.

So let us be clear about what this framework does *not* claim.

It does not claim that every participant in these historical processes was cynical or malevolent. Most were not. The Crusader who marched east, the colonist who crossed the Atlantic, the pioneer who ventured west, the soldier who served in Iraq—these were ordinary human beings, seeking meaning, security, and purpose. They loved their families, mourned their dead, and tried, in their own way, to live good lives.

It does not claim that religious or moral belief is merely a mask for power. Belief is real. It shapes action. It gives life meaning. To suggest otherwise is to misunderstand what it means to be human.

It does not claim that all outcomes of Western expansion have been negative. The tradition that produced the Crusades also produced universities, hospitals, and works of art. The civilization that engaged in colonialism also developed concepts of human rights, democracy, and universal dignity. We are not required to choose between celebration and condemnation.

What the framework does claim is this: **Sincere belief and material power have repeatedly become entangled in ways that serve the interests of the powerful while providing cosmic meaning to the population, and this entanglement has produced consequences that neither group fully intended or fully perceived.**

The tragedy is not that people believe. The tragedy is that their belief can be woven into systems of violence and exploitation without their knowledge or consent.

## Part VIII: The Gentle Purpose—Seeing as Liberation

If this framework is not about blame, what is its purpose? Why offer this lens at all?

The answer is that **seeing clearly is itself a form of liberation.** Systems of power maintain themselves partly by becoming invisible. They become the water in which we swim, the air we breathe. We do not question them because we do not see them. The fusion of elite interest and popular narrative is most powerful when it is most hidden.

To see the pattern is to begin to question. Not to reject everything, but to ask: In what ways might my sincere beliefs be serving purposes I have not considered? In what ways might the narratives I absorb from my culture be channeling my energy toward ends I would not endorse if I saw them clearly? In what ways might I be participating in systems that I would oppose if I understood them?

These are not comfortable questions. But they are the questions of maturity. A child accepts the stories they are told. An adult learns to examine them. A society that cannot examine its own stories is a society that cannot grow.

The gentle purpose of this lens is to invite that examination. Not to force it, not to shame those who decline it, but to offer it as a tool for those who seek greater clarity.

## Conclusion: The Continuous Crusade and the Possibility of Awakening

We have traced a pattern across a millennium. From the Crusader marching to Jerusalem, to the Conquistador sailing for the New World, to the Puritan building a city on a hill, to the pioneer fulfilling Manifest Destiny, to the modern soldier fighting to spread freedom, to the believer supporting Israel to hasten the End Times—through all these, we have seen the same engine at work.

It is the engine of power fused with meaning, of elite ambition channeling popular faith, of sincere belief serving purposes beyond itself. It is the continuous crusade.

To name it is not to end it. Patterns this deep do not dissolve simply because they are recognized. But recognition is a beginning. It is the moment when we stop being merely carried by the current and begin to see the current itself. It is the moment when we can ask, for the first time: Do I want to go where this current is taking me? Is this really the story I want to be part of?

These questions are not comfortable. They are not easy. But they are the questions of moral awakening. And if the continuous crusade has taught us anything, it is that such awakening is always possible—and always, for those who benefit from the current, deeply threatening.

The purpose of this gentle inquiry has been to make that awakening a little more possible, a little less frightening, and a little more available to all of us who swim in waters we did not create and do not fully see.
