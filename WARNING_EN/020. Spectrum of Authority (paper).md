# The Spectrum of Authority

***From Explicit Enforcement to Implicit Virtue: How Revolutionary Ideas Propagate Against Elite Resistance and Why Transformation Requires Intrinsic Authority***

**Abstract**

This paper extends the existing economic framework of organizational authority by proposing a conceptual spectrum ranging from pure explicit authority (enforced through monitoring and sanctions) to implicit authority (where an idea's intrinsic truth or utility commands acceptance without promotion or enforcement). Building upon van den Brink and Gilles' foundational distinction between explicit and latent authority , Aghion and Tirole's work on formal and real authority, and Baker, Gibbons, and Murphy's analysis of informal authority in organizations , this paper introduces a third category—implicit authority—that operates outside formal power structures entirely. The central contribution demonstrates how implicit authority explains the propagation of revolutionary ideologies and transformative innovations *despite* the active resistance of elites who command conventional levers of power. Drawing on critical realist frameworks of social transformation  and contemporary theories of experiential authority , the analysis suggests that authority flows not only from position, enforcement capacity, and resource control, but fundamentally from perceived truth, utility, and moral resonance. The paper advances a core thesis: **genuine revolutionary change requires implicit authority; movements that rely solely on counter-hegemonic force, propaganda, or institutional capture are destined to reproduce the structures they criticize** . This framework illuminates how marginalized movements overturn established orders, how scientific paradigms shift against entrenched interests, and how moral revolutions succeed where force alone fails—while explaining why so many ostensibly revolutionary movements merely substitute one ruling elite for another.

## 1. Introduction: The Paradox of Revolutionary Persistence

The study of authority within organizations and societies has long occupied a central place in economic, political, and sociological theory. Traditional approaches conceptualize authority as flowing from hierarchical position, property rights, contractual arrangements, or the state's monopoly on legitimate violence. From Hobbes's Leviathan to Weber's typology of traditional, charismatic, and legal-rational authority , from Grossman and Hart's property rights framework to contemporary theories of organizational control, the dominant paradigm holds that those who command resources, institutions, and enforcement mechanisms ultimately command compliance.

Yet anyone who has observed revolutionary change recognizes this picture is radically incomplete. Hannah Arendt observes that "if authority is to be defined at all, then it must be in contradistinction to both coercion by force and persuasion through arguments" . Indeed, it is only when authority fails that force or persuasion is used to elicit compliance. History is replete with examples of ideas that triumphed against the full weight of established power. Christianity spread through the Roman Empire despite imperial persecution. The scientific revolution overturned Aristotelian orthodoxy despite the Inquisition. Abolitionist movements ended centuries of slavery despite entrenched economic interests. Democratic ideals toppled monarchies despite their control over armies. Civil rights movements prevailed against state-sanctioned segregation despite police power and legal sanctions. Decentralized cryptocurrencies challenge monetary monopolies; open-source software outcompetes corporate products with no marketing budget.

How do such ideas propagate? How do revolutionary ideologies gain ground when those controlling explicit authority—the state, the economy, established institutions—actively array against them? This paper argues such propagation becomes explicable only when we recognize a form of authority operating independently of formal power structures: **implicit authority**—the capacity of an idea, argument, or practice to command acceptance based solely on its perceived intrinsic merit, independent of its proponent's hierarchical position or the presence of enforcement mechanisms.

This is not to suggest implicit authority replaces explicit authority. Revolutionary movements rarely succeed by persuasion alone; they must eventually contend with power's hard realities. Rather, the argument is that implicit authority explains how ideas gain initial purchase, spread through networks of believers, sustain commitment under persecution, and eventually reach a tipping point where explicit authority must adapt or collapse. The relationship between implicit and explicit authority is dynamic and dialectical: ideas propagating through intrinsic virtue can, over time, generate new forms of explicit authority, while entrenched explicit authority constantly seeks to suppress or co-opt the implicit authority of challenging ideas .

The paper's central thesis, developed throughout, is that **genuine revolutionary change requires implicit authority**. Movements lacking ideas with sufficient intrinsic authority—ideas capable of reorganizing perception, commanding assent through truth and utility, and generating self-sustaining cascades of adoption—inevitably fall back on the mechanisms they sought to overthrow. They reproduce hierarchical structures because they possess no alternative source of authority to animate and guide transformation. As critical realist analysis of revolution demonstrates, agents who consciously seek to preserve and reproduce social structures engage in "passive revolutions" when they view those structures as historical constructs rather than natural phenomena . Without implicit authority, even well-intentioned revolutionaries become architects of new hierarchies.

The paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 reviews established frameworks of explicit, latent, formal, real, and informal authority, showing how even sophisticated models remain tethered to hierarchical premises . Section 3 introduces implicit authority, articulating its mechanisms and distinguishing it from related concepts. Section 4 develops a theory of what makes an idea intrinsically authoritative—the "anatomy" of a self-propagating idea. Section 5 analyzes propagation dynamics against elite resistance, explaining how implicit authority overcomes active opposition. Section 6 advances the core thesis: why revolution requires implicit authority and why its absence guarantees structural reproduction. Section 7 explores implications, and Section 8 concludes.

## 2. The Established Framework: Authority Within Hierarchy

**2.1 Explicit Authority**

Van den Brink and Gilles define explicit authority as the "myopic exercise of authority" occurring when decision makers actively enforce directives through monitoring and sanctions . In their game-theoretic model, explicit authority emerges when monitoring costs are sufficiently low—when superiors observe subordinate behavior at reasonable cost and impose consequences for non-compliance.

This conception aligns with traditional understandings across disciplines. Hobbes grounds political authority in the sovereign's capacity to enforce the social contract. Weber's legal-rational authority, while legitimated by procedural norms, ultimately rests on the state's monopoly on legitimate violence . Williamson's hierarchical control work emphasizes monitoring and enforcement as essential to organizational governance. Grossman and Hart's property rights approach grounds authority in the ability to exclude others from valuable assets. Explicit authority is, in essence, authority backed by the capacity to punish or withhold.

In societal terms, explicit authority corresponds to state power, the legal system, military and police—institutions compelling compliance through force or its threat. It also includes employer authority over employees, landlords over tenants, creditors over debtors—relationships where resource control enables enforcement.

**2.2 Latent Authority**

More innovative is van den Brink and Gilles's concept of latent authority: situations where "subordinates submit themselves to authority even though such authority is not enforced explicitly" . Latent authority represents voluntary compliance that nonetheless remains embedded within hierarchical relationships.

Crucially, van den Brink and Gilles demonstrate latent authority can be sustained as equilibrium under certain conditions. When enforcement costs are low enough, explicit and latent authority may coexist; when enforcement costs rise, latent authority may persist even after explicit authority becomes infeasible . This suggests authority relationships can survive the removal of enforcement mechanisms, provided subordinates have internalized them.

The key insight: authority can operate without active exercise. A manager whose directives are followed without monitoring possesses latent authority over subordinates. A government whose citizens obey laws when no police are present possesses latent authority over its populace. The authority remains latent because it need not be activated to be effective.

Latent authority corresponds to what political theorists call legitimacy, Gramsci's hegemony, and Bourdieu's symbolic power. It is hierarchical relationships' internalization such that compliance becomes habitual, natural, or morally obligatory.

**2.3 Formal and Real Authority**

Aghion and Tirole distinguish between formal authority (the right to decide) and real authority (effective control over decisions). Real authority is determined by information structure: subordinates gain real authority when they possess information principals lack, and when formal delegation gives them scope to act on that information.

This framework helps explain how authority can diverge from formal position. A formally subordinate employee may exercise considerable real authority if they control critical information. Conversely, a formal superior may find authority hollow if they lack information needed for meaningful decisions. In societal terms, this explains the power of technical experts, trusted advisors, and informal influencers who shape decisions despite lacking formal decision rights.

**2.4 Informal Authority in Organizations**

Baker, Gibbons, and Murphy offer a complementary analysis of informal authority . They assert that decision rights in organizations are not contractible—the boss can always overturn a subordinate's decision, so formal authority resides only at the top. Although decision rights cannot be formally delegated, they may be informally delegated through self-enforcing relational contracts. All subordinates' decision rights are "loaned, not owned" .

Their model examines informal authority in two informational environments. In the "informed-boss" model, the boss has information to assess projects before ratification. Delegation is feasible if the boss values reputation more than gains from reneging. In the "uninformed-boss" model, the boss lacks information to assess projects before ratification but observes results afterward—here, the subordinate's reputation for using authority appropriately is at stake .

This analysis captures two familiar features: bosses often choose not to overturn bad decisions because doing so would reduce subordinate effort and enthusiasm; subordinates frequently face opportunities to abuse authority but refrain because abuse would lead to retraction. The key insight is that informal authority operates through self-enforcing relational contracts rather than formal delegation—yet it remains tethered to hierarchical structure.

**2.5 The Limits of Existing Frameworks for Understanding Revolutionary Change**

While these frameworks represent significant advances, they remain fundamentally inadequate for explaining how revolutionary ideologies propagate against elite resistance. Consider their shared limitations:

**Latent authority still presupposes hierarchy.** Latent authority explains why subordinates comply with superiors even without enforcement—but cannot explain why people comply with ideas originating *outside* hierarchy, or why they defect from hierarchy altogether. Latent authority is existing power relations' internalization; it cannot account for *new* authority emerging to challenge those relations.

**Real and informal authority still flow from position.** In Aghion and Tirole's framework, real authority derives from information asymmetries within existing organizational structure. In Baker, Gibbons, and Murphy's framework, informal authority operates through relational contracts that presuppose hierarchical relationships . The informed subordinate's power is still a function of their role; real authority cannot explain the authority of an outsider's idea that hierarchy would prefer to suppress.

**All frameworks are person-centered, not idea-centered.** In explicit, latent, formal, real, and informal authority, authority attaches to persons occupying positions (or potentially occupying them). None can account for phenomena where ideas command assent *regardless of source*—where authority inheres in the proposition itself, not the proposer.

**None address propagation against active opposition.** Existing frameworks assume authority operates within cooperative or non-antagonistic contexts. They do not address situations where those with explicit authority actively work to suppress competing authority sources—the very situation characterizing revolutionary change.

**None explain the crucial distinction between genuine transformation and structural reproduction.** As critical realist analysis demonstrates, agents may unconsciously transform social structures amid social disintegration ("classic bourgeois revolutions") or consciously seek to preserve and reproduce social structures ("passive revolutions") . The difference hinges on whether movements possess ideational resources enabling genuine transformation rather than mere elite substitution.

To understand how revolutionary ideologies propagate despite elite resistance—and why some revolutions transform while others merely reproduce—we need a concept of authority untethered from hierarchy, not centered on persons, and independent of established power's cooperation. We need the concept of *implicit authority*.

## 3. Implicit Authority: The Power of the Idea Itself

**3.1 Defining Implicit Authority**

I propose the following definition: **implicit authority** is the capacity of an idea, argument, practice, or worldview to command acceptance or compliance based solely on its perceived intrinsic merit, independent of its proponent's hierarchical position, the presence of enforcement mechanisms, or the backing of established institutions.

Several features distinguish implicit authority from forms previously discussed:

First, **implicit authority inheres in propositions rather than persons.** It is the authority of the claim itself, not the claimant. When a compelling argument persuades regardless of source, when a brilliant insight spreads without sponsorship, when a practice is adopted because it works rather than because someone mandated it—these are manifestations of implicit authority.

Second, **implicit authority operates outside and often against formal hierarchies.** It can flow upward, downward, or laterally without regard to organizational or social structure. Indeed, its most dramatic manifestations occur when it flows *against* hierarchy—when marginalized and powerless people's ideas gain traction despite elite active opposition.

Third, **implicit authority requires no enforcement—enforcement would be irrelevant to its operation.** A proposition accepted on its merits needs no sanction backing. The "enforcement" is internal to the rational agent: to reject a compelling argument is to embrace inconsistency, to ignore demonstrated utility is to choose failure, to deny moral truth is to violate one's own conscience.

Fourth, **implicit authority often operates without conscious recognition.** We follow good ideas without necessarily reflecting that we do so. We absorb worldviews making sense of our experience without recognizing them as "authoritative." The most powerful forms of implicit authority are those so naturalized they appear not as authority but as simple reality.

**3.2 The Mechanisms of Implicit Authority**

What explains implicit authority's operation? Several complementary mechanisms, grounded in fundamental features of human cognition and sociality, suggest themselves:

**Epistemic deference.** Humans are reasoning creatures seeking true beliefs and effective practices. This is not merely normative but descriptive: maintaining false beliefs carries high cognitive costs; ineffective practices are often lethal. When we encounter an apparently sound argument or useful idea, we adopt it because doing so serves our epistemic and practical interests. The idea's authority derives from its apparent truth or utility. This mechanism operates even—especially—when the idea comes from a source lacking social standing. A slave's insight into better farming techniques, a woman's observation about child mortality, a heretic's argument about textual interpretation—these can compel assent through intrinsic merit despite proponents' low status.

**Consensual validation and social proof.** Humans are social creatures relying on others for information about the world. Ideas commanding widespread assent acquire authority through social proof—not because of enforcement mechanisms, but because consensus signals credibility . This mechanism operates independently of hierarchy: a grassroots movement gaining adherents creates its own momentum, as each new adherent provides social proof for the next. Importantly, when an idea is *true* or *useful*, social proof amplifies its implicit authority rather than creating authority from nothing. The consensus is itself evidence of the idea's merit.

**Practical success and demonstrated utility.** Ideas that work acquire authority through demonstrated effectiveness. An agricultural technique increasing yields, a medical practice reducing mortality, a form of social organization reducing conflict, a technology enhancing capability—these propagate because they produce results, not because mandated . The authority of demonstrated success is particularly powerful because self-reinforcing: each successful application provides additional evidence and creates additional adherents who demonstrate success to others.

**Intrinsic persuasiveness and logical force.** Some arguments simply convince. Their logical structure, evidential support, and rhetorical force command assent regardless of who offers them. This is the authority of reason itself. A mathematical proof, a compelling logical deduction, a powerful analogy, a narrative making sense of disparate experiences—these carry their own warrant. They persuade because they make the world more intelligible, resolve contradictions, reveal patterns not previously seen.

**Moral resonance and identity alignment.** Some ideas command assent because they articulate and give voice to deeply held values, intuitions, or identities. When a revolutionary ideology gives expression to inchoate injustice feelings, when a religious teaching articulates spiritual intuitions, when a political philosophy resonates with lived experience—the ideas propagate not because new but because they feel like truer expressions of what people already believed or felt . This is the authority of recognition. The idea resonates because it aligns with an individual's sense of self, moral intuitions, or cultural identity. Accepting the idea feels like affirming one's own identity—a deeply motivating force.

**Experiential anchoring.** Contemporary scholarship on authority emphasizes the role of experience itself as an authority source . "Biopolitical" or "experiential" authority can be generated through reference to various experiences, performances, or intensities of life including creativity, radicalism, risk-taking, experimentation, suffering, and proximity to death . Ideas offering practices or techniques that generate verifiable experience—a meditation practice producing genuine peace, an organizing technique producing genuine power, a scientific method producing genuine prediction—anchor themselves in undeniable personal experience. Elites can argue against theory, but they cannot argue against your experience.

**3.3 Implicit Authority Distinguished from Latent Authority**

The distinction between latent and implicit authority is critical for understanding revolutionary change:

| Dimension | Latent Authority | Implicit Authority |
|-----------|------------------|---------------------|
| **Locus** | Inheres in persons (superiors) | Inheres in ideas/propositions |
| **Structure** | Presupposes and reinforces hierarchy | Independent of and potentially hostile to hierarchy |
| **Basis** | Internalized acceptance of role | Perceived merit of content |
| **Direction** | Typically downward | Omni-directional |
| **Mechanism** | Socialization, habituation, ideology | Reason, evidence, utility, moral intuition, experience |
| **Relation to power** | Legitimates existing power | Challenges existing power |
| **Enforcement** | Possible but unnecessary | Irrelevant—compliance is internal |
| **Example** | Following manager's unenforced directive | Adopting revolutionary idea despite elite opposition |

Latent authority, in van den Brink and Gilles's conception, represents hierarchical relationships' internalization. Subordinates follow because they accept the superior's *right* to direct, even when that right is not actively exercised. The authority remains attached to person and role, and its effect is to stabilize existing hierarchy .

Implicit authority, by contrast, represents the operation of reason, evidence, moral intuition, and experience independent of hierarchy—and often in opposition to it. We follow an idea not because of who proposed it but because of what it is. The authority attaches to the idea itself, and its effect may be to destabilize or overturn existing hierarchies.

Latent authority explains why hierarchies persist even without active enforcement. Implicit authority explains how hierarchies can be overthrown even *with* active enforcement.

## 4. The Anatomy of an Authoritative Idea: What Makes an Idea Self-Propagating?

To claim an idea possesses "implicit authority" is to make a strong statement about its intrinsic properties. It suggests the idea carries within itself the seeds of its own propagation, requiring no external energy input—no propaganda budget, legal mandate, institutional sponsorship, elite endorsement—to compel acceptance and motivate dissemination. This is the conceptual equivalent of a perpetual motion machine in the social and intellectual realm. What, then, are the constituent parts of such an idea? What gives it this auto-propagating force capable of overcoming active elite resistance?

We can deconstruct implicit authority's foundations into several interconnected dimensions. These are not mutually exclusive but often combine to create an irresistible conviction force that no amount of external suppression can permanently contain.

**4.1 Internal Coherence and Logical Necessity (The Authority of Logic)**

The most fundamental source of implicit authority is the argument's internal structure. An idea that is logically sound, where conclusions follow inexorably from premises, commands assent through reason's force alone. To disagree is to place oneself in logical inconsistency—a cognitively uncomfortable state humans strongly motivate to resolve. This is epistemic deference's purest form: we submit to the argument not because we want to, but because rational thought's structure leaves us no alternative. The authority lies in the argument's *syntax*, not its *semantic* content.

A mathematical proof is the archetypal example; its authority is undeniable regardless of the mathematician scribbling it on a napkin. But logical force operates in more everyday contexts. When a revolutionary argument exposes contradictions in ruling ideology—when it shows elites' professed principles are inconsistent with their enforced practices—it gains authority that cannot be reasoned away. The elite may suppress the argument, but they cannot refute it without abandoning their own professed commitments. This is why revolutionary movements so often begin by taking ruling ideology *at its word*, demanding its promises be made real.

**4.2 Empirical Adequacy and Predictive Power (The Authority of Reality)**

An idea gains immense implicit authority when it demonstrates superior ability to model, predict, or manipulate reality. This is the authority of "it works." A scientific theory that successfully predicts a novel phenomenon, a farming technique consistently yielding higher crops, a form of social organization reducing conflict and increasing flourishing, a military tactic winning battles against superior forces—these propagate because they provide tangible advantage in navigating the world.

The feedback loop with reality *is* the enforcement mechanism. To ignore an idea with strong empirical adequacy is to choose failure over success, ignorance over understanding, death over survival. The idea's authority is constantly reinforced by the non-human arbiter of physical and social worlds. Elites can suppress the idea, but they cannot suppress the reality the idea accurately describes—and eventually, the mismatch between elite's preferred narrative and reality ordinary people experience becomes unsustainable.

This explains why revolutionary ideas often gain first adherents among those most directly exposed to empirical reality: workers experiencing exploitation, soldiers witnessing defeat, farmers suffering crop failure, patients dying despite official medicine. Their lived experience provides constant validation of revolutionary ideas and constant disconfirmation of official ideology.

**4.3 Consonance with Deeply Held Values and Identity (The Authority of the Self)**

An idea can achieve profound authority not by changing minds, but by articulating and giving voice to pre-existing, perhaps unspoken, values, intuitions, and identities. When a revolutionary leader articulates a populace's frustration, when a religious prophet gives words to spiritual longings, when a philosopher articulates a generation's unease—the ideas propagate not because new but because they feel like truer expressions of what people already believed.

This is the authority of recognition. The idea resonates because it aligns with an individual's sense of self, moral intuitions, experience of injustice, or cultural identity. Accepting the idea feels like affirming one's own identity—a deeply motivating force. The energy for propagation comes from the individual's own psychological need for self-consistency and authentic expression.

This mechanism is particularly powerful in revolutionary contexts because it creates adherents willing to sacrifice for the idea. When an idea is experienced as an expression of one's deepest self, threats against the idea are experienced as threats against the self—and resisted accordingly. This is why martyrs are so effective for revolutionary movements: their willingness to die demonstrates, with ultimate seriousness, that the idea has become identical with the self.

**4.4 Parsimony and Elegance (The Authority of Aesthetics)**

There is a profound aesthetic dimension to intellectual authority. An idea explaining a wide range of phenomena with a small set of elegant principles possesses compelling beauty. This is often called Occam's razor—the principle that among competing hypotheses, the one with fewest assumptions should be selected. The human mind draws toward patterns and simplicity. An elegant idea is easier to grasp, remember, and communicate. Its authority is aesthetic and cognitive; it *feels* right because it imposes order on complexity with minimal cognitive effort.

This parsimony signals deep understanding and is a powerful, self-sustaining attractor for belief. Revolutionary ideologies explaining diverse grievances—economic exploitation, political oppression, cultural disrespect, foreign domination—with unified frameworks have aesthetic advantage over piecemeal critiques. The simplicity of "class struggle," "racial oppression," or "colonial domination" as explanations for manifold ills gives these ideas cognitive elegance aiding propagation.

**4.5 Generative Capacity and Fertility (The Authority of Fruitfulness)**

A particularly powerful source of implicit authority is an idea's capacity to generate new insights, discoveries, and applications. Fertile ideas—those opening new lines of inquiry, suggesting new experiments, revealing new connections, enabling new practices—command assent because they prove worth through continued productivity.

This is most visible in science, where fruitful research programs attract adherents because they offer promising directions for future work. But it operates in revolutionary contexts as well. An ideology helping adherents make sense of new situations, providing guidance for action in unforeseen circumstances, generating satisfying interpretations of events—such ideology proves worth through continued utility. It propagates because it keeps proving useful.

**4.6 Experiential Accessibility and Anchoring**

Building on contemporary scholarship, we must add a sixth dimension: the idea's capacity to be anchored in direct human experience . Ideas that remain purely abstract are vulnerable to elite co-optation and distortion. Ideas that can be *tested* in lived experience—that offer practices, techniques, or ways of being that generate verifiable results—acquire authority no amount of propaganda can counteract.

The authority-producing capacities of community and aesthetics are key issues here, pointing to vexed relationships between politics and policing, inventiveness and violence . When an idea generates communities of practice where adherents collectively validate each other's experiences, its authority becomes embedded in social fabric, not just individual belief.

**4.7 The Interplay of Dimensions**

These dimensions are not independent. They interact and reinforce each other in ways creating virtually unstoppable momentum. An idea that is logically coherent, empirically adequate, resonant with identity, aesthetically elegant, experientially accessible, and fruitful over time possesses implicit authority of such magnitude that no amount of external suppression can permanently contain it. The elite may burn books and kill adherents, but they cannot kill the idea, because the idea keeps being rediscovered, keeps proving itself, keeps resonating with each new generation's experience and intuition.

This is why revolutionary movements that fail often still plant seeds flowering decades or centuries later. The idea's implicit authority survives its carriers' defeat, awaiting only favorable conditions to re-emerge.

## 5. The Dynamics of Propagation: How Implicit Authority Overcomes Elite Resistance

If the previous section described the "fuel" of an authoritative idea, this section describes the engine—the mechanisms by which its authority creates a self-sustaining propagation cycle capable of operating against active opposition from those commanding explicit authority.

**5.1 The Zero-Energy Propagation Cycle**

Consider how an idea with genuine implicit authority propagates absent elite resistance. The cycle is self-sustaining:

1. **Utility/Insight:** An individual encounters an idea (e.g., more efficient farming technique, compelling injustice critique, satisfying spiritual teaching).

2. **Adoption:** The individual adopts the idea because its *intrinsic merit* commands assent. This is the primary act of submission to implicit authority.

3. **Emergent Advocacy:** By adopting and using the idea, the individual becomes an unwitting propagation vector. They need not be convinced to "spread the word"; their improved outcomes, changed behavior, or simple mention in conversation acts as living demonstration of merit.

4. **Social Proof:** Observers see the adopter's success, satisfaction, or conviction. This creates social proof. The idea's authority is now reinforced by a second layer: *others have accepted it, and it seems to work for them* .

5. **Replication:** A new observer, convinced by combined force of the idea's apparent merit and adoption social proof, adopts the idea themselves, returning to step 2.

In this cycle, propagation's initial "energy" comes solely from the idea's intrinsic merit. Each adopter becomes a passive transmitter, their very behavior serving as compelling advertisement. No centralized marketing, propaganda, or enforcement is needed. The idea spreads like a virus, its "fitness" determined by its usefulness and resonance with human cognitive and social structures.

**5.2 Propagation Under Conditions of Elite Resistance**

Now introduce active elite resistance. The elite, commanding explicit authority, deploys resources to suppress the idea: censorship, persecution, co-optation, ridicule, legal sanction, violence. How can the idea continue propagating under these conditions?

The answer lies in propagation cycle modification under suppression. Each mechanism of implicit authority becomes, under suppression, a mechanism of *resistance* to suppression:

**Persecution as Authentication.** When elites persecute an idea and its adherents, they inadvertently signal the idea is *dangerous to power*. For those already disposed to doubt elite authority, persecution authenticates the idea as genuinely threatening established order. The fact that "they" don't want you to know this becomes a reason to investigate it. Persecution creates curiosity where indifference might otherwise prevail.

**Suppression as Evidence.** When elites suppress empirical evidence supporting revolutionary ideas, they create situations where ordinary people's lived experience constantly contradicts official narratives. The farmer whose yields increase using banned techniques, the worker whose conditions improve through forbidden organization, the patient who recovers using suppressed knowledge—these experiences provide daily validation of revolutionary ideas and daily disconfirmation of elite ideology. Suppression becomes self-defeating because it creates permanent counter-evidence reservoirs in everyday life.

**Martyrdom as Amplification.** When elites kill idea adherents, they create martyrs whose sacrifice demonstrates commitment depth the idea inspires. Martyrdom provides the most powerful possible social proof: here is someone willing to die for this idea. The willingness to sacrifice life itself signals, with ultimate seriousness, that the idea is worth more than biological existence. This creates profound resonance with identity and values, as observers confront whether they too would die for their beliefs.

**Illegality as Distinction.** When elites make an idea illegal, they create clear boundaries between official world and truth world. Illegal ideas become property of those willing to risk sanction, creating communities of trust and mutual commitment. These communities, bound by shared risk, become highly effective propagation networks. The very fact of illegality screens out casually interested and ensures genuine commitment among adherents.

**Cognitive Dissonance as Engine.** When elites' actions contradict their professed principles—preaching freedom while practicing oppression, equality while maintaining hierarchy, justice while committing injustice—they create cognitive dissonance revolutionary ideas can resolve. The revolutionary idea naming this contradiction—exposing hypocrisy and offering explanation—gains authority precisely because it makes sense of dissonant experience.

**Experiential Authority Under Conditions of Risk.** Contemporary scholarship emphasizes that experiences of suffering, risk-taking, and proximity to death can themselves generate authority . Underground movements create conditions where adherents undergo transformative experiences—risking arrest, enduring hardship, facing danger—that generate experiential authority no safe, comfortable movement could produce.

**5.3 The Cascade as Empirical Signature**

Your earlier insight—that the mark of a naturally authoritative idea is "the observation of a cascade of divergence from the expected norm over time"—finds rigorous formulation here. A truly authoritative idea doesn't convince individuals linearly one by one. It creates a *phase transition* in the social-epistemic field. The signature is non-linear: slow accumulation below a threshold, then sudden, accelerating divergence from expected pattern.

This distinguishes implicit authority from mere popularity or propaganda. Propaganda produces compliance requiring continuous energy input; remove the propaganda machine, and behavior reverts to norm. A cascade driven by implicit authority, by contrast, is self-sustaining. Each new adopter becomes transmitter, and divergence accelerates until new norm established.

The *cascade of divergence* is thus the empirical fingerprint of an idea carrying its own propagation mechanism. And this cascade is amplified, not diminished, by elite suppression—provided the idea possesses genuine intrinsic merit.

## 6. The Revolutionary Thesis: Why Transformation Requires Implicit Authority

We now arrive at the paper's central thesis, building on our dialogue: **genuine revolutionary change requires implicit authority; movements lacking it are destined to reproduce the structures they criticize.**

This thesis operates at multiple levels of analysis and carries profound implications for understanding social transformation.

**6.1 The Problem of Structural Reproduction**

Critical realist analysis of revolution identifies a fundamental puzzle: when and how do agents consciously reproduce or unconsciously transform social structures? . Drawing on Roy Bhaskar's transformational model of social activity (TMSA), scholars distinguish between situations where agents' unconscious actions transform social structures amid social disintegration ("classic bourgeois revolutions") and situations where agents consciously seek to preserve and reproduce social structures ("passive revolutions") .

The crucial insight is that "agents consciously seek to preserve and reproduce social structures" when those structures, marked by inequality and hierarchies, are viewed as historical constructs rather than natural phenomena . In other words, revolutionary actors who *understand* that social structures are constructed and changeable may nonetheless reproduce those structures if they lack alternative sources of authority to guide transformation.

This is the paradox of revolution without implicit authority: movements that successfully overthrow existing elites but lack ideas with sufficient intrinsic authority to reorganize perception, motivate new forms of social relation, and command assent through merit rather than force inevitably fall back on familiar patterns. They establish new hierarchies because hierarchy is all they know. They exercise power through explicit authority because they possess no alternative.

**6.2 Why Implicit Authority Breaks the Reproduction Cycle**

Implicit authority breaks the reproduction cycle through several mechanisms:

**Perceptual Reorganization.** An idea with genuine implicit authority doesn't just add information to existing worldviews—it *reconfigures the interpretive apparatus itself*. It functions as paradigm shift at the individual level, a Gestalt switch reorganizing perception, not just belief. This is why such ideas can displace even the most entrenched fundamentalism. Fundamentalism is not merely a set of beliefs but a *perceptual closure*—a way of seeing systematically filtering evidence and interpreting everything through fixed grid. An idea capable of displacing fundamentalism must *open* that closure, reorganizing perception so invisible becomes visible, impossible becomes thinkable.

This perceptual reorganization is the foundation of genuine transformation. Without it, revolutionaries remain trapped in the categories of the old order. They may seize power, but they will exercise it according to patterns internalized from their oppressors.

**Alternative Authority Source.** Movements lacking implicit authority have no authority source to draw upon except the explicit authority they seek to overthrow—or the latent authority they have internalized from the old regime. When they triumph, they therefore have no way to constitute new social relations except through the only authority they know: explicit enforcement.

Implicit authority provides an alternative. Movements animated by ideas with genuine intrinsic merit can draw upon that merit as authority source. Decisions can be legitimated by reference to truth, utility, moral resonance—not just by reference to who holds power. This makes possible forms of social organization not centered on hierarchy.

**Self-Sustaining Compliance.** Explicit authority requires continuous energy input: monitoring, enforcement, sanctions. Remove the enforcer, and compliance ceases. Implicit authority, by contrast, is self-sustaining. People comply because they recognize the idea's merit, not because they fear punishment. A society organized around implicit authority—around ideas whose authority is intrinsic—requires far less enforcement energy than one organized around explicit authority.

This is why revolutionary movements animated by implicit authority can sustain themselves through persecution and why post-revolutionary societies animated by such ideas can maintain order without tyranny.

**Resistance to Co-optation.** Ideas lacking intrinsic merit are easily co-opted. Elites can absorb them, domesticate them, strip them of revolutionary implications. But ideas with genuine implicit authority resist co-optation because their authority doesn't depend on elite endorsement. The elite may pretend to embrace the idea, but their actions reveal whether they truly honor it. When they act inconsistently with the idea's implications, the idea itself provides resources for critique.

This is why movements animated by implicit authority maintain their integrity through institutionalization—and why movements lacking it degenerate into the very structures they opposed.

**6.3 Historical Evidence for the Thesis**

The thesis that revolution requires implicit authority illuminates patterns across revolutionary history:

**Successful Transformations.** The American Revolution succeeded not primarily through military force but through ideas whose implicit authority commanded assent: self-evident truths, unalienable rights, consent of the governed. These ideas propagated through their intrinsic merit, not through enforcement. They reorganized perception, making monarchy appear unnatural and self-government appear obvious.

The abolitionist movement succeeded because its core ideas—human equality, the evil of treating persons as property—possessed moral resonance that no amount of elite suppression could counteract. The implicit authority of these ideas generated cascades of conversion, creating movements that eventually overwhelmed slaveholding interests.

The civil rights movement drew on ideas whose authority was intrinsic: the dignity of every person, the injustice of segregation, the power of nonviolent witness. These ideas reorganized perception, making segregation appear not just illegal but obscene. Their implicit authority sustained commitment through persecution and eventually transformed national consciousness.

**Failed Transformations and Structural Reproduction.** By contrast, revolutions animated primarily by will to power, ethnic nationalism, or ideological systems lacking genuine intrinsic merit have consistently reproduced the hierarchies they overthrew. The French Revolution's descent into Terror, the Russian Revolution's degeneration into Stalinism, countless post-colonial revolutions' replacement of European elites with native elites—these illustrate the thesis. Movements lacking ideas with sufficient implicit authority to reorganize perception and motivate new forms of relation fell back on familiar patterns of explicit authority and hierarchical control.

As critical realist analysis suggests, these represent "passive revolutions" where agents consciously seek to preserve social structures even while overthrowing particular elites . The structures persist because no alternative authoritative vision exists to guide transformation.

**6.4 The Diagnostic Question**

The thesis implies a diagnostic question for any revolutionary movement: **Does your movement possess ideas with sufficient implicit authority to command assent through intrinsic merit alone? Can these ideas propagate without propaganda, without enforcement, without elite endorsement—indeed, can they propagate *against* active suppression?**

If the answer is no, the movement may still succeed in overthrowing existing elites. But it will reproduce the structures it criticizes. It will establish new hierarchies, new orthodoxies, new enforcement mechanisms. It will become what it beheld.

If the answer is yes—if the movement's ideas genuinely possess implicit authority, if they can reorganize perception, if they generate self-sustaining cascades—then genuine transformation becomes possible. Not guaranteed, but possible. The movement has resources to constitute new forms of social relation, not just new rulers.

## 7. Implications: Understanding Revolution, Power, and Social Change

The framework of implicit authority, culminating in the revolutionary thesis, illuminates several features of social change that have puzzled theorists working within frameworks privileging explicit authority.

**7.1 The Puzzle of Initial Adhesion**

Why do people initially embrace revolutionary ideas when costs are high and success probability low? The implicit authority framework suggests benefits are not primarily material or strategic but cognitive and moral. People embrace revolutionary ideas because those ideas make better sense of their experience than official alternatives. The idea's authority is intrinsic: it explains what was puzzling, names what was inchoate, validates what was denied. The initial adherent is not a rational calculator of revolutionary success but a truth-seeker whose epistemic interests are served by the idea.

**7.2 The Puzzle of Commitment Under Persecution**

Why do people maintain commitment to revolutionary ideas when persecution intensifies? The implicit authority framework suggests persecution, rather than undermining commitment, can strengthen it by authenticating the idea as genuinely threatening to power, by creating communities of shared risk, and by forcing adherents to clarify and deepen understanding. The idea costing you something becomes more valuable, not less.

**7.3 The Puzzle of Tipping Points**

How do revolutionary movements reach tipping points where they become suddenly unstoppable? The implicit authority framework suggests propagation under suppression follows non-linear dynamics. For long periods, the idea spreads slowly through true believer networks, invisible to elites who dismiss them as fringe. But each new adherent becomes a propagation vector, and social proof's cumulative effect eventually reaches thresholds where the idea's authority becomes visible and undeniable. At this point, elite explicit authority crumbles with astonishing speed, as latent authority (internalized acceptance of old order) evaporates and even formal position holders defect to new consensus.

**7.4 The Dialectic of Implicit and Explicit Authority**

Revolutionary success eventually transforms implicit authority into explicit authority. The triumphant movement must establish new institutions, new laws, new enforcement mechanisms. The implicit authority carrying the revolution now must be codified, stabilized, and defended.

And here lies the deepest irony: the new explicit authority, in stabilizing the revolution, creates conditions for new revolutionary ideas to challenge *it*. The dialectic continues. Implicit authority is the permanent possibility of revolution, the eternal recourse of marginalized against powerful, the guarantee that no elite can permanently suppress truth, utility, and moral resonance.

**7.5 The Meta-Implication: This Framework as Test Case**

The framework implies something about itself. If implicit authority is real—if ideas with intrinsic merit can propagate through their own power—then this idea about implicit authority should itself be capable of propagation. It should generate cascades. It should reorganize perception. It should be recognizable as true by those encountering it, not because we persuade them, but because it makes sense of their experience.

Whether this occurs remains to be seen. But the framework provides criteria for evaluation: watch for cascades of divergence from expected norms; observe whether the idea propagates despite resistance; note whether it reorganizes how people perceive power, authority, and revolution.

## 8. Conclusion: The Permanent Possibility of Transformation

This paper has argued for expanding our conception of authority beyond hierarchical frameworks dominating economics, political theory, and organizational sociology. Van den Brink and Gilles's distinction between explicit and latent authority , Aghion and Tirole's distinction between formal and real authority, and Baker, Gibbons, and Murphy's analysis of informal authority  have significantly advanced our understanding of how authority operates within established structures. Yet these frameworks remain tethered to hierarchical premises and cannot explain the most dramatic instances of authority: the power of revolutionary ideas to propagate against the active resistance of those commanding conventional power levers.

Implicit authority—the authority of ideas accepted on intrinsic merit—represents a qualitatively distinct phenomenon requiring separate theoretical treatment. Unlike latent authority, which internalizes hierarchy, implicit authority transcends and often challenges hierarchy. The good idea propagates because of its goodness, not its source, and propagates most dramatically when those in power are most determined to suppress it.

The paper's central thesis is that **genuine revolutionary change requires implicit authority**. Movements animated solely by will to power, by hatred of existing elites, by ethnic solidarity, or by ideological systems lacking genuine intrinsic merit—these may overthrow particular rulers, but they will reproduce the structures they criticize. They will establish new hierarchies because they lack alternative authority sources to guide transformation. They will exercise power through explicit authority because they possess no other.

Conversely, movements animated by ideas with genuine implicit authority—ideas that are true, useful, morally resonant, experientially accessible, and fruitful—have resources for genuine transformation. Such ideas reorganize perception, making new forms of social relation imaginable and desirable. They command assent without enforcement, generating self-sustaining cascades of adoption. They resist co-optation because their authority doesn't depend on elite endorsement. They sustain commitment through persecution because they speak to deepest human needs for truth, meaning, and justice.

The spectrum from explicit enforcement to implicit virtue captures the full range of ways compliance and acceptance are secured in human society. At one end stands the threat of sanction; at the other end stands the force of the better argument, the demonstrated result, the resonant truth, the transformative experience. Between them lies the rich territory where power, reason, experience, and moral intuition interact to shape how we live together.

And at the far end of that spectrum lies the permanent possibility of transformation—the guarantee that authority, in its deepest sense, belongs ultimately not to those commanding armies but to those speaking truth. Not to those controlling institutions but to those whose ideas reorganize perception. Not to those wielding force but to those whose ideas propagate through intrinsic merit alone.

This is why revolutionary change requires implicit authority. And this is why implicit authority will always be the enemy of those creating and maintaining hierarchies of power—not because it seeks destruction, but because its very operation bypasses the control mechanisms upon which hierarchy depends. The systemic self-preservation response of hierarchical structures, amplified and distorted through epistemic path dependence, methodological challenges, funding incentives, and professional risk, works to marginalize this concept from serious academic inquiry. But the concept remains true regardless of its recognition.

The mark of a naturally authoritative idea is the cascade of divergence from expected norms over time. It operates as catalyst, modifying receptors' worldviews, displacing even the most entrenched fundamentalism. Such ideas are rare. They are precious. And they are the only reliable foundation for genuine human transformation.
