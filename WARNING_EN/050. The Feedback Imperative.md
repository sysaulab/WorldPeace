# The Feedback Loop

***Why Systems Collapses***

## Introduction: The Two Helpers

Imagine two people who want to make the world better.

The first spends every day helping individuals. They feed the hungry, comfort the sick, listen to the brokenhearted. Their hands are always busy with the person right in front of them. By any normal measure, they are a good person.

The second ignores the cries of individuals. Instead, they work on changing the structures that create suffering in the first place—laws, economies, power systems. Their work takes decades. Most people never see what they do. In fact, to those who are suffering now, they look cruel. Why aren't they helping?

Who is more moral?

This question has haunted philosophers for centuries. But it's not just an abstract puzzle. It gets to something deep about how systems work—and how they fail.

This essay is about what happens when the first kind of helper, the one with all the good intentions, actually makes things worse without knowing it. It's about how kindness can be co-opted, how compassion can become complicity, and what it takes to build systems that don't just soothe suffering but actually stop producing it.

The core idea is simple: **systems that cannot hear themselves tend toward horror.** The rest of this essay explains what that means, why it matters, and how it plays out at every scale—from your own mind to the future of civilization.

## Part One: The Congregation That Meant Well

Let me tell you a story.

There's a community being slowly crushed. The jobs pay poverty wages. The landlords charge whatever they want because there's nowhere else to go. The government has pulled back services for decades. People are hungry, sick, and exhausted.

Into this situation comes a congregation. They are good people with big hearts. They open a food bank. They start a free clinic. They offer counseling. They pour themselves out in service.

By any local measure, they are saints. The people they feed love them. The people they heal bless them.

But something strange happens. The exploitation doesn't stop. In fact, it continues exactly as before. The landlords keep raising rents. The employers keep paying starvation wages. The government feels no pressure to restore services because someone else is handling the mess.

The congregation has become a shock absorber for an unjust system. They are taking the worst outputs of exploitation and quietly managing them. The screams of suffering are muffled by their kindness. The system never has to hear what it's doing because the congregation has turned down the volume.

This is not a story about hypocrites. It's a story about good people caught in a trap they cannot see. Every act of kindness, considered in isolation, is beautiful. But the pattern of all those acts together props up the very thing they hate.

The congregation is "locally nice" but "globally evil"—not because they intend evil, but because their goodness is being used.

This is what co-option looks like. Your virtue gets absorbed into a system and starts serving purposes you never intended.

## Part Two: The Nazi Who Loved His Dog

If that story seems too abstract, consider a real one that is infinitely darker.

The Nazis murdered six million Jews. They also murdered millions of others—Slavs, Roma, disabled people, political prisoners. They did it with factories and railways and bureaucratic efficiency. It was the most organized genocide in human history.

But here's something less known: the Nazis also passed the most comprehensive animal protection laws the world had ever seen. In 1933, they outlawed kosher butchering. Hermann Göring, one of the top Nazis, threatened to send animal abusers to concentration camps. There were laws about how lobsters could be boiled, how horses could be worked, how experiments on dogs could be conducted.

An SS officer could come home from a day of organizing mass murder and lovingly feed his German Shepherd. This actually happened. These men were not pretending. They genuinely loved their dogs.

How is that possible?

The answer is boundary work. The Nazis built a moral universe with rigid boundaries. Inside the boundary were Aryans and, to some extent, animals they could identify with—dogs, horses, wolves. These beings deserved compassion. Outside the boundary were "subhumans"—Jews, Roma, Slavs—who deserved nothing.

The compassion for animals was real. It was also a fence. It defined who was inside the circle of humanity and, by extension, who was outside. The same emotional capacity that made an SS officer kind to his dog made it possible for him to exterminate "vermin."

This is the same pattern as the congregation, but pushed to its extreme. Local virtue—kindness to animals—was integrated into global evil. The system heard animal pain but could not hear human pain because the humans had been redefined out of existence.

## Part Three: What Anthropology Teaches Us

These stories are not just anecdotes. They reveal patterns that anthropologists have studied for generations. Four thinkers, in particular, help us understand what's going on.

**Gregory Bateson** was a anthropologist who thought like a cyberneticist. He saw that everything is connected in loops, not lines. The congregation thinks linearly: suffering happens, they respond, suffering decreases. But the loop actually goes: suffering happens, they respond, system stabilizes, suffering continues. They never see the loop because they're only looking at the line.

Bateson also noticed that people get stuck in patterns of escalation. The empathic congregation and the ruthless revolutionary can't understand each other. Each sees the other as morally defective. This mutual incomprehension—what Bateson called schismogenesis—prevents them from learning from each other.

And he saw that focused purpose without systemic wisdom is dangerous. The congregation has a clear purpose: relieve suffering. But because they don't see the system, their purpose serves the system. Wisdom would mean holding both: the compassion and the analysis, the local and the global.

**Roy Rappaport** studied how rituals regulate societies. He saw that rituals transmit information while also managing it. The congregation's charity is a ritual. It communicates "we care" while also channeling suffering into forms that don't threaten the system.

More importantly, Rappaport saw that rituals become sacred—and the sacred cannot be questioned. The congregation's goodness becomes sanctified. Asking "Is this charity actually helping?" feels like asking "Is it wrong to be good?" The question itself becomes unaskable. So feedback stops.

**Mary Douglas** gave us a map of why different groups see the world differently. She had two dimensions: group (how tight the boundaries are around a community) and grid (how rigid the roles are inside it).

The congregation is high-group, low-grid—tight boundaries, loose internal roles. This produces an egalitarian cosmology where everyone takes care of each other. But it also produces blindness to structure. They see individual suffering, not systemic causes. The problem is always a bad landlord, never the rental system.

The revolutionary is often low-group, high-grid—weak community bonds, strong role definition. This produces systemic vision but local detachment. They see the structure but not the faces. Neither is complete. Both are trapped by their cosmology.

**Victor Turner** studied how societies change through crisis. He saw a pattern: breach, crisis, redress, and then either reintegration or permanent split.

The congregation gets stuck at redress. They respond to the crisis of suffering, but their response doesn't lead to transformation. It just stabilizes the original breach. The social drama stalls. The system never has to truly change because the redress phase never ends.

Turner also studied liminality—the in-between space where old structures dissolve and new ones haven't formed. This is where real change happens. But it's uncomfortable. The congregation flees liminality into the certainty of action. They can't stand not knowing what to do, so they do what they know—even if it perpetuates the problem.

## Part Four: The Revolutionary's Deficit

If local empathy can be co-opted, what about its absence?

This is uncomfortable territory. But the logic is clear: if the congregation's compassion muffles feedback, then someone who doesn't feel that compassion might be the one who amplifies it. The revolutionary who ignores individual suffering might be the one who forces the system to change.

Consider John Brown. He murdered pro-slavery settlers in Kansas. He led a raid on Harpers Ferry that got his sons killed. In his own time, many considered him insane or evil. But he also helped make the Civil War inevitable, and the Civil War ended slavery. Was he moral?

Or consider the suffragettes who smashed windows and went on hunger strikes and threw themselves under horses. Locally, they were destructive. Globally, they helped shatter a system of political exclusion.

This doesn't mean psychopathy is good. There's a difference between the revolutionary who lacks empathy for a purpose and the psychopath who lacks it entirely. The revolutionary cares about future humanity; the psychopath cares about no one. The revolutionary can recover empathy after the struggle; the psychopath cannot.

But the paradox stands: sometimes the people who change the world are the ones who cannot be bought off by the satisfaction of being locally nice. They see the system, not the faces. This makes them dangerous, but sometimes necessary.

## Part Five: Energy and Information—The Physical Foundation

To understand why feedback matters, we have to go deeper than stories and anthropology. We have to go to physics.

Everything that exists requires energy to keep existing. Stars burn fuel. Cells metabolize. Economies consume resources. This isn't metaphor—it's thermodynamics. Order is not the absence of chaos but the local defiance of it through constant energy flow. A living thing is not a static object but a dynamic pattern maintained by throughput.

This has moral implications.

If systems persist by maintaining organized energy flow, then actions that disrupt those flows—ecological collapse, social fragmentation, trauma—are not just unfortunate. They are destabilizing interventions into living networks. Morality, in this view, is about what enhances or degrades the conditions that make complex life possible.

Energy alone isn't enough. It has to be guided. That's where information comes in. Information tells energy where to go. A river without banks floods everywhere. Energy without information is waste. Information without energy is inert. Feedback loops couple them.

When the congregation soothes suffering, they are moving energy (food, medicine) through the system. But they are also transmitting information—or failing to. When their charity becomes routine, it stops carrying information. The system stops being surprised by suffering because suffering is always already managed. The signal degrades to noise.

Norbert Wiener, who founded cybernetics, saw this clearly. He defined cybernetics as the science of control and communication in animals and machines. But he also applied it to society. His insight: systems persist through feedback. No feedback, no adaptation. Distorted feedback, collapse.

An ethical society, for Wiener, was not one without error. It was one capable of correcting error. The question is always: can this system hear itself?

## Part Six: The Systemic Moral Field

Put all this together—anthropology, thermodynamics, cybernetics—and a picture emerges.

Morality is not a set of rules handed down from on high. It's not just about intentions or consequences. It's about patterns. Specifically, it's about the patterns of energy and information flow that make complex systems possible.

Think of it like a field. In physics, a field is not a thing but a distributed influence—gravitational, electromagnetic—that shapes behavior without central command. A moral field, analogously, is the distributed set of forces that orients agents toward or away from systemic coherence.

This field has properties:

**It's relational.** It arises between agents, not within isolated individuals. The congregation's morality isn't just inside them; it's in their relationships with each other and with the suffering they serve.

**It's emergent.** You can't reduce it to single rules. The complexity of the system generates moral properties that aren't present in the parts.

**It's dynamic.** It evolves as systems become more complex. What counts as moral at one scale may be different at another.

**It's nonlocal.** Actions propagate consequences far beyond their immediate context. The congregation's food bank affects landlords across town who never see it.

This means morality must be evaluated across scales. The same action can be locally stabilizing and globally destabilizing. The congregation's charity feeds people (good locally) while enabling exploitation (bad globally). The revolutionary's ruthlessness causes suffering (bad locally) while transforming systems (good globally).

There's no simple answer. The moral field requires us to hold multiple scales at once.

## Part Seven: Transposing the Framework

Here's the powerful thing: this logic applies at every scale. The pattern repeats.

**At the ecological scale:** Ecosystems maintain themselves through feedback—predator-prey cycles, nutrient cycling, fire regimes. When humans suppress these loops—eliminating wolves, suppressing fires, creating monocultures—the system loses resilience.

Conservation, then, is not about preserving static states. It's about restoring feedback. Rewilding means bringing back the wolves so the deer populations can regulate themselves. Letting fires burn so the forest can regenerate. This is "cruel" locally—wolves kill deer, fires destroy trees—but necessary globally.

The ecological equivalent of the congregation is captive breeding programs that save individual animals while their habitat is destroyed. The pain is soothed; the cause continues.

**At the economic scale:** Markets are information systems. Prices transmit signals about scarcity, demand, externalities. When externalities aren't priced—pollution, exploitation, depletion—the signals are distorted. The market can't hear what it's doing.

The economic congregation is corporate social responsibility. It soothes outrage without changing extraction. Carbon offsets let emissions continue. Fair trade slightly improves conditions while leaving trade structures intact.

The economic revolutionary is degrowth—accepting short-term contraction for long-term sustainability. Locally painful, globally necessary.

**At the political scale:** Democracies are feedback systems. Elections, protests, journalism transmit signals from people to power. When these are suppressed—gerrymandering, media consolidation, lobbying capture—the signals distort.

The political congregation is the non-profit that provides services the state should provide. They take pressure off government to function. Food banks become permanent instead of emergency, and the state never hears hunger.

The political revolutionary is the abolitionist who refuses to reform broken institutions, insisting instead that people feel their absence until they build alternatives.

**At the technological scale:** AI systems learn from feedback. But the feedback can be distorted. Engagement algorithms optimize for attention, not well-being. Content moderation muffles signal while amplifying noise.

The technological congregation is the AI ethics board that legitimizes deployment without structural change. "Responsible AI" that soothes concern while extraction continues.

The technological revolutionary builds systems that can feel their own effects—AI that registers harm, platforms that hear distortion.

**At the psychological scale:** Your own mind is a feedback system. Emotions signal needs. Pain signals damage. Anxiety signals threat. When you suppress these—through numbing, distraction, dissociation—you develop pathology.

The psychological congregation is coping mechanisms that soothe without addressing cause. Comfort eating, substance use, endless scrolling—all muffle internal feedback.

The psychological revolutionary is therapy that insists on facing what was avoided—trauma processing, grief work, existential confrontation.

**At the civilizational scale:** Civilizations are all these systems nested together. Their feedback loops operate across centuries. When a civilization suppresses signals—through denial, ideology, distraction—it drifts toward collapse.

The civilizational congregation is philanthropy that addresses symptoms while the climate burns. Tree planting while extraction continues. Peace-building while resource depletion drives conflict.

The civilizational revolutionary is the truth-teller—the scientist, the whistleblower, the prophet—who insists on reality despite comfort.

**At the cosmic scale:** The universe itself is a system—expanding, cooling, complexifying. Stars form through gravitational feedback. Life emerges through planetary feedback. We are part of that.

The anthropic principle as feedback: we exist because the universe's parameters allowed life. But if we destroy those conditions, we terminate the feedback.

The cosmic revolutionary is humanity itself, if we choose to become the universe's capacity for self-correction rather than its pathology.

## Part Eight: The Meta-Principle

Across all these scales, one pattern holds:

> **A system's health is proportional to its capacity to register its own effects and transform accordingly.**

Pathology is feedback suppression at any scale.

Health is feedback transparency—signals that are clear, channels that are open, responses that are possible.

This is not metaphor. Energy flows through all systems. Information guides all flows. Feedback couples them. Morality is the practice of maintaining these couplings.

The congregation soothes, muffling signals. This is necessary up to a point—without it, systems would collapse into chaos before transformation could happen.

The revolutionary amplifies, forcing signals. This is necessary up to a point—without it, systems would stabilize into permanent injustice.

The bifocal agent—rare, difficult, essential—does both. They hold the hand and ask why it hurts. They soothe and organize. They are the system becoming self-aware at the point of pain.

## Part Nine: What This Means for How We Live

If this framework is right, it changes how we think about everything.

**In education:** We should teach systems literacy, not just facts. People need to understand feedback loops, scale, emergence. Ethics becomes not rule-following but cybernetic competence—the ability to navigate complex systems.

**In governance:** We should design institutions that can hear themselves. Transparency, accountability, adaptivity. Not structures that suppress feedback but structures that welcome it.

**In economics:** We should internalize externalities. Make prices tell the truth. Let markets feel their effects. This will be painful locally—truth always is—but necessary globally.

**In technology:** We should build systems that can feel their consequences. AI that registers harm. Platforms that hear distortion. Not systems optimized for extraction but systems optimized for feedback.

**In psychology:** We should cultivate internal feedback. Feel what is felt. Let pain inform. This is the work of therapy, of meditation, of honest self-reflection.

**In civilization:** We should choose transformation over collapse. Hear the signals while there is time. This is the great work of our century.

## Part Ten: Nature Becoming Self-Aware

Here is the deepest implication.

We are not separate from nature. We are nature—complex, living, evolving—becoming aware of itself. Our moral systems are not imposed from outside. They are the universe learning to listen to itself.

The congregation's kindness, without systemic awareness, becomes cruelty. The revolutionary's ruthlessness, without local care, becomes new pathology.

The feedback imperative is the only absolute: **systems must be able to hear themselves.**

If they can, they tend toward justice.

If they cannot, they tend toward horror.

Our job, at every scale—individual, community, civilization, species—is to build the yes and dismantle the no.

This is not philosophy. It is cybernetics. It is thermodynamics. It is anthropology. It is the universe, through us, learning to listen.
