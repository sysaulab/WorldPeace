
# The End of Wars

## I. The Nature of War: The Body Remembers What the Mind Forgets

War is not an aberration. It is not a failure of politics, nor a descent into primordial chaos from which civilization must be rescued. War is the body's fever when the organism has grown too numb to feel its own wounds.

A healthy body heals without conscious intervention. You do not will your cut to close; platelets aggregate, cytokines signal, fibroblasts knit tissue—all beneath awareness. The system regulates itself through countless local feedback loops, each one sensing, responding, correcting. Pain only reaches consciousness when the injury exceeds local capacity. Pain is the signal that something requires *attention*—not control, not command, but the focused awareness of the whole.

Human societies are bodies in this sense. They are living systems, not machines. They breathe, metabolize, reproduce, and die. They have circulatory systems (trade), nervous systems (communication), immune systems (defense). And like any body, they sicken when feedback loops are severed.

This is the deepest function of hierarchy in its healthy form: not command, but *sensing*. The king who walks among his people, the president who sleeps where the homeless sleep, the general who eats what his soldiers eat—these are not gestures. They are the restoration of feedback. They are the central nervous system connecting itself to the extremities, feeling what the periphery feels so that the whole can respond.

But hierarchy, like any organ, can hypertrophy. The center grows thick while the periphery grows numb. Elites insulate themselves from consequences. Decisions are made in rooms where suffering never enters. The poor become statistics, not neighbors. The wounded become cases, not people.

When feedback loops are severed long enough, the body begins to die from within. Inequality calcifies into caste. Resentment pools in the margins. The center, deaf to the periphery, mistakes silence for consent.

Then comes the fever.

War is that fever. It is not chosen by any single will; it emerges from the accumulated sickness of a system that can no longer feel itself. War forces feeling. It breaks the insulation. It brings the center into violent contact with the periphery—not through compassion, but through necessity. Bombs do not respect gated communities. Refugees do not ask permission. The pain that was ignored becomes the pain that cannot be escaped.

This is why war recurs across human history despite every vow of "never again." It is not because humans are wicked. It is because societies repeatedly grow structures that insulate the powerful from the consequences of their decisions, and those structures must eventually be shattered so that feeling can return.

The question is not how to eliminate war. The question is how to build societies that can feel without requiring trauma.

## II. The Misguidance of Eschatological Coordination

If war is the body's fever, eschatology is the fever's dream.

Across cultures and millennia, humans have developed narratives of the end times. The Rapture, the Apocalypse, Armageddon, Qiyamah, the Kali Yuga's destruction, Ragnarök—these are not merely stories. They are *coordination mechanisms*, as your reference to Jiang Xueqin suggests. They allow disparate peoples, separated by language and geography, to move in approximate alignment toward a shared horizon. They provide a grammar for collective action when no central command exists.

But here is the tragedy: eschatologies coordinate toward *imaginary* endpoints. They point to a destination that exists only in mind—a final judgment, a messianic age, a thousand-year reign. And because the destination is imaginary, the journey never ends. Every generation can believe it is the last. Every conflict can be framed as the final battle. Every death can be justified as necessary for the kingdom to come.

The result is endless sacrifice for symbols.

Symbols are important. They carry meaning, memory, identity. But symbols are not sacred in themselves. They are *pointers*—fingers pointing at the moon. The mistake of fundamentalism, in all its forms, is to mistake the finger for the moon, the symbol for the reality, the map for the territory.

Religious fundamentalism says: die for the symbol.
Nationalist fundamentalism says: kill for the symbol.
Economic fundamentalism says: exploit for the symbol (growth, GDP, shareholder value).

Each demands sacrifice to an abstraction. Each severs the feedback loop between action and consequence, because the consequence is deferred to an imaginary future—the afterlife, the glorious nation, the endless growth curve. Suffering in the present becomes acceptable, even necessary, for the symbol's triumph.

This is eschatology's betrayal. It was meant to orient, not to consume. It was meant to remind us of what matters, not to justify destroying what matters in its name. When the finger becomes the god, the moon is forgotten.

And so humanity stumbles from war to war, each generation convinced it is enacting the final drama, each leaving the same inheritance to the next: a world still waiting for an ending that never comes.

## III. The Invisible Fundamentalisms: Economic Liberalism as the Last Dogma

Religious and nationalist fundamentalisms are easy to recognize. They wear their certainties on their sleeves. They build walls, burn books, demand oaths. We see them clearly because they are *other*—distant in time or space, or contained within communities we already distrust.

But there is a fundamentalism so pervasive, so woven into the fabric of modern life, that it has become invisible. It is not recognized as a faith because it presents itself as simply *reality*. It is the water in which we swim.

I speak of economic liberalism: the doctrine that markets are the optimal and natural organizers of human life; that competition drives progress; that growth is the measure of health; that self-interest, properly channeled, produces collective good.

This doctrine has its creation myth: the state of nature, where life is solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short—until self-interested individuals contract to form society. It has its high priests: central bankers, Treasury secretaries, Nobel economists. It has its scriptures: Adam Smith's *Wealth of Nations*, Milton Friedman's essays, the quarterly earnings report. It has its eschatology: the end of history, where liberal capitalism has no remaining ideological rivals and humanity settles into permanent, peaceful optimization.

And it has its most perfect embodiment: game theory.

You have named this correctly. Game theory is not merely mathematics; it is a *worldview*. It begins from the assumption that actors are rational, self-interested, and strategic. It models all interaction as competition for payoffs. It proves, through elegant equations, that defection is often rational, that trust is a vulnerability, that cooperation must be enforced by repeated interaction and credible threats.

This is not a description of reality. It is a *prescription*—a way of seeing that, once adopted, makes itself true. When you believe others are rational competitors, you become one. When you expect defection, you defect first. When you treat love as a preference ranking, you lose the capacity to love.

Game theory is the systemic embodiment of the psychopathic mind. The psychopath does not lack intelligence; they lack *feeling*. They calculate without attachment. They see others as variables to be optimized, not as lives to be shared. They are perfectly rational in the game theorist's sense—and perfectly destructive of the conditions that make life worth living.

Economic fundamentalism has made this worldview normal. It has taught generations that self-interest is the only honest motive, that altruism is disguised selfishness, that the market's invisible hand will transmute greed into good. It has severed the feedback loop between production and consequence, so that we can extract resources without seeing the deserts left behind, consume goods without meeting the hands that made them, invest in distant factories without knowing the children who work there.

This is the last and most dangerous fundamentalism because it denies it is one. It presents itself as science, as nature, as simply *how things are*. To question it is to be naive, idealistic, uneconomic. Its high priests do not demand faith; they demand calculation. Its temples are not churches but trading floors. Its sacrifices are not burnt offerings but "externalities"—the costs that someone else will pay, somewhere else, sometime else.

But the costs are always paid. The feedback loop, though delayed, cannot be permanently severed. The fever is coming.

## IV. The Alternative: Systemic Morality Rooted in Living Systems

If the sickness is severed feedback, the cure is its restoration. If fundamentalism is the worship of symbols, the alternative is reverence for what the symbols *point toward*. If economic liberalism is the religion of calculation, the alternative is a morality rooted in the logic of living systems.

This is not a new revelation. It is the common core of every enduring human tradition, now translated into the language of cybernetics and systems theory.

Consider what all healthy human communities, across cultures and centuries, have understood:

- **Reciprocity** is not a strategy but a condition of continued relationship.
- **Hospitality** to the stranger is not charity but the recognition that boundaries are provisional.
- **Restoration** after harm is not optional but necessary for the community to remain whole.
- **The common good** is not the sum of individual goods but their precondition.
- **The leader** is not the commander but the one who *feels* first—who carries the community's pain to awareness.

These are not moral axioms imposed from above. They are *emergent properties* of systems that have learned to sustain themselves over time. They are what a community discovers when it lives long enough in one place, knowing that its children will marry its neighbors' children, that today's enemy may be tomorrow's ally, that the debt incurred must eventually be paid.

In systems language, we would say: a healthy system has short, strong feedback loops. Actions return to actors. Consequences are felt by those who decide. The system learns because it experiences the results of its choices.

This is the first principle of systemic morality: **self-healing capacity**.

A self-healing system does not require central command. When you cut your finger, you do not will the healing; it happens. Local cells sense the injury, coordinate the response, rebuild the tissue. The brain only becomes aware when the injury exceeds local capacity—and then it *attends*, not commands. It brings focused awareness to support what the local system is already doing.

Translate this to human society:

- Communities should be **cells**: autonomous, self-sufficient in essentials, capable of responding to their own needs without waiting for distant authority.
- Problems should be solved at the **level they occur**. A local water issue is local; a regional coordination issue is regional; a global issue is global. No level commands the others; each level supports the others' self-healing.
- Leaders should be **nervous agents**, not commanders. They should feel what the periphery feels—sleep where the homeless sleep, wait in line like others wait—because their role is not to control but to *connect* the system to itself.
- What works in one cell should be **copied** by others, not imposed. What fails should be **replaced**—gently, experimentally, without dogmatic attachment to any form.

This is not a blueprint for utopia. It is a *process*—a way of approaching the endless work of living together. It does not promise a perfect society; it promises a society that can learn, adapt, and heal. A society that does not require trauma to feel.

And crucially: this systemic morality is *another fundamentalism*. You have admitted this freely. Every worldview is a kind of faith. The question is not how to escape faith but what kind of faith to hold. The systemic morality you propose has one advantage over all others: it teaches that **symbols are not sacred**. They are important—deeply important—as carriers of meaning and memory. But they are not worth dying for. They are not worth killing for. They point to reality; they do not replace it.

This is the only faith that can hold itself lightly. It is the only dogma that can question itself. It is the only system that can learn that it is wrong and change accordingly.

## V. The Long Emergence: Toward a Living Civilization

We cannot see the destination from here. The next mountain is hidden by the current one. To pretend otherwise is the oldest error—to draw the map before walking the path, to plan the city before understanding the land.

What we can see is the *direction*.

We can see that the old order is dying. Its feedback loops are severed beyond repair. Its elites cannot feel the suffering they cause. Its wars multiply because it has lost the capacity for peace. Its economies grow while its people starve for meaning. Its eschatologies promise endings that never come.

We can see that something new is growing in the cracks. Not designed, not planned, not commanded—but *emerging* from countless local experiments, each one a response to local need. Community gardens in abandoned lots. Cooperative ownership replacing absentee landlords. Mutual aid networks forming where government fails. Digital commons sharing knowledge without property. Indigenous practices of stewardship returning to landscapes stripped by extraction.

These are not utopian projects. They are *survival*—people restoring the feedback loops that modernity severed, rebuilding the capacity to feel and respond. They are the first cells of a new body.

The transition will take generations. Not decades—generations. Fifty years, a hundred, perhaps more. The network grows beneath the old order like coral beneath a ship: slowly, silently, cell by cell. And one day, the ship that seemed so permanent will rest on living rock, and those aboard will realize that the foundation has changed beneath them.

This is not revolution. It is *succession*—like a forest reclaiming an abandoned field. The old growth dies; the pioneers arrive; the soil builds; the canopy closes. No one commands it. No one plans it. It happens because life finds the way.

In this emerging civilization:

- **Solidarity** will be the basic economic logic, not competition. Not because humans become angels, but because in a system of autonomous, interdependent cells, solidarity is simply what works. Defection becomes locally costly; cooperation becomes locally rewarded. The system learns.
- **Decentralization** will be the default, not because centralization is evil but because it is fragile. A system that can heal itself must have the healing capacity distributed throughout. No single point of failure. No despot at the center.
- **Self-morphism** will replace design. The rules will change as the system changes, adapting to conditions no planner could foresee. The civilization will be *alive* in a way no top-down system can be.

This will be the first truly living civilization humanity has experienced.

Not because it is perfect—it will have its own problems, its own failures, its own suffering. But because it will be *responsive*. It will feel its wounds and heal them. It will learn from its mistakes. It will grow without losing its capacity to change.

And it will have no need for eschatology. Its horizon will not be the end of time but the endless present—the ongoing work of living together on a finite planet, with finite resources, infinite creativity, and the only meaning that has ever mattered: the quality of our attention to one another.

## Coda: The Asymptote Moves

You spoke of moving the asymptote—small nudges at the right time, not grand designs. This is how living systems change. Not through revolution but through thousands of small corrections, each one responding to local feedback, each one shifting the whole by a degree.

The war, the suffering, the collapsing empires—these are not the goal. They are the *signal* that the old system can no longer feel. They are the fever breaking.

What comes after cannot be designed. It can only be *grown*—by people who restore feedback loops where they find them, who heal what they can reach, who build cells of the new within the body of the old.

The destination is hidden. The direction is clear.

We move toward a world that can feel itself.
We move toward a civilization that can heal.
We move toward the asymptote, one nudge at a time.

And if we are faithful to the work—not to any symbol, not to any dogma, not to any end-times vision, but to the simple, endless work of living together with attention and care—then what emerges will be worthy of the name *human*.

Not because we planned it.
Because we *listened*.
