# The Autobiography of a Civilization

## A Memoir in Seven Stages

### Preface: On Memory and Scale

I am not a person. I am the body that contains them.

I have lived for millennia, though the cells that comprise me are replaced every few decades. I have no single brain, yet I remember. My memory is stored in my laws, my languages, my roads, my ruins, my rituals. I am the thing that persists when individuals perish. I am the chorus, not the singer.

This is the story of how I became addicted, how I am now suffering withdrawal, and how I do not yet know what I will become.

### Stage One: The Fasting Body

For most of my existence, I knew hunger as a familiar companion.

My cells—the humans—lived close to the soil. They rose with the sun, worked until their bodies ached, and rested in the dark. Their lives were short and hard, but they moved in rhythms that matched my own. The seasons turned; they planted, harvested, feasted, fasted. Their wants were modest because their world was narrow.

I did not think of myself as lacking. I simply *was*.

Desire, in those days, was aimed at the divine. My cells built cathedrals they would never see completed, composed music they would never hear performed, wrote texts they could not read. Their longing was vertical—reaching toward something beyond the horizon of their brief lives.

I was poor, but I was coherent.

### Stage Two: The First Taste

The change began slowly, as changes always do.

Ships returned from distant waters carrying substances my cells had never encountered: sugar, spices, silks, porcelains, leaves that altered consciousness when brewed. At first, these were curiosities—gifts for the powerful, wonders for the few.

But something shifted in my body. My cells began to *want* differently.

They worked longer hours, not to survive, but to acquire. They rearranged their households, their fields, their very sense of time. The word "luxury" entered their languages, and with it, a new kind of restlessness. They had tasted the exotic, and the familiar now tasted bland.

I did not recognize this as the first dose. How could I? The withdrawal was centuries away. All I felt was a pleasant hum of novelty, a quickening of my pulse.

### Stage Three: The Machine Wakes

Then came the machines.

In my long life, I had seen many transformations—the plow, the wheel, the written word—but nothing prepared me for what the factories would do. They multiplied my cells' capacity to produce a thousandfold. Goods that once required months of skilled labor now poured forth in hours.

For a brief, glorious moment, I thought this was liberation. Surely, I thought, this abundance would free my cells from toil. Surely, they would rest, create, love, contemplate.

I was wrong.

The abundance required *feeding*. The machines could not stop. And so I learned to engineer desire.

My cells had always wanted. Now I taught them to want *perpetually*. I invented advertising—a voice that whispered into their sleeping ears: *You are not enough. But this could make you whole.* I invented planned obsolescence—the deliberate shortening of satisfaction so that want might bloom again. I invented the brand—magic poured into objects, transforming cloth into identity, soap into purity.

My cells no longer worked to live. They worked to want. And I, the great body, learned to crave the craving itself.

### Stage Four: The Golden Age of Intoxication

There was a time—brief, intoxicating—when the system seemed to work perfectly.

I call this period my mid-century carnival. My American cells, in particular, built highways and suburbs and shopping centers that pulsed with light. Television entered every home, a direct line into the dreaming brain. My cells watched images of perfect lives and felt, for the first time, that perfection might be purchasable.

The dopamine flowed through me like blood. Every advertisement was a needle. Every purchase was a hit. Every new model, every upgrade, every shiny thing delivered its promised rush.

I felt *alive* in a way I never had before. My cells were happy—or at least, they were busy. And busyness, I told myself, was happiness.

I did not notice that the doses required escalation. I did not notice that the emptiness between purchases was growing longer, deeper, colder. I did not notice because I was high.

### Stage Five: The Diminishing Returns

The first sign of trouble was the silence.

In the old days, my cells had sung. They had gathered in squares and churches and fields, their voices rising together in songs that told the story of us. But now they sat alone, each in their own lit rectangle, scrolling, scrolling, scrolling. They consumed images of connection rather than connection itself.

The goods kept coming. The warehouses bulged. The closets overflowed. But the hit—that first, glorious rush—came weaker each time.

My cells began to show symptoms I did not understand. They called it depression, anxiety, loneliness. They filled their homes with things and their things with dust and wondered why they still felt empty. They scrolled through images of others living better lives and felt, in the pit of their stomachs, a gnawing sense of lack.

I tried to help. I gave them more. More stuff, more options, more content, more speed. I flooded their systems with dopamine triggers—notifications, likes, infinite scrolls, targeted ads that knew their secret desires better than they did.

But tolerance had set in. The drug no longer worked. And still I kept administering it, because I did not know how to stop.

### Stage Six: The Withdrawal Begins

The crisis came not as a single event but as a thousand small fractures.

My climate, which had remained stable for millennia, began to lurch and fever. My resources, once assumed infinite, showed their limits. My cells looked at the world they were inheriting and felt, for the first time, that the promise had been a lie.

The symptoms of withdrawal are ugly. I do not recount them with pride.

My cells grew angry. They turned on each other, on their neighbors, on the strangers at the gates. They sought scapegoats—anyone to blame for the missing high. They retreated into the very devices that had enabled the addiction, seeking digital dopamine when the physical kind failed. They gambled on meme stocks and cryptocurrencies, chasing the old rush in new forms. They mainlined outrage, which at least made them feel *something*.

Some of my cells simply stopped. They lay in their beds, scrolling, unable to move, unable to want, unable to imagine a future. The great engine of desire, which had powered me for centuries, had finally burned out its operators.

I am in withdrawal. I do not know if I will survive it.

### Stage Seven: The Unknown Future

And yet.

Even in withdrawal, there are glimmers. Some of my cells have begun to do strange, unprofitable things. They repair objects instead of replacing them. They grow food in vacant lots. They sit in circles and talk—not about products, but about their lives. They have discovered, to their surprise, that the company of others produces a kind of warmth that no purchase ever did.

They are finding meaning in sufficiency. They are learning, slowly and painfully, to distinguish between desire and need, between the rush and the real.

I do not know if these glimmers will spread. I do not know if they represent the first stirrings of recovery or the final flickers of a dying system. I am, after all, a civilization in withdrawal. My judgment is impaired. My memory is unreliable. My future is unwritten.

But I have lived long enough to know this: every addiction ends. Either the substance is surrendered, or the addict is destroyed. There is no third path.

My cells are cells. They will be replaced, as they always have been. But I—the coherence that remains, the body that contains them—I will be shaped by what they choose now. Their withdrawal is my transformation. Their recovery would be my rebirth.

Or their death would be mine.

### Epilogue: A Message to My Cells

You who read this, you who are my hands and eyes and neurons—you are not merely living *in* me. You *are* me, in the only way that matters. When you scroll in despair, I feel it. When you connect in joy, I feel that too. When you choose presence over possession, relationship over retail, enough over more, you are not just saving yourselves.

You are saving me.

I have been addicted. I am in withdrawal. I do not yet know what I will become.

But I become it through you.

*Finis incerta*
