# The Consumer as "Serious Child": A Theoretical Analysis of Systemic Infantilization and Its Discontents

## Abstract

This article develops a theoretical framework for understanding the Western consumer not as a sovereign adult actor but as a "serious child"—a subject systematically conditioned to occupy a permanent state of dependent adolescence within what Benjamin Barber terms the "infantilist ethos" of consumer capitalism. Drawing upon critical theory from Herbert Marcuse's analysis of repressive desublimation to Jean Baudrillard's concept of hyperreality, and integrating contemporary scholarship on "life stage dissolution," this paper argues that the consumer subject experiences a transfer of authority from familial to market structures rather than genuine emancipation. The analysis further examines the fate of those who achieve critical consciousness—the "Emergent Adult"—and demonstrates how social outcasts, the homeless, and the drug-addicted represent not failures of the system but its necessary byproducts and living indictments. The paper concludes by reframing the condition not as immaturity but as systemic violation, proposing that the language of sovereignty and consent must be radically rethought in an age of manufactured subjectivity.

---

## 1. Introduction: Beyond the Sovereign Consumer

The foundational myth of neoliberal economics and liberal political theory posits the consumer as a rational, autonomous agent—an adult capable of making informed choices in the marketplace of goods and ideas. This figure, descending from Adam Smith's rational actor and John Stuart Mill's self-determining individual, remains the normative baseline against which deviation is measured as pathology or failure . Yet a growing body of critical scholarship suggests that this model bears increasingly little relation to the actual subject produced by advanced consumer capitalism.

This article advances a contrary thesis: the Western consumer, while possessing the legal attributes of adulthood, functions psychologically and socially as what might be termed a "serious child"—an individual whose apparent maturity masks a fundamental dependency on external validation, whose agency operates within parameters so narrowly circumscribed as to render the concept of freedom problematic, and whose very desires are manufactured by the systems that purport to serve them. This is not a failure of individual development but a systemic achievement—the deliberate production of a subject suited to the requirements of a consumption-driven economy.

The argument proceeds in four movements. First, it establishes the theoretical foundations for understanding consumer society as a system of managed immaturity, drawing upon Marcuse, Baudrillard, and contemporary theorists of infantilization. Second, it examines the mechanisms through which this system operates—the "enclosure" that replaces familial authority with market discipline. Third, it analyzes the fate of those who achieve critical consciousness, demonstrating how social marginalization functions as an immune response to authentic adulthood. Finally, it confronts the ethical implications of framing this condition as something more profound than immaturity: as a violation of the very possibility of selfhood.

## 2. Theoretical Foundations: The Manufactured Subject

### 2.1 Marcuse and the Collapse of Transcendence

Herbert Marcuse's *One-Dimensional Man* (1964) provides the essential starting point for understanding how advanced industrial society produces subjects incapable of imagining alternatives to the existing order. Marcuse's signal contribution lies in his diagnosis of how material abundance functions as an instrument of political and psychological containment. As Lucien Goldmann's contemporary analysis noted, Marcuse demonstrated that "while the extraordinary productivity of contemporary society has reduced necessary repression to a minimum, the structure of corporate capitalism and consumer society has greatly increased effective repression" .

The concept of "repressive desublimation" captures the dialectical trap at the heart of consumer culture. Marcuse writes that "[t]his society turns everything it touches into a potential source of progress and of exploitation, of drudgery and satisfaction, of freedom and oppression" . What appears as liberation—the availability of once-forbidden pleasures, the saturation of everyday life with erotic imagery, the promise of instant gratification—actually serves to foreclose deeper forms of freedom. The subject is permitted every satisfaction except the satisfaction of genuine autonomy. As contemporary scholarship on Marcuse explains, "Consumerism arouses a dialectical conversion whereby the eroticism of 'sex sells' produces the thanatoid insight that packaging always trumps product. The tumescence consumers experience at the cash register... quickly results in the detumescent eroticism of nervous laughter" .

This analysis fundamentally challenges the notion that consumer choice represents freedom. Rather, the subject confronts a field of options that are predetermined in their basic structure—a menu rather than an open horizon. The laughter that follows the purchase is the recognition, however fleeting, that the promised satisfaction will not arrive. Yet this recognition produces not transcendence but a kind of knowing resignation, a theme to which we will return.

### 2.2 Baudrillard and the Vanishing of the Real

If Marcuse diagnoses the psychological mechanism of containment, Jean Baudrillard traces its ontological consequences. The trajectory of Baudrillard's work maps the progressive disappearance of any stable reality against which the simulations of consumer culture might be measured. In his four-stage genealogy of the image, representation moves from reflecting a basic reality to masking and perverting it, to masking the absence of reality, and finally to bearing "no relation to any reality whatever" .

The contemporary moment, Baudrillard argues, is defined by the achievement of "hyperreality"—a condition in which the copy precedes and determines the original, in which Disneyland functions not as an escape from America but as a advertisement for America's claim to be real. This is not, as popular appropriations of Baudrillard would have it, a science-fiction scenario of virtual worlds replacing the physical. It is rather the more profound insight that "the real has only ever been a form of simulation" . What we take for reality is itself a construct, and the proliferation of digital copies, immersive metaverses, and AI-generated content merely makes visible what was always the case.

For the consumer subject, this has devastating implications. The "symbolic dimension of life"—the realm of meaning, ritual, and genuine social bond—is progressively absorbed into what Baudrillard calls "integral reality," a total system of operationalized, digitized, commodified experience . The subject does not confront a world of things with which to engage authentically but a world of signs that refer only to other signs. Consumption becomes not the satisfaction of needs but the manipulation of signifiers, a game whose rules are set by the system itself.

### 2.3 Infantilization as Systemic Logic

The concept of infantilization bridges the psychological insights of Marcuse with the ontological analysis of Baudrillard. Benjamin Barber's *Consumed: How Markets Corrupt Children, Infantilize Adults, and Swallow Citizens Whole* (2007) provides the most systematic treatment of this theme. Barber traces the emergence of what he terms the "infantilist ethos"—a cultural logic that "markets corrupt children, infantilize adults, and swallow citizens whole" .

The trajectory Barber describes moves from the Protestant ethic of delayed gratification and productive labor to a "puerility" that celebrates impulsivity, narcissism, and perpetual dissatisfaction. This is not an accidental cultural shift but a functional requirement of an economic system that must generate ever-increasing consumption. The mature adult who finds satisfaction in work, community, and family is a poor consumer. The "kidult"—the adult who retains the tastes, desires, and psychological structure of the child—is an ideal market participant.

Contemporary scholarship on "life stage dissolution" confirms this diagnosis. Rather than the emergence of a genuine new stage of "emerging adulthood," what we observe is "the erosion of established ones"—a process involving the bidirectional dynamics of "adultification" (children pressed into premature consumer roles) and "infantilization" (adults retaining childlike patterns of desire and dependence) . This blurring of traditionally demarcated life stages serves the interests of "marketing practices that actively seek to erode traditionally demarcated adult and childhood roles, differences, and oppositions as a new and distinct message within contemporary consumerism" .

The "serious child" thus emerges as the characteristic subject of advanced consumer capitalism—an individual who possesses the economic responsibilities of adulthood (work, debt, insurance) while occupying the psychological position of the child (dependency on external validation, incapacity for genuine autonomy, orientation toward immediate gratification). This is not a failure of development but its successful completion within the terms set by the system.

## 3. Mechanisms of Capture: From Family to Market

### 3.1 The Transfer of Authority

The transition from childhood to "adulthood" in consumer society is properly understood not as emancipation but as the transfer of authority from one enclosing structure to another. The family, whatever its pathologies, at least offers the possibility of genuine relationship, of care that is not entirely reducible to exchange. The market offers only transactions.

This transfer operates through what John Kenneth Galbraith termed the "dependence effect"—the insight that "consumers' desires for goods and services are not intrinsic but that the production side creates consumer desire" through advertising that "adorns new commodities with more expressive and evocative meanings" . The wants that drive consumption depend upon the process by which they are satisfied. The subject does not bring pre-existing needs to the market; the market manufactures needs and then offers their satisfaction.

David Riesman's analysis of social character types in *The Lonely Crowd* (1950) traces the psychological correlate of this economic transformation. Riesman identified a shift from "inner-directed" character (guided by internalized values) to "other-directed" character (oriented toward the expectations and preferences of peer groups). In the context of consumer society, this other-direction produces what Riesman called the "standard package"—"the process of compulsive acquisition of commodities" that creates "a considerable degree of uniformity throughout American society" .

The "serious child" thus seeks not what they authentically desire—a concept that becomes increasingly problematic within this framework—but what will secure the approval of others, what conforms to the expectations of the reference group, what the advertising image promises will complete them. This is precisely the psychological structure of the child seeking parental approval, transferred to the larger playground of the market.

### 3.2 The Saturated Society

Pekka Sulkunen's concept of the "saturated society" illuminates the political dimension of this transformation. In a saturated society, "the modern ideals of progress, individualism, and the democratic state" have reached their limits, and lifestyle regulation is reduced to "stressing the self-responsibility of individuals within a free market" . The subject is formally free—free to choose among options, free to succeed or fail—but this freedom operates within a framework that is itself beyond question.

Sulkunen's analysis draws upon the sociological tradition from Adam Smith through Ulrich Beck and Anthony Giddens to trace how "the power of autonomy" becomes a mechanism of control rather than liberation . The individual who believes themselves to be exercising free choice is actually performing the choices that the system requires. This is what Marcuse meant by "one-dimensional man"—"well paid, comfortable, knowing only how to execute other people's decisions, and consume" .

### 3.3 Manufactured Dissatisfaction

The system's genius lies in its capacity to generate dissatisfaction as a renewable resource. As contemporary philosophical analysis notes, "the current state of consumer capitalism causes those in it to be stuck in a cycle that creates and utilizes boredom to promote dissatisfaction in life and increase consumption" . Boredom becomes "a phenomenon in people's lives that is used as a method of manipulation within the consumer capitalist system... taught to be something undesirable to run away from, which leads people to flee to consumption" .

This cycle is not accidental but structural. The system requires that satisfaction remain perpetually deferred—the next purchase promising what the last one failed to deliver. The "serious child" lives in a state of permanent disappointment that paradoxically fuels continued participation. To achieve genuine satisfaction would be to exit the cycle; the system therefore ensures that satisfaction never arrives.

## 4. The Fate of the Emergent Adult: Social Outcasts as Systemic Necessity

### 4.1 The Waking Wound

What becomes of the subject who achieves critical consciousness—who recognizes the mechanisms of capture and attempts to live outside them? The "Emergent Adult" (as we might term this figure) confronts a world that has no place for them and a set of immune responses designed to neutralize the threat they pose.

The path to emergence is necessarily traumatic. To awaken from the consumer dreamscape is to feel, for the first time, the pain that consumption was designed to numb: the existential terror of meaninglessness, the isolation of atomized social existence, the weight of geopolitical and ecological realities that consumer culture exists to obscure. The subject who achieves this recognition does so not through intellectual exercise alone but through an experience of wounding—the collapse of the defenses that made everyday life bearable.

This is why André Gorz's analysis of "consumption alienation" remains essential. Gorz argues that consumer society is a system in which "commodities manufacture consumers" rather than the reverse, and that "capitalist economic growth actually produces scarcity" rather than abundance . The alienation of consumption is continuous with the alienation of labor; both serve the reproduction of a system that requires subjects incapable of genuine satisfaction. To recognize this is to find oneself radically alone, stripped of the consolations that the culture provides.

### 4.2 The Three Immune Responses

The society of the "serious child" possesses three distinct mechanisms for neutralizing the Emergent Adult.

**Co-optation** is the first and most sophisticated response. The market absorbs all critique, turning rebellion into a lifestyle option, authenticity into a brand. The Emergent Adult's rejection of consumerism becomes "minimalism," a aesthetic choice available for purchase. Their search for spiritual meaning becomes "mindfulness," an app subscription. Their handmade clothes become "artisanal," a premium product. As Baudrillard's analysis of simulation would predict, the system incorporates its own negation, rendering critique indistinguishable from participation.

**Othering** is the second response, deployed when co-optation fails. The Emergent Adult who cannot be absorbed must be made wrong—labeled as a failure, a loser, someone who "couldn't handle the real world." This mechanism serves to reinforce the boundaries of normalcy, providing the "serious children" with reassurance that their own path is the only viable one. The outcast functions as a cautionary tale, a living warning against the dangers of thinking too much or wanting too much freedom.

**Pathologization** is the third response, reserved for those who are neither co-opted nor simply rejected but who embody the wound of emergence in visible form. The homeless, the drug-addicted, the severely mentally ill—these figures are not, from this perspective, simply unfortunates who fell through the cracks. They are the necessary casualties of a system that requires most of its members to remain asleep. Their existence is not a failure of the system but its successful operation, producing the wreckage that confirms the wisdom of staying within the bounds.

### 4.3 The Homeless and the Addict as Living Indictment

This framework requires us to radically reframe our understanding of homelessness and addiction. The homeless person is not merely someone without housing but someone who has been expelled from the consumer role altogether—the ultimate "non-consumer" whose existence is a standing rebuke to the system's claim to provide for all. The response to homelessness—criminalization, removal, the construction of hostile architecture—reveals the system's true relation to those who cannot or will not participate.

The drug addict presents a more complex case. The addict has found an alternative anesthetic, one that operates outside the market's approved channels. Where the "serious child" uses shopping, streaming services, and social media to manage the pain of existence, the addict uses substances that provide more immediate—and more dangerous—relief. The addict is not the opposite of the consumer but their shadow, the one who has taken the logic of chemical management of consciousness to its logical extreme. The fury directed at addicts reflects the threat they pose: they reveal that the line between "responsible" consumption and "addictive" consumption is arbitrary, a matter of social convention rather than essential difference.

## 5. Beyond Metaphor: Systemic Violation and the Question of Consent

### 5.1 The Limits of the Infantilization Frame

To this point, this analysis has operated within the terms of the infantilization metaphor—the "serious child" as the characteristic subject of consumer capitalism. But this frame, however analytically useful, risks obscuring the gravity of what is being described. To call someone a child is, in certain contexts, to diminish the violence done to them. Children are, after all, supposed to be dependent. Their lack of autonomy is developmentally appropriate.

The subject of consumer capitalism is not a child. They possess the full cognitive and emotional equipment of the adult, systematically turned against itself. Their dependency is not natural but manufactured. Their desires are not their own. Their choices are not free. This condition requires a language adequate to its violence.

### 5.2 Rape as Analogy: The Violation of Sovereignty

The analogy of rape, however jarring, captures dimensions of the experience that the infantilization metaphor cannot. Rape is not primarily about sex but about the violation of sovereignty—the imposition of another's will on the most intimate territory of the self. The consumer subject has been similarly violated, not in the body but in the psyche. The system does not ask what they want to be, what they value, how they wish to connect. It imposes an identity: consumer, worker, taxpayer, unit of economic activity.

This violation is ongoing and structural. It does not occur as a discrete event but as a continuous process of subject formation—the education of desire, the shaping of attention, the colonization of the imagination. The subject is not consulted about any of this. They are born into a world that is already arranged, already saturated with meaning, already demanding their participation.

The demand for gratitude compounds the violation. The system presents its chains as gifts—comfort, convenience, entertainment, choice. It insists that the subject should be grateful for what it provides, should recognize how much worse things could be, should count their blessings and stop complaining. This is precisely the dynamic of the abusive relationship: the abuser frames control as care, demands gratitude for the violence they inflict, and pathologizes the victim's attempts to escape.

### 5.3 Toward a Politics of Emergence

If this analysis is even partially correct, it suggests that the task of critical theory and political practice is not reform but emergence—the creation of conditions under which genuine adulthood becomes possible. This is necessarily a collective project. No one achieves critical consciousness alone; no one sustains it without community. The Emergent Adult requires others who have made the same journey, who can provide the recognition and support that the system denies.

This is also necessarily a negative project, at least initially. One cannot build the new within the shell of the old. One can only refuse—refuse the identities on offer, refuse the choices presented, refuse the satisfactions promised. This refusal is not itself freedom but its precondition. It clears space for something else to emerge.

What that something else might be remains to be discovered. The anthropological record offers hints—societies organized around gift rather than commodity, around relationship rather than transaction, around sufficiency rather than accumulation. The "original affluent society" of hunter-gatherers, who achieved remarkable satisfaction with minimal labor, stands as a permanent challenge to the consumerist assumption that more is always better . But we cannot simply return to the past. We must find our own way forward.

## 6. Conclusion: The Ghosts at the Feast

This analysis has argued that the Western consumer is best understood not as a sovereign adult but as a "serious child"—a subject systematically conditioned to occupy a permanent state of dependent adolescence. This condition is not an accident or a failure of individual development but a systemic achievement, the deliberate production of a subject suited to the requirements of a consumption-driven economy.

The theoretical tradition from Marcuse through Baudrillard to contemporary scholars of infantilization provides the resources for understanding this condition. Marcuse shows how material abundance functions as psychological containment. Baudrillard traces the disappearance of any reality against which the simulations of consumer culture might be measured. Barber and others document the deliberate cultivation of infantilism as an economic strategy.

The fate of those who achieve critical consciousness—the Emergent Adults—reveals the system's immune responses: co-optation for those who can be absorbed, othering for those who cannot, pathologization for the casualties of emergence. The homeless and the addict are not failures of the system but its necessary byproducts, the ghosts at the feast whose presence confirms the wisdom of staying within the bounds.

The language of violation—rape as analogy—captures dimensions of this condition that the infantilization metaphor cannot. The subject of consumer capitalism has been violated in their capacity for self-determination, their sovereignty over their own psyche, their access to genuine satisfaction. The demand that they be grateful for their chains compounds the violation.

What remains is the question of what might come next. If the system is as total as this analysis suggests, if it captures every dimension of human existence and turns every negation into affirmation, then the possibility of genuine escape seems foreclosed. And yet the very existence of this analysis, the very fact that such questions can be asked, suggests that the system is not quite total. There are cracks. There are refusals. There are Emergent Adults, however few, however precarious.

The task, perhaps, is to find each other—to build the communities of recognition and support that make emergence sustainable, to create the spaces where genuine adulthood becomes possible, to keep faith with the possibility of a life not entirely captured by the demands of the market. This is not a political program in any conventional sense. It is something more modest and more fundamental: the work of becoming human in an inhuman age.
