# The Game

## A Book for People Who Feel Stupid (But Aren't)

### (A Note Before We Start)

You are not stupid.

Stupid people don't worry about being stupid. They're too busy being confident and wrong.

You're just swimming in water that was made to be confusing. The people running the game *want* you to feel stupid. It's easier to take your money when you don't understand how money works.

So let's fix that. Simple words. Short sentences. No shame.

## PART ONE: WHAT IS THIS THING?

### Chapter 1: The Village

Imagine a village.

Not on TV. Not in a movie. Just a real village with real people.

There's a baker. He makes bread. He's good at it.
There's a farmer. She grows vegetables. She's good at it.
There's a hunter. He brings meat. He's good at it.
There's a healer. She knows which plants fix fevers. She's good at it.
There's a storyteller. He remembers the past and makes people laugh. He's... okay, he's not great at anything except talking, but people like him.

In the beginning, they trade.

The baker gives bread to the farmer. The farmer gives vegetables to the baker. Simple.

But soon they have a problem.

The hunter wants bread. But the baker doesn't need meat today. He needs his roof fixed. The roof fixer (let's call her the builder) doesn't need bread. She needs vegetables. The farmer has vegetables but doesn't need anything right now because her garden is full and she's tired of trading.

So everyone is stuck.

This is called the **double coincidence of wants**. It's a fancy way of saying: trading is hard when you have to find someone who has what you want *and* wants what you have at the exact same moment.

You know this feeling. You've got something to offer. Someone else has something you need. But you can't make the match.

The village needs a solution.

### Chapter 2: The Shells

Someone gets an idea.

"Let's all agree on one thing," they say. "One thing that everyone will accept. Then you can trade your bread for that thing, and later trade that thing for vegetables, even if the vegetable person doesn't want bread."

Genius.

So they pick something. Shells. Pretty shells from the beach. Everyone agrees: these shells are worth something. Not because the shells themselves are useful—you can't eat them, can't wear them, can't build with them. But because *everyone agrees* they're useful for trading.

Now the baker can sell bread for shells. The farmer can sell vegetables for shells. The hunter can sell meat for shells. And with those shells, they can buy anything from anyone, anytime.

The shells are **money**.

That's all money is. A thing everyone agrees to use for trading.

It doesn't have to be shells. It can be:
- Gold (shiny, rare, doesn't rot)
- Silver (also shiny)
- Cows (less good—they die, need feeding, take up space)
- Cigarettes (worked in prisons and wars)
- Seashells (worked in many places)
- Rocks with holes in them (worked on one island so hard they had to stop because people kept chopping down trees to make more rocks—true story)

Money is just a **tool for trading**. Nothing more magical than that.

### Chapter 3: The Promise

But shells are heavy. And you can't carry many. And someone might steal them.

So someone else gets an idea.

"I'll keep everyone's shells in my big safe house," they say. "And I'll give you a piece of paper that says: 'Whoever brings this paper gets 10 shells from my safe.'"

Now people trade the paper instead of the shells. The paper is lighter. Safer. Easier.

This is the next step. The paper isn't valuable by itself. It's valuable because it's a **promise**. A promise that you can trade it for real shells whenever you want.

This is called **representative money**. The paper *represents* the real thing somewhere else.

Later, people get even lazier. They stop keeping shells in the safe. They just trust that the paper is worth something because... well, because everyone says it is. And because the person giving the paper (now it's the government) says it's worth something.

This is called **fiat money**. From the Latin word for "let it be done." As in: "Let this paper be money because we say so."

That's what dollars are. And euros. And yen. Just paper (or numbers on screens) that we all agree to pretend are worth things.

That's it. That's the big secret. Money is a shared hallucination. A dream we all agree to have together.

## PART TWO: THE GAME BOARD

### Chapter 4: The Factory

Now let's speed up time. Thousands of years. The village is gone. Now we have cities. Factories. Computers. Complex stuff.

But the basic game is the same.

Some people own things. These people are called **capitalists**. The word comes from "capital," which just means "stuff you use to make more stuff." A sewing machine is capital. A tractor is capital. A factory is capital. A computer is capital.

If you own capital, you can do two things:
1. Use it yourself to make things
2. Let other people use it, and charge them

Most people don't own much capital. They own their hands, their brains, their time. That's it. These people are called **workers**.

Workers go to the people who own capital and say: "Let me use your factory for eight hours. I'll give you my time and energy. You give me money."

This is called a **job**.

The capitalist says: "Great. I'll pay you $20 per hour. You'll make things that I sell for $100 per hour of your work. The difference—$80—is mine, because I own the factory."

That $80 difference is called **profit**.

This is not evil. This is just how the game works. The capitalist took the risk of building the factory. The capitalist pays for the machines, the lights, the taxes. If the business fails, the capitalist loses everything. The worker just finds another job.

But here's the thing that matters:

**Over time, people who own capital get richer faster than people who work.**

Why? Because the worker gets paid once for their time. That's it. The capitalist gets paid over and over from the thing they own.

Example:
- Maria the baker works 8 hours, makes 100 loaves, gets paid $200. She goes home. Tomorrow she starts over.
- Carlos the bakery owner owns the oven, the mixer, the building. He pays Maria $200. He sells the bread for $500. He keeps $300. He didn't bake anything. He just *owned* things.

This is the basic pattern of vanilla capitalism. It's been this way for a long time.

### Chapter 5: The Bank

Remember the safe house with the shells? It got bigger. Much bigger.

Now it's called a **bank**.

Banks do three simple things:

**First**, they hold your money safely. You put in $100. You take out $100 later. Sometimes they even pay you a little extra for leaving it with them. That extra is called **interest**. It's like rent on your money.

**Second**, they lend money to people who need it. Someone wants to start a bakery. They need $50,000 for an oven and a mixer. The bank says: "We'll lend you the money. But you have to pay back $60,000 over ten years." That extra $10,000 is also interest. It's rent on their money.

**Third—and this is the weird part—they lend out money they don't actually have.**

You put $100 in the bank. The bank keeps $10 in its vault (this is called the **reserve**). The other $90, they lend to someone else.

That someone else spends the $90 at a store. The store puts that $90 in their bank account. That bank keeps $9 and lends out $81.

And on and on.

Suddenly, your original $100 has become $1,000 of loans and spending. The money *multiplied*.

This is called **fractional reserve banking**. It sounds complicated. It's not. It's just: banks lend out most of the money people give them, and that money becomes new money somewhere else.

This is how the money supply grows. Not by printing more dollars. By lending the same dollars over and over.

Is this a trick? Kind of. But it works as long as everyone trusts it. If everyone shows up at once demanding their money back—that's called a **bank run**. Then the bank fails, because they lent out most of it. That's what happened in the Great Depression. That's what happens in every financial crisis.

### Chapter 6: The Stock

Now things get really interesting.

A company gets big. Really big. Too big for one person to own.

So they split it into pieces. Tiny pieces. And they sell those pieces to anyone who wants to buy.

Each piece is called a **share** or a **stock**.

If you own one share of Apple, you own a tiny, tiny piece of Apple. Like owning one brick of a giant castle.

Why would you buy a share? Two reasons:

**First**, sometimes the company shares its profits with owners. That's called a **dividend**. If Apple makes a billion dollars, they might send some of that to everyone who owns shares. A little check in the mail.

**Second**, you hope the share becomes more valuable than what you paid. If you buy a share for $10 and later someone will pay $20 for it, you made money without doing anything.

This is called **investing**.

The place where people buy and sell these tiny pieces is called the **stock market**.

Here's the trick: the price of a share isn't really about what the company is worth today. It's about what people *think* it will be worth tomorrow. Next year. In ten years.

So the stock market is really a giant machine for guessing the future. Millions of people guessing. Buying and selling based on their guesses. And the guesses affect what actually happens.

It's weird. It's a little crazy. But it's how the game works.

## PART THREE: THE RULES (THAT NOBODY TELLS YOU)

### Chapter 7: Growth Must Happen

Here's the first secret rule:

**The whole system has to grow. Forever.**

Not because growth is nice. Because if growth stops, the system breaks.

Think about it:

Companies borrow money from banks. They have to pay it back with interest. That means they need to make *more* money than they started with.

People invest in stocks. They expect the stocks to be worth *more* next year than this year.

Workers want raises. They want to buy *more* next year than this year.

The whole thing is built on "more." Always more. Next year bigger than this year.

This is called **growth**.

And here's the problem:

The planet is not growing. There's only so much land. Only so much water. Only so much wood and metal and clean air.

So the system has to keep finding new ways to grow even when the physical stuff runs out.

Sometimes this is good—better phones, better medicine, better ways of doing things.

Sometimes this is fake—making things that break quickly so you have to buy new ones (this is called **planned obsolescence**). Creating problems so they can sell you solutions. Convincing you that you need things you don't actually need.

And sometimes this is destructive—cutting down forests, polluting rivers, burning coal, because the growth must happen and the costs can be pushed onto someone else (usually poor people, usually future people).

The system doesn't care. The system just needs to grow.

### Chapter 8: Debt Is a Chain

Here's the second secret rule:

**Debt is how the system controls you.**

Not in a conspiracy way. In a mechanical way.

Think about your life:

You need a place to live. Houses cost more money than you have. So you borrow money—a **mortgage**. Now you have to work for 30 years to pay it back. If you stop working, you lose the house.

You need to learn things to get a good job. School costs money. So you borrow money—**student loans**. Now you have to work to pay that back too.

You need a car to get to work. Car loan.

You need things for your house. Credit card.

By the time you're 30, you owe money to banks, to the government, to companies you've never met. You can't stop working. You can't say "I'm done with this game." Because if you stop, the people you owe money to can take your stuff. Your house. Your car. Even some of your future money (they'll take it from your paycheck before you see it).

This is not an accident. The system is designed this way.

Not because some evil people in a room planned it. But because the people who benefit from it kept making rules that helped themselves, and over time, the rules became this.

Debt is a chain. But it's a golden chain. It gives you things now that you couldn't afford otherwise. A house. An education. A car. A life.

The question is whether the chain is worth the things it buys.

### Chapter 9: Money Makes Money

Here's the third secret rule:

**If you have money, it's easy to make more money. If you don't have money, it's hard to get any.**

This is the big one. The one they don't teach in school.

Remember Maria and Carlos? The worker and the owner?

Carlos has money. He can:
- Buy more ovens, hire more Marias
- Invest in the stock market
- Buy rental properties
- Lend money and charge interest
- Pay people to figure out clever ways to make even more money

Every dollar he has is working for him, making more dollars, even while he sleeps.

Maria has her hands. She works, she gets paid, she spends almost everything on rent and food and bills. At the end of the month, maybe she saves $20. Her money is not working for her. She's working for her money.

Over time, the gap grows.

Carlos gets richer without doing anything. Maria works harder and harder just to stay in place.

This is called the **wealth gap**. It's been growing for 50 years. In the US, the richest 1% now have more wealth than the bottom 90% combined.

That's not an opinion. That's math.

And here's the cruelest part:

When people like Maria complain, they're told they're lazy. Or stupid. Or they should have chosen better parents.

But it's not about laziness. It's about the rules. The rules were written by people like Carlos. Of course the rules help people like Carlos.

## PART FOUR: WHAT THEY DON'T WANT YOU TO SEE

### Chapter 10: The Invisible Work

There's a whole world of work that doesn't show up in the money game.

Who raised you? Probably your mother, or someone like her. Did she get paid? No.

Who takes care of old people? Mostly daughters. Mostly unpaid.

Who keeps communities together? Who organizes the potluck, checks on the sick neighbor, volunteers at the school? Mostly women. Mostly unpaid.

This is called **care work**. It's the foundation of everything. Without it, none of the paid work could happen. Children would starve. The sick would die. The old would be alone.

But it doesn't count in the economy. GDP doesn't measure it. Taxes don't touch it. It's invisible.

The money game sits on top of this invisible world like a house on a foundation. Then pretends the foundation isn't there.

### Chapter 11: The Free Stuff We're Killing

There's another invisible world: the stuff nobody owns.

The air. The ocean. The forests. The bees that pollinate crops. The stable climate that lets us grow food.

Nobody owns these things. They belong to everyone. They're called the **commons**.

Companies use the commons to make money. They dump waste in the air and water. They cut down forests. They burn fuel that changes the climate. They do this for free. Then they sell you things and keep the profit.

Later, when the air is poison and the water is toxic and the climate is crazy, who pays to fix it? You do. Through taxes. Through higher prices. Through your children's ruined future.

This is called an **externality**. It's a fancy word for "costs that are pushed onto someone else."

The money game is great at making things. It's terrible at tracking costs.

### Chapter 12: The Stories We Believe

The money game runs on stories. Stories we tell ourselves about how the world works.

**Story One: "People are naturally selfish."**

Really? Then why do parents sacrifice for children? Why do people risk their lives to save strangers? Why do soldiers die for their country? Why do volunteers show up after a disaster?

Selfishness is real. But so is generosity, love, solidarity. The money game pretends only one of these exists.

**Story Two: "More stuff makes you happier."**

For a while, yes. If you're starving, food makes you happy. If you're cold, a coat makes you happy. If you're lonely, a friend makes you happy—wait, friends aren't stuff.

After a certain point, more stuff doesn't make people happier. But the money game needs you to believe it does. Otherwise you'd stop buying.

**Story Three: "This is the only way."**

This is the biggest story of all. The one the discredited authors you wrote about kept getting killed for questioning.

The story says: capitalism is human nature. It's the way things have to be. Any alternative leads to starvation and tyranny.

But that's just a story. There have been many ways to organize human life. There will be many more. This one is not eternal.

The people who benefit from this story need you to believe it's the only story. Because if you believe another story is possible, you might start building it.

## PART FIVE: WHAT YOU CAN DO

### Chapter 13: See Clearly

Before you can do anything, you have to see.

Not with angry eyes. Not with hopeless eyes. Just with open eyes.

See the game for what it is:

- Money is a tool, not a god
- Growth is a requirement, not a blessing
- Debt is a chain, not just a helper
- The rules favor people who already have money
- There's invisible work holding everything up
- There's invisible wealth (the commons) being destroyed
- The stories you're told are not the only stories

Seeing clearly is the first step. Most people never take it. They stay inside the dream their whole lives.

You're already seeing. That's why you're reading this book. That's why you asked.

### Chapter 14: Protect Yourself

You're in the game whether you like it or not. So play it smart.

**Learn the rules.** Understand interest. Understand credit. Understand how taxes work. The game is complicated on purpose. But the basics are simple. Learn them.

**Own something.** Anything. A house if you can. A piece of land. A small business. Shares in a company. Even a little ownership is better than none. Remember: owners get paid while they sleep. Workers only get paid while they work.

**Avoid bad debt.** Some debt helps you—a mortgage for a house you can afford, a loan for education that leads to better work. Some debt hurts you—credit cards for things you don't need, loans with crazy interest rates. Know the difference.

**Build community.** The money game wants you alone, isolated, dependent on buying things. Real wealth is neighbors who help each other. Friends who show up. Family that feeds you. These things don't show up on a balance sheet. They matter more.

**Keep some cash.** For emergencies. For opportunities. For the ability to say no to a job that's killing you. Money is freedom. A little bit of money is a little bit of freedom.

### Chapter 15: Opt Out Where You Can

You can't leave the game completely. Not unless you want to live in a cave and eat berries.

But you can opt out at the edges.

**Grow food.** Even a tomato plant on a balcony. Even herbs in a window. Every bite you grow yourself is a bite you didn't buy.

**Fix things.** Learn to mend clothes, repair appliances, patch holes. The game wants you to throw away and buy new. Don't.

**Share.** Borrow instead of buy. Lend instead of sell. Start a tool library with neighbors. The game wants everything to be a transaction. Make it a relationship instead.

**Give.** Real gifts. With no expectation of return. This breaks the money spell completely. The game has no answer for generosity.

**Connect.** Talk to your neighbors. Know their names. Help them when they're struggling. The game wants you isolated in your private box with your private stuff. Isolation makes you weak. Connection makes you strong.

### Chapter 16: Imagine Something Else

This is the most important chapter.

You have to imagine something else.

Not because you're going to build it tomorrow. Not because you have the answer. But because without imagination, there's no possibility of change.

The people who run the game want you to believe this is the only game. They want you to stop imagining. They want you to think "that's just how things are."

They're wrong.

There have been societies based on gift, not exchange.
There have been societies based on sharing, not owning.
There have been societies based on enough, not more.
There have been societies based on relationship, not transaction.

Most of them were destroyed or crushed by the money-game societies. But they existed. They exist now, in pockets, if you know where to look.

And new ones are being built. Right now. By people who saw the game clearly and decided to play a different one.

They're building:

- **Co-ops** where workers own the business together
- **Community land trusts** where land is owned by everyone and can't be sold for profit
- **Time banks** where people trade hours of help instead of dollars
- **Gift economies** where things are given freely and circulate through gratitude
- **Repair cafes** where people fix things together instead of throwing them away
- **Mutual aid networks** where neighbors take care of each other without waiting for the government or the market

These are small. They're fragile. They're not going to replace the money game tomorrow.

But they're seeds. And seeds grow when the soil is ready.

### Chapter 17: Don't Be a Hero

One last thing.

You're not going to save the world. Not by yourself. Not in a big dramatic way that makes a movie about you.

That's not how change happens.

Change happens when millions of people do small things. When they see clearly. When they protect themselves and each other. When they opt out where they can. When they build little pockets of something different. When they plant seeds that will grow long after they're gone.

You don't need to be a hero. You just need to be a human, doing human things, with other humans.

The game wants you to think you're powerless. It wants you to wait for a savior. It wants you to feel stupid and small.

You're not stupid. You're not small. You're one of billions, and billions of small things add up to big things.

The seeds are already planted. You're holding one right now.

## PART SIX: WHAT NOW?

### Chapter 18: The Short Version

If you forget everything else, remember this:

**Money is a tool.** Nothing more. Don't worship it. Don't let it define you.

**The game has rules.** They were written by people with money. They benefit people with money. Know the rules so you can play without being played.

**Growth is required.** This is the system's biggest weakness. It can't stop growing, but the planet can't support infinite growth. Something has to give.

**Debt is a chain.** Use it carefully. Don't let it own you.

**Ownership is power.** Own something if you can. Even a little.

**Invisible work holds everything up.** See it. Honor it. Do some of it yourself.

**The commons are being destroyed.** They belong to all of us. Fight for them.

**The stories are not true.** You can imagine something else.

**You are not alone.** There are others who see. Find them. Hold them. Let them hold you.

**Plant seeds.** You may not see them grow. That's okay. Your job is to plant.

### Chapter 19: A Letter to You

Dear you,

I don't know you. I'll never meet you. But I wrote this for you anyway.

Because someone wrote something for me once, a long time ago, when I was confused and felt stupid and didn't understand why the world worked the way it did.

That someone said: "You're not stupid. The game is confusing on purpose. Here's how it works."

It changed my life. Not because I suddenly became rich. Not because I figured everything out. But because I stopped feeling crazy. I stopped thinking the problem was me.

The problem is the game. The problem is the rules. The problem is the stories.

You are not the problem.

You are a person, alive at a strange time, trying to make sense of a world that doesn't make much sense. That's not stupid. That's human.

Keep asking questions. Keep seeing clearly. Keep planting seeds.

The game will try to convince you that nothing you do matters. That's a lie. Everything matters. Every choice. Every connection. Every moment of seeing clearly and acting with love.

The game doesn't want you to know that. But you know it now.

So go. Live. Love. Plant.

The harvest is not yours to see. But it will come.

It always does.
