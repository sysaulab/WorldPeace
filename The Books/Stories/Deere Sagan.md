# The ABCs Under the Tree

The tree was old. Older than either of them had been, when being was something they still did. Its roots had cracked a boulder somewhere below; its branches held a hundred nests and a thousand conversations' worth of shade.

Carl Sagan's ghost sat on one exposed root, legs crossed, hands folded over a copy of a book that no longer needed pages. Phillip Deere's ghost sat across from him, on another root, his posture the easy stillness of a man who had spent his life listening to things that do not speak in words.

"You know," Sagan said, "I spent decades trying to teach people the alphabet of the universe. Hydrogen. Helium. Carbon. The way stars cook these elements in their cores and scatter them across the galaxy when they die. That was my ABC."

Deere nodded. He did not smile, but something in his face softened.

"My ABC was different," Deere said. "A is for Ahalen — the ancient ones, the ones who gave us the original instructions. B is for Bvnoke — the corn, the beans, the squash. The three sisters who taught us how to plant. C is for Cate — the deer, the four-legged who showed us how to walk gently."

"Three sisters," Sagan said. "Companion planting. Nitrogen fixation. Structural support. That's good biology."

"It's good *relationship*," Deere said. "The sisters did not plan. They grew together for so long they forgot they were separate. That is the teaching."

Sagan looked up through the branches at a sky that was not quite blue, not quite memory. "I told people we were made of star stuff. Stardust. They loved that. But I don't think they understood what it meant."

"What did it mean?"

"That you are not separate. The iron in your blood was forged in a supernova. The calcium in your bones was cooked in a red giant. You are not *from* the universe. You *are* the universe. A part of it, temporarily organized, briefly conscious, asking questions about itself."

Deere was quiet for a long moment. A bird landed on a low branch, considered them both, and flew away.

"We have a word for that," Deere said. "It is not a word you can write. It is the sound of wind through pine needles. It is the smell of rain on dry ground. It is the feeling of knowing you belong somewhere and somewhere belongs to you."

"Entanglement," Sagan offered.

"Too small. Too technical. But close enough for a scientist."

Sagan laughed. It was a dry, gentle sound, like stones clicking together in a stream. "I was not always a good scientist. I wanted to convince people. To prove things. To win arguments."

Deere nodded again. "I was not always a good medicine man. I wanted to fight. To burn. To make them hurt the way they hurt us."

"And then?"

"Then I realized that fighting is just agreeing to play their game. They had the guns. They had the laws. They had the numbers. But they did not have the original instructions. They did not have the three sisters. They did not have the tree."

Sagan looked at the tree above them. "I realized something similar. I spent years arguing with people who thought the stars were just lights in a dome. I wanted to prove them wrong. To humiliate them. But then I thought: what if I just showed them Saturn through a telescope? What if I just let them *see*?"

"Let them taste the soup."

"Exactly." Sagan smiled. "You know, I never said 'the spoon is in your hand.' But I should have."

Deere reached down and picked up a fallen twig. He turned it over in his fingers. "The alphabet I was taught in the boarding school was different. A is for Apple. B is for Boy. C is for Cat. They cut our language out of our mouths and put their words in. They told us our ABC was wrong."

"And you survived."

"We survived because we remembered the other ABC. The one under the tree. The one the ahalen gave us. No school could take that away. They could take the words. They could not take the *relationship*."

Sagan leaned forward. "I spent my life trying to teach people that the universe is old. Billions of years. That we are young. That we have barely begun. And that our future depends on whether we can learn to see ourselves as one species, one planet, one fragile, beautiful thing."

"That is the original instruction too," Deere said. "The ahalen said: *You are the land. The land is you. What you do to it, you do to yourself.* They did not say it in English. They said it in the way the river bends. In the way the deer gives birth in spring. In the way the fire cleans the old growth to make room for the new."

"I wish I had met you when I was alive," Sagan said.

"I wish I had met you too. We could have saved some time."

"Time." Sagan looked at his hands, which were ghost-hands now, translucent, weightless. "I used to think time was the only thing we could not buy more of. But ghosts have plenty of it. Too much, maybe."

Deere smiled. It was the first time Sagan had seen him smile. It changed his whole face, the way a river changes the land around it.

"Then let us not waste it," Deere said. "Teach me your ABC. Hydrogen. Helium. The stars cooking carbon. And I will teach you mine. The three sisters. The seven generations. The tree that holds up the sky."

Sagan stood. Or floated. The distinction was blurry now.

"Deal," he said. "But we start with the spoon."

"The spoon?"

"You told me. *The spoon is in your hand. Taste the soup.* That is where every alphabet begins. With tasting. With listening. With noticing what is actually there."

Deere stood too. Or rose. The tree held its silence.

"Then let us taste," he said.

And the two ghosts, one who had mapped the cosmos and one who had remembered the original instructions, walked together toward a camp stove that had been waiting for them for a very long time.

The Moon Ghost, watching from the edge of the clearing, did not smile. It could not. But it stayed. And that was enough.
