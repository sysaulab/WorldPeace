# Chapter 4: Dinner with a Ghost

The evening had come soft and gray, the kind of evening that made the city feel like a single enormous room with too many corners. Joshua was sitting on the steps of a closed laundromat on 74th Street, eating a piece of bread he had been given by a baker on Roosevelt. The baker had come out at closing with a bag of day-old rolls and seen him standing quietly across the street. "You look like you could use this," the baker had said, a little embarrassed. Joshua had thanked him and asked his name—Mr. Okada—and then said it back to him, as if tasting it. Mr. Okada had gone home feeling oddly seen.

Now Joshua sat on the cool stone steps, tearing the bread into small pieces and eating them slowly. The paper bracelet had frayed to a soft fringe at the edges, but the name was still there: JOSHUA CARPENTER. He did not look like a man waiting. He looked like a man who had arrived.

The woman approached from the north, walking with the particular rhythm of someone who needed to be noticed but not remembered. She wore a short skirt and a jacket too thin for the season, and her heels made a sound like small, exhausted hammers. Her face was carefully made up, but the care was old, professional, a mask that had been applied so many times it had become indistinguishable from the skin beneath. She stopped a few feet from the steps and looked at him with an expression that was half performance, half exhaustion.

"Hey," she said. "You looking for company?"

Joshua looked up at her. His hands paused on the bread. He took her in without hurry, without the quick scanning assessment she was used to—the inventory of body parts, the calculation of cost. He looked at her face, her eyes, the set of her shoulders beneath the thin jacket. He looked at her as if she were a landscape he had not expected to encounter and was grateful to see.

"You're cold," he said. It was not a question.

She blinked. The line was supposed to go differently. She had a script. He was supposed to name a service and a price, or look away in shame, or insult her. He was supposed to be a transaction.

"I'm fine," she said, automatic.

He moved over on the step, making room. "Sit. I have bread. It's a day old, but it's good."

She didn't move. Suspicion flickered behind the mask. "You a cop?"

"No."

"Social worker?"

"No."

"Some kind of church thing?"

"No." He tore another piece of bread and held it out to her. "Just someone eating dinner. I'd like company. No money, no anything else. Just eat with me."

She looked at the bread. She looked at the step beside him. She looked at her own hands, which were chapped and slightly trembling from the cold or from something older. Then, for reasons she would spend weeks trying to name, she sat down.

He gave her half the roll. She held it in both hands like a small animal she was afraid to crush.

"What's your name?" he asked.

She almost gave the work name, the one she used with clients. Something stopped her. "Lena."

"Lena," he repeated, the same way he had said Mr. Okada's name, as if it mattered that he get the sound right. "Thank you for sitting with me, Lena."

She ate the bread. She hadn't realized she was hungry. She had trained herself not to feel hunger, the same way she had trained herself not to feel cold, not to feel the weight of men, not to feel the particular loneliness that arrived at three in the morning when the last client was gone and the money was counted and the apartment was silent. She had built a wall between herself and her body, and the wall had kept her alive. But the bread was warm, and the step was solid, and the man beside her wasn't looking at her body. He was looking at the street, at the sky, at the bread in his own hands, and when he did look at her it was with a gentleness that made the wall feel thin.

"You don't want anything," she said. It was almost an accusation.

"I wanted company. You're here. That's enough."

"That's not how the world works."

"No," he said. "It's not."

She finished the bread. He offered her the last piece. She took it. They sat in silence for a while, watching the cars pass, the streetlights flicker on, a couple arguing on the corner over a phone.

"You know what I am," she said finally. Her voice had changed; the mask was slipping, and beneath it was something raw, almost defiant. "You know what I do."

"Yes."

"And you still gave me your bread."

"I gave you my bread because you were hungry."

"I'm a—" She stopped. The word she usually used, the one the men used, the one the world used, felt suddenly like a stone in her throat. "I sell myself. That's what I do."

Joshua turned and looked at her. His gaze was steady, patient, unafraid. "You've been selling something that was never for sale," he said. "But that's not who you are."

She laughed, a sharp, bitter sound. "You don't know me."

"No," he said. "But I see you. And the you I see is not for sale. She's just hungry and cold and she hasn't been looked at without a price in a very long time."

The laugh died. Something cracked in her face, a fault line opening, and then her eyes were wet and she was trying to hold herself together with her arms crossed tight across her chest. She had not cried in front of anyone in six years. She had not let anyone see her without the mask in longer than she could remember. But there was no transaction here, no performance, no demand. There was only the step, the bread, and the stranger who had called her by her real name.

"You're crazy," she whispered, and the words were almost the same words Ronald had spoken, on a different street, in a different morning, with a different wound. "You're completely crazy."

"Maybe," Joshua said. "But you're still here, and you're still you. And that's not nothing."

She cried. He didn't touch her, didn't offer comfort beyond his presence. He just sat beside her, quiet, while the city roared on around them. When she was done, her face was streaked and bare, and she looked younger, and much older, all at once.

"I don't know how to stop," she said.

"I'm not asking you to stop."

"Then what do you want?"

He stood and offered her his hand. She took it. He helped her to her feet, and for a moment they stood face to face on the laundromat steps, two strangers who had shared bread and a silence that felt like a door opening.

"Whatever you do next," he said, "you don't have to do it as a ghost. You're not invisible. I see you."

She walked away. She didn't look back. She walked five blocks to her apartment, locked the door, and sat on the edge of her bed in the dark. She didn't know what had happened. She didn't know who he was. She only knew that someone had looked at her—not at the mask, not at the body, not at the transaction—and had not flinched.

She didn't work that night. She didn't work the next night. She didn't know what she would do instead. But the wall between herself and her body had a crack in it now, and through the crack, faint and terrifying, came the first light she had seen in years.

---

Joshua slept on the same bench in the same park, the taste of bread still on his tongue. The paper bracelet was a ghost on his wrist, almost unreadable now, but still there.

And somewhere in the vast machinery of the city, a video was still circulating, a system was still forgetting his face, and a yellow tag sat in a queue marked *pending review*. The night passed, and he did not dream.
