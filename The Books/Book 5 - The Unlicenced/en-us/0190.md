# Chapter 19: The Summons

The crisis, when it came, had been building for years—a slow accumulation of grievances and provocations and failures of nerve that no one had been willing to name until it was too late to stop them. Two great power blocs, each convinced of its own righteousness, each armed with weapons that could end everything, had maneuvered themselves to the edge of a precipice. A disputed border. A downed aircraft. A deadline that came and went while the world held its breath. Diplomats had talked for weeks in windowless rooms, and the talking had changed nothing. Sanctions had been imposed, then escalated, then escalated again. Navies had moved into position. Evacuation flights had begun.

Now, on the morning of what might become the last day of peace, the Secretary-General of the United Nations was sitting in her office on the thirty-eighth floor of the Secretariat building, staring at a screen that showed satellite imagery of warships in the South China Sea. Her name was Amara Diallo. She was sixty-one years old, the daughter of Ghanaian diplomats, a woman who had spent her life believing that talk could prevent war if the talk was honest enough and the timing was right. She had never believed it less than she did today.

"Both sides have rejected the ceasefire proposal," said her chief of staff, a thin Swedish man named Lindström who had not slept in three days. "The Americans want language condemning the territorial incursion. The Chinese want language affirming their historical claims. Neither will accept the compromise text. We've been at this for six hours."

"What about the back channel?" Diallo asked.

"Dead. Beijing won't take the call. Washington is in bunker mode. The French are trying, but no one trusts the French."

Diallo closed her eyes. The weight of the moment pressed down on her, a physical sensation, like standing at the bottom of the ocean and feeling the whole sea above you. She had spent her career preparing for this kind of crisis, and now that it was here, all her preparation felt like a child's drawing of a flood.

"We need something else," she said. "Something that breaks the pattern."

"With respect, Madam Secretary-General, we've tried everything. Shuttle diplomacy, emergency resolutions, personal appeals from the Pope. Nothing is moving them."

Diallo opened her eyes. On her desk, amid the piles of briefing papers and situation reports, was a tablet that her press secretary had left her that morning. The screen showed a frozen video frame: a thin man in a blue shirt, sitting silently in a marble room while commissioners banged a gavel and demanded he speak. The headline beneath read: THE MAN WHO WOULDN'T TALK: FEDERAL TRIBUNAL ENDS IN STALEMATE.

"Who is this?" Diallo asked, turning the tablet toward Lindström.

"That's the Joshua Carpenter case. The silent hearing. It's been all over the news."

"I know who he is. I mean, who is he really?"

"No one knows. He appeared in New York two months ago with no memory, no ID. Since then he's been..." Lindström searched for the word. "Disrupting things. Not violently. Just by being. People who meet him change. He washed a homeless man's feet on the street, and the video got fifty million views. He sat with a sex offender, and the man's lawyer says he's a different person. He carried a paralyzed veteran on his back through the Bronx. A talk show host broke down crying during an interview. A billionaire philanthropist has reportedly started learning the names of his kitchen staff."

"And the government detained him?"

"They tried to classify him as a social contagion. A memetic threat. They convened a tribunal to determine whether he should be held indefinitely. He didn't say a single word in his own defense."

"And?"

"The tribunal adjourned without a ruling. The legal framework was so shaky that no one wanted to be the one to sign the order. He's still in administrative custody, but his supporters are growing. It's becoming a cause célèbre."

Diallo looked at the screen for a long time. The man in the image was not handsome or charismatic or powerful. He looked tired and ordinary and completely at peace. In the chaos of the past weeks, she had barely registered the story. Now, staring at his face, she felt something shift in her chest.

"You said the Pope's appeal didn't move them," she said slowly.

"It didn't."

"Because they expected it. The Pope always appeals for peace. It's part of the script. What if we offered them something they don't expect?"

Lindström frowned. "I'm not following."

"The leaders are meeting in Geneva in forty-eight hours. The last-chance summit. Every major power will be in the room. What if we invited this man to address them?"

Lindström stared at her. "You want to bring a homeless amnesiac who hasn't spoken a word in weeks to address the most important diplomatic gathering in a generation?"

"No. I want to bring the only person in the world who seems to be able to make people stop and think and feel something other than fear. The Pope couldn't do it. The diplomats can't do it. Maybe he can."

"With respect, that's insane."

"Yes," Diallo said. "It is. But we are out of sane options."

---

The request traveled through channels that had never been used for anything like it. The State Department was consulted, then the White House, then the Department of Homeland Security. The Chinese foreign ministry was informed through the Swiss embassy. The Russians demanded to know if this was an American psychological operation. The French, as always, offered to host an alternative meeting. No one had a clear authority to grant or deny the request because no one had ever imagined a request like it.

In the end, it was a young Justice Department lawyer named Sarah Okonkwo—no relation to the librarian in Queens—who signed the order releasing Joshua from administrative custody. She had been assigned to the case because no one senior wanted to touch it. She had watched the silent hearing from the back of the room, and she had gone home that night and told her husband that she had seen something she couldn't explain. When the UN request landed on her desk, she signed it without hesitation.

"If this goes wrong," her supervisor said, "it's your career."

"If this goes right," she replied, "it won't matter."

---

They brought Joshua from his cell in the early hours of the morning. He was thinner than he had been at the tribunal, his face more drawn, but his eyes were the same—calm, patient, unafraid. The guards who had watched over him for the past week had found themselves unsettled by his presence, not because he did anything strange but because he did nothing at all. He sat. He ate when food was brought. He slept when the lights were dimmed. He never asked for anything. He never complained. He never spoke.

The guard who unlocked his cell that morning was named Reyes—no relation to the officer who had found him on the park bench, though the coincidence would later be noted by people who noted such things. Reyes had been working in federal detention for eight years, and he had never seen a prisoner like this one.

"You're being released," Reyes said. "Some kind of diplomatic thing. They're taking you to the UN."

Joshua looked at him. He didn't speak, but he nodded, as if this were exactly what he had been expecting.

"Any questions?" Reyes asked.

Joshua shook his head.

"You know you're supposed to talk there, right? At the UN. They're going to want you to talk."

A small smile crossed Joshua's face, the first expression Reyes had seen on it in a week.

"Don't worry," Joshua said. His voice was hoarse from disuse, but it carried the same strange gentleness it had always carried. "I'll know what to say when the time comes."

---

The motorcade took him through streets that had been cleared of traffic, a gesture of urgency that felt, to those who witnessed it, like a small surrender. The city had been living under the shadow of the crisis for weeks. People walked faster. They looked at their phones more often, scanning for updates. The unspoken question hung in the air of every subway car, every office, every kitchen table: was this it? Was this how it ended?

The man in the back of the black sedan looked out the window at the passing city. He saw the faces. He saw the fear. He saw the exhaustion and the hope and the fragile, stubborn persistence of people who were still buying groceries and walking dogs and kissing their children goodnight even as the world tilted toward the abyss.

"What are you thinking?" asked the woman sitting beside him. It was Parvati. She had been waiting outside the detention facility when they brought him out, her credentials still valid for another forty-eight hours, her resignation not yet processed. She had insisted on riding with him.

"I'm thinking about all the people," Joshua said. "Every window has someone behind it. Every car has someone driving it. They're all afraid, and they're all pretending not to be. They're all hungry to be seen."

"Can you help them?"

"I don't know. But I can see them. That's all I've ever done."

The sedan crossed the Queensboro Bridge into Manhattan. The UN headquarters rose ahead of them, a tower of glass and stone that had been built to house the world's hopes and had spent the decades since watching those hopes collide with the world's realities. The flags of the member states hung limp in the still morning air. The plaza was crowded with cameras and journalists and protesters and security personnel and the great, churning machinery of global attention.

Joshua looked at the building, at the flags, at the crowd. His face was calm.

"They're all going to want something from me," he said.

"Yes."

"They're going to want me to take a side."

"Probably."

He nodded slowly. "They won't get what they want."

The sedan pulled through the security gate and stopped at the delegates' entrance. A UN protocol officer was waiting, a young woman with a headset and a clipboard and the particular harried efficiency of someone who had been told to make the impossible possible in under twenty-four hours.

"Mr. Carpenter," she said. "Welcome. If you'll follow me, we'll take you to the preparation room. The Secretary-General would like to brief you before the session."

Joshua stepped out of the car. He was still wearing the blue shirt from the broadcast, now rumpled and worn, and his bare feet were pale against the gray pavement. The protocol officer looked at his feet, then at her clipboard, then back at his feet.

"Do you need shoes?" she asked.

"No," Joshua said. "I'm fine."

"Are you sure? The General Assembly hall is—"

"I'm sure."

She nodded, making a note on her clipboard that no one would ever read, and led him inside.

---

The preparation room was a small office on the second floor, normally used for pre-session briefings for junior diplomats. Today it held the Secretary-General, two of her senior advisors, and a television showing live coverage of the delegations arriving at the main entrance. The sound was muted, but the images were clear: grim-faced men and women in dark suits, stepping out of limousines and armored vehicles, their aides trailing behind them like the wake of a ship.

Amara Diallo stood when Joshua entered. She had seen the videos, read the reports, studied the photographs. None of them had prepared her for the reality of the man who now stood in front of her, barefoot and calm, looking at her with the same attention she imagined he gave everyone.

"Mr. Carpenter," she said. "Thank you for coming. I know this is... unusual."

"Yes," he said.

"I don't know if anyone has explained the situation to you. The world is closer to war than it has been in forty years. The leaders in that hall represent every major power, and they are locked in a confrontation that none of them seems able to stop. I've brought you here because I've run out of everything else. I don't know what you can do. I don't even know what you are. But I've seen what happens to people when they meet you. And I thought—I hoped—that maybe something would happen to them too."

Joshua looked at her. He saw the exhaustion in her face, the weight of the years and the failures and the hope that she kept feeding despite everything.

"You're very tired," he said.

Diallo blinked. She had been expecting a response to her speech, to the gravity of the moment, to the historical significance of what she was asking. Instead, he had seen her.

"Yes," she said quietly. "I am."

"You've been carrying this alone. You don't have to."

The words were so simple, so unexpected, that Diallo felt something crack open in her chest. She had been Secretary-General for six years, and in all that time, no one had told her she didn't have to carry it alone. Everyone assumed she was strong enough. Everyone assumed she could handle it. This barefoot stranger had looked at her for ten seconds and seen the truth.

"What should I do?" she asked.

"You should rest. Later. Right now, you should take me to the hall."

She nodded, collecting herself. "Do you know what you're going to say?"

"I'll know when I'm there."

"They're going to ask you to choose a side. That's what they do. They frame everything as a choice between us and them. It's the only language they understand."

"I know," Joshua said. "It's the language of orphans who've forgotten they have the same father."

Diallo stared at him. Then, for the first time in weeks, she smiled.

"Are you always like this?" she asked.

"I don't know. I've only been awake for two months."

She laughed, a small, surprised sound that seemed to startle her as much as anyone. Then she straightened her jacket and gestured toward the door.

"The hall is this way. They're ready for you."

Joshua followed her out of the room and down the long corridor toward the General Assembly hall. The protocol officer walked ahead of them, speaking into her headset. Security personnel flanked the doorways. The television cameras were already rolling, their red lights blinking in the dim hallway.

At the entrance to the hall, Diallo stopped.

"This is as far as I go. I'll be on the floor, but you'll be at the podium alone."

"All right."

"Whatever happens in there—thank you. For coming. For being willing to try."

Joshua looked at her. "Thank you for asking. Most people don't."

The doors opened. The hall stretched before them, vast and green and filled with the representatives of every nation on earth. The murmur of a thousand conversations died as the delegates turned to look at the man who had walked barefoot into the center of world history.

Joshua stepped forward. The doors closed behind him. The world held its breath.

And somewhere in the gallery, Parvati found a seat and opened her notebook—a new one, blank, waiting for whatever came next.
