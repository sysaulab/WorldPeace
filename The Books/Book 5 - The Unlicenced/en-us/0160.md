# Chapter 16: The Prime-Time Examination

It was billed as a conversation, but it was a trial.

The network had been pursuing Joshua for two weeks, ever since the Vance Kessler interview had shattered every ratings projection the news division had made that year. The footage of a seasoned provocateur weeping on his own set had become the most replayed clip in the network's history, and the executives wanted more. They wanted the man himself, in a format they could control.

What they built was a hybrid: part town hall, part panel discussion, part Sunday morning talk show. A studio audience of two hundred people, carefully selected to represent a cross-section of the viewing public. A moderator named Elaine Chu, a veteran journalist with an Emmy and a reputation for fairness that she had earned by never taking a position on anything. A panel of four experts: a psychiatrist, a theologian, a social critic, and a former White House advisor who had been chosen for his ability to generate the kind of clipped, confrontational soundbites that played well on social media. And at the center of it all, a single chair where the man called Joshua would sit.

The program was called *The Joshua Phenomenon: Saint, Sickness, or Spectacle?* The title had been argued over for three days. The marketing department had wanted something punchier. The legal department had wanted something safer. The chyron was a compromise.

Joshua arrived on foot, as he always did. He had been found that morning in a community garden in Brooklyn, helping a group of retirees plant winter vegetables. The production assistant sent to retrieve him had expected resistance, or at least eccentricity. Instead, Joshua had brushed the dirt from his hands and asked if there would be food at the studio. The assistant, flustered, had said yes. Joshua had nodded and followed him to the car.

He walked onto the set barefoot, his shoes left at the door. He was wearing a clean shirt—someone had found one for him, a simple blue button-down that fit well enough—and his hair was still damp from a sink. He sat in the chair at the center of the set and looked out at the audience with the same expression he wore in parks and on benches and in the kitchens of billionaires: present, patient, unafraid.

Elaine Chu began with the question everyone had been asking.

"Who are you?"

"I don't know," Joshua said. "I woke up in a park seven weeks ago. I had a bracelet with a name. That's all I have."

The psychiatrist on the panel, Dr. Naomi Cross, leaned forward. She was a woman of fifty-five with silver glasses and a careful, measured voice. "You claim to have no memory of your life before that morning. No childhood. No family. No education. That's a textbook presentation of dissociative fugue. Have you been evaluated?"

"Yes. Three times. The doctors said similar things."

"And you rejected their diagnoses."

"I didn't reject them. I just didn't accept that the diagnosis was the most important thing about me. They wanted to treat my amnesia. I wanted to live my life. Those aren't the same thing."

"What's more important than understanding your own condition?" Dr. Cross asked.

"The people in front of me," Joshua said.

The theologian, a professor named Dr. Ibrahim Mansour, spoke next. He was a slight man with a trimmed gray beard and the kind of gentle precision that came from decades of academic debate. "You've been called a prophet. A holy man. Some have even used the word messiah, though I think that's premature at best. How do you respond to those labels?"

"I don't. Labels are for people who need to put things in boxes. I'm not in a box."

"But you must have some self-understanding. You wash feet. You forgive sins. You speak of God's kingdom. These are not neutral actions. They carry theological weight."

"I do what's in front of me," Joshua said. "If that carries weight, it's because the weight was already there. I didn't invent love. I just pay attention."

The social critic, a woman named Robin Voss who had built a career on identifying cultural pathologies, wasn't satisfied. "That's too easy," she said. "You're doing something that's clearly resonating on a massive scale, and you're refusing to take responsibility for it. You're acting like a leader while pretending you're not leading. That's either naive or manipulative."

"Leading people where?" Joshua asked.

"Wherever you're going. You have followers. They gather on rooftops. They imitate your actions. They talk about you like you've changed their lives."

"I haven't changed anyone's life. People change their own lives. I just sit with them while they do it."

"That's a distinction without a difference."

"It's the only distinction that matters," Joshua said. "If I change you, you're dependent on me. If you change yourself, you're free. I don't want followers. I want people who are free."

The audience was quiet. The panel was quiet. Elaine Chu, who had moderated a hundred debates and knew how to find the friction in any exchange, seemed momentarily at a loss.

It was the former White House advisor, a man named Roger Ainsley, who broke the silence. He had been waiting for his moment, and he took it with the practiced timing of someone who had spent decades in rooms where power was the only currency.

"Let's be honest about what's happening here," Ainsley said. "You're building a movement. Maybe you don't call it that. Maybe you don't even intend it. But you have the attention of millions of people. You've disrupted a major television broadcast. You've embarrassed one of the wealthiest philanthropists in the world. You've been denounced by an interfaith council. And you're doing all of it without any accountability, without any structure, and without any clear agenda. That's not a spiritual phenomenon. That's a power vacuum waiting to be filled."

"I don't want power," Joshua said.

"Everyone wants power."

"No. Everyone wants to be seen. Power is just a substitute. It's what people chase when they've given up on being loved."

Ainsley stared at him. The line had landed in the room like a stone dropped into still water, and the ripples were still spreading. The psychiatrist was nodding slowly. The theologian had removed his glasses. The social critic was looking at Joshua with an expression that had shifted from skepticism to something more uncertain.

"Then what do you want?" Ainsley asked. "If you don't want power, if you don't want followers, if you don't want money or fame or influence—what do you want?"

Joshua was quiet for a moment. The cameras tightened. The audience held its breath.

"I want people to stop being hungry," he said. "Not for food. For being seen. Every person I've met since I woke up has been starving for someone to look at them without wanting something. Without judging them. Without trying to fix them or use them or sell them something. Just looking. Just seeing. That's all I do. And if that's disruptive, it's because we've built a world where being seen is the rarest thing there is."

The commercial break came at exactly the right moment, which meant the producers had missed their cue by thirty seconds because they were watching the broadcast instead of timing it.

During the break, the panel sat in strained silence. Ainsley checked his phone. Dr. Cross wrote something on a notepad and then crossed it out. Dr. Mansour stared at the ceiling. Robin Voss approached Joshua, who was still sitting in the center chair, drinking a glass of water someone had brought him.

"Can I ask you something off the record?" she said.

"There's no off the record," Joshua said. "But you can ask."

"Why don't you just explain yourself? Give people a framework. A philosophy. Something they can hold onto. You'd be less threatening if people could categorize you."

"I'm not trying to be less threatening."

"That's my point. You could do more good if you let people understand you."

"Could I? Or would I just be easier to manage?"

Voss opened her mouth, then closed it. The question hung between them, unanswered.

The second half of the broadcast was dominated by questions from the audience.

A woman stood, middle-aged, her hands gripping the microphone like a lifeline. "I have a son," she said. "He's been in and out of treatment for years. Addiction, mental health, the whole thing. Nothing helps. Nothing sticks. What would you do if you met him?"

"I'd sit with him," Joshua said.

"That's it?"

"That's the beginning. Everything else starts from there."

A young man stood, his voice shaking. "I watched the interview with Vance Kessler. I never cry. I cried for an hour. Why did that happen?"

"Because you saw someone being seen," Joshua said. "And you recognized the hunger. It's in you too. It's in everyone."

A man in a suit, polished and skeptical. "What's your endgame? You must have one. Everyone has an endgame."

"I don't know what an endgame is," Joshua said. "I woke up seven weeks ago. I've been living day by day since then. I'm not building toward something. I'm just here."

A woman with gray hair and a gentle face. "Do you believe in God?"

"Yes."

"Which God?"

"The one who sees us. The one who doesn't look away."

A teenager in the back row, her voice small but steady. "Are you ever afraid?"

Joshua paused. The pause was long, but it wasn't uncomfortable. It was the kind of pause that made people lean forward.

"Yes," he said. "Sometimes. Not of being hurt. Not of dying. But of missing someone. Of walking past a person who needed to be seen and not noticing. That's the only fear I have."

The broadcast ended. The lights came up. The audience filed out in the same strange silence that had followed the Vance Kessler interview, the same uncertain hush, as if they had witnessed something they didn't know how to name.

Joshua walked off the set. He found his shoes where he had left them and put them on. He walked out the front door of the studio, into the cool night air, and stood on the sidewalk, looking up at the city lights.

A woman was standing on the corner, waiting for him.

She was South Asian, mid-thirties, dressed in plain clothes that were too plain to be anything but deliberate. She had a notebook in her hand and an expression on her face that was trying very hard to be professional and not quite succeeding. She had been standing there for three hours, waiting for the broadcast to end, waiting for him to emerge.

"Joshua," she said. "My name is Parvati. I've been watching you for three weeks."

He looked at her. The look was the same look he gave everyone—open, patient, without a trace of surprise.

"I know," he said. "I've seen you."

Parvati felt something shift in her chest, a door opening that she had been holding closed for a very long time.

"I need to talk to you," she said.

"All right," Joshua said. "Let's walk."
