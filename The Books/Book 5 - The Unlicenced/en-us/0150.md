# Chapter 15: The Religious Tribunal

The summons came not as an invitation but as a request that was not a request—the kind of communication that wore the language of welcome while carrying the weight of command. It was delivered by a young priest named Father Tomas, who found Joshua sitting on the steps of a church in Harlem, a church he had never entered, waiting for nothing in particular.

Father Tomas was thirty-four, earnest and anxious, a man who had entered the priesthood hoping to serve the poor and had spent the last eight years serving committees. He had been sent by his bishop with a letter and a car and instructions to bring the man called Joshua to a meeting of the Council of Faith Leaders of Greater New York. The council was an interfaith body that had been formed in the aftermath of a crisis and had survived by never addressing anything that might cause another crisis. Its members represented the great traditions—Catholic, mainline Protestant, evangelical, Jewish, Muslim, Hindu, Sikh. They met four times a year to issue statements about peace and unity and the importance of dialogue. They had never summoned anyone before.

"Bishop Vasquez would like to speak with you," Father Tomas said, standing awkwardly on the church steps, the letter damp in his hand. "The council has some questions. About your ministry."

"I don't have a ministry," Joshua said.

"Your work, then. Your... presence."

Joshua looked at the young priest, at the letter, at the black car idling at the curb. He stood and brushed the dust from his borrowed pants.

"All right," he said. "Take me to them."

---

The council met in a conference room at an interfaith center on the Upper East Side, a building of glass and limestone that had been donated by a philanthropist who believed that religion was important for social cohesion and best kept contained. The room was long and narrow, dominated by a table of dark wood that reflected the faces of the people seated around it like a still, dark pond. There were fourteen of them, men and women in dark suits and clerical collars and hijabs and kippot and the particular vestments of authority. They had files in front of them—photographs, news clippings, transcripts of the Vance Kessler interview, witness statements gathered by their staff.

Joshua walked into the room barefoot. He had taken off his shoes at the door, for reasons no one could discern.

Bishop Vasquez, a man of sixty-seven with a face carved by decades of institutional patience, gestured to an empty chair at the foot of the table. "Please, sit."

Joshua sat. He folded his hands on the table and looked around the room with the same open, unhurried attention he gave to parks and street corners and the faces of strangers. He didn't seem intimidated. He didn't seem defiant. He seemed present, which was somehow more unsettling.

"We've asked you here," the bishop began, "because your activities have attracted a great deal of attention. Some of it has been... theologically suggestive. People are calling you a holy man. A prophet. Some have even used the word messiah." He paused, letting the word hang in the air. "We felt it was important to understand who you are and by what authority you do what you do."

"I don't have an authority," Joshua said.

"You wash feet. You forgive sins. You carry the broken on your back. You speak of God's kingdom as if you have some special knowledge of it. That implies authority."

"Does it? I thought it implied love."

A woman at the table, Rabbi Cohen, leaned forward. She was fifty-three, sharp-eyed, the leader of a large Reform congregation, and she had spent her career navigating the tensions between tradition and change. "Love is not the issue," she said. "Everyone here believes in love. The issue is structure. Accountability. You operate outside every system—no denomination, no ordination, no oversight. You're a loose cannon."

"I'm not a cannon," Joshua said. "I don't fire anything at anyone."

"You know what I mean."

"I do. You mean I'm not under your control."

The room stiffened. A young evangelical pastor named Marcus Hale, who had built a megachurch on the strength of his charisma and his certainty, set down his pen with a click.

"We're not trying to control you," he said. "We're trying to protect people from error. False prophets arise all the time. The early church dealt with them. Every generation deals with them. Charisma without accountability is just manipulation dressed up as inspiration."

"Have I manipulated anyone?"

"You've—" Hale paused. The answer was no, and everyone in the room knew it. The man had never asked for money, never solicited followers, never told anyone what to believe or how to live. He just sat with people. He just saw them. And somehow that seeing had become the most disruptive force any of them had encountered in years.

"You've created confusion," said Imam Abdullah, a quiet man with a gray beard and eyes that had seen too much suffering to be easily impressed. "People are looking to you instead of to their traditions. Instead of to their communities. You're pulling them away from the structures that hold them accountable."

"Are the structures holding them?" Joshua asked. "Or are they just holding them in place?"

The imam didn't answer.

Bishop Vasquez took a different approach. He had prepared for this. He had read the reports, watched the videos, consulted with theologians. He had decided that the best way to handle this man was not to attack but to absorb—to find a place for him within the system, where his energy could be channeled and his danger neutralized.

"We're not your enemies," the bishop said. "We're shepherds. We understand that God works in unexpected ways. But we also have a responsibility to test the spirits, as Scripture commands. So let us test. Tell us who you are. Where you come from. What you believe."

Joshua was quiet for a moment. He looked down at his hands, which were calloused and still, then back up at the faces around the table.

"I woke up in a park six weeks ago with no memory," he said. "I had a paper bracelet with a name on it. That's all I know about where I come from. What I believe is very simple: everyone is hungry to be seen, and most people are starving. I look at them. That's all. I don't have a theology. I don't have a doctrine. I don't have a five-year plan."

"Then why do people keep calling you a prophet?" asked a woman at the far end of the table, a Sikh community leader named Gurinder Kaur. "Why do they keep saying you speak with the authority of God?"

"Because they're starving," Joshua said. "And I feed them. If the shepherds were feeding the sheep, the sheep wouldn't keep wandering off to find food in the wilderness."

The silence that followed was deep and uncomfortable. Several people looked down at their files. Several others looked at the bishop.

"You're accusing us of failing our flocks," said Pastor Hale.

"I'm not accusing anyone. I'm describing what I see. You asked me what I believe. I believe the hungry should be fed. The lonely should be accompanied. The broken should be carried. If that threatens your structures, maybe the problem isn't with me."

Rabbi Cohen spoke again, her voice softer now, less prosecutorial. "I understand what you're saying. I do. But you have to understand our position. We've spent our lives building communities that can sustain people. Communities that last beyond a single charismatic moment. What you're doing—it's beautiful, but it's fragile. It depends entirely on you. What happens when you're gone?"

"I don't know," Joshua said. "I haven't left yet."

"But you will. Everyone does. And then what? People who've put their faith in you will be lost. They'll have no structure to fall back on."

"They'll have each other. They'll have the memory of being seen. That might be enough."

"Memory isn't enough," said the imam. "People need law. They need ritual. They need a framework that outlasts emotion."

"They need love," Joshua said. "Love is a framework. It's the only one that holds."

The bishop leaned back in his chair. He looked tired. The meeting was not going the way he had planned. He had expected a fanatic, or a fraud, or at least someone who could be reasoned with. Instead he was facing a man who spoke in sentences that sounded simple but opened under your feet like trapdoors.

"One more question," the bishop said. "By what authority do you forgive sins?"

The question was the question, the one the council had been building toward all evening. It was the theological heart of the matter. Forgiveness was not a small thing. Forgiveness was the province of God, mediated through the structures God had established—through priests, through rituals, through the long, careful machinery of grace. If this man was forgiving sins without a license, he was either God or a blasphemer.

Joshua looked at the bishop for a long moment.

"By what authority do you retain them?" he asked.

"I'm sorry?"

"You asked by what authority I forgive. I'm asking by what authority you don't. Every one of you at this table has the power to forgive and the power to withhold forgiveness. You exercise that power every day. You decide who's welcome and who's excluded. Who's clean and who's unclean. Who deserves grace and who doesn't. Where did you get that authority?"

"We were ordained," said Pastor Hale. "We were called by our communities and set apart by our traditions. We didn't just wake up on a bench and decide we were holy."

"No," Joshua said. "You didn't. You studied. You trained. You earned your positions. And now you sit in this room, behind this table, deciding who gets to be loved and who doesn't. I didn't earn anything. I didn't study anything. I just woke up and started loving the people who were in front of me. If that's a threat to your authority, then maybe your authority is the problem."

The room was very still. The bishop's face had gone pale. Rabbi Cohen was staring at her hands. Imam Abdullah's eyes were closed.

"You're saying," the bishop said slowly, "that our authority is a barrier to God."

"I'm saying that any authority that doesn't lead to love is not from God. You asked me by what authority I forgive sins. I forgive them because the people in front of me are dying under the weight of them, and someone has to. If you want me to stop, then you do it. You forgive them. You sit with the sex worker and the sex offender and the man who can't forgive himself. You carry the veteran on your back. You wash the feet of the homeless. You do it, and I'll stop. But you won't do it. You'll keep meeting in this room, issuing statements about love, while the people you're supposed to love are starving on the street."

He stood. The council members looked up at him, their faces a mosaic of reactions—anger, shame, confusion, and something else, something quieter, that might have been recognition.

"I didn't come here to fight you," Joshua said. "I came because you asked. But I don't have what you're looking for. I don't have a license. I don't have a commission. I don't have a title you can recognize. I just have the people in front of me, and I love them. If that makes me a heretic, then I'm a heretic. But I'm not the one who needs to explain myself."

He walked to the door. At the threshold, he turned.

"You said the sheep need a structure," he said, looking at the bishop. "They do. But not this one. Not one that spends more energy protecting itself than feeding the hungry. When you're ready to tear the walls down, you know where to find me. I'll be on the benches. With the sheep."

He left. The door closed behind him with a soft, final sound.

---

The council sat in silence for a long time.

Bishop Vasquez was the first to speak. "We need to issue a statement."

"What kind of statement?" asked Rabbi Cohen.

"A clarification. A warning, if necessary. We can't let this go unanswered."

"Because he's dangerous?" asked Gurinder Kaur. "Or because he's right?"

The question hung in the air, unanswered.

Pastor Hale was already drafting notes on his tablet. "Heresy," he muttered. "Classic antinomian heresy. Rejection of all moral structure. We've seen this before."

"Have we?" asked Imam Abdullah. "He didn't reject morality. He rejected our monopoly on it."

"That's the same thing."

"Is it?"

The argument went on for another hour. In the end, the council voted to issue a statement. It would be carefully worded, expressing appreciation for the subject's evident compassion while raising concerns about his lack of accountability, his extra-institutional approach, and his implicit critique of established religious authority. It would not use the word *heresy*, but it would come very close.

The statement was released the following morning. It made the news, briefly, in the way that religious statements make the news—a segment on a cable channel, an article in a religion blog, a flurry of discussion in the forums where people argued about theology and culture and the future of faith.

And somewhere in the Bronx, a barefoot man was sitting on a bench, watching children play in the autumn leaves, while a woman named Parvati stood across the street, her notebook in her pocket, working up the courage to cross the line she had been drawing her whole life.
