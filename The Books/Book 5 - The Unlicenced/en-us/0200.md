# Chapter 20: The Body and the Organs

The General Assembly hall was a room built to hold the world, and today it held more than the world—it held the world's fear.

Every seat was filled. The green marble podium stood at the center of the concentric rings of delegates, a small island in a sea of dark suits and serious faces. The great golden seal of the United Nations hung above the dais, a symbol of unity that had never felt more ironic than it did this morning. The air was heavy with the particular tension of a moment that everyone knew would be remembered, regardless of how it ended.

The leaders sat in the front rows. The American president, a woman who had been elected on a promise of strength and had spent her term discovering that strength was easier to promise than to wield. The Chinese premier, a man whose face revealed nothing and whose country's warships were at that very moment positioned within striking distance of the American fleet. The Russian foreign minister, the British prime minister, the French president, the heads of state and foreign ministers and permanent representatives of one hundred and ninety-three nations, all of them arranged in their assigned seats like pieces on a board that no one knew how to play anymore.

Above them, in the gallery, the observers sat in a different kind of silence. Parvati was there, her notebook open to a blank page, her pen uncapped and ready, though she had no idea what she would write. Elise was there, the producer who had booked Joshua for the Vance Kessler show, now here as a representative of a network that had decided this was the most important story in the world. Father Tomas was there, the young priest who had defied his bishop, sitting with his hands clasped and his lips moving in silent prayer. And scattered among the journalists and the NGO representatives and the diplomatic staff were others—Maya Santos, Marcus in his wheelchair, Lena in her new blouse—the unlikely congregation that had followed their barefoot shepherd into the very center of power.

The Secretary-General, Amara Diallo, rose from her seat and approached the podium. Her face was calm, but those who knew her could see the exhaustion beneath the calm, the sleepless nights and the failed negotiations and the hope that had been whittled down to a splinter.

"Distinguished delegates," she said, her voice carrying through the hall with the aid of the microphone, "we are assembled here today because the world stands at a precipice. The issues that divide us are real. The grievances are genuine. The fears are not imaginary. But the path we are on leads only to catastrophe, and all our efforts to find another path have failed. In such moments, wisdom sometimes comes from unexpected places. It is my privilege—and my desperate hope—to present to you a man who has been called many things: a vagrant, a holy fool, a social contagion, a prophet. He calls himself nothing at all. I ask you to listen to what he has to say."

She stepped back from the podium. The hall was perfectly still. The cameras zoomed in on the side door, where a figure had just appeared.

Joshua walked into the General Assembly hall barefoot.

He wore the same blue shirt from the broadcast, now wrinkled and worn at the collar. His hair was uncombed. His hands were empty. He walked with the same unhurried pace he had used on a park bench in Queens, on a sidewalk outside a bodega, through the kitchen of a billionaire's mansion. He walked as if the marble floor had been laid for him alone, as if the assembled power of the world were a landscape he had been traveling toward his whole brief life.

He reached the podium. He did not adjust the microphone. He did not look at the notes that had been placed there for him, notes he had not asked for and would not read. He looked out at the faces before him—the presidents and prime ministers, the ambassadors and ministers, the aides and translators and security personnel—and he saw them. Each of them. All of them.

The silence stretched for ten seconds, twenty, thirty. It was the same silence that had filled the marble room at his tribunal, but this room was larger, and the silence was larger, and it seemed to press against the walls as if it might break them open.

Then he spoke.

---

"There were two women," he said, "who came to a king. They were fighting over a child. Each claimed to be the mother. Each demanded justice. The king listened to their arguments. Then he said, 'Bring me a sword. Cut the child in two, and give half to one woman and half to the other.'"

He paused. His voice was not loud, but it carried, the way water carries through stone.

"The real mother said, 'No. Let her have the child. Just don't kill it.' The other woman said, 'Cut it. Let it be neither mine nor hers.' And the king knew, then, who the true mother was. He gave the child to the one who would rather lose everything than see it die."

He looked across the hall, his eyes moving from face to face, nation to nation, power to power.

"You have brought me here to ask which of your enemies I will help you kill."

The silence that followed was absolute. No one coughed. No one shifted. The translators had stopped translating, their hands frozen over their consoles.

"Some of you dress the request in the silk of order. Others in the sackcloth of justice. But the question beneath the rags is the same. You want me to bless your bombs. You want me to sanctify your side. You want me to tell you that God is with you and against them. You have been asking this question for as long as there have been nations, and you have always found someone willing to give you the answer you wanted."

He leaned forward slightly. His bare feet were pale against the green marble.

"I am not that someone."

A murmur stirred in the hall, a ripple of reaction that moved through the delegates like wind through wheat. The American president shifted in her seat. The Chinese premier's face remained impassive, but his hands, folded on the table in front of him, had gone very still.

"When you ask me which child should live—the son holding the gun or the daughter beneath its shadow—you are asking a parent to choose between their right eye and their left. You are asking the body to declare the heart superior to the lungs. There are no superior organs. There is only the body, alive, or the body, dying."

He lifted his hand and touched his own chest, over the heart.

"Humanity is one child. One body. One parent's heart. You have forgotten this. You have built your nations on the forgetting of this. You have drawn lines on maps and called them identities, called them destinies, called them reasons to kill. But the lines are not real. The child is real. The body is real. The parent who grieves over every wound, on every side, is real."

The Russian foreign minister leaned over to his aide and whispered something. The aide did not respond. He was staring at the podium, his mouth slightly open.

"You say you want peace," Joshua continued, "but you want it by the sword. You want peace without justice for your enemies. You want peace without repentance. You want peace on your terms. But peace on your terms is not peace. It is victory. And victory sows the seeds of the next war. You have been sowing and reaping, sowing and reaping, for all of human history. The fields are soaked in blood, and you keep asking for more seed."

He stepped away from the podium. He walked to the edge of the dais, closer to the delegates, closer to the leaders, closer to the cameras that were broadcasting his image to every corner of the earth.

"I will not take your side. I will not condemn your enemies. They are not your enemies. They are your estranged limbs. You are bleeding out from the amputation, and you are asking me to hand you the saw."

The American president stood up.

It was a breach of protocol, a breach of decorum, a breach of everything that the General Assembly was supposed to be. Her security detail tensed. Her aides reached for her arm. She shook them off.

"Then what are we supposed to do?" she asked. Her voice was raw, stripped of the polish that had carried her through a hundred speeches. "We have treaties. We have obligations. We have citizens to protect. You're telling us to just—what? Let them attack? Let them take what they want?"

Joshua looked at her. He did not flinch from her anger. He did not bow to her authority. He saw her, as he saw everyone, and what he saw was not a president but a person—exhausted, terrified, carrying a weight that no human being was built to carry.

"If your child was holding a knife to the throat of your other child," he said, "would you take a side? Would you arm one against the other? Would you choose which one to save?"

"That's not the same—"

"It is exactly the same. The only difference is the scale. You have convinced yourself that scale changes the moral calculus. It doesn't. A thousand deaths are not more acceptable than one. A million are not more strategic. You have just stopped seeing the faces. I see them. I see all of them."

The Chinese premier rose to his feet.

He was an old man, a man who had survived purges and power struggles and the long, grinding machinery of a political system that rewarded patience and punished vulnerability. He had not risen by showing his heart. But something in the barefoot man's words had reached through the armor.

"Our grievances are not imaginary," he said, his English precise and careful. "Our claims are rooted in history, in law, in the rights of our people. Are you saying we should simply abandon them?"

"I am saying that your grievances will still be there after the war," Joshua said. "But your children will not. Your cities will not. The history you are fighting over will be buried under the rubble of everything you built to protect it. Is your claim worth that? Is any claim worth that?"

The premier said nothing. Joshua looked at him for a long moment, then turned back to the full assembly.

"Go back to your parliaments," he said. "Go back to your presidents and your premiers and your generals. Tell them Jesus Bond—" and here a strange murmur rippled through the hall, because the name was not one he had ever used, not one anyone had heard, and it landed like a spark on dry tinder, "—will not be your chaplain. He will not bless your bombs. He will not tweet your outrage. He will not give you God dressed in your flag."

The American president flinched. The Chinese premier closed his eyes.

"If you want my help," Joshua said, "you must come to me with the other nation's hand in yours, and ask not 'Who should win?' but 'How do we both stop dying?' Until then, I am of no use to you. And you are a danger to the only child that matters: the one you keep trying to cut in two."

He fell silent. The silence rushed back into the hall, heavier than before, full of the things that had been said and the things that had been heard and the things that no one knew how to respond to.

Then he stepped down from the dais.

He walked up the aisle, between the rows of delegates, past the presidents and the premiers and the foreign ministers. As he passed, some of them stood. Not all. Not even most. But some. A young ambassador from a small island nation that was sinking beneath the rising sea. An older diplomat from a country that had been at war for so long that no one remembered the cause. The French president, who had tears on his face and did not try to hide them. The American president, who reached out as if to touch his arm and then let her hand fall.

He walked through them all, and he did not speak again.

---

At the back of the hall, before the great bronze doors, he stopped. He turned. He looked back at the assembly, at the leaders of the world, at the cameras that were still broadcasting, at the billions of people watching from every continent and every time zone and every station of life.

"One day," he said, "you will come to me with the other nation's hand in yours. One day, you will ask the right question. I will be waiting. I have always been waiting."

He pushed open the doors and walked out into the corridor. The doors swung shut behind him with a sound like a long exhale.

---

For a long moment, no one moved.

Then Amara Diallo walked to the podium. Her face was wet. She did not wipe it.

"The General Assembly will now move into closed session," she said. "I would ask all delegates to remain in the hall. The Secretary-General requests a moment of silence. Not for prayer. For reflection. For the question we have just been asked."

She stepped back. The hall fell into a silence that was not empty but full—full of the words that still hung in the air, full of the image of the barefoot man walking through the seats of power, full of the impossible, undeniable question he had left behind.

And in that silence, something began to shift. Not a resolution. Not a treaty. Not yet. But a shift. A softening. A crack in the wall that had been building for decades. It would take weeks for that crack to widen into a ceasefire, months for it to widen into negotiations, years for it to widen into something that might, if the world was lucky and stubborn and brave, become peace.

But the crack was there. The stone had been set down. The child was still alive.

---

Joshua walked out of the United Nations building alone.

The plaza was crowded with journalists and protesters and security personnel, but they parted around him as if they had practiced for it. No one tried to stop him. No one tried to interview him. The cameras filmed him, but from a distance, as if they were afraid to get too close.

He walked east, toward the river. The sun was setting over Manhattan, painting the towers in shades of gold and rose. The air was cool and clean. The city hummed with its eternal life, the millions of stories unfolding in the millions of windows, the millions of hungers and hopes and fears.

He found a bench near the water. He sat down. He looked out at the river, at the bridges, at the boats moving slowly toward the harbor.

And then, for the first time since he had woken up in a park in Queens with no name and no past and no explanation, he closed his eyes and let out a long, slow breath.

He was still there when the sun went down. He was still there when the stars came out. He was still there when the news broke that the American and Chinese delegations had agreed to a temporary ceasefire to allow for further talks. He was still there when the city began its slow, tentative exhale, a world that had been holding its breath for weeks finally allowing itself to believe that tomorrow might come after all.

And he was still there when Parvati found him, hours later, sitting alone in the dark.

She sat down beside him. She didn't speak. Neither did he. They just sat together, watching the river, two people who had been strangers once and were strangers no longer.

After a long time, she said, "What happens now?"

He opened his eyes. He looked at the water. He looked at the sky. He looked at her.

"I don't know," he said. "I've never gotten this far before."

She laughed, a small, quiet sound that surprised her. Then she leaned back on the bench and looked up at the stars.

"Thank you," she said. "For not taking a side."

"There were never sides to take," he said. "There were only people. There have always been only people."

They sat together in the quiet, and the river flowed, and the city breathed, and somewhere far away, in a marble hall that had witnessed the beginning of something no one yet understood, a young diplomat was picking up a phone and calling his counterpart on the other side of the world, not to negotiate, not to threaten, but to ask a question that had never occurred to him before: "How do we both stop dying?"

And the story, which had begun on a bench with a paper bracelet and a man with no name, continued on, as stories do, long after the telling was done.
