# Chapter 9: The Hacker

The prison was an old one, built in the years when the state believed that thick walls and narrow windows could contain the problems it didn't want to solve. It sat on the edge of a town that had died slowly after the mill closed, its only thriving industry now the incarceration of men from the city two hours south. The buses brought them in, and the buses rarely brought them out.

The chaplain's name was Thomas Ng. He was sixty-one, a former hospital orderly who had found God in the basement of a Pentecostal church and then found his vocation in the long, fluorescent corridors of the correctional system. He had been doing this work for nineteen years, and he had buried his optimism so deep that he no longer remembered where he'd put it. But he kept showing up. That was the thing. He kept showing up.

The young man in Cell 247 was named Eli. He was twenty-three years old, pale and thin with the kind of posture that came from spending ten thousand hours in front of a screen. He had been in the prison for fourteen months, awaiting trial for a crime that had made national news: a ransomware attack on a regional hospital system that had locked out medical records for six days. In those six days, a woman in the intensive care unit had died because her medication history was inaccessible. She was sixty-eight years old. She had three grandchildren. Her name was Dorothy.

Eli had not intended to kill anyone. He had been nineteen when he wrote the code, a contractor for a hacking collective that had promised him money and respect and a place in a world he had never felt he belonged to. The attack was supposed to be a demonstration, a flex, a way to force the hospital to pay a ransom and the hackers to prove their power. The death was collateral. The death was an accident. The death was still death, and Eli was still its cause, and the state was seeking life without parole.

He had not spoken to anyone in three months.

---

Chaplain Ng had tried. He had sat outside the cell door every Tuesday, speaking softly through the meal slot, offering prayer, offering presence, offering the thin, worn comfort of a man who had seen too many young men waste away in boxes. Eli had never responded. He lay on his bunk, facing the wall, his food tray untouched, his body slowly consuming itself. The medical staff had talked about a feeding tube. The psych staff had adjusted his medications twice. Nothing changed.

Then Chaplain Ng met a man in a shirt that had once been white, standing at the prison gate in the rain.

It was a Tuesday, early morning. Ng was arriving for his shift when he saw the man standing outside the perimeter fence, not trying to enter, not holding a sign, just standing. He was soaked through, his hair plastered to his forehead, his eyes calm.

"Can I help you?" Ng asked.

"I'd like to visit someone," the man said.

"Visiting hours are Thursday and Sunday. You need to be on the approved list."

"I'm not on the list."

"Then I can't help you."

The man nodded, as if this were perfectly reasonable. "The young man in Cell 247," he said. "Eli. He hasn't spoken in three months."

Ng stopped. "How do you know that?"

"I heard about him. I'd like to see him."

"Who are you?"

"My name is Joshua."

Ng looked at him for a long moment. The rain was falling harder now, but the man didn't seem to notice. He stood there like a tree, rooted and patient, waiting for something he seemed certain would come.

"I'll see what I can do," Ng said, surprising himself.

---

It took two days. Ng had to call in a favor from a deputy warden who owed him for the funeral of his son. He had to list the visitor as a "spiritual advisor," which was technically not a lie. He had to convince the security chief that the man was not a journalist, not an activist, not a threat—just someone who wanted to sit with a boy who was dying by inches in a concrete box.

When Joshua walked into the visitation room, Eli was already there, hunched in a plastic chair, his wrists cuffed to a metal ring on the table. His eyes were hollow. His skin was gray. He looked like a photograph that had been left too long in the sun.

Joshua sat down across from him. He didn't speak. He just sat, his hands folded on the table, his gaze steady and undemanding. The guard in the corner watched them with bored suspicion.

Five minutes passed. Ten. Eli didn't move, didn't look up, didn't acknowledge that anyone else was in the room.

Fifteen minutes.

"Who are you," Eli said. It was less a question than a sound forced out of a body that had forgotten how to make words.

"Someone who wanted to sit with you."

"Why."

"Because you're alone."

Eli's eyes flicked up, then down again. "I deserve to be alone."

"Maybe. But you are alone, and I'm here. Those two things can both be true."

Another silence. The guard shifted his weight.

"You know what I did," Eli said.

"Yes."

"Then why are you here? You some kind of chaplain? You want me to confess? You want me to find Jesus?" The words were brittle, edged with a sarcasm that was trying very hard to be cruel and only managing to be exhausted.

"I don't want anything," Joshua said.

"Everyone wants something."

"I want to know what you loved. Before you learned to hate."

The question hung in the air. Eli stared at him. Something flickered behind the hollow eyes—a movement, a stirring, a door that had been sealed shut for a very long time beginning to shift in its frame.

"What?"

"You didn't always want to hurt people. You didn't always write code to break things. Before all of this, there was something you loved. Something you were good at. Something that made you feel alive. What was it?"

Eli's jaw tightened. He looked away, toward the blank wall, toward the barred window, toward anywhere but the man across from him. His hands trembled against the cuffs.

"Music," he said, so quietly the guard might not have heard it.

"Music?"

"I played piano. When I was a kid. I was good at it. My grandmother taught me." His voice cracked on the word *grandmother*. "She died. Before all this. She died, and I didn't—I couldn't—"

He stopped. His chest was heaving. The room was very still.

"She loved you," Joshua said.

"Yes."

"And you loved her."

"Yes."

"And when she died, something broke."

Eli didn't answer. His eyes were wet now, the tears coming in a slow, terrible flood that he had been holding back for years, for his whole life maybe, and the wall that had kept them in was cracking and there was nothing he could do to stop it.

"I didn't mean to kill that woman," he said. "Dorothy. I didn't even know her name until the trial. I didn't mean for anyone to die. I just wanted—I wanted to be seen. I wanted to matter. I wanted someone to look at me and think I was worth something."

Joshua nodded. He didn't offer absolution. He didn't offer comfort. He just stayed, present, while the young man across from him fell apart and put himself back together in a new shape.

"You can't undo what happened," Joshua said. "You can't bring Dorothy back. You can't unmake the grief you caused. That will be with you forever."

"I know."

"But you can still be the boy your grandmother taught to play piano. You can still love something. You can still make something instead of breaking it. Even here. Even now."

"How?"

"I don't know. That's your question to answer. But you can't answer it if you're dead. And you've been dying. You've been dying since you got here."

Eli wiped his face with the back of his cuffed hand. The gesture was childlike, helpless, utterly human.

"I don't know if I can do this," he said. "I don't know if I deserve to do this."

"No one knows what they deserve," Joshua said. "But you're still breathing. That means something is still possible."

The guard announced that time was up. Joshua stood. Eli looked up at him, and for the first time in three months, his eyes were not empty. They were terrified, and they were alive.

"Will you come back?" Eli asked.

"If I can."

"Thank you. For asking. About what I loved."

Joshua nodded. "It's still in there, Eli. The music. It didn't die with her. It's still in there."

He walked out of the visitation room. Chaplain Ng was waiting in the corridor, his arms crossed, his face unreadable.

"I don't know who you are," Ng said. "But that boy hasn't spoken to anyone in three months."

"He was ready," Joshua said.

"Ready for what?"

"To remember that he's still a person."

Ng looked at him for a long time. "You're not a chaplain."

"No."

"What are you, then?"

Joshua smiled, a small, tired expression that contained something Ng couldn't name. "Someone who woke up here," he said. "Same as everyone else."

He walked out of the prison, into the gray afternoon light. The rain had stopped. The road was wet and shining. He put his hands in his pockets and walked toward the bus stop, a thin man in a thin shirt, carrying nothing, heading nowhere in particular.

Behind him, in Cell 247, a young man named Eli was humming. It was a melody his grandmother had taught him, half-remembered, fragile as a match flame. The guard on duty heard it and paused in the corridor. He didn't know the tune. He didn't know what it meant. But he stood there for a moment, listening, before he moved on.

---

In Virginia, the analyst named Parvati had finished her review. The pattern was unmistakable. The man in the gray jacket—now a man in a white shirt, now a man with no jacket at all—was moving through the city, through the system, leaving behind him a trail of altered people. The homeless man. The sex worker. The psychiatric patients. The lawyer. And now a young offender in a state prison, accessed without credentials, without authorization, without any visible explanation.

Parvati wrote her report in careful, measured language. She recommended escalation to a live monitoring team. She recommended cross-referencing with law enforcement and social services. She recommended flagging the subject as a "potential social destabilizer — nonviolent."

She did not use the word "spy." She did not use the word "mission." But she stayed late that night, staring at the file, and when the cleaning crew came through, she was still there, trying to understand why she felt, against all her training and all her skepticism, that she was watching something that was not supposed to exist.
