# Chapter 11: The Talk-Show Host

His name was Vance Kessler, and he had built an empire on contempt.

The *Vance Kessler Hour* aired weeknights at eight on a cable network that had long since abandoned the distinction between news and theater. His audience was loyal and enormous—three million viewers a night, more on YouTube, more still on the podcasts and the clips and the viral moments that circulated like a low-grade fever through the body politic. He was fifty-one years old, built like a former athlete who had lost the war with middle age, with a face that had been reconstructed by a hair transplant and a jawline that had been sculpted by a cosmetic dentist. His voice was a baritone instrument tuned to the frequency of righteous indignation.

He had built his career on enemies. They changed with the news cycle—immigrants, socialists, coastal elites, tech moguls, members of Congress, celebrities who said the wrong thing at the wrong time—but the structure never changed. There was always an enemy, and Vance Kessler was always the one brave enough to name them. His viewers loved him for it. His critics called him a demagogue. His producers called him a genius.

The video of the feet-washing had crossed his desk three weeks ago. His team had flagged it as "potential culture war content," but Vance had dismissed it. Another homeless man. Another weird street thing. Not his beat.

Then the lawyer. Then the hacker. Then the billionaire's gala.

His researchers started digging. They found the park bench, the psychiatric ward, the missing persons gap, the trail of altered people that the man in the photographs left behind him like footprints in wet cement. They found the social worker who had written nothing. They found the charge nurse who had kept a paper bracelet. They found a woman in Queens who had been a sex worker and was now enrolled in a community college, studying to be a counselor, and who had told a friend that a stranger on a laundromat step had saved her life.

The story was strange. It was compelling. And no one had landed an interview.

"Get him," Vance told his producer. "I don't care how. Find him and book him."

---

They found him in Brooklyn, three days later, sitting in a public garden between two apartment buildings, watching bees move through a patch of lavender.

The producer's name was Elise. She was thirty-six, sharp and exhausted, and she had been working for Vance Kessler for six years. She had booked senators and movie stars and once, memorably, a fugitive who had agreed to an exclusive before turning himself in. She had never booked anyone like this.

The man had no phone. No email. No address. No social media. He existed only in the reports of people who had met him, and the reports were so strange, so uniformly unsettling, that Elise had begun to wonder if the researchers were playing a prank.

But here he was. Sitting in a garden. Watching bees.

"Excuse me," Elise said. "My name is Elise. I work for Vance Kessler. The television host."

He looked up at her. His eyes were brown, or maybe hazel. His clothes were clean but borrowed-looking—a green shirt with a faded logo, khaki pants that were too short at the ankle. He was barefoot again. His shoes were placed neatly under the bench.

"Hello, Elise," he said.

"Mr. Kessler would like to have you on his show. For an interview. You've been in the news a bit, and he thinks his audience would be very interested in your story."

"I don't have a story."

"So I've heard. But people are interested anyway. What you're doing—the feet, the hospital, the prison—it's got people talking. Mr. Kessler wants to talk to you about it."

He was quiet for a moment, watching a bee land on a flower, drink, and lift away.

"Vance Kessler is very angry," Joshua said.

Elise blinked. "I'm sorry?"

"He's been angry for a long time. He built his whole life on it. And now he's tired, and the anger is all he has left, and he's afraid that if he lets it go, there won't be anything underneath."

Elise stared at him. She had worked for Vance Kessler for six years. She had seen him in every mood—triumphant, frustrated, bored, manic, exhausted. She had never thought of him as afraid. But something in what this man said landed in her chest like a stone dropped into still water.

"Are you... do you know him?" she asked.

"No. I've never met him."

"Then how—"

"I don't know," Joshua said. "It's just what I see."

Elise took a breath. She was a professional. She had a job to do. "Will you come on the show?"

"If he wants me to, yes."

"He does."

"Then I'll come."

"No demands? No conditions? No topic restrictions?"

"No."

Elise nodded slowly. She had booked hundreds of guests. None of them had ever been this easy. None of them had ever been this strange.

"There's a car," she said. "It can take you to the studio now, if you're free."

Joshua stood. He put on his shoes—brown leather, a hole in the sole—and followed her to the car. He didn't ask about the format, the questions, the audience. He didn't ask for water or a mirror or a moment to prepare. He just sat in the back seat, watching the city pass, as calm as if he were being driven to a friend's house for dinner.

Elise sat in the front, checking her phone, and found that her hands were trembling.

---

The studio was a cathedral of light and noise.

It had been built in the 1980s for a talk show that no longer existed, and it had been renovated in layers, each new host adding their own vision, so that the current incarnation felt like a temple designed by a committee of people who had never spoken to each other. The set was dark wood and brushed steel, with a massive video wall that displayed the show's logo and, during commercial breaks, slow-motion footage of Vance Kessler shaking hands with soldiers.

The audience was already seated—two hundred people who had lined up on West 57th Street for the chance to be part of the broadcast. They were the faithful, the loyal, the people who watched every night and posted in the forums and bought the merchandise. They had been warmed up by a comedian who told jokes about the news cycle. They were ready.

Vance was in his dressing room, going over the research brief for the third time. The brief was thin. The man had no past. No criminal record. No political affiliation. No social media footprint. No known associates except for the people he had helped, and none of them had agreed to be interviewed either.

"Who is this guy?" Vance asked Elise, who was standing in the doorway.

"I don't know," she said. "I really don't."

"Is he crazy? Is this some kind of performance art?"

"I don't think so. I think he's just... what he is."

"And what is that?"

Elise paused. "I think that's what you're going to find out."

Vance looked at her. She looked back at him. There was something in her expression he couldn't read—a softness, maybe, or a wariness, or some third thing that had never been there before.

"Fine," he said. "Let's do this."

---

The introduction was classic Vance. He walked onto the set to thunderous applause, waved to the crowd, settled into his chair with the practiced ease of a man who had spent thirty years in front of cameras. The music faded. The lights dimmed. The video wall displayed the guest's name, though there was no photograph to accompany it.

"My next guest is a mystery," Vance said to the camera. "Three weeks ago, he appeared in a park in Queens with no memory, no ID, and no explanation for how he got there. Since then, he's been washing homeless people's feet on the street, walking into psychiatric wards, visiting convicted criminals in prison, and crashing billionaire galas where he reportedly ignored every VIP in the room to hang out in the kitchen. Some people say he's a saint. Some people say he's a menace. Some people say he needs medication. Tonight, you can decide for yourself. Please welcome the man known only as Joshua."

Joshua walked onto the set.

He did not wave. He did not smile. He walked with the same unhurried pace he had used on the sidewalk in Queens, in the corridor of the hospital, in the kitchen of the Sterling estate. He sat down in the guest chair and folded his hands in his lap and looked at Vance Kessler with an expression that was not hostile, not nervous, not eager to please. It was simply... present.

The audience applauded, because they had been told to, but the applause was uncertain, a wave that broke against something solid and retreated.

"Thank you for coming," Vance said. "Really. We don't usually have guests who are this hard to track down."

"I wasn't hard to track down," Joshua said. "I was in a garden. Your producer found me."

"Right. Yes. She did." Vance smiled his practiced smile. "So let's start with the obvious question. Who are you?"

"I don't know. I woke up in a park. I had a bracelet with a name on it. Joshua Carpenter. That's all I have."

"No memory at all? No family? No childhood? No job?"

"Nothing before the park."

"And that doesn't bother you?"

"No."

"Why not?"

Joshua paused. The pause was not awkward. It was the kind of pause that made people lean forward, expecting something important.

"Because there's so much after the park," he said. "Every day, something new. Someone new. I wake up, and the world is there, and the people are there. Why would I spend my time looking for something that's gone when there's so much right here?"

Vance shifted in his chair. This was not the interview he had planned. He had planned to corner this man, to expose him, to reveal him as either a fraud or a fool. But the man was not cornering. He was just sitting there, answering questions as if they were genuine, as if the whole apparatus of television and audience and ratings was a thin veil he could see straight through.

"Some people say you're mentally ill," Vance said. "That you're having a psychotic break. That your calm is a symptom, not a virtue."

"I know. The doctors told me that."

"Do you think you're mentally ill?"

"I don't think so. But I also don't think the label matters very much. If I'm ill and I help someone, does the illness make the help less real? If I'm well and I hurt someone, does the wellness make the hurt less painful? The question isn't whether I'm crazy. The question is whether what I do is good."

"And what is it that you do? How would you describe it?"

"I pay attention," Joshua said. "That's all. People are starving for attention. Not the kind where someone watches them to judge them, or sell them something, or make a point. Just the kind where someone sees them and doesn't look away. That's all I do. I look, and I don't look away."

Vance felt something shift in his chest. He didn't like it. He had been doing this for thirty years, and he had never felt this particular sensation—a kind of exposure, as if the cameras had turned around, as if the audience were watching him instead of the other way around.

"You were at Adrian Sterling's gala last week," Vance said, trying to regain control. "Some people are saying you embarrassed him. That you humiliated him in front of his guests."

"I didn't embarrass him. I thanked his staff. If he felt embarrassed, that's between him and himself."

"Do you think billionaires should be embarrassed?"

"I think anyone who can't see the people standing six feet away from them should be curious about why. That's not embarrassment. That's an invitation."

"An invitation to what?"

"To be human again. To be in the same room as the rest of us. The wall between rich and poor, powerful and powerless—it's not made of money. It's made of attention. You stop paying attention, you build a wall. You start paying attention, the wall comes down. That's all."

Vance stared at him. The audience was very quiet. The cameras were very still. The producer in the control room was gripping the edge of her console, not sure whether to cut to commercial or let this keep going.

"And what about me?" Vance asked.

The question came out before he could stop it. He hadn't planned it. He hadn't even thought it. It just rose up from somewhere beneath his ribs and pushed its way into the air, and now it was hanging there, and three million people were watching, and Vance Kessler had never been this naked in front of anyone in his life.

"What about you?" Joshua asked.

"You said I was angry. You said it to my producer. She told me."

"Yes."

"Do you think I'm angry?"

Joshua looked at him. The look was long and steady and entirely without malice. It was the look of a doctor examining a wound that had been hidden for so long the patient had forgotten it was there.

"I think you learned a long time ago that anger kept you safe," Joshua said. "I think something happened to you, when you were young, and the anger was the only thing that made the hurt bearable. And then you got older, and the anger became your job, and now you're very good at your job, and you're very rich, and you're very famous. But the hurt is still there. And the anger is still there. And you're tired. You've been tired for years. You just can't admit it, because if you let the anger go, you're afraid there won't be anything left."

The silence in the studio was absolute. No one coughed. No one shifted. The cameras hummed. The lights burned.

"Who told you that?" Vance asked. His voice was barely a whisper.

"No one. I can just see it."

"See what?"

"The little boy who's still in there. The one who was hurt. The one who learned to fight because fighting was the only way to survive. He's still in there. He's very tired. And he wants someone to tell him it's okay to stop."

Vance Kessler's eyes were wet. He didn't try to hide it. He had been hiding things for thirty years, and he didn't have the strength for it anymore.

"My father," he said. "He used to—" He stopped. His voice broke.

"I know," Joshua said.

They sat in silence for a long moment. The control room was frozen. The producer didn't know what to do. The network had never cut to commercial during an emotional breakdown, but it had also never broadcast one that wasn't rehearsed.

Then Vance spoke again, his voice ragged, his face bare.

"What do I do?"

"The same thing everyone else does," Joshua said. "You start by paying attention. To the people around you. To yourself. To the hurt instead of the anger. You've been fighting a war your whole life. The war is over. You can put down the sword."

"I don't know if I know how."

"No one does. You learn by doing it."

Vance looked at the camera. The red light was on. Three million people were watching. He had built a career on enemies, and now, in front of all of them, he was crying.

"I'm sorry," he said. He didn't say what he was sorry for. He didn't need to.

"Don't be sorry," Joshua said. "Just be here. That's enough."

---

The broadcast ended seven minutes early. The network filled the time with ads. The audience filed out in silence, no one sure what they had witnessed, no one sure what to feel.

Vance Kessler sat in his dressing room for two hours after the show. He didn't take calls. He didn't read the comments, the tweets, the hot takes that were already blooming across the internet like mushrooms after rain. He just sat, staring at the wall, while Elise sat with him, saying nothing.

"Is he still here?" Vance asked finally.

"Joshua? He left. Walked out the front door. Security didn't try to stop him."

"Where did he go?"

"No one knows. He just walks."

Vance nodded. He looked at his hands. He looked at the mirror, the lights, the Emmy on the shelf, the photographs of himself with presidents and celebrities and people who had once been his enemies and were now his friends. It all looked different. It all looked like a set.

"Find out where he is tomorrow," Vance said. "I want to talk to him again. Not on camera."

"Okay."

"And Elise?"

"Yeah?"

"Thank you. For bringing him here."

Elise nodded. She didn't say that she was already thinking about quitting, that she had been thinking about it for months, that watching her boss break down on live television had cracked something open in her that she wasn't sure she could close again.

She just sat with him, in the quiet dressing room, while the city roared on outside, and somewhere a barefoot man was walking through the streets of Manhattan, a new name on his lips and no destination in mind.

---

The comments section on the network's website was a study in the taxonomy of human reaction.

*Staged. Obviously staged. Vance is pivoting to the faith demo.*

*That was the most beautiful thing I've ever seen on television.*

*Who is this guy? Is he a cult leader? This feels like the start of a cult.*

*I haven't cried in ten years. I just cried for twenty straight minutes.*

*Vance got played. That's what happens when you let a manipulative drifter on your show.*

*My father was like that. I know exactly what Vance was talking about. I know exactly.*

*Ratings stunt. Nothing more.*

*Why is everyone so cynical? A man showed vulnerability on television and you're all calling it a stunt. Maybe the stunt is your cynicism.*

*That guy didn't say a single political thing. No takes. No angles. He just... saw him. How is that possible?*

*We need more of this. Whatever this is.*

*We need less of this. This is how manipulation works. He's a blank screen and you're all projecting onto him.*

*I don't know what I just watched but I feel different.*

*Vance Kessler has been a bully for twenty years. One conversation doesn't change that.*

*Doesn't it?*

The comments went on for thousands of posts, and they followed the same strange pattern that the analyst in Virginia had observed before. They did not polarize cleanly. They did not divide into camps. They fractured and reformed and fractured again, and underneath all of them ran a current of something that was not quite belief and not quite dismissal, but a third thing—an unease, a hunger, a sense that the world had tilted slightly on its axis and no one had yet figured out the new angle.

---

Parvati watched the broadcast live.

She had set up an alert for the subject's name, or what passed for his name, and when the network had announced the interview, she had cleared her schedule. She watched the whole thing, from the uncertain applause at the beginning to the long, silent shot of Vance Kessler sitting alone in his chair after the guest had walked off the set.

When it was over, she sat very still for a long time.

Then she opened the file and added a new entry: *Subject appeared on national television. Host known for confrontational, partisan style. Subject did not confront. Host experienced visible emotional collapse. Audience response anomalous. Subject departed on foot, unaccompanied. Current location unknown.*

She closed the file. She stared at the screen. The tag was no longer amber. It was something else entirely—a color that did not appear on any threat matrix she had ever seen.

She went to her supervisor the next morning and requested a field assignment.

"Where?" the supervisor asked.

"New York."

"Why?"

"Because I need to see him in person," she said. "I can't do this from a screen anymore."

The supervisor looked at her for a long time. He had seen analysts burn out before. He had seen them get too close, lose perspective, start to believe in things that didn't exist. But Parvati was his best analyst. She had never asked for anything before.

"Two weeks," he said. "Observe only. No contact."

"Understood."

She flew out the next morning. She packed light—a laptop, a change of clothes, a photograph of the man who called himself Joshua, printed on paper, folded in her pocket.

She didn't know what she was looking for. She only knew that something was happening, something that did not fit into any system she understood, and she needed to be close enough to see it with her own eyes.

The plane lifted off. The city waited. And somewhere below, a man without a past was walking through a park, barefoot, watching the light change through the trees.
