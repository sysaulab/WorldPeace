# Chapter 17: The Friend Who Talked

They walked east from the studio, away from the lights and the cameras and the last of the audience lingering on the sidewalk. Parvati kept pace beside him, her notebook clutched in one hand, her heart beating faster than she wanted to admit. She had rehearsed this moment a hundred times in her hotel room, a thousand times in her head. None of the rehearsals had prepared her for the reality of his presence—the calm, the ordinariness, the way he walked as if he had all the time in the world and no destination in particular.

"You know who I am," she said. It wasn't a question.

"I've seen you," Joshua said. "Outside the laundromat. In the park when I was with Marcus. Across the street from the church where I slept last Tuesday. You've been very patient."

"You never said anything."

"You weren't ready to talk. Now you are."

She didn't know how to respond to that. She had spent sixteen days observing him, and he had spent sixteen days observing her observing him, and he had simply waited for her to be ready. It was so strange, so entirely outside the logic of her profession, that she felt the ground shift under her feet.

"I'm an analyst," she said. "I work for an agency that monitors... patterns. Social patterns. Potential disruptions."

"I know."

"How could you know that?"

"I didn't know the details. But I knew you were watching. And I knew you were working for someone. You have the look."

"What look?"

"The look of someone carrying a question that doesn't belong to them. Someone gave you that question. You've been holding it for a long time, and it's getting heavy."

Parvati stopped walking. They were on a quiet street in Hell's Kitchen, the traffic noise distant, the sidewalk empty. She felt exposed in a way that had nothing to do with her cover being blown. She had been seen, and the seeing was more thorough than any interrogation she had ever conducted.

"My agency thinks you're a threat," she said. "Not a violent threat. A social one. A memetic contagion, they call it. Something that spreads through populations and changes behavior. They've been tracking you since the feet-washing video."

"And what do you think?"

"I don't know what I think. That's why I needed to talk to you."

Joshua nodded and started walking again. She fell in beside him.

"They're going to try to stop you," she said. "Eventually. When they decide you're too unpredictable. That's what they do. They contain things they can't categorize."

"Will you help them?"

The question was asked without accusation, as if he were asking whether she preferred tea or coffee. Parvati felt her throat tighten.

"I don't know. I've spent my whole career serving systems. It's what I'm good at. It's what I was trained for. But I've been watching you, and I don't—" She stopped. "I don't think you're what they say you are."

"What do they say I am?"

"A destabilizer. A potential catalyst for social unrest. Someone who's undermining the structures that keep the peace."

"And what do you say I am?"

Parvati was quiet for a long time. They passed a bodega, its neon sign flickering. They passed a man walking his dog, a woman locking up a shop, a child staring out an apartment window.

"I think you're just what you say you are," she said finally. "Someone who woke up and started paying attention. And I think that's the most terrifying thing the system has ever seen."

Joshua smiled. It was a small smile, tired and warm. "You're very honest."

"I'm trying to be. I don't know how to do this."

"You're doing it."

They walked in silence for another block. Then Joshua stopped and turned to her.

"You came here because you wanted to understand. Now you understand. What will you do with it?"

"I don't know."

"Then sit with the not knowing for a while. It's not a bad place to be."

---

Four hundred miles away, in a windowless room in Virginia, a different conversation was unfolding.

The room was similar to the one where Director Keller had briefed his team, but smaller, more intimate, designed for pressure. Two men sat on one side of the table: Agent Morrison, the counterintelligence officer from the briefing, and a younger man named Harris, who had the soft face of a chaplain and the cold eyes of someone who had been doing interrogations for a very long time. Across from them, still wearing the orange jumpsuit of a federal inmate, sat Eli.

The hacker. The boy who had loved music. The young man who had cried in a visitation room when a stranger asked him what he loved before he learned to hate.

He looked different now. Three weeks of eating again, of talking to a therapist, of humming his grandmother's melodies in the dark. He had put on weight. His eyes were clearer. But he was still terrified. He had been terrified since the moment the guards pulled him out of his cell and told him he had visitors from Washington.

"We're not here to threaten you," Morrison said. "We're here to offer you something."

"An offer," Eli said. His voice was steadier than it had been in months, but only just.

"A deal. Your case is strong, Eli. The prosecution has you dead to rights. Life without parole is the minimum you're looking at. You know this."

Eli nodded. He knew.

"We can change that. We have influence with the Department of Justice. We can get your charges reduced. Involuntary manslaughter instead of felony murder. Time served, maybe a few more years in a minimum-security facility with a music program and a library. You'd be out before you were thirty."

"In exchange for what?"

Morrison leaned forward. "The man who visited you. Joshua. The one who calls himself Joshua. We need to know where he is, and we need you to help us bring him in."

The room went very still. Eli stared at the two men across the table, and something inside him—something that had been slowly, tentatively rebuilding itself—began to crack.

"Why?" he asked. "He hasn't done anything."

"That's not your concern. Your concern is your future. And the future of the people who depend on you."

"I don't have anyone who depends on me."

"Your mother is still alive. She visits you once a month. She's been writing letters to the parole board. She's not well, Eli. If you get life without parole, you'll never see her again outside these walls."

Eli's hands began to tremble. He had spent fourteen months in prison, had accepted the probability that he would die there. But the prospect of a way out—of music, of sunlight, of his mother—opened a door inside him that he had sealed shut.

"What do I have to do?" he asked.

---

The plan was simple. Eli would be transferred to a holding facility in New York, ostensibly for a court appearance. He would be given a monitored phone and instructed to contact Joshua—he had been told how, through the loose network of people who moved around the edges of the rooftop gatherings. He would arrange a meeting. And when Joshua arrived, the agents would be waiting.

"Is he dangerous?" Eli asked, his voice small.

"We don't believe so. But we need to contain him. For public safety."

"What does that mean, contain him?"

"It means we'll take him somewhere he can be evaluated. Treated, if necessary. He'll be safe."

Eli didn't believe them. Or rather, he believed them and he didn't, both at once. He believed that they would take Joshua away, and he believed that Joshua would be safe, and he also believed that safe was a word that meant different things in different mouths. But he was tired. He was so tired. And the thought of his mother, of a music room, of a window that opened onto something other than a concrete wall—these thoughts were stronger than his doubt.

"Okay," he said. "I'll do it."

Morrison nodded. Harris made a note. The deal was recorded, the paperwork drawn up, the signatures collected. Eli was led back to his cell, his hands still trembling, his chest tight with something that felt like hope and tasted like ash.

---

The call came three days later.

Joshua was sitting in the community garden in Brooklyn where he had helped plant vegetables the week before. The autumn sun was thin and warm on his face. Parvati was with him. She had not left his side since the night of the broadcast, though she still wasn't sure why. She had told him everything now—about her agency, her mission, her supervisor, her growing certainty that she could not do this job anymore. He had listened, as he always listened, and said nothing except that she was welcome to stay.

A young man from the rooftop gatherings appeared at the garden gate, breathless, holding a phone.

"Joshua. It's Eli. The guy from the prison. He says he needs to see you. He's out—some kind of transfer. He's in the city. He said it's urgent."

Joshua stood. He looked at the phone, at the young man's anxious face, at Parvati, who was watching him with a sudden, sharp alertness.

"Where is he?" Joshua asked.

"Some address in Queens. An apartment. He said he'd meet you there."

Joshua took the phone and listened to the message. Eli's voice, shaky and rushed, giving an address and a time. He sounded scared. He sounded like someone who needed to be seen.

"Will you go?" Parvati asked.

"Yes."

"Joshua—" She stopped, unsure what she was trying to say. Her training was screaming at her. This was how operations worked. A compromised asset. A baited meeting. She had seen it a dozen times in a dozen reports. But she was not an analyst anymore, not really. She was a woman standing in a garden, and the man she had spent three weeks studying was about to walk into a trap.

"I know what it is," Joshua said gently. "He's afraid. He's been offered something. He doesn't know how to say no."

"Then why are you going?"

"Because he needs to be seen. Even now. Especially now."

Parvati felt something break open in her chest. She had spent her whole career believing that people were threats to be managed, problems to be solved, patterns to be predicted. And here was a man who knew he was walking into a betrayal and was going anyway, not to confront, not to escape, but to love the one who was betraying him.

"I'm coming with you," she said.

"You don't have to."

"I want to."

They walked together through the streets of Brooklyn, then Queens, a thin man in borrowed clothes and a woman with a notebook she no longer opened. The address was a basement apartment in a neighborhood that time had forgotten, its windows dark, its door ajar.

Joshua pushed the door open and walked inside.

The apartment was empty. Bare walls, a single bulb hanging from the ceiling, a table with two chairs. Eli was sitting in one of them, his body hunched, his face wet with tears. He looked up when Joshua entered, and the shame in his eyes was a living thing.

"I'm sorry," Eli said. "I'm sorry. They said they'd let me go. They said they'd let me see my mother. I'm sorry."

Joshua walked toward him. He didn't look at the shadows in the corners of the room, didn't acknowledge the agents who were already moving in from the hallway. He walked straight to Eli, knelt beside his chair, and took his hands.

"I know," Joshua said. "You were afraid. You did what you thought you had to do."

"I didn't want to. I didn't—"

"Look at me."

Eli lifted his eyes.

"This doesn't change anything," Joshua said. "You're still the boy who loved music. You're still the one your grandmother taught to play. Nothing they offered you, nothing you did, can take that away."

Eli broke. His whole body shook with sobs. Joshua held his hands, steady and warm, while the agents moved into the room and surrounded them.

"Step away," one of them said.

Joshua looked up. Parvati was standing in the doorway, her face pale, her hands clenched at her sides.

"Who are you?" the agent asked her.

"Someone who works for you," she said. "Or used to."

She pulled out her identification and held it up. The agent studied it, then her face, then Joshua, then Eli, who was still crying, still holding Joshua's hands as if they were the only solid thing in the world.

"We're taking him in," the agent said. "You can ride with us."

"No," Parvati said. "I'll walk."

The agents lifted Joshua to his feet. He didn't resist. He looked back at Eli, still sitting in the chair, his body wracked with grief and relief and a shame that would take years to heal.

"Keep humming," Joshua said. "The melody is still there."

Then they took him away.

Parvati watched them load him into an unmarked van. She watched the van pull away from the curb and disappear into the evening traffic. She stood on the sidewalk for a long time, the notebook heavy in her pocket, her career in ruins, her heart beating with something that felt, for the first time in her life, like certainty.

She turned and walked back into the apartment. Eli was still there, alone now, his face buried in his hands.

"Come on," she said. "I'll take you back to the halfway house."

He looked up at her. "You're not arresting me?"

"No. I'm not arresting anyone anymore."

She helped him to his feet. They walked out of the apartment together, the betrayer and the analyst, both of them carrying something they hadn't carried before: the memory of a man who had walked into a trap and spent his last moments of freedom comforting the friend who had set it.

---

The van drove east, toward an airfield where a plane was waiting. Joshua sat in the back, his hands cuffed loosely in front of him, his expression unchanged. The agents flanking him were silent, unnerved by his calm.

"Are you afraid?" one of them asked, not sure why he was asking.

"No," Joshua said.

"You should be."

"Should I?"

The agent had no answer. The van rumbled on through the darkening city. Outside the window, the lights of Queens flickered past, then the lights of Long Island, then the long, flat darkness of the coast.

And somewhere behind them, in a small apartment in Queens, a young man was humming a melody his grandmother had taught him, a melody that had survived the prison and the betrayal and the shame. And a woman who had spent her life categorizing threats was walking through the streets of New York with no destination and no plan, free for the first time in her life.

And the story, which had begun on a park bench with a paper bracelet and a man with no name, was entering its final turn.
