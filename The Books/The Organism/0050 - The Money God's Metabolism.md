# Chapter 5: The Money God's Metabolism

Every living body has a metabolism. That is the process of taking in energy, using it to grow and heal and move, and getting rid of waste. In a healthy body, the metabolism works smoothly. The body takes what it needs. It uses it well. It lets go of what it does not need.

A healthy human society also has a metabolism. Money is supposed to be part of that metabolism. It is a signal. It says: *This person did something useful. Give them resources so they can keep doing it.* Like blood carrying oxygen to where it is needed, money should flow toward the people and places that keep the body alive. Farmers. Teachers. Healers. Builders. Caregivers.

But the sick body has a metabolic disorder. The signal breaks. The blood stops flowing where it is needed and starts pooling where it is not.

The Money God hijacks the metabolism. Under the idol's rule, money no longer flows toward care. It flows toward more money. The richest parts of the body hoard resources while the working parts starve. Speculation—gambling on money itself—replaces production. The signal becomes noise.

Think of it like this. You eat a meal. In a healthy body, the nutrients go to your muscles, your brain, your immune system. In the sick body, the nutrients all go to fat cells that just sit there, growing larger, demanding more. The rest of the body weakens. The heart strains. The limbs grow thin. But the fat cells keep screaming *more, more, more*.

This is what the Money God does to humanity. Wealth piles up in places where it does nothing but demand more wealth. Meanwhile, the people who grow the food, teach the children, tend the sick, and hold communities together cannot afford to live. The body starves the parts that keep it alive. It gorges the parts that are killing it.

The book CONDENSATE explains how this metabolic disorder works inside individual human brains. There is a chemical in your brain called dopamine. Dopamine is about wanting, not liking. It is the anticipation of a reward, not the reward itself. When you scroll social media hoping for likes, that is dopamine. When you buy a lottery ticket, that is dopamine. When an ad makes you want something you did not know existed five seconds ago, that is dopamine.

The sick body's economy runs on hijacked dopamine. Advertising, social media platforms, video games designed to hook you, products made to break so you buy new ones, stock market apps that make trading feel like gambling—all of it weapons the wanting. But the liking never arrives. Or it arrives and then fades fast, so you need another hit.

The body chases and chases and never catches. It craves and craves and never tastes. It is a brain stuck in a loop of wanting, and the loop makes certain people very rich. The sickness is profitable. That is why it is so hard to stop.

The metabolism breaks in two directions at once. Money spins faster and faster in the gambling halls of the wealthy, doing nothing but making more money. Meanwhile, care—the actual work of keeping people alive and well—cannot keep up. Parents raising children. Neighbors checking on elders. Volunteers cleaning parks. Artists making beauty. These things create the real wealth of the body, but money often misses them entirely. The people who do this work burn out, unpaid and unseen. The people who extract and hoard burn bright, rewarded and admired.

The body is in metabolic crisis. It consumes what does not nourish it. It starves what does. It stores energy in the form of hoarded wealth while its own immune system collapses and its own limbs go numb.

And because the membrane blocks the pain signals, the body does not even notice. It looks at the stock market going up and thinks, *I am healthy*. It looks at billionaires on magazine covers and thinks, *I am successful*. It does not see the burned-out caregivers. It does not hear the hungry children. It does not feel the dying soil.

This is the metabolism of a body in slow suicide. The idol demands more. The membrane hides the damage. The gradient pushes unfeeling leaders to the top. War convulses the body when the pressure gets too high. And the cycle spins on.

But a body can recover its metabolism. It can learn to send blood where it is needed. It can learn to tell the difference between a signal and noise. It can learn to reward care and punish harm.

That is the healing. And the healing begins when the body remembers how to feel.
