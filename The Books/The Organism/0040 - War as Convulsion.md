# Chapter 4: War as Convulsion

When the sickness grows deep enough, the body does something terrible. It starts to attack itself.

You have seen a person with a high fever shake and thrash. Their own muscles turn against them. They cannot control their limbs. The shaking is not a plan. It is a symptom of a body in crisis.

Humanity does the same thing. It turns parts of itself into enemies. It draws lines on maps and calls one side good and the other side evil. It sends its own young people to kill other young people. It burns cities, poisons rivers, shatters families. It calls these spasms *war*.

War is not a break from the sickness. War is the sickness at its worst. It is the fever spiking. It is the convulsion.

Think about what happens during a war. The membrane—the shield around the Money idol—grows thicker than ever. Governments tell their people only what keeps them fighting. The enemy becomes less than human in stories and images. The pain signals from the other side are blocked completely. Your own dead are heroes. Their dead are numbers. Your bombs are justice. Their bombs are evil.

This fog is not an accident. It is made on purpose. Leaders know that if the fog lifts, the killing might stop. And for some leaders, the killing is useful. It keeps them in power. It keeps the money flowing to weapons makers. It keeps people scared and obedient.

The book CONDENSATE tells a famous story that proves this point. It happened on Christmas Eve in 1914, during the First World War. Soldiers on opposite sides of the trenches—British and German, French and Belgian—stopped shooting. They climbed out of their holes. They walked into the open ground between them. They shared food. They shared photographs of their families. They played football. They sang songs together.

The fog had lifted. For a few hours, they saw each other as human beings. And peace broke out. Spontaneously. Without orders. Without permission.

This terrified the generals and the politicians on all sides. The next year, they made sure it didn't happen again. They rotated troops so no one would get friendly with the enemy. They increased artillery barrages so no one would have a quiet moment to think. They punished fraternization with death.

The fog was stitched back together. The convulsion continued. Four years. Twenty million dead.

This is the pattern of the sick body. War comes. The membrane thickens. The powers that be claim emergency authority. Those emergency powers never go away. After the war, a new normal settles in. The body is more controlled, more exhausted, more willing to accept things it would have rejected before.

Then the cycle repeats. A new enemy is found or manufactured. The fog rolls in again. The body convulses again. Each time, the body loses more of itself. Each time, the memory of health grows fainter.

The slow suicide creeps forward. The organism mistakes self-destruction for self-defense. The light dims a little more. And those who benefit from the sickness make sure the fog never fully clears.
