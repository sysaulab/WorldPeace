# Chapter 10: Why Being Nice Is Evil

Let me be precise before I begin. I am not against kindness. Kindness is one of the few things that has kept our species from devouring itself. Kindness is the willingness to seek another's good, even at cost to oneself. It is active, attentive, often uncomfortable.

Niceness is not kindness. Niceness is the performance of agreeability in order to avoid conflict. It prioritizes smooth surfaces over honest connection. It smiles while the house burns down. It is not a virtue; it is a social anesthetic. And in the doses we have been taught to administer, it is doing enormous harm.

### The Performance

You know the performance. Someone asks how you are, and you say "fine" even though you are not fine, because telling the truth would require them to respond, and you have learned that most people do not actually want the truth. They want the ritual. You go to a meeting where a terrible decision is about to be made, and nobody speaks up, because speaking up would be rude, would be negative, would make everyone uncomfortable. Later, over drinks, everyone agrees the decision was stupid. But the decision stands, and the consequences unfold, and the nice people who knew better have protected the comfort of the room at the expense of the truth.

In a relationship, niceness means swallowing your needs until you choke. It means never mentioning the resentment that is accumulating, layer by layer, because a confrontation might upset the other person. It means letting your partner continue a behavior that is slowly destroying the bond, because telling them would be unpleasant. The relationship looks peaceful from the outside. Inside, it is dead, preserved in the formaldehyde of perpetual pleasantness.

### How Niceness Breaks the Compass

Apply the three questions to a nice environment—a polite office, a conflict-avoidant family, a country club conversation about nothing that matters.

**Is feedback flowing?** No. Feedback is precisely what niceness suppresses. When someone has caused harm, the nice response is to say nothing. The person who caused the harm never learns. The person who was harmed swallows the pain alone. The system continues, unchanged, producing more harm, because the loop that would correct it has been severed. Niceness is the lubricant that allows injustice to run smoothly.

**Is curiosity alive?** No. Niceness is the enemy of genuine curiosity. Real curiosity asks difficult questions, risks awkwardness, probes into what might be uncomfortable. Niceness keeps the conversation on safe topics. It does not want to know what is really happening below the surface, because knowing might require a response, and responding might be difficult.

**Are the vulnerable protected?** They are the ones most harmed. The victim of gossip does not have their dignity defended, because defending them would make the gossipers uncomfortable. The junior employee being exploited does not have an ally at the meeting, because being an ally would mean being confrontational. The child who senses something wrong in the family is told to smile, to be polite, to not make a fuss. The vulnerable are sacrificed for the pleasantness of the powerful. This is not a minor failing. It is the very structure of how niceness functions.

### The Asymmetry of Niceness

Niceness is not distributed equally in any hierarchy. It flows upward. The powerful are surrounded by niceness. Assistants are nice. Staff are nice. Everyone agrees, everyone smiles, no one displeases. The powerful become insulated in a cushion of pleasant lies. They can cause enormous damage and never feel the sting of it, because the people around them have been trained, by the logic of the system, to protect their comfort at all costs.

The vulnerable, by contrast, receive very little niceness. They are told hard truths, often brutally. They are told they are lazy, undeserving, responsible for their own suffering. Or they are ignored entirely. No one is smoothing their path. No one is protecting them from discomfort. They live in the raw, unvarnished reality that the powerful are shielded from.

Niceness, in its social function, is a tool of hierarchy. It is one of the ways the powerful are protected from the consequences of their actions. And it is dressed in the language of virtue—being pleasant, being agreeable, keeping the peace. But the peace it keeps is the peace of a graveyard. Everyone is quiet because everyone is afraid to speak.

### The Alternative

I am not asking you to become cruel. I am not asking you to weaponize honesty as a cover for aggression. Cruelty is just the other side of the same broken coin. The person who is "brutally honest" is often just as unwilling to receive feedback as the person who is perpetually nice. They want to dish it out, not receive it.

The alternative to niceness is **kindness in truth**. It is the willingness to speak what is real, even when it is hard, out of a genuine care for the other person and for the health of the system. It looks like this:

- Telling your partner, with love, that something they are doing is hurting you, instead of burying it until you explode or leave.
- Telling your boss, with respect, that a decision will have consequences they may not have considered, instead of nodding and then complaining to your colleagues.
- Telling your friend, with compassion, that you are worried about them, instead of pretending everything is fine while you watch them spiral.
- Refusing to laugh at a racist joke, not because you are self-righteous, but because your silence would be a lie about what you believe.

Kindness is not always smooth. It can be awkward. It can cause temporary pain. But it is real. It comes from a genuine desire for the other's good, and for the health of the relationship or community. Niceness comes from a desire to avoid my own discomfort. The two are opposites, and they lead to opposite outcomes.

### The Courage to Displease

There is a cost to speaking truth in an environment of niceness. You may be seen as difficult, negative, combative. You may lose social standing, or a job, or a relationship. I am not minimizing that cost.

But the cost of perpetual niceness is far higher. It is the slow death of your own soul, as you learn to swallow your perceptions until you can no longer trust yourself. It is the quiet destruction of relationships, as the unspoken accumulates until love is replaced by a brittle politeness. It is the maintenance of systems of harm that continue, decade after decade, because no one inside them is willing to be rude enough to name the harm.

The compass demands a certain kind of courage. The courage to keep feedback flowing even when it is uncomfortable. The courage to stay curious even when the questions threaten the peace. The courage to protect the vulnerable even when the powerful will be displeased. That courage is not loud or heroic. It is quiet, persistent, and entirely available to ordinary people.

You do not have to be a saint. You do not have to be perfect. You only have to be willing, in one conversation, in one meeting, in one relationship, to choose truth over comfort. That is the crack in the fog. That is the door opening a little wider.
