# Prologue: The First Witness

October 15, 1947. White Sands Proving Ground, New Mexico. 02:14 hours.

The barracks were empty. The other men had been debriefed and dismissed hours ago, their memories already blurring into the official story that was being typed up somewhere in a windowless room. Harold Evans sat on the edge of his cot, a flashlight propped on the pillow beside him, its beam a narrow cone of yellow in the darkness. He had a notebook open on his knee—not the leather-bound one the intelligence officer had confiscated, but a cheap spiral thing he had bought at the base commissary three weeks ago and never used.

He was writing.

His hand was steady, but the words came slowly. He was not trying to record the ultimatum. Those words were already locked in his skull, and he would write them down later, in a different notebook, in a code only one other person would recognise. For now, he was writing something else. Something he could not explain.

*The metal was warm. Not hot from re-entry. Warm like skin. Like it had been alive for a very long time and was only pretending to be dead.*

He paused. The flashlight flickered, and he tapped it against his knee until the beam steadied. Outside, the desert wind was rising, scraping sand against the corrugated walls of the barracks. Somewhere in the distance, a generator coughed and died.

*The light came out of the disc like it was breathing. Not a beam. Not a projection. It gathered itself. Shaped itself. And when it was finished, it looked at me.*

He stopped writing and stared at the page. The next words were the ones he could not explain, the ones that would get him sent to a psychiatrist if he ever spoke them aloud. But this notebook was not for the psychiatrists. It was not for the debriefing officers or the brass at Majestic or any of the men who had already decided what the story would be. It was for someone else. Someone who might find it, years from now, when the truth had been buried so deep that no one remembered it had ever existed.

*It looked at me. Not at the colonel. Not at the soldiers with their guns. At me. It knew my name. It didn't say it, but I could feel it, the way you feel someone watching you from across a room. And its eyes—they weren't human, but they held something I recognised. Sorrow. Regret. As if it had done this before, many times, and knew how it was going to end.*

The flashlight dimmed again. Harold set down the pen and rubbed his eyes. He had not slept in thirty-six hours. His coffee thermos, the green enamel one his mother had given him, sat empty on the floor beside the cot. He thought about his children—Thomas, who was five and already too clever for his own good; James, who was barely a year old and still cried in the night; Elena, who was not yet born but whose name they had already chosen. He thought about the world they would inherit, and the words the woman of light had spoken, and the clock that had started ticking the moment the disc opened in the sand.

*They're going to change it,* he wrote. *Whatever I say, they'll change it. They'll make it sound like a gift, like a blessing, like something we should be grateful for. But it wasn't a gift. It was a warning. And warnings don't mean anything if no one remembers them.*

He tore the page from the notebook. He folded it carefully, once, twice, until it was a small square no larger than a matchbook. Then he reached for his jacket—the standard-issue field jacket he had worn into the desert that morning—and felt along the lining until he found the small tear he had noticed weeks ago and never mended.

He slipped the folded page into the tear. He pressed the lining flat. The paper was invisible, a secret buried in fabric, waiting.

He did not know if anyone would ever find it. He did not know if the truth would survive him, or his children, or the century that stretched ahead like a road into darkness. But he had written it down. He had hidden it. He had done the one thing the woman of light had not expected: he had refused to forget.

The flashlight died. Harold sat in the darkness, his hand pressed against the lining of his jacket, feeling the faint ridge of the paper beneath the fabric. The desert wind moaned against the walls. The generator stayed dead. And somewhere, out in the gypsum sand, a disc of warm metal waited for the dawn.

The first witness had planted his seed.

It would take a hundred years to grow.
