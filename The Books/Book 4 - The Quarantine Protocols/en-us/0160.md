# Chapter 16: The Final Year Before

October 2046. One year remained.

Berlin had changed. The city was a palimpsest of the twentieth century's worst impulses and the twenty-first century's desperate adaptations—concrete bunkers converted into vertical farms, the Brandenburg Gate framed by atmospheric processors that laboured to scrub the air of carbon. The streets were quiet, patrolled by municipal drones that enforced the curfews that had become permanent after the Summer Riots of '38. The population was thinner than it had been a decade ago, winnowed by migration and declining birth rates and the slow, grinding attrition of a world that was running out of future.

In a rented apartment on the fifth floor of a building that had once housed East German bureaucrats, two old men were preparing to commit an act of planetary treason.

David Evans was seventy-eight years old. His hair was white, his spine curved by decades of desk work and the particular osteoporosis of a life spent underground. His hands shook now, a familial tremor that the Gardener-derived medical technology could not entirely correct. But his eyes—pale blue, the Evans eyes—were still sharp, still watchful, still burning with a purpose that had outlived everyone who had tried to extinguish it.

Felix Alexov was seventy-six. He was thinner than he had been in Vienna eighteen years ago, his face gaunt, his movements careful and deliberate. A heart attack in 2042 had nearly killed him, and his recovery had been slow and incomplete. His doctors had told him to avoid stress, to rest, to let his body heal. He had ignored them. There was no time for rest. There had never been time for rest.

The apartment was bare except for the equipment: a bank of computers, a portable satellite uplink, a small fusion generator that hummed in the corner. The walls were covered with printouts and photographs and handwritten notes, the accumulated evidence of eighteen years of collaboration. In the centre of the room, on a metal table, sat a single data drive. It contained everything.

"We're ready," Felix said. His voice was thin, carrying the faint wheeze of a man whose lungs no longer worked as they should. "The upload is prepared. The satellite link is active. The encryption keys are set. As soon as we transmit, the information will be routed through twelve separate relays and broadcast across every frequency we could access. News networks, social platforms, emergency alert systems—everything we could reach."

"And Majestic-12?" David asked.

"They will block it. They have countermeasures in place—we've known that for years. But they cannot block it instantly. We've designed the transmission to fragment and replicate. Even if they shut down ninety percent of the nodes, something will get through. The question is whether it will be enough."

David walked to the window and looked out at the grey Berlin skyline. The city was quiet in the early morning light, the streets empty except for a single municipal cleaning drone that moved silently along the pavement. He thought about his father, Thomas, who had spent his life suppressing the truth that they were about to release. He thought about his grandfather, Harold, who had written the truth down and watched it be buried. He thought about Munich, and 9/11, and the Tower, and every death he had failed to prevent.

"Even if it gets through," he said, "there's no guarantee anyone will believe it. We're releasing documents that have been classified for a century. The Gardeners are a myth. The ultimatum is a conspiracy theory. Most people have never heard of it, and the ones who have think it's a lie."

"Some will believe," Felix said. "Not many. But some. And that may be enough. When the Gardeners make their announcement—and they will—there will be a record. Evidence that the truth existed before the quarantine. Evidence that we tried to warn them."

David turned from the window. Felix was holding the data drive in his hands, his fingers curled around it as if it were a relic. The drive contained the complete reconstruction they had spent eighteen years assembling. The original ultimatum, transcribed by Harold Evans in 1947. The Antarctic Accord, with its redacted threat of containment. The sanitization protocol, with its clinical description of neurological dampening. The memos from Thomas Evans, documenting the managed conflict and the deliberate suppression of the truth. The intelligence reports on Gardener technology, Gardener communications, Gardener patterns of behaviour. The names of the dead—the Rosenbergs, the athletes in Munich, the victims of every war that Operation Greenhouse had helped to perpetuate. And a statement, written by David and Felix together, explaining what the documents meant and what was coming.

It was the truth. The complete truth. The truth that Harold Evans had died trying to preserve.

"Then we do it," David said. "Now. Before they find us."

Felix nodded. He inserted the drive into the primary computer and began the upload sequence. The screen filled with code, a cascade of encryption protocols and routing algorithms. The satellite uplink hummed to life, its dish rotating on the rooftop above them. Somewhere in geostationary orbit, a communications satellite that had been quietly compromised by Felix's network three years earlier began to align itself for the broadcast.

The transmission began.

For a moment—a single, suspended moment—the truth was free. It was racing through fibre-optic cables and microwave relays and the invisible architecture of the global communications network. It was reaching servers in Singapore and São Paulo and Lagos and the dozen other cities where Felix's network had planted nodes. It was—

The door exploded inward.

The breach charge was military-grade, designed to incapacitate without killing. The shockwave threw David against the wall, his shoulder cracking against the plaster, his vision blurring into white noise. Felix was slammed backward, his chair tipping, his body hitting the floor with a sound that was lost in the roar of the blast.

Black figures poured through the door. Tactical armour. Full-face helmets. Weapons raised. Majestic-12 operational security, the final, brutal arm of an organisation that had spent a century perfecting the art of silence.

David tried to stand. A boot pressed him back to the floor. A voice, filtered through a helmet speaker, said something he could not understand. He watched, helpless, as one of the black figures approached the computer and fired a single, precise shot into the processor. The screen went dark. The satellite uplink screamed static and went silent.

The upload had been running for twelve seconds. Twelve seconds of fragmented, partial data, racing through the network, reaching some nodes but not all. It was not enough. It would never be enough.

And then the black figure turned toward Felix.

Felix was on the floor, his body curled around something—the data drive, the master copy, still clutched in his hands. His face was grey, his breathing ragged, his eyes wide with a pain that was not just physical. He looked at David, and their eyes met across the chaos of the room.

"Run," Felix whispered. The word was barely audible, a breath, a final instruction.

He did not run. He could not run. He was seventy-six years old, and his heart was failing, and the black figures were already reaching for him, dragging him up, pulling the data drive from his fingers.

"Don't—" David started, but the boot pressed harder against his chest.

Felix's body went rigid. His hand flew to his chest. His mouth opened, but no sound came. And then, with a terrible, quiet finality, he collapsed. The black figures caught him, lowered him to the floor, checked for a pulse. One of them shook his head.

Felix Alexov, the son of Elena, the grandson of Harold, the man who had carried the truth from Leningrad to Vienna to Berlin, was dead.

David stared at the body of his cousin, the man he had only known for eighteen years, the man who had become the closest thing to a brother he had ever had. The data drive lay on the floor beside Felix's hand, its casing cracked, its contents possibly intact, possibly not.

"We have what we came for," one of the black figures said. "Secure the prisoner."

They pulled David to his feet. His shoulder screamed. His vision swam. The apartment was filling with more figures now—technicians, analysts, the clean-up crew that would erase every trace of what had happened here. He watched them bag the computers, the satellite uplink, the printouts on the walls. He watched them lift Felix's body onto a stretcher and cover his face with a sheet.

They dragged him out of the apartment, down the stairs, into a black van that was waiting in the street. The van had no windows. The doors closed, and the engine started, and the vehicle began to move through the quiet streets of Berlin, taking David Evans to a destination that no one would ever know.

He sat in the darkness, his hands cuffed, his shoulder throbbing, and thought about the transmission. Twelve seconds. He did not know how much had gotten through. He did not know if it would matter. He knew only that Felix was dead, and the truth was still buried, and the deadline was still approaching.

One year. One year, and the Gardeners would come, and everything they had done would have been for nothing.

He closed his eyes. He did not pray. He had never believed in God. But he thought about his grandfather, Harold Evans, standing in the desert ninety-nine years ago, watching a disc of warm metal open in the sand. He thought about the words Harold had written, the words that had been preserved through a century of silence and sacrifice and blood.

*Use the time we have given you. It is the only thing they cannot take.*

The time was almost up.

---

The van took him to an airfield. An unmarked jet took him across the Atlantic. He was not told the destination. He was not told anything. His captors spoke to each other in low, professional voices, discussing logistics and security protocols. They did not look at him. He was a package, not a person.

He did not resist. Resistance was pointless. He was seventy-eight years old, injured, exhausted, and carrying a grief that was too vast for action. He sat in the belly of the jet and watched the hours pass and thought about Felix. The way Felix had smiled in Vienna, raising his coffee cup in a toast. The way Felix had talked about his mother, Elena, the fierce old woman who had never stopped believing. The way Felix had said, at their last meeting, that he was not afraid to die—he was only afraid that the truth would die with him.

The truth had not died. The data drive was in the hands of Majestic-12, but copies existed. Felix had hidden backups in places that David did not know, entrusted to network members who would not be found. The transmission had been partial, but something had gotten through. It was not a victory. But it was not a complete defeat.

He held onto that thought. It was the only thing he had left.

---

The jet landed at an airfield in a desert. David was led out into the blinding sun, the heat hitting him like a physical force. The landscape was familiar in a way that made his stomach clench: white gypsum sand, distant mountains, a sky so blue it hurt to look at it.

White Sands. They had brought him back to the beginning.

The facility was built into the side of a low hill, a concrete bunker that had been constructed decades ago and expanded over the years. It was the original containment site, the place where the disc had sat for decades, studied and guarded and finally, in some classified operation, removed. The disc was gone now. But the facility remained, and it had been repurposed for a different kind of containment.

They put him in a cell. It was clean, well-lit, furnished with a bed and a toilet and a sink. There were no windows. There was no clock. There was only the hum of the ventilation system and the occasional rattle of a food tray being pushed through a slot in the door.

He was interrogated three times. The questions were always the same: What was on the drive? Who else had copies? Where were the backups hidden? He answered with silence. Not the principled silence of a martyr, but the exhausted silence of a man who had nothing left to give. He did not know where the backups were. He did not know who else Felix had trusted. And even if he had known, he would not have told them. The truth was out there now, fragmented and partial but real. That was enough.

The interrogators stopped coming after the third session. David was left alone in his cell, with nothing but the hum of the ventilation and the slow, grinding passage of time.

---

He did not know how long he had been there when the broadcast began.

It started as a hum, a low vibration that seemed to come from the walls themselves. Then the screen appeared—not a screen that had been there before, but a projection that materialised in the air above the door of his cell, a rectangle of pale blue light that flickered once and then resolved into an image.

The image was a clock. A simple countdown timer, black numbers on a white background, ticking down the days, hours, minutes, seconds.

Three hundred and sixty-four days. Twenty-three hours. Fifty-nine minutes. Fifty-eight seconds.

David stared at the clock. He had seen something like it before, in a dream, or a memory, or a fragment of the ultimatum that his grandfather had written down. *You have one hundred years.* The timer was the visual representation of that deadline, the final, undeniable proof that the Gardeners were real and the ultimatum was true and the century was almost over.

He was not the only one seeing it. He knew that. The clock would be appearing on every screen in the world—every phone, every television, every computer, every billboard. The Gardeners were no longer hiding. They were no longer waiting. They were announcing themselves, in the most direct way possible, to every human being on Earth.

The truth was out. Not through David's transmission, not through Felix's network, but through the Gardeners themselves. They had chosen their moment, and they had chosen it precisely.

Three hundred and sixty-four days. One year. And then the quarantine would begin.

David lay back on his cot and stared at the clock. It ticked. The hum continued. Somewhere in the facility, alarms were probably sounding, and people were probably panicking, and the machinery of Majestic-12 was probably scrambling to contain what could not be contained. But in his cell, there was only silence and the steady, remorseless march of time.

He thought about Felix, dead on the floor in Berlin, the data drive still clutched in his hand. He thought about Maya, the young ecologist in Canada, the cousin he had never met but whose name Felix had mentioned with a kind of hope. He thought about his father, Thomas, who had spent his life managing the silence and had died before the silence could break.

And he thought about his grandfather, Harold Evans, who had stood in this very desert ninety-nine years ago and written down words that the world had tried to forget.

*You have one hundred years. Use them wisely.*

The century was over. The time for wisdom was past. The only thing left was the reckoning.

David closed his eyes. The clock continued to tick. And somewhere beyond the walls of his cell, the world began to wake up to the truth.
