# Chapter 20: The Prisoner

January 2048. Nine months remained.

The desert was cold in winter, a fact that surprised those who knew it only from photographs. The gypsum sands of White Sands did not hold heat the way darker soils did; they reflected the pale winter sun with a brightness that hurt the eyes and offered no warmth. At night, the temperature dropped below freezing, and the wind came down from the mountains with a sharpness that cut through clothing and skin and settled into the bones.

Maya had been travelling for six weeks. The journey from Halifax had taken her across the continent in a series of trains, trucks, and long stretches on foot through landscapes that were emptying of human life. The Transitional Authority's evacuation was accelerating. Whole regions had been cleared, their populations shipped to the megacities, their towns left to the silent reclamation of the natural world. She had passed through the outskirts of Chicago and seen the arcology rising on the lakefront, a vast pyramid of white material that seemed to grow organically from the ruins of the old city. She had crossed the Mississippi on a barge operated by a religious sect that believed the Gardeners were angels and the quarantine was the Rapture. She had walked through Kansas wheat fields that were already returning to prairie, the grain unharvested, the farmhouses dark.

And now she was in El Paso, in a bar on the south side of a city that had become a ghost town. The border with Mexico had ceased to exist months ago; the old nations had dissolved into the new habitation zones, and the Transitional Authority recognized no boundaries but its own. The bar was called La Última Esperanza, a name that Maya's limited Spanish translated as "The Last Hope." It seemed appropriate.

The man she was looking for sat in a corner booth, his back to the wall, his eyes on the door. He was in his sixties, thick-bodied, with grey stubble and the kind of watchful stillness that came from decades of operational security. His name, according to Irina's notes, was Marcus Cole. He had spent thirty years in Majestic-12 as a facility security officer before the broadcast. His father had been Richard Cole, the CSIS agent who had visited James Evans's farm in 1990, asking questions about Harold's box. The irony of that connection was not lost on Maya: the son of the man who had tried to uncover the truth was now the man who might help her preserve it.

"Marcus Cole?" she said, sliding into the booth across from him.

He looked at her with eyes that were the colour of the desert sky—pale, washed-out, unsurprised. "You're the Evans girl."

"Maya."

"I know who you are. Irina sent word." He took a drink from a glass that contained something dark and local. "You want to get into White Sands."

"I want to get my cousin out of White Sands."

Cole laughed. It was a short, humourless sound. "David Evans is seventy-eight years old, he's been in solitary confinement for over a year, and the facility is guarded by the Transitional Authority's internal security division. You don't get him out. You don't get anyone out."

"The Liaison said he was not silenced. Not yet. If he's still there, he's still alive, and if he's still alive—"

"The Liaison." Cole's expression flickered. "You actually met him."

"In Halifax. In the archives. He gave me something." Maya reached into her coat and withdrew the chip, holding it up so he could see the faint pulse of light within. "The counter-agent. The reversal formula. It exists. David spent his entire life looking for proof that the dampening could be reversed, and now I have it. But I need him to help me understand what to do with it. He was the Deputy Director of Majestic-12. He knows the architecture. He knows the players. He knows things I don't."

Cole stared at the chip. The bar was quiet, the other patrons lost in their own private miseries. "You're telling me the dampening can be reversed."

"I'm telling you the cages have a key. And I need to get that key to the person who can use it."

There was a long silence. Cole finished his drink and set the glass down with a deliberate care. "There's a service tunnel," he said finally. "Runs from the old missile silo on the east side of the range to the sublevel of the main facility. I helped build it, back in the '20s. It was designed for emergency evacuation, but it works both ways. The Authority knows about it, but they haven't sealed it yet—too much infrastructure still running through it. Ventilation, power relays, that kind of thing."

"Can you get me in?"

"I can get you to the entrance. What you do after that is on you." He leaned forward, his voice dropping. "But you need to understand something. David Evans is not the man you think he is. I knew him. I worked for him. He was the Deputy Director, yes, but before that he was an architect of Operation Greenhouse. He wrote scripts for wars. He facilitated deaths that would make you sick if you knew the details. He let Munich happen. He was in the Tower on 9/11 because he had seen the intelligence and done nothing. He is not a hero. He is a man who spent his life doing terrible things for reasons he believed were necessary, and then spent the rest of his life trying to undo them. That does not make him good. It makes him complicated."

"I know what he did," Maya said quietly. "I read the archives. I know about Munich, and 9/11, and everything else. But he's also the man who spent twenty years trying to break the silence. He's the man who worked with Felix to gather the truth. He's the man who was arrested trying to broadcast it. And he's my family." She met Cole's eyes. "Whatever he was, whatever he did, he's the only one left who can help me."

Cole held her gaze for a long moment. Then he nodded, a single, decisive movement. "Be at the old silo at midnight. I'll have the access codes. Bring warm clothes—the tunnel isn't heated, and it's a four-mile walk."

He slid out of the booth and walked toward the door. He did not look back. Maya sat alone in the corner, the chip pulsing in her hand, the weight of what she was about to do settling over her like the desert cold.

---

The service tunnel was exactly as Cole had described it: a concrete tube, eight feet in diameter, stretching into darkness beneath the desert floor. It smelled of stale air and old machinery, and the only light came from the emergency strips on the walls, a faint green glow that pulsed in time with the ventilation fans. Maya walked for two hours, her footsteps echoing in the silence, her breath clouding in the cold air.

Cole had given her the access codes, a hand-drawn map of the facility's lower levels, and a small device that he said would disable the electronic locks on the detention level. He had not wished her luck. He had simply said, "Don't get caught," and then he had driven away into the desert night.

The tunnel ended at a steel door marked with a faded Majestic-12 insignia. Maya entered the code Cole had given her, and the door slid open with a hiss of hydraulics. She stepped into a corridor that was brighter and warmer than the tunnel—the service level of the White Sands containment facility, still operational, still maintained by whatever skeleton crew the Transitional Authority had left in place.

She navigated by memory, following the route Cole had mapped. Left at the junction. Down the stairwell to Sublevel 3. Past the decommissioned laboratory where the disc had been studied for decades before it was removed. Past the storage rooms filled with equipment that had not been touched in years. The facility was quiet, eerily so, the hum of the ventilation the only sound. She passed no guards. Cole had said the Authority's resources were stretched thin, that most of the security personnel had been redeployed to the megacity intake centers. The detention level was automated, monitored remotely, designed to hold prisoners who were not important enough to guard in person.

She reached the cellblock at the end of the corridor. There were twelve cells, arranged in two rows of six, each sealed with a transparent barrier that glowed faintly blue. Eleven of the cells were empty. In the twelfth, a man sat on a narrow cot, his back against the wall, his eyes closed.

David Evans was thinner than she had expected, his body frail, his white hair unkempt. But his face—the high cheekbones, the watchful eyes, the stubborn set of his jaw—was unmistakably Evans. He looked like her grandfather. He looked like her.

She pressed the device Cole had given her against the lock. The barrier flickered and vanished.

David opened his eyes.

"I was wondering when you'd get here," he said. His voice was hoarse, unused, but it carried a dry, weary humour. "You're Maya, I assume. You have Harold's eyes."

"You know who I am?"

"I know the family resemblance. And I know that only an Evans would be stubborn enough to break into a maximum-security detention facility." He swung his legs off the cot and stood, slowly, his body stiff from months of confinement. "Is Felix with you?"

"Felix is dead. He died in Berlin. The Authority raided your transmission."

David closed his eyes. For a moment, he looked every one of his seventy-eight years. "I know. I was there. I watched him die." He opened his eyes and looked at her. "Who else?"

"Elena died in 2019. Thomas died in 2018. James—my grandfather—died in 2032. My mother Marie died of cancer in 2025. Irina is alive. I met her on the train. She's running what's left of Felix's network."

"Then it's just the three of us. You, me, and Irina. The last of the Evans line." He walked toward her, his steps unsteady but determined. "You didn't come here just to tell me about the family. You came for a reason."

Maya reached into her coat and pulled out the chip. "The Liaison gave me this. In Halifax. It's the counter-agent. Resequencing Protocol 7. The dampening can be reversed."

David took the chip and held it up to the light. His hand was shaking, but his voice was steady. "You're certain?"

"I read the full protocol. The dampening suppresses the prefrontal cortex and the limbic system. It leaves the subject alive, conscious, but incapable of higher reasoning or emotional complexity. The counter-agent reverses the suppression. It takes months, but it works. The cages have a key."

"And the Liaison gave this to you. Directly."

"He said he was dying. He said his faction was losing. He said that if I didn't take the chip, the truth would die with him."

David was silent for a long moment. The overhead lights hummed. The ventilation fans cycled on and off. When he spoke, his voice was thick with an emotion Maya could not name.

"I spent thirty years looking for this. My father spent his life believing the dampening was permanent. Felix died trying to prove it wasn't. And you just... walked into an archive and were handed the key."

"It wasn't that simple."

"No. It never is." He handed the chip back to her. "What do you plan to do with it?"

"Spread it. Irina's network is seeding the data into the public networks. It will take months, but it will reach people. And when the quarantine begins, when the dampening is imposed, there will be people who know that it isn't forever. There will be people who can fight back."

"Fight back." David smiled, a thin, bitter expression. "You sound like Felix. He always believed resistance was possible. I was never so sure."

"What do you believe?"

He looked at her, and his eyes—pale blue, the Evans eyes—were tired and old and carried the weight of a century of secrets. "I believe that our grandfather was right. That the truth matters. That we should have told the world a hundred years ago, instead of burying it. That everything my father did—the balance, the managed conflict, the silence—was a mistake. Not because it was wrong, but because it didn't work. The quarantine is coming anyway. All we did was delay it and make it worse."

"Then help me make it better. Help me finish what you and Felix started."

David looked around the cell, at the bare walls, the narrow cot, the transparent barrier that had held him for more than a year. Then he looked at Maya—the great-granddaughter of Harold Evans, the inheritor, the one who had come all this way to find him.

"What do you need me to do?"

"I need you to tell me everything you know. The architecture. The players. The things you never put in the files. Irina can get the data out, but she doesn't understand the politics. You do. You were the Deputy Director. You know who can be trusted and who can't. You know where the bodies are buried."

David laughed. It was a cracked, painful sound, but it was a laugh. "Literally, in some cases." He steadied himself against the wall. "All right. Let's get out of this cell, and I'll tell you everything. But we don't have much time. The Authority runs a remote diagnostic on this level every six hours. When they realize my cell is empty, they'll lock down the facility."

"I have a way out. A service tunnel. It runs four miles under the desert to an old missile silo."

"Marcus Cole," David said. "I should have guessed. His father tried to break the silence once. It's fitting that his son would help us do the same."

He walked out of the cell, and Maya fell into step beside him. They moved through the corridor together, the old man and the young woman, the Deputy Director and the ecologist, the two branches of the Evans family finally united in the same cause.

"One thing," David said as they reached the stairwell. "When we get out of here, I need to go to Geneva."

"Geneva? Why?"

"Because that's where the Antarctic Accord was signed, a hundred years ago. And that's where the Gardeners will make their final announcement, when the quarantine begins. The broadcast said the deadline was absolute, but the Emissary—the one you met—she said something to me, a long time ago. She said that the quarantine was a failsafe, not a punishment. That there might be room for negotiation, if we could prove we had changed."

"The Liaison said his faction was losing. That the hardliners were in control."

"Then we need to reach the ones who are left. The ones who still believe we deserve a chance." David paused at the base of the stairwell, his breath coming hard, his body trembling with exhaustion. "I have spent my life managing crises I could not solve. Let me try to solve this one. Let me try to talk to them."

Maya looked at him—the frail body, the tired eyes, the stubborn determination that had kept him alive through a year of solitary confinement—and she nodded.

"Geneva, then. But first we have to get out of here."

They climbed the stairs together, into the cold desert night, into the darkness that stretched between the old world and the new. Behind them, the facility hummed with automated systems that had not yet noticed their absence. Ahead of them, the tunnel waited, and the desert, and the long road west.

The countdown clock was still ticking. 274 days. 6 hours. 11 minutes.

But for the first time in a hundred years, the Evans family was united. And the truth was in their hands.
