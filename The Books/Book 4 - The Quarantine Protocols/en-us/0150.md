**Chapter 15: The Ecologist**

June 2038. The boreal forest was dying, and Maya Evans was documenting its final days.

She was thirty years old now, a field ecologist with the Canadian National Research Council, and she had spent the past six years measuring the slow, catastrophic collapse of the planet's northern lungs. The forest stretched across the Canadian Shield like a green mantle, a billion acres of spruce and pine and tamarack that had once been one of the world's great carbon sinks. Now it was a carbon source. The trees were succumbing to drought and beetle infestations that no winter was cold enough to kill. The permafrost beneath them was thawing, releasing methane that bubbled up through the black spruce bogs like the earth itself was exhaling its last breath. The data was irrefutable. The trends were terminal. And Maya wrote her reports, filed her findings, and watched the politicians do nothing.

She stood at the edge of a clearing fifty kilometres north of Lake Athabasca, a place she had visited every summer since her graduate work. The clearing had once been a dense stand of black spruce, sixty years old, rooted in a thick mat of sphagnum moss. Now it was a graveyard. The trees were skeletal, their needles gone, their bark peeling in long, grey strips. The moss was dry and brittle underfoot, cracking like old bones. The air, which should have smelled of resin and damp earth, smelled of dust and decay.

She knelt and pressed her palm to the soil. It was warm. Too warm. The permafrost table had dropped three metres since her first visit in 2032. The ground that had been frozen for ten thousand years was melting into slurry, and the methane readings were off the scale.

"Still dead," said a voice behind her.

She turned. Tomas Reyes was her research partner, a soil scientist from the University of Manitoba with a perpetually sunburned face and a grim sense of humour. He was carrying a field monitor in one hand and a thermos of coffee in the other.

"Still dead," Maya agreed. "Methane is up twelve percent from last year. The bog is releasing more than a midsize city."

Tomas handed her the thermos. She drank. The coffee was bitter and lukewarm, but it was something. They stood together in the graveyard of trees, two scientists measuring the end of a world.

"Have you seen the news?" Tomas asked.

"I stopped watching the news."

"You'll want to see this one." He pulled a tablet from his pack and held it up. The screen showed a UN press briefing, the Secretary-General looking pale and exhausted behind a podium. The chyron read: *General Assembly approves Regrowth Zone framework. Evacuation protocols under review.*

"Regrowth Zones," Maya said. "That's a new euphemism."

"Quarantine zones. They're calling them Regrowth Zones now. The UN passed the resolution last night. Seventy percent of the Earth's land surface, off-limits to human habitation. They're framing it as an ecological necessity."

"Because it is an ecological necessity. We've destroyed the planet. The only way it heals is if we get out of the way."

"You sound like one of them."

Maya looked at him sharply. "One of who?"

"The conspiracy people. The ones who say it's not our idea. The ones who say something is making us do it."

Maya said nothing. She took another drink of the bitter coffee and stared at the dead trees.

She knew about the conspiracy theories. Everyone did. They had been circulating for decades, growing louder as the world grew more desperate. The visitors. The gifts. The ultimatum. The idea that humanity's slow, grinding collapse was not just the consequence of its own failures but the deliberate design of something larger and colder and utterly beyond human control. Most people dismissed it as fantasy. Maya was not most people.

She had opened the box beneath the barn when she was twenty-one, a week after her grandfather James died.

---

The funeral had been a small affair, held in the white frame church in the valley where James Evans had attended services for fifty years without ever quite believing in God. The farm had been sold by then—Maya's mother Marie had no desire to work the land, and Maya was already deep in her undergraduate studies—but the family had gathered one last time to say goodbye. After the service, while the neighbours were still eating casserole in the church basement, Maya had driven out to the farm alone.

The barn was still standing, though the roof sagged and the red paint had faded to a pale, bleeding pink. She had climbed to the hay loft, moved the bales that had not been touched in nine years, and pried up the floorboard. The box was there, exactly where her grandfather had told her it would be.

She had broken the rusted clasp and opened the lid.

The letter was still legible, though the paper had yellowed and the ink had faded. She had read it by the light of her phone, sitting in the hay loft where her grandfather had sat forty-nine years earlier, and she had felt the world shift beneath her.

*In October 1947, I was stationed at White Sands Proving Ground in New Mexico. I was the first person to detect the object that landed in the desert. I was there when it opened. I saw the being that emerged. I heard the words it spoke.*

*The world you know is built on a lie.*

She had read the ultimatum. She had read her great-grandfather's cramped, urgent handwriting, the words he had written down before the military could erase them. *One hundred years. Restore the biodiversity. If you fail, we will impose a solution.* She had read his plea to her grandfather—*the truth may be the only thing that matters when the deadline arrives*—and she had understood, for the first time, the locked room inside the man who had raised her.

Her grandfather had known. He had known since 1971, when the government man came asking questions and he broke the lock on his father's box. He had carried the secret for forty years, tending his wheat and his cattle and his silence, waiting for a moment that never came.

Now the secret was hers.

She had not known what to do with it. She was twenty-one, a student of ecology, already watching the world crumble around her. The ultimatum confirmed everything she had feared and gave it a shape, a timeline, a purpose. But it also paralysed her. What could she do? She was one person. The deadline was thirty years away. The powers that had buried the truth—the Majestic-12, the governments, the military-industrial complexes—were still in control. Her great-grandfather had tried to warn people and had been silenced. Her great-uncle Thomas had tried to manage the crisis and had become a monster. Her great-aunt Elena had defected to the Soviet Union and had died without seeing the change she had fought for.

What chance did Maya have?

She had buried herself in her work. She had become an ecologist, the most rigorous and objective kind of scientist she could imagine, and she had spent her career documenting the collapse. She told herself that the data mattered. That the truth, even if it was only a fragment of the full truth, was worth preserving. That when the deadline arrived, someone would need to know exactly what had been lost.

But she had never shown anyone the letter. She had never spoken of the ultimatum. She had become, in her own way, as silent as her grandfather.

---

The Gardener found her at dusk.

Maya was alone in the field station, a prefabricated cabin on the shore of a nameless lake, writing her daily report. Tomas had driven into Fort McMurray for supplies. The generator hummed in the background. The light through the window was the colour of whisky, slanting low across the water.

The door opened without a sound, and a figure stepped inside.

Maya looked up. Her first thought was that it was a hiker, lost or injured, though no hikers came this far north. Her second thought was that it was not human.

The figure was shaped like a woman—tall, slender, with skin the colour of dark soil and eyes that held no reflection. She wore a simple grey robe, and her feet were bare on the plywood floor. She moved with a fluidity that was almost, but not quite, natural, as if her body were a costume she had not yet fully learned to wear. The air around her was still, utterly silent, as if the world itself was holding its breath.

Maya stood. Her chair scraped against the floor. She did not scream. She did not run. She was a scientist. She had been trained to observe.

"You are Maya Evans," the figure said. Her voice was calm, low, without any discernible accent. It was the voice of someone who had spoken a thousand languages and had forgotten which one was her native tongue.

"Yes."

"I am a representative of the Gardeners. You may call me Emissary."

Maya's heart was hammering against her ribs, but her voice was steady. "I know who you are. My great-grandfather saw one of you. In 1947."

The Emissary inclined her head, a gesture that was almost a nod. "Harold Evans. The first witness. We have watched your family for a very long time."

"Why are you here?"

"You have been selected as a witness. When the quarantine begins—and it will begin—there will need to be humans who can speak to both sides. Who understand what has happened and why. Who can mediate between the managed populations and the Gardeners. You have been chosen."

Maya stared at her. The generator hummed. The lake lapped against the shore.

"Chosen," she repeated. "By who?"

"By us. By your lineage. By the circumstances of your life. Your great-grandfather recorded the original ultimatum. Your grandfather preserved it. Your mother raised you in the shadow of the truth without knowing it. And you became an ecologist—someone who understands, in precise detail, the damage your species has done. You are uniquely qualified."

"I'm not qualified for anything. I'm a field scientist. I measure methane and tree mortality. I write reports no one reads."

"You are the inheritor. Whether you accept it or not is irrelevant. The role exists. It has existed since the moment Harold Evans wrote down our words in the desert."

Maya felt something cold move through her. Not fear. Something older than fear. Something her grandfather would have recognized.

"I don't want your role," she said. "I don't want anything from you. You gave us a century to fix our planet, and we failed, and now you're going to lock us in cages. I've read the protocol. Neurological dampening. Non-sapience. You're not saving us. You're erasing us."

The Emissary's expression did not change, but something in the air shifted. The silence deepened.

"You have read fragments of the protocol," she said. "Leaked documents. Partial transcripts. You do not understand the full context."

"Then explain it to me."

"The quarantine is not a punishment. It is a failsafe. Your species has reached a point where its technological capacity exceeds its ecological wisdom. If you continue on your current trajectory, you will destroy not only yourselves but the entire biosphere of this planet. The Regrowth Zones will heal the damage. The cities will preserve your population in conditions of safety and stability. The neurological dampening is not a lobotomy. It is a temporary suppression of the aggressive, territorial, and extractive impulses that have driven your species to the brink. It can be reversed. In time. When the biosphere has recovered and your species has learned to live within its limits."

"Temporary," Maya said. "How long is temporary?"

"Centuries. Millennia. The timescale is not relevant to individual human lifespans. But it is not eternal. No quarantine is eternal. The goal is always eventual reintegration."

"Reintegration. You're going to take away everything that makes us human, and you're calling it reintegration."

The Emissary was silent for a moment. The light through the window was fading, and her form seemed to shimmer at the edges, as if she were not entirely solid.

"Your great-grandfather," she said, "asked a similar question. In the desert, in 1947. He asked if there was any version of this in which humanity passed the test. I will tell you what I told him, though he did not live to hear it. There were many such versions. In some, he destroyed his diary. In some, his children shared their knowledge. In some, your governments cooperated instead of competing. In none of those versions did your species as a whole choose collaboration over fear. That is why we are here. Not because we wanted this. Because you chose it."

"*We* chose it? I didn't choose anything. My grandfather didn't choose anything. He was a farmer. He grew wheat. He never hurt anyone."

"You are the inheritor. You carry the choices of your ancestors whether you wish to or not."

Maya felt something break inside her. Not her resolve. Something softer, something she had been holding onto without knowing it. The hope that the ultimatum was a lie. The hope that the Gardeners were a myth. The hope that the world was dying of natural causes and not of a deliberate, managed failure.

"Why are you telling me this?" she asked. "Why now?"

"Because the deadline is nine years away. Because your cousins—David Evans and Felix Alexov—have been working to gather the truth and prepare for the quarantine. Because the networks they have built will need guidance when the announcement comes. And because you are the last of Harold's line who has not yet chosen a side."

"My cousins?"

"David Evans. Felix Alexov. They met in Vienna ten years ago. They have been collaborating ever since. They believe the truth can still change the outcome. We are not certain they are wrong. But we are not certain they are right. The quarantine will proceed. The only question is whether the transition can be made less traumatic by those who understand it."

Maya sat down heavily. Her legs were shaking. The Emissary stood motionless, her dark eyes fixed on Maya's face.

"If I say no," Maya said. "If I refuse. What happens?"

"Nothing. You continue your work. You write your reports. You watch the world end. The quarantine will come, and you will be processed like everyone else. Your knowledge will be suppressed. Your memory of this conversation will be dampened. You will live a quiet, peaceful life in a megacity, and you will never know what you lost."

"And if I say yes?"

"You will carry the truth the way your great-grandfather carried it. You will bear witness. When the announcement comes—and it will come—you will speak to those who will listen. You will help them understand that the quarantine is not an extinction. You will be a bridge between the managed and the managers. It will not make you popular. It will not make you safe. But it may make the difference between chaos and something that resembles acceptance."

Maya looked out the window. The lake was dark now, the last light fading from the sky. The stars were coming out, cold and indifferent, the same stars her great-grandfather had seen in the desert ninety-one years ago.

She thought about her grandfather James, the quiet farmer who had carried the weight without complaint. She thought about her great-grandfather Harold, who had written the truth down and watched it be buried. She thought about David and Felix, the cousins she had never met, who had been working in the shadows to prepare for a deadline that no one else believed in.

She was not a hero. She was not a spy. She was a scientist who measured dead trees and melting permafrost. But she was also an Evans. And the Evans family carried the truth, even when it crushed them.

"I'll do it," she said. "I don't know what good it will do. But I'll do it."

The Emissary inclined her head again. "You will be contacted when the time is right. Prepare yourself. The years ahead will be difficult."

"When will the announcement come?"

"When the quarantine is ready. When the cities are built and the zones are designated. When the mechanisms are in place. We do not give deadlines we cannot keep."

She turned toward the door. Maya stood, her heart still hammering.

"Wait. What about my cousins? Can I contact them?"

"You will find them when the time is right. They are already looking for you." The Emissary paused at the threshold. "Your great-grandfather asked one more question, in the desert. He asked if there was any mercy in what we were doing. I told him that mercy is not a concept we fully understand. But I have observed your species for a very long time, and I have learned something of it. The quarantine is not merciful. But it is not cruel. It is necessary. And necessity, in the end, is the only language the universe speaks."

She stepped through the door, and the darkness swallowed her. The generator hummed. The lake lapped against the shore.

Maya sat alone in the cabin, staring at the empty doorway, and tried to remember how to breathe.

---

Tomas returned two hours later. He found her sitting at the desk, the report unfinished, her face pale and still.

"Hey," he said, setting down the supplies. "You okay? You look like you saw a ghost."

"Something like that," Maya said.

She did not tell him the truth. She did not tell anyone. She finished her report, filed her findings, and continued her work. But something had shifted inside her. The weight her grandfather had carried, the weight her great-grandfather had carried, had settled onto her shoulders. It was heavy, but it was also strangely familiar, as if it had been waiting for her all along.

Nine years. The deadline was nine years away.

She did not know what she would do when it arrived. She did not know if her cousins' plan would work, or if the Emissary's promises were true, or if the quarantine would be as gentle as they claimed. But she knew, with a certainty that went deeper than science, that she would carry the truth until the end.

The Evans family had been keeping secrets for ninety-one years. The time for secrets was almost over.

She looked out at the dark lake, at the cold stars, at the dying forest she had dedicated her life to measuring.

And she waited.
