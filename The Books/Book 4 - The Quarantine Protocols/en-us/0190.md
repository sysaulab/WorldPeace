# Chapter 19: The Train West

December 2047. Ten months remained.

The Halifax intake center had processed Maya's registration with the impersonal efficiency of a system that had been designed by intelligences for whom bureaucracy was not an inconvenience but an art form. She was assigned a habitation zone—the Atlantic Megacity, a sprawling arcology that was rising on the ruins of what had once been Moncton—and given a departure window of February 2048. She was told to wait. She was told to be patient. She was told that compliance ensured safety.

She did not wait.

The data chip from the Liaison was sewn into the lining of her coat, next to her great-grandfather's letter. The photographed documents from the archive were encrypted and stored on her tablet, which she kept in a waterproof case inside her bag. She had tried to upload the information a dozen times—public networks, mesh nodes, the remnant servers of the old internet—but the Transitional Authority's filters were absolute. Every transmission was scanned. Every file was flagged. The Gardeners had not come to negotiate, and they had not come to permit dissent.

If the truth was going to reach the world, it would have to travel by hand.

She bought passage on a westbound train with the last of the untraceable currency she had withdrawn before the banking systems were absorbed into the transitional economy. The train was one of the old transcontinental routes, the Canadian, which had been repurposed for refugee transport and supply distribution. It ran on a reduced schedule, once every three days, and it was packed with the displaced and the desperate: families from the flooded coasts, farmers from the dying prairies, people who had lost everything and were being shuttled toward megacities they had never seen.

Maya found a seat in a compartment near the back of the train, a narrow bench by a window that looked out onto the grey Atlantic. The harbour was crowded with ships—military vessels, cargo haulers, the strange, smooth-hulled craft of the Gardener Transitional Authority that moved without visible propulsion. The city was receding into mist as the train pulled away, its spires and rooftops and the scaffolded skeleton of the new megacity intake towers fading into the distance.

She was leaving Halifax behind. She was heading west, toward the interior, toward the desert where her great-grandfather had stood a century ago and where her cousin David was still imprisoned. She did not know how she would reach him. She did not know if he was even still alive. But the Liaison had said he was not silenced. Not yet. And that was enough.

The train rocked into the long, slow rhythm of the rails. Maya closed her eyes and tried not to think about the countdown clock.

---

The dining car was a converted sleeper, its tables bolted to the floor, its windows grimy with the accumulated dust of a thousand journeys. Maya sat alone with a bowl of thin soup and a piece of bread that tasted of the replicator technology the Gardeners had begun distributing—nutritious, flavourless, perfectly adequate. Around her, the other passengers talked in low, anxious voices. The broadcast. The deadline. The cities. The zones. The same words, repeated in a dozen languages, the liturgy of a world that was ending.

A woman sat down across from her.

She was older than Maya—sixty, perhaps, with iron-grey hair pulled back in a severe bun and a face that had once been handsome and was now simply weathered. Her eyes were dark and watchful, and she moved with the careful economy of someone who had spent her life in dangerous places. She set down her own bowl of soup and looked at Maya with an expression that was not quite curiosity.

"You're Maya Evans," the woman said. It was not a question.

Maya's hand moved instinctively toward her bag, toward the tablet, toward the chip. "Who are you?"

"My name is Irina." The woman paused, as if weighing whether to say more. "Irina Alexova. Felix was my father."

The dining car seemed to go very still. Maya stared at the woman, searching her face for the Evans features—the pale blue eyes, the high cheekbones, the watchful expression—and found them, faint but unmistakable, beneath the grey hair and the weathered skin.

"Felix had a daughter," Maya said slowly. "I knew he had a daughter. But I thought—"

"You thought I was dead. Most people do. It's safer that way." Irina leaned forward, her voice dropping. "I have been running my father's network since he was killed in Berlin. What is left of it. The Transitional Authority has been hunting us for years. We've lost two-thirds of our nodes. But we are still operational. And we have been looking for you."

"Looking for me?"

"You are the inheritor. That is what the Emissary called you. That is what the Liaison told you in Halifax." Irina's eyes flickered with something that might have been amusement. "Yes, we know about the Liaison. We know about the chip. We have sources inside the Authority—not many, but enough. When you accessed the archives, it triggered an alert. One of our people intercepted it before the Authority could respond."

Maya felt the world tilt. She had thought she was alone. She had thought the network was shattered and the truth was buried and she was the only one left carrying the weight. But here was Irina—Felix's daughter, her cousin, the woman who had been fighting in the shadows while Maya was measuring dead trees.

"Why didn't you contact me sooner?"

"Because we had to be certain. The Authority is very good at setting traps. My father walked into one in Berlin, and it cost him his life. I was not going to make the same mistake." Irina reached into her coat and withdrew a small, flat device—a data tablet, military-grade, its screen dark. "The chip the Liaison gave you. Do you still have it?"

Maya hesitated. Trust was a luxury she could not afford. But Irina was family. Irina was Felix's daughter. And if the network still existed, if there was still a way to disseminate the truth, then this might be the only chance she had.

She unbuttoned her coat, carefully, and withdrew the chip from its hiding place. It pulsed faintly in her palm, warm and alive.

Irina took the chip and inserted it into her tablet. The screen flickered to life, filling with lines of code and chemical formulae and the dense, technical language of the Resequencing Protocol. Irina read in silence for a long moment. Then she looked up, and her expression was fierce.

"It's real. The counter-agent. My father spent his whole life looking for proof that the dampening could be reversed. He never found it." She handed the chip back to Maya. "This changes everything. If we can get this information out—really out, not just fragments and whispers—it will give people something to hold onto. Something to fight for."

"Can you do it? Can you broadcast it?"

"Not from here. The Authority's filters are too strong. But there are still places they haven't reached. The mesh networks in the Pacific. The underground servers in the old Siberian bunkers. The satellite relay that my father's people left in orbit." Irina's fingers moved over the tablet, copying the data, encrypting it, routing it through channels that Maya could not follow. "I can get this to our remaining nodes. They can seed it into the public networks, a little at a time, where the Authority won't notice until it's too late. It will take weeks. Months. But it will spread."

"And then what?"

"And then people will know. The dampening isn't forever. The cages have a key. That knowledge alone might be enough to keep resistance alive. Even after the quarantine begins." Irina closed the tablet and looked at Maya with an intensity that was almost frightening. "But you can't stay here. The Authority will be looking for you now. They know someone accessed the archives, and they will trace it back to you. You need to get to David."

"David is in White Sands. I don't even know if he's still alive."

"He is alive. Our sources confirmed it three days ago. He is being held in the old containment facility, the same place where the original disc was studied. They are keeping him isolated, but they have not dampened him yet. They are waiting for something—we don't know what."

"How am I supposed to reach him? White Sands is in the middle of a Regrowth Zone. The border is sealed."

"There are ways. Old tunnels. Smuggler routes. People who know how to cross." Irina reached into her coat again and pulled out a folded map, hand-drawn, marked with coordinates and notes in a language Maya did not recognize. "This will get you to the perimeter. From there, you will need to find a guide. There is a man in El Paso—a former Majestic-12 security officer who defected after the broadcast. He knows the facility. He can get you inside."

"Why would he help me?"

"Because my father saved his life, a long time ago. And because he believes, as we do, that the truth matters." Irina stood, tucking the tablet back into her coat. "I have to go. The Authority has checkpoints at the next station, and I cannot be found with this data. But we will meet again, Maya. When the broadcast begins, you will know."

She turned to leave. Maya caught her arm.

"Wait. You said you're Felix's daughter. That makes you my cousin. The same as David."

"Yes."

"Then why don't I know you? Why did no one ever tell me you existed?"

Irina's expression softened, just slightly. "Because my father believed that the fewer people who knew about me, the safer I would be. He was wrong, in the end. The Authority found him anyway. But he was right about one thing: the Evans family carries the truth, and the truth is dangerous. I have been hiding my whole life. You have been living in the open, waiting for the truth to find you. Both paths have their costs."

She squeezed Maya's hand, a brief, fierce gesture. "Take care of yourself, cousin. The road ahead is hard. But you are not walking it alone."

And then she was gone, slipping through the dining car and into the next compartment, vanishing into the crowd of refugees and the frightened and the lost.

---

Maya sat alone for a long time. The soup had gone cold. The bread was untouched. Outside the window, the landscape had changed: the Atlantic was far behind, and the train was moving through the forests of New Brunswick, the trees skeletal and grey, the snow falling in thin, intermittent flurries.

She thought about Irina, the cousin she had never known, the woman who had been hiding in the shadows while Maya lived in the light. She thought about Felix, dead on a floor in Berlin, the data drive still clutched in his hand. She thought about David, imprisoned in the desert, waiting for something that no one could name.

The network was still alive. The truth was still spreading. And Maya Evans, the last of Harold's line who had not yet been silenced, was heading west, toward the desert, toward the place where it had all begun.

She reached into her coat and touched the chip, the letter, the photograph of the protocol document. The weight was heavy, but it was also familiar. It was the weight her great-grandfather had carried. The weight her grandfather had carried. The weight that had been passed down through four generations, waiting for a moment that had finally arrived.

The train rocked on. The countdown clock ticked. And somewhere beyond the horizon, the Regrowth Zones were waiting, silent and immense and utterly indifferent to the lives that were being uprooted within them.

Maya closed her eyes and let the rhythm of the rails carry her forward.

She was not alone. She never had been.
