# Chapter 17: The Broadcast

October 14, 2047. One hundred years to the day since Harold Evans watched a disc of warm metal open in the New Mexico desert, the Gardeners spoke to the world.

It was a Tuesday. Maya Evans was in the kitchen of her rented house in Prince Albert, Saskatchewan, a small city on the edge of the boreal forest that had become a waystation for climate refugees and a quiet corner of a world that was running out of quiet corners. She had moved here three years ago, after the field station funding dried up, after Tomas took a job in Vancouver and the research project collapsed under the weight of government indifference. She was thirty-nine years old, working remotely for a conservation nonprofit that was fighting a losing battle against the dying forests, and she was drinking tea from a chipped mug that had belonged to her grandfather.

The tablet on the kitchen counter flickered. Every screen in the house flickered: the tablet, the wall display, the old phone she kept in a drawer for emergencies. The flicker was simultaneous, a global blink that reset every display on Earth to the same channel. Maya picked up the tablet just as the image resolved.

A woman made of light. Tall, slender, with skin the colour of polished obsidian and eyes that held the depth of a clear night sky. She was not human, but she wore a human form as a courtesy—a hologram, a projection, a message that had been delivered many times before on many other worlds. Maya had seen this face once before, in a cabin on a nameless lake nine years ago. The Emissary. But this was not the quiet, almost gentle being who had spoken to her in the twilight. This was the voice of judgment, amplified for a planet.

"People of Earth." The voice was calm, precise, without cruelty but without any discernible warmth. It came from every speaker, every earpiece, every cochlear implant on the planet. It spoke in every language simultaneously, a seamless translation that matched lip movements that had never been human. "We are the Gardeners. We have been with you for longer than your recorded history. One hundred years ago, we delivered an ultimatum to your leaders. We gave you a century to restore the biodiversity of your planet to pre-industrial levels. That century has now expired. You have failed."

Maya set down her mug. Her hands were steady. She had been waiting for this moment, in some buried part of herself, since the night she opened her grandfather's box at twenty-one. She had been waiting since the Emissary appeared in her field station. She had been waiting since she first learned the word *ultimatum* and understood what it meant.

The hologram continued. "The imposed solution will now begin. We will announce the terms only once. Listen carefully."

A map of the world appeared behind her, rotating slowly, rendered in cold blue light. The continents were overlaid with vast red zones—seventy percent of the Earth's land surface, Maya calculated automatically, her ecologist's brain still working even as the rest of her reeled. The Amazon basin. The Congo. The Siberian taiga. The Canadian boreal. The oceans, pulsing with the same red overlay. The Regrowth Zones. The places where no human would ever walk again.

"In one year," the Emissary said, "all human populations must relocate to the designated habitation zones. These zones are the cities and their immediate surroundings that have been prepared for your containment. The cities are self-sufficient. They are powered by clean energy. They are provisioned with replicator technology that will meet all material needs. You will not need to leave them. You will not be permitted to leave them."

Maya felt the words like a blade. *You will not need to leave them. You will not be permitted to leave them.* The phrasing was careful, deliberate. One clause promised comfort. The other promised imprisonment.

"Any human found outside a designated habitation zone after the deadline will be sanitized. Sanitization is not execution. It is a process of neurological adjustment that will render non-compliant individuals docile and non-sapient. They will be relocated to the Regrowth Zones, where they will live out their lives in a state of managed simplicity. This is not a punishment. It is a failsafe. Your species has demonstrated an inability to coexist with the biosphere of this planet. We are correcting the imbalance."

The map continued to rotate. Maya watched the red zones pulse, and she thought about the boreal forest—the dead spruce, the thawing permafrost, the methane bubbling up through the bogs. She had spent her career documenting that death. The Gardeners were not wrong about the damage. They were not wrong about the failure. But the solution—the sanitization, the containment, the erasure of everything that made humanity a free species—was a cure that was indistinguishable from the disease.

The Emissary paused. Her vast, depthless eyes seemed to sweep across the world, and for a moment—just a moment—Maya felt them land on her. As if the hologram knew exactly where she was. As if the message was personal.

"We regret the necessity. We take no pleasure in what must be done. But the alternative is your extinction and the death of this world. You have one year. Use it to say goodbye to the world you have lost. Use it to prepare for the world you will inherit. The cities are ready. The quarantine will begin on October 14, 2048. There will be no extension. There will be no negotiation. There will be no exceptions."

The hologram flickered. The map faded. The Emissary's face remained for a moment longer, and then her expression shifted—softened, almost imperceptibly, into something that might have been sorrow.

"Your species is not the first to face this threshold. It will not be the last. We have seen civilizations rise and fall, and we have learned that the only mercy is clarity. We offer you clarity. The choice is no longer yours to make, but the manner of your transition is. Go to the cities. Accept the containment. Learn to live within the boundaries we have set. And perhaps, in time—centuries, millennia—you will be ready to rejoin the community of free species."

The screen went black.

The silence that followed was the loudest thing Maya had ever heard. No birds. No wind. No traffic. The whole world, holding its breath.

Then the screaming began.

---

She did not scream. She did not cry. She walked to the bedroom, opened the closet, and pulled down the small wooden box from the top shelf. The box her grandfather had given her, the one she had opened at twenty-one, the one that contained the letter and the transcript and the truth. She carried it to the kitchen table and opened it.

The letter was still there. The transcript was still there. She unfolded the yellowed paper and read the words her great-grandfather had written in the desert, one hundred years ago tonight.

*You will not be permitted to leave the cities. Your populations will be relocated. Your species will continue, but you will no longer be the authors of your own existence. You will be managed. Contained. Preserved in a state that does not threaten the larger ecological balance.*

*You will not be permitted to leave the cities.*

The broadcast had said: *You will not need to leave them.*

The difference was a single word. *Permitted* versus *need*. One version described a cage. The other described a convenience. The Emissary, in the broadcast, had chosen the softer language—the language of provision, not imprisonment. But Harold Evans, the first witness, had written down the original words. And the original words were unambiguous.

Maya had known this for years. She had read the transcript a hundred times. But hearing the broadcast, hearing the sanitized version delivered to the entire planet, she understood something that she had not fully grasped before. The Gardeners were not just imposing a quarantine. They were shaping the narrative. They were making the cage sound like a shelter. And most of humanity—terrified, desperate, facing the collapse of everything they had ever known—would believe it.

She folded the transcript carefully and returned it to the box. Then she sat at the kitchen table, staring at the blank tablet screen, and tried to think.

She was not a hero. She was not a spy. She was an ecologist who measured dead trees and thawing permafrost. But she was also an Evans. And the Evans family had been carrying the truth for a hundred years, waiting for a moment that had finally arrived.

The broadcast had not mentioned the sanitization protocol in detail. It had not described the neurological dampening, the suppression of higher cognitive functions, the reduction of non-compliant humans to a state of docile simplicity. It had called it "neurological adjustment" and "managed simplicity." Euphemisms. The truth was in the documents her cousins had died to protect—the documents that were now scattered, fragmented, hidden in backups that might or might not still exist.

David. Felix. She had never met them. She had tried to contact them after the Emissary's visit, had searched for them in every database she could access, had found only fragments: a marriage record for David Evans in 2010, a death certificate for Felix Alexov dated 2046, a sealed file that might have been David's but was classified beyond her reach. Felix was dead. David was missing, probably dead as well. The network they had built was shattered. The truth they had gathered was lost or buried.

And she was the last Evans standing.

She opened the tablet again. The screen was still black, but the network was slowly coming back online—text feeds, emergency broadcasts, the first wave of panic spreading across the remaining platforms. She navigated to the messaging app and found the name she had not contacted in years. Tomas Reyes. The soil scientist. The friend who had stood with her in the graveyard of trees and had never quite believed her when she hinted at the truth.

She typed a single line: *I need your help. Can you meet me in Halifax?*

The response came almost immediately. *What's in Halifax?*

*The truth. Or what's left of it.*

---

The train journey would take days, if the trains were still running. The roads were already clogged with panicked evacuees, even though the deadline was a year away. The airports had been shut down within hours of the broadcast. The old world was coming apart at the seams, and the new world—the world of megacities and replicators and neurological adjustment—was not yet ready to receive them.

Maya packed a single bag. The box went in first, wrapped in her grandfather's old oilcloth. She added clothes, water, the last of the canned food from the pantry. She did not know what she would find in Halifax. The Majestic-12 archives were supposed to be there, beneath the old university library—she had pieced that together from fragments of her great-grandfather's letter and the few documents she had been able to access online. If the archives still existed, if they had not been destroyed in the chaos, they might contain the full, unredacted version of the sanitization protocol. The truth about what the Gardeners really intended. The evidence that the broadcast had lied.

It was a thin hope. But it was the only hope she had.

She locked the door of the house and walked to the train station. The streets were quiet, the kind of quiet that comes before a storm. The screens in the shop windows were still flickering with the countdown clock: 364 days, 23 hours, 47 minutes. The red zones on the map pulsed like a heartbeat.

She bought a ticket with the last of her savings. The platform was crowded with people who looked like she felt—stunned, hollow-eyed, clutching their children and their belongings and their fragile, breaking faith in a world that had just been taken away from them. She found a seat by the window, pressed her forehead against the cold glass, and watched the prairie recede into the distance.

The wheat fields were gone now, replaced by subdivisions and solar farms and the abandoned husks of gas stations that had not operated in decades. But beyond the human landscape, the land was still there—the broad, flat valley of the North Saskatchewan, the poplar bluffs, the sloughs that glinted silver in the autumn light. The land her grandfather had farmed. The land she had walked as a child, asking questions about a box under the barn.

*You will not be permitted to leave the cities.*

She thought about her grandfather James, who had carried the weight for forty years and had never once complained. She thought about her great-grandfather Harold, who had written the truth down and watched it be buried. She thought about David and Felix, the cousins she had never met, who had died trying to break the silence. And she thought about the Emissary, standing in the doorway of her field station, telling her that she had been chosen.

She was the inheritor. The last witness. The only one left who could carry the truth to the end.

She closed her eyes. The train rocked beneath her. The countdown clock ticked in the corner of every screen, and the world rushed toward a future that no one had chosen.

And somewhere ahead, in a city on the edge of the Atlantic, the archives were waiting.
