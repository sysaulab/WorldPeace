# Chapter 12: The Farm

August 2020. The wheat was ready, a sea of gold that stretched from the farmhouse to the distant line of poplars that marked the eastern edge of James Evans's land. The combine had broken down twice in the past week, and the forecast called for thunderstorms by Thursday, so they were working from first light until the sun dropped below the prairie horizon. Maya was twelve years old, and this was her seventh harvest, and she knew the rhythms of the farm the way other children knew the rhythms of school or church or the changing seasons of television.

She was tall for her age, lanky and brown from the sun, with hair the colour of wheat straw pulled back in a rough ponytail. Her hands were calloused from pulling weeds and hauling buckets and the thousand small tasks that a farm demanded. She liked the work. She liked the way her body felt at the end of a long day—tired in a way that was clean and earned, not the dull exhaustion of sitting in a classroom. She liked the silence of the fields, broken only by wind and birdsong and the distant rumble of the combine.

Her grandfather was seventy years old now, his hair white, his face weathered to the texture of old leather. He moved slower than he used to, but he refused to admit it, and he still put in fourteen-hour days during harvest. Maya's mother, Marie, said he was too stubborn for his own good. James said stubbornness was the only thing that had kept the farm alive through forty years of drought and flood and falling grain prices and the slow, grinding decline of everything that had once made sense about the world.

Maya loved him with a fierceness that surprised her sometimes. He was not a demonstrative man—he did not give hugs or say I love you—but he showed his affection in other ways. The way he taught her to drive the tractor when she was ten, his hands patient on the wheel. The way he saved the best pieces of pie for her at dinner. The way he told her stories about the old days, before the climate really started to change, before the cities started to swell with refugees from the coasts, before the news became a litany of disasters that everyone tried to ignore.

She knew there was something he wasn't telling her. She had known it for as long as she could remember. There was a locked room inside her grandfather, a place he went sometimes when he thought no one was looking. She would catch him staring out at the fields with an expression that was not quite sadness and not quite fear—something deeper, something older, something that had been with him for a very long time.

She had learned not to ask. But she was twelve now, and she was beginning to understand that the world was larger and stranger than the farm, and that her family had secrets that no one wanted to explain.

---

The day was hot, the kind of prairie heat that made the air shimmer above the wheat. Maya was driving the grain truck, a battered Ford that had been old when her grandfather bought it twenty years ago. She was too young for a license, but the farm had its own rules, and she had been driving the truck since she was tall enough to reach the pedals. Her job was to follow the combine as it worked its way across the field, catching the golden stream of grain that poured from the auger, then hauling it back to the bins when the truck was full.

The combine was driven by a hired man named Pete, a quiet, sunburned farmer from down the valley who helped out during harvest. James rode in the cab with him, monitoring the yield, watching the sky for weather. When the hopper was full, Pete would signal, and Maya would pull the truck alongside. The combine's auger would swing out, and the grain would pour into the truck bed with a sound like a waterfall of kernels. It was precise, rhythmic work, and Maya was good at it.

By mid-afternoon, they had cut forty acres. James called a break, and they parked the machines at the edge of the field and sat in the shade of the grain truck, drinking water from a metal canteen that was cold from the well. Pete wandered off to check his phone, a futile gesture since the signal barely reached this far out. Maya sat beside her grandfather, her back against the tire, her eyes closed against the sun.

"Grandpa," she said, "can I ask you something?"

James took a long drink from the canteen before answering. "You can ask."

"There's a box under the barn. In the hay loft. I found it last winter when I was looking for the cat."

She opened her eyes and looked at him. His face had gone very still, the way it did when someone mentioned the government or the war or the years he had spent in the United States before he came to Canada.

"I wasn't snooping," she said quickly. "I was just looking for the cat. She had kittens, remember? And I moved some bales, and there was a loose floorboard, and underneath—"

"I know which box you mean." James set the canteen down and rubbed his eyes with the heels of his hands. The gesture was weary, ancient, the gesture of a man who had been carrying something heavy for a very long time. "Did you open it?"

"No. It has a lock. A broken lock, but I didn't open it. I put the floorboard back and I didn't tell anyone." She paused. "But I think about it. I think about it a lot."

James was silent. The wheat rustled in the wind. Somewhere in the distance, a meadowlark sang its three-note song.

"It belonged to my father," James said finally. "Your great-grandfather. His name was Harold."

"You never talk about him."

"I know. I should. He was a good man. He carried something—something heavy—and it crushed him in the end. I've spent my whole life trying not to let it crush me too."

"What was it? What was he carrying?"

James looked out at the field, at the endless golden wheat that he had planted and tended and harvested for forty years. He was not a man who told stories. He was a man who worked and kept silent and let the land speak for him. But Maya was his granddaughter, and she was twelve years old, and she was asking a question that he had been dreading and hoping for since the day she was born.

"When I was a young man," he said, "younger than your mother is now, my father gave me that box. He told me to keep it safe. He told me that if anyone ever came asking questions—anyone from the government, anyone who knew about his work—I would know when it was time to open it. He died a few years later. I never opened the box. Not for a long time."

"What made you open it?"

"A man came to the farm. A government man. He asked questions about my father, about what he knew, about whether he had left anything behind. I told him I didn't know anything. But after he left, I went out to the barn and I broke the lock and I opened the box."

Maya leaned forward. "What was inside?"

"A letter. From my father. And a piece of paper with words on it. Words that he wrote down a long time ago, in 1947, when he saw something that he was never supposed to see."

"What did he see?"

James turned to look at her. His eyes were pale blue, the same eyes that Harold Evans had carried into the desert, the same eyes that Thomas Evans had inherited, the same eyes that looked out from Maya's face when she stared into the mirror. "He saw something land in the desert. Something that wasn't from this world. And he heard a message. An ultimatum."

Maya felt the word settle into her chest. *Ultimatum.* She did not fully understand what it meant, but she understood that it was important, that it was the reason for her grandfather's silences, the reason for the locked room inside him.

"What did the message say?" she asked.

James shook his head. "I can't tell you that. Not yet. You're too young, and the weight is too heavy, and I've spent my whole life trying to protect you from it." He reached out and took her hand, a gesture so rare and so unexpected that Maya felt tears prick at the corners of her eyes. "But I promise you this, Maya. When the time is right—when you're old enough to understand—I will give you the box. And you will read the letter. And you will know what your great-grandfather saw."

"When will that be?"

"I don't know. Soon, maybe. The world is changing. The things my father warned about—they're getting closer. You can feel it, can't you? The storms, the fires, the way everything feels like it's coming apart."

Maya nodded. She could feel it. Everyone could feel it. The news was full of disasters—floods in Bangladesh, fires in Australia, crops failing in places where crops had always grown. The cities were swollen with people who had fled the coasts, and the governments were passing laws that made everyone nervous, and there was a sense, a quiet, creeping sense, that something was ending.

"My father thought he could stop it," James said. "My brother—your great-uncle Thomas—he thought he could manage it. My sister Elena, she thought she could fight it. But the truth is, no one knows how this ends. The only thing we can do is hold onto the truth and wait for the moment when it matters."

"And the box?" Maya asked. "The words that great-grandpa wrote?"

"They're the truth. The original truth. The one they tried to bury." He squeezed her hand and let go. "Now. We have forty more acres to cut before the rain comes. And your mother will be wondering why we're sitting in the shade like a pair of old men."

Maya smiled, but her mind was still turning over the words he had spoken. *Ultimatum. Something that wasn't from this world. The original truth.* She thought about the box under the barn, the broken lock, the letter she had not read. She thought about her great-grandfather, a man she had never met, standing in a desert in 1947 and seeing something that had frightened him so badly that he had spent the rest of his life trying to warn people.

She climbed back into the grain truck and started the engine. The combine rumbled to life ahead of her. The harvest continued.

But she did not stop thinking about the box.

---

That night, Maya dreamed.

She was standing in a desert, the sand white and blinding under a noon sun. The heat was immense, pressing down on her from all sides, and the sky was a vast, empty blue. Before her was a disc of metal, sixty feet across, embedded in the sand like a stone dropped into water. The metal was warm—she could feel the heat radiating from it, even from a distance.

The disc opened. Light poured out, golden and soft, and the light took shape. A woman. Tall, slender, with skin the colour of polished obsidian and eyes that held no warmth at all. She was beautiful, in the way that a storm was beautiful—terrible and indifferent and utterly beyond human scale.

The woman looked at Maya, and Maya felt something cold and vast move through her, something that knew her name and her history and the name of every person she had ever loved.

*You are the inheritor,* the woman said. Her voice was calm and absolute, the voice of someone who had delivered this message many times before and would deliver it many times again. *You will carry what your great-grandfather carried. You will see what he saw. And you will choose, when the time comes, what to do with the truth.*

"What truth?" Maya asked, but her voice was swallowed by the desert wind.

The woman did not answer. The light began to retract, folding back into the disc, and the disc began to close, and Maya was left standing in the sand, alone, the sun beating down on her shoulders and the weight of something immense pressing against her chest.

She woke gasping, her sheets tangled around her legs, her heart pounding. Her room was dark, the familiar shapes of her dresser and bookshelf and window outlined against the faint glow of the yard light outside. The dream was already fading, the details slipping away like water through fingers. The woman. The desert. The disc. The words.

She remembered the words.

*You will choose, when the time comes, what to do with the truth.*

She lay back against her pillow and stared at the ceiling. The dream had felt real—more real than any dream she had ever had. But it was just a dream. It had to be. The box under the barn, her grandfather's story, the heat of the harvest day—her mind had simply put them together into something strange and vivid.

But even as she told herself this, she knew it was not true. There was something else in the dream, something that did not come from her own memories. The woman's voice. The weight of her gaze. The sense that Maya had been seen, recognized, marked.

She did not sleep again that night. She lay in the darkness, listening to the wind in the wheat, waiting for the dawn.

When morning came, the dream had faded to a faint, nagging echo. She would not remember it fully until years later, when the world had changed and the ultimatum had been announced and the disc in the desert was no longer a secret.

But the seed had been planted. The truth was waiting, buried beneath the barn, wrapped in oilcloth and silence. And Maya Evans, twelve years old, had just taken the first step on a path that would lead her to the heart of everything her family had tried to hide.
