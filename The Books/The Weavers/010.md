## Chapter 10: The Workshop

The third SRO was the worst of the three. Its roof leaked in seventeen places. Its electrical system was a fire waiting to happen. Its basement had been used as a dumping ground for decades—not for refrigerators and mattresses, but for hazardous waste. Paint cans. Batteries. Aerosol containers. Things that should have been disposed of properly, but hadn't been.

"This is where we put the workshop," Dev said.

Leo looked at the toxic mess. "You're joking."

"I never joke about tools."

"Last week you said you never joke about food."

"Those are my only two subjects." Dev stepped carefully through the debris, avoiding a rusted drum of unknown origin. "The floor is solid. The ceiling is high. There's a loading door to the alley. It's perfect."

"It's a Superfund site."

"It's a workshop," Dev said. "It just needs cleaning."

### The Clean

Cleaning the basement took two weeks. They wore masks, gloves, coveralls. They sorted the hazardous waste into drums provided by the city's hazardous waste program—a service, it turned out, that was free if you knew who to call. Oso knew who to call. Oso knew everyone.

They scrubbed the floors with industrial degreaser. They patched the holes in the walls with concrete patch. They replaced the broken windows with wire-reinforced glass salvaged from a demolition site.

Dev installed lights—LED tubes, bright and efficient, powered by a sub-panel he wired himself. The basement had never been so bright. It had never been so clean.

"Now it's a workshop," Dev said.

"What's the difference between a basement and a workshop?" Leo asked.

"Tools," Dev said. "And purpose."

### The Tools

The tools came from everywhere.

A table saw from a carpenter who was retiring and moving to a condo. "Take it," he said. "I have no room. My wife wants it gone." Dev took it.

A drill press from a high school that was closing its shop program. "We're not teaching trades anymore," the principal said. "We're teaching coding." Dev took it. He did not comment on the irony.

A band saw from a man whose father had died. "He never used it. He just collected it. Like everything else." Dev took it. He promised to use it.

Welding equipment from a fabrication shop that had gone bankrupt. The receiver was selling everything for pennies. Oso traded a month of soup for a MIG welder, an oxy-acetylene torch, and a collection of clamps and jigs.

Hand tools from garage sales, flea markets, estate sales. Screwdrivers, wrenches, pliers, hammers, levels, squares. Dev organized them on pegboards, each tool outlined in marker so everyone knew where it belonged.

"A place for everything," he said, "and everything in its place."

"That's very philosophical," Leo said.

"That's very practical," Dev said. "Philosophy is what you do when you can't find your hammer."

### The First Repair

The workshop's first customer was a woman from the neighborhood. Her toaster had stopped working. It was a cheap toaster, the kind you buy at a discount store for twenty dollars. She could have thrown it away. She could have bought a new one. But she had no money, and she had heard about the trust.

"Can you fix it?" she asked.

Dev looked at the toaster. He plugged it in. Nothing.

"The heating element," he said. "Or the thermostat. Or both."

He opened the toaster. He tested the connections with a multimeter. He found a broken wire, soldered it, tested again. The toaster glowed.

"It'll work," he said. "For a while. But you should probably save up for a new one."

"How much?"

"Nothing. It was a five-minute fix."

The woman tried to pay him. He refused. She left with her toaster, smiling. Leo watched her go.

"You could have charged her," he said.

"I could have. But then she wouldn't come back. And she'll tell her friends. And they'll come. And then we'll have a business."

"That's not how business works."

"That's how trust works," Dev said. "There's a difference."

### The Pricing

Dev thought hard about pricing. He did not want to charge poor people for essential repairs. But he also needed to generate surplus for the trust—to buy more tools, to fix the roof, to keep the lights on.

He came up with a sliding scale.

| Service | Cost |
|---------|------|
| Small appliance repair (toasters, lamps, radios) | Free or pay what you can |
| Large appliance repair (fridges, stoves, washers) | $20–$50, depending on income |
| Tool rental (drills, saws, welders) | $5/day, with deposit |
| Custom fabrication (parts, brackets, repairs) | Cost of materials + $10/hour |
| Classes (basic electronics, welding, woodworking) | Free for trust members; $10 for others |

"It's not perfect," Dev said. "But it's a start."

"What if someone can't pay at all?"

"Then they work. An hour in the garden. An hour cleaning the school. An hour cooking in the kitchen. Everyone has something to give."

Leo wrote this down. He was writing a lot of things down.

### The Classes

The classes were the surprise success. Dev had assumed people would want cheap repairs. He had not assumed they would want to learn.

But they came. Retirees who wanted to fix their own things. Young people who wanted skills for jobs that didn't exist yet. Parents who wanted to teach their children. Children who wanted to teach their parents.

The first class was "Basic Electronics: How to Solder Without Burning Yourself." Dev taught it to a crowd of twelve, including Sylvain, who had never soldered anything in his life.

"Hold the iron like a pencil," Dev said. "Touch it to the joint. Feed the solder. Don't breathe the fumes."

Sylvain's first joint was ugly—a blob of solder that connected nothing to nothing. His second was better. His third was almost professional.

"I made something," Sylvain said, holding up a circuit board with a new capacitor.

"You repaired something," Dev said. "There's a difference."

Sylvain did not care about the difference. He had fixed a radio that had been broken for years. He gave it to Serena, who played it in her Jeep, filling the parking lot with static and oldies.

### The Night Shift

Dev worked late. He was always the last one in the workshop, cleaning the tools, organizing the pegboard, sweeping the floor.

Leo stayed with him sometimes, sitting on a stool, watching.

"Why do you do this?" Leo asked one night. "You could be working anywhere. You have skills."

Dev didn't look up from the drill press he was calibrating.

"I worked anywhere," he said. "I worked in factories. I worked in repair shops. I worked for people who treated me like a machine. Fix this. Fix that. Don't talk. Don't think. Just work."

"And now?"

"Now I work for people who say thank you. Who bring me soup. Who ask me to teach their children. Who see me as a person, not a tool." He paused. "That's why I do this."

Leo thought about this. He thought about his dissertation—six years of work for an audience of three committee members who had barely read it. He thought about the trust—the loading bay, the garden, the school, the workshop. He thought about the difference between being a tool and being a person.

"I understand," he said.

Dev nodded. He went back to calibrating.

### The Profit

The workshop turned a profit in its third month. Not a large profit—a few hundred dollars—but a profit. The money went into the trust's surplus, split three ways: paid shifts, cash dividend, tip pool.

Dev's paid shift share was the largest. He did not spend it. He gave most of it away—to the school, to the garden, to the kitchen. He kept only what he needed for rent and food.

"You earned that money," Leo said. "You should keep it."

"I kept it," Dev said. "I kept it in the community. That's where it belongs."

Leo wrote this down. He was learning that wealth was not what you held. It was what you let go.

### Side Sketch 12: The Oppenheimer Suburbans – The Soldering Lesson

*Cut to: The quiet side street. Evening. Sylvain is sitting in his camping chair, holding a soldering iron. He is trying to solder a wire to a switch. The wire is not cooperating.*

**Serena (watching):** "You're doing it wrong."

**Sylvain:** "I'm doing it exactly as Dev taught me."

**Serena:** "Dev is a professional. You are a philosopher."

**Sylvain:** "I'm not a philosopher. I'm a parking lot attendant."

**The Guy (from under his truck):** "You're not an attendant. You're just a guy who lives in a car."

**Sylvain:** "Same thing."

*He touches the soldering iron to the wire. The wire smokes. The switch falls off.*

**Sylvain:** "I hate this."

**Serena:** "Give it to me."

*She takes the iron. She solders the wire to the switch in three seconds. The joint is perfect.*

**Sylvain:** "How did you do that?"

**Serena:** "I used to build model airplanes. With my father. Before he died."

*She hands him the switch. He stares at it.*

**Sylvain:** "You never told me that."

**Serena:** "You never asked."

*She walks away. Sylvain looks at the switch. He looks at the soldering iron. He tries again. The second joint is better. Not perfect, but better.*
