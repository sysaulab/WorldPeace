## Chapter 2: The Constitution

Leo had never written a constitution before. He had read dozens—the American, the French, the German Basic Law, various revolutionary fragments from the nineteenth century. He had deconstructed them, analyzed them, critiqued their hidden assumptions and their rhetorical strategies. He had never imagined he would be asked to write one on a napkin, by firelight, in a loading bay behind an abandoned mall.

But here he was.

The morning after his first meeting, he sat on his log in Oppenheimer Park with a fresh notebook—bought with his last five dollars from the corner store—and a pen that was already running out of ink. He had written "Constitution of the Səlilwət Community Trust" at the top of the first page. Below it, he had drawn a line. Below the line, nothing.

He had no idea where to start.

He had spent six years learning to write for an audience of three dissertation committee members. He had mastered the art of the footnote, the qualifying clause, the strategic ambiguity. He had never written for a group of people who would simply refuse to follow a rule they didn't like.

Mira found him there, staring at the blank page.

"Stuck?"

"Philosophically," Leo said.

"That's the worst kind." She sat down beside him. "Tell me."

He gestured at the notebook. "A constitution is supposed to be a framework for governance. But governance assumes people will follow the rules. What happens when they don't? What happens when someone takes more than their share? What happens when someone refuses to work? What happens when someone gets violent?"

Mira was quiet for a moment. Then she said, "You're thinking like a philosopher. Try thinking like a grandmother."

"A grandmother?"

"I raised three kids in a two‑bedroom apartment in Surrey. You know how I kept them from killing each other?" She didn't wait for an answer. "I didn't. They fought. I just made sure they knew that after the fight, they still had to eat dinner together. You can't stop conflict. You can only make sure the table is still there when it's over."

Leo wrote something down. He wasn't sure what.

### The Second Meeting

That night, the loading bay was fuller. Word had spread. New faces appeared: a retired teacher, a former construction worker, a young couple who had been evicted from their apartment when the building was sold. Someone had brought a guitar. Someone else had brought a dog.

Mira called the meeting to order by banging a ladle against the side of the soup pot.

"First order of business," she said. "The philosopher has been working on the constitution. Leo, you're up."

Leo stood. He had three pages of notes, most of them crossed out. He cleared his throat.

"The constitution has three parts," he said. "Membership. Governance. Economics."

He waited for someone to object. No one did.

**"Membership,"** he continued. "Anyone who lives in the trust's buildings is a member. Anyone who doesn't live there is not a member. You can't own shares if you don't live here. You can't vote if you don't live here. You can't take surplus if you don't live here."

"That's just common sense," said the former construction worker. His name was Pete. He had a beard that looked like it had survived a fire.

**"Governance,"** Leo said. "We have three levels. Floor meetings for each residential floor. Tower councils for each building. And a Community Congress for everyone, meeting quarterly. Decisions are made by majority vote, but the Congress can delegate authority to the councils and floors for local matters."

"How do we decide who gets on the councils?" someone asked.

"You elect them," Leo said. "Every year. No term limits, but anyone can be recalled if enough members sign a petition."

"That's just democracy," said the retired teacher. Her name was Margaret. She had kind eyes and a sharp tongue.

"Yes," Leo said. "It is."

**"Economics,"** he said. This was the hard part. He took a breath.

"The trust will produce surplus—goods we make, services we provide, things we sell to the outside world. That surplus will be split three ways. One third to the people who do the paid shifts—the work that no one volunteers for, like cleaning toilets and fixing pipes. One third equally to every member as a cash dividend. And one third as a tip pool."

"A tip pool?" Pete frowned. "Like waiters?"

"Not exactly." Leo pulled out his notes. "The tip pool is distributed by members to other members. You can't spend your share of the tip pool until you give it away. You have to gift it to someone else. Once you give it, it becomes spendable for them."

Silence.

Kiah spoke first. "So if you want your full share, you have to give half of it away."

"One third," Leo said. "But yes."

"Why?" asked Margaret.

"Because it forces recognition," Leo said. "You have to notice what other people are doing. You have to acknowledge their contributions. You can't just hoard."

Mira was nodding slowly. "It's the potlatch. You give to show you have. But you also give to show you *see*."

The discussion that followed lasted three hours.

### The Argument

Some people loved the idea. They called it beautiful, visionary, the kind of thing that could actually change how people related to each other.

Some people hated it. They called it intrusive, manipulative, a violation of personal autonomy.

"I don't want to have to beg for my own money," said a man named Frank, who had been living in his car for two years. "It's my share. Let me spend it how I want."

"You're not begging," said Kiah. "You're giving. There's a difference."

"Same thing from where I sit."

"If you can't see the difference," Mira said quietly, "you haven't been paying attention to how we've been surviving all these years. We give each other things every day. Food. Smokes. A place to sleep. A ride to the clinic. That's not begging. That's being human."

The argument went around and around. Leo took notes. He crossed things out. He wrote new things. He crossed those out too.

Finally, someone suggested a compromise.

"What if we split the tip pool into two parts?" said the young woman with the laptop—Kiah. "Half goes to individual gifting. The other half goes to a community fund that everyone can vote on. That way, you still have to give, but you also have collective control over some of it."

Leo wrote this down. It was messy. It was complicated. It was, he realized, exactly the kind of thing that emerged from real people arguing in real time, not from a philosopher's thought experiment.

He looked at his notebook. The page was full of scribbles, arrows, question marks, and one sentence that he had underlined three times:

*"The goal is not to eliminate conflict. The goal is to make sure the table is still there when it's over."*

### The Vote

At midnight, Mira called for a vote on the economic framework. Not the details—those would be hammered out later—but the principle: surplus split three ways, with a portion requiring gifting.

Hands went up. Leo counted. Twenty‑seven for. Eleven against. Four abstentions.

"The ayes have it," Mira said. "We have a constitution. Sort of."

Someone laughed. Someone else passed around a bottle of something that was probably not legal. The guitar came out. The dog wagged its tail.

Leo sat in the corner, nursing a cup of tea, watching the firelight play on the faces of people who, three weeks ago, had been strangers. They were not friends, exactly. They were not family. They were something else—something provisional, something fragile, something that might break at any moment.

But tonight, it held.

### Side Sketch 4: The Oppenheimer Suburbans – Randall's Headphones

*Cut to: The quiet side street. Late afternoon. Randall's van is idling, as always. Sylvain is sitting in his camping chair, reading a paperback. Serena is walking Gimpy on a leash made of rope. The Guy is under his truck, doing something that involves a lot of swearing.*

*Randall's van door opens. Randall emerges. He is not wearing his headphones.*

*This is unprecedented.*

**Sylvain:** "Randall? You okay?"

*Randall stands in the sunlight, blinking. He looks like a man who has forgotten what the world sounds like without a layer of foam and circuitry between him and it.*

**Randall (quietly):** "They broke."

**Sylvain:** "Your headphones?"

*Randall nods. He winces as a car horn sounds three blocks away.*

**Serena (approaching with Gimpy):** "Oh no. Randall, that's terrible."

**The Guy (from under the truck):** "Can you fix them?"

**Randall:** "The solder joint. On the left earcup. I can see it. I just... can't hold the iron steady anymore."

*Sylvain stands up. He has no idea how to solder. But he knows someone who does.*

**Sylvain:** "Wait here."

*He jogs across the park toward the loading bay. The trust people are having a meeting. He doesn't go to the meeting. He goes to the back, where Dev is soldering a circuit board for the knitting machine.*

**Sylvain:** "Hey. You're the electrician, right?"

*Dev looks up. He is a wiry man with goggles pushed up on his forehead.*

**Dev:** "I'm a guy who fixes things. What do you need?"

**Sylvain:** "Headphones. A friend's. The solder joint broke. He can't do it himself."

*Dev considers this. He looks at the knitting machine. He looks at Sylvain.*

**Dev:** "Give me five minutes."

*Dev finishes his current joint—the soldering kind—and packs a small tool kit. He follows Sylvain across the park. The trust people watch them go but say nothing. The loading bay has an open door policy.*

*At the parking lot, Dev kneels beside Randall's van. Randall hands him the headphones with trembling hands. Dev opens the earcup, examines the break, and resolders the joint in under a minute. He tests the connection. He hands them back.*

**Dev:** "Should hold. But you might want to reinforce it with some epoxy when you get a chance."

*Randall puts on the headphones. The world softens. His shoulders drop. He breathes.*

**Randall:** "Thank you."

*It is the first time anyone in the parking lot has heard him speak more than one word at a time. They are all quiet, listening.*

**The Guy (emerging from under the truck, greasy and grinning):** "You sound like a human."

**Randall (almost a smile):** "I am a human."

*Dev packs his tools. He looks at the parking lot—the cars, the camping chairs, the rooftop cargo net, the sleeping bags visible through curtain gaps.*

**Dev:** "You guys should come to the loading bay sometime. We have soup."

**Sylvain:** "We have soup too."

**Dev:** "Ours is better."

**Sylvain:** "Ours is free."

**Dev:** "Ours is also free."

*They look at each other. It is not a challenge. It is an acknowledgment.*

**Sylvain:** "Maybe."

*Dev nods. He walks back across the park. Randall retreats into his van. Serena resumes walking Gimpy. The Guy goes back under his truck.*

*Sylvain sits in his camping chair. He opens his paperback. He does not read. He listens to the sound of Randall's van idling—a soft, steady hum, like a heartbeat.*

*End of Side Sketch 4.*
