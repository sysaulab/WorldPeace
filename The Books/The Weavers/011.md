## Chapter 11: The Market

The first farmers' market of the season was held in a parking lot on Commercial Drive, three blocks from the park. It was a modest affair: a dozen tents, a few tables, some local farmers selling kale and honey, a woman with a van full of bread, a man with a guitar playing songs no one recognized.

The trust had rented a table. It cost twenty dollars for the day—a fortune, in their economy, but a necessary investment.

"We're selling scarves," Kiah said. "And mushrooms. And repair vouchers."

"Repair vouchers?" Leo asked.

"Dev's idea. People pay in advance for a repair. We give them a card. They bring it in when something breaks."

"That's not how repair shops work."

"This is not a repair shop. It's a trust."

Leo helped load the table into The Guy's truck—Bertha, the unkillable. The Guy drove slowly, carefully, as if the table were made of glass. It was the most gentle Leo had ever seen him drive.

### The Setup

They arrived at six in the morning. The parking lot was empty, still dark. Kiah directed the setup: table here, scarves displayed here, mushrooms in a cooler here, repair vouchers in a tin box here.

"Scarves are ten dollars," she said. "Mushrooms are five dollars a bag. Repair vouchers are twenty dollars for an hour of labor."

"That's cheap," Leo said.

"That's what people can pay. If they want to pay more, they can. If they can't pay, they can trade."

"Trade what?"

"Anything. Vegetables. Eggs. A ride somewhere. A story."

Leo set up a sign. He had painted it the night before, on a piece of cardboard: **SƏLILWƏT COMMUNITY TRUST — SCARVES • MUSHROOMS • REPAIRS — ALL WELCOME**. The letters were crooked. The paint was smeared. It was the most beautiful sign he had ever made.

### The First Customer

The first customer came at seven. She was an older woman, bundled in a coat that had seen better decades. She walked slowly, leaning on a cane.

"Are those scarves?" she asked.

"Yes," Kiah said. "Handmade. On a machine we fixed ourselves."

The woman picked up a scarf—blue, soft, with a pattern of waves. She held it to her face.

"It's beautiful."

"Ten dollars."

The woman opened her purse. She had four dollars and some change. She looked at the scarf. She looked at her purse. She started to put the scarf down.

"Take it," Kiah said.

"I can't afford it."

"Take it. You can pay later. Or you can bring us something. Soup. A book. A story. Whatever you have."

The woman hesitated. Then she took the scarf. She wrapped it around her neck. She smiled.

"Thank you."

"You're welcome. That's what we're here for."

The woman walked away, her step lighter. Leo watched her go.

"We just gave away a scarf," he said.

"We lent it," Kiah said. "She'll pay it forward. That's how it works."

### The Rush

The rush came at nine. A crowd of people—shoppers, vendors, curious neighbors—descended on the table. They picked up scarves, examined the mushrooms, asked about repairs.

Leo sold three scarves in ten minutes. He was not good at sales. He stammered. He forgot prices. He apologized too much.

But people bought anyway. They bought because the scarves were beautiful. They bought because the mushrooms were fresh. They bought because they wanted to be part of something that wasn't about profit.

By noon, they had sold twelve scarves, twenty bags of mushrooms, and five repair vouchers. The tin box was full of cash—bills and coins, crumpled and shiny, old and new.

"We made money," Leo said.

"We made community," Kiah said. "The money is just the excuse."

### The Trade

In the afternoon, a young man approached the table. He was carrying a cardboard box.

"I heard you take trades," he said.

"We do," Kiah said. "What do you have?"

He opened the box. Inside were tomatoes—dozens of them, red and ripe and fragrant.

"I grew them. In my backyard. I have too many."

Kiah examined a tomato. She bit into it. Juice ran down her chin.

"These are the best tomatoes I've ever had."

"They're just tomatoes."

"They're just tomatoes," Kiah agreed. "What do you want for them?"

The young man looked at the scarves. He looked at the mushrooms. He looked at the repair vouchers.

"I want a scarf. For my mother. She's cold."

Kiah handed him a scarf—red, soft, with a pattern of leaves. He took it. He handed her the box of tomatoes.

"Thank you."

"Thank you. Tell your mother we said hello."

The young man walked away. The tomatoes sat on the table, red and ripe and fragrant.

"Now we make sauce," Oso said, appearing from nowhere.

"Where did you come from?" Leo asked.

"I smelled the tomatoes. From across the parking lot."

Oso took the box. He carried it to the truck, carefully, as if it held gold.

### The End of the Day

The market closed at four. They packed the table into the truck. They counted the money: two hundred and thirty-seven dollars.

"That's more than we've ever had," Leo said.

"That's enough to pay for the table next week," Kiah said. "And the week after. And the week after that."

They drove back to the loading bay. The sun was setting. The sky was orange and pink and purple.

Oso made tomato sauce. He simmered it for hours, with garlic and onions and herbs from the garden. The loading bay smelled like Italy, like home, like something Leo had never known but somehow remembered.

They ate the sauce on bread, with cheese from a woman who had traded it for a scarf. They ate until they were full. They ate until the pot was empty.

"We did it," Leo said.

"We started," Kiah said. "There's a difference."

### The Night

Leo walked back to his tent. He passed the quiet side street. Sylvain was in his camping chair, reading *The Hobbit* by flashlight.

"How was the market?" Sylvain asked.

"Good. We made money. We made friends. We made tomato sauce."

"That sounds like a good day."

"It was a good day."

Leo sat on the curb. Gimpy came over and sat on his feet.

"Sylvain, why don't you come to the market? You could help."

"I help here."

"I know. But you could help there too."

Sylvain was quiet for a long time.

"I don't like crowds," he said finally. "Too many people. Too much noise. Too many questions."

"You don't have to talk. You could just sit. Like you sit here."

Sylvain considered this. He turned a page.

"Maybe," he said. "Not next week. But maybe."

Leo nodded. He went to his tent. He wrote in his notebook:

*"The market is not a place to sell. It is a place to meet. To trade. To remember that we are not alone. That every scarf is a story. Every tomato is a gift. Every dollar is a promise."*

He closed the notebook. He slept.

### Side Sketch 13: The Oppenheimer Suburbans – The First Tomato Sauce

*Cut to: The quiet side street. Evening. The Guy is sitting in his camping chair, eating bread with tomato sauce. He is making sounds that are not appropriate for polite company.*

**Serena:** "Are you okay?"

**The Guy (mouth full):** "This is the best thing I've ever eaten."

**Sylvain:** "It's just tomato sauce."

**The Guy:** "It's tomatoes from a backyard. Grown by a stranger. Simmered by Oso. Eaten by me. That's not 'just' anything."

*He takes another bite. He closes his eyes.*

**The Guy:** "My mother used to make sauce like this. Before she got sick."

**Serena:** "What happened to her?"

**The Guy:** "She died. The sauce died with her. Until today."

*He finishes his bread. He wipes his mouth with his sleeve.*

**The Guy:** "I'm going to learn how to make this. Oso said he'd teach me."

**Sylvain:** "You? Cook?"

**The Guy:** "I can fix an engine. I can grow a tomato. I can learn to make sauce."

*He stands up. He walks to his truck. He sits in the driver's seat, staring at the dashboard.*

**Sylvain (to Serena):** "Is he okay?"

**Serena:** "He's remembering. That's hard work."

*They sit in silence. The truck idles. The stars turn.*

*End of Side Sketch 13.*
