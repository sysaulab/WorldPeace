## Chapter 7: The Kitchen

The basement of the middle SRO had been a laundry room, then a storage space, then a shooting gallery. The floor was stained concrete. The walls were water damaged. The ceiling was a maze of exposed pipes and dangling wires. It smelled of mildew, bleach, and something sweet that Oso identified as "old blood."

"It's perfect," he said.

Leo looked around. "This is perfect?"

"For a kitchen? No. For a challenge? Yes." Oso walked to the center of the room, turning slowly, his arms spread. "We need a stove. A commercial stove. Six burners, maybe eight. A griddle. An oven that can fit a whole deer. A prep table. A three‑sink station. Shelving. A walk‑in cooler if we can manage it. And ventilation. Lots of ventilation."

"Where are we going to get that?"

Oso smiled. It was not a reassuring smile. "We're going to trade. We're going to scavenge. We're going to build. And we're going to ask for help."

"Who's going to help us?"

"Everyone," Oso said. "That's the point."

### The Stove

The stove came from a restaurant supply company that had gone bankrupt. Dev heard about it from a friend who knew a guy who had been a delivery driver. The warehouse was in Burnaby, locked and guarded, but the receiver had been hired to liquidate the assets.

Oso went to see him. His name was Harold. He was a man who had seen better days and worse decisions.

"I can't give you anything for free," Harold said. "I have creditors. I have a wife. I have a mortgage."

"I'm not asking for free," Oso said. "I'm asking for a trade."

"What do you have to trade?"

Oso thought. He had the wool. He had the mushrooms. He had the soup. But those were not enough.

"I have a story," he said.

Harold raised an eyebrow. "A story?"

"About a trust. About a community. About people who have nothing building something from nothing. And I have soup. Really good soup."

Harold was quiet for a long time. Then he laughed.

"You're crazy," he said.

"Yes," Oso said. "But I'm also hungry. And so are the people I feed."

Harold sold him the stove for five hundred dollars—a fraction of its value—and a bowl of soup every day for a month. Oso delivered the soup himself, every morning, before the loading bay even woke up.

### The Ventilation

The ventilation system was the hardest part. A commercial stove produces heat, smoke, grease, and carbon monoxide. Without proper ventilation, the basement would become a death trap.

Dev designed the system. He scavenged ductwork from a demolition site, a fan from a junked HVAC unit, and a control panel from an old industrial dishwasher. He spent three weeks welding, wiring, and testing.

"It's not code," he said when he was finished. "But it works."

"What's the difference?" Leo asked.

"Code is what the city wants. Working is what we need."

The system hummed when the stove was on, pulling smoke and heat up through the ductwork and out through a vent Dev had cut into the alley wall. It was loud. It was ugly. It worked.

### The Prep Table

The prep table came from a church that was closing its kitchen. The congregation had dwindled, then disappeared. The building was being sold. The kitchen was being gutted.

Kiah heard about it from a woman she knew from the park. She went to see the pastor, a man named Reverend Thompson who had been trying to hold the congregation together for twenty years.

"You can have the table," he said. "Take anything you want. It's all going to the landfill anyway."

"Thank you," Kiah said.

"Don't thank me. Thank God. Or thank the real estate market. One of them is responsible."

They took the table. They took the shelves, the sinks, the pots and pans. They took the industrial can opener that had been there since 1972. They took everything that wasn't nailed down, and then they took the nails.

### The First Meal

The kitchen was finished on a Tuesday. Oso stood at the stove, his hands on his hips, surveying his domain. The prep table was against the far wall. The shelves were stocked with canned goods, dried beans, and sacks of rice. The sinks were clean. The floor was swept.

"Now we cook," he said.

He cooked for six hours. He made chili—enough for fifty people. He used the venison, the mushrooms, the carrots from the community garden. He used beans from the food bank, tomatoes from a farmer's market that had thrown them away at closing time, and spices that had been donated by a woman who had found them in her late mother's pantry.

At seven o'clock, the first residents arrived. They came from the SROs, from the park, from the quiet side street. They brought bowls and spoons and hungry stomachs.

Oso ladled chili into each bowl. He watched them eat. He watched their faces change—from tired to warm, from hollow to full.

"This is good," Old Mike said.

"This is the best chili I've ever had," Debra said.

Little Tony said nothing. He just ate. Then he ate again. Then he went back for thirds.

Oso leaned against the prep table, wiping his brow.

"We did it," he said.

"We did it," Leo agreed.

They stood in the kitchen, listening to the sound of people eating. It was the best sound Leo had ever heard.

### The Night Shift

After the meal, after the bowls were washed and the leftovers stored, Leo stayed in the kitchen. He sat on a milk crate, watching Oso clean the stove.

"You never told me why you became a chef," Leo said.

Oso didn't look up. "I didn't become a chef. I became a cook. There's a difference."

"What's the difference?"

"A chef has a restaurant. A cook has a kitchen. A restaurant can be taken away. A kitchen is just a place where you make food for people who need it."

He scrubbed the griddle with a metal brush. The sound was harsh, rhythmic.

"I lost my restaurant in the crash," he said. "The bank took it. The equipment. The recipes. Everything. I thought it was the end."

"But it wasn't."

"No. It was the beginning." He put down the brush. "I realized I didn't need the restaurant. I just needed the stove. And the people. The food is just an excuse."

Leo thought about this. He thought about his dissertation—four hundred pages, six years of his life, locked in a PDF on a university server that no one would ever read.

"I know what you mean," he said.

Oso nodded. He didn't ask for details. He just went back to cleaning.

### Side Sketch 9: The Oppenheimer Suburbans – The Portable Toilet

*Cut to: The quiet side street. One month after the councillor's visit. A flatbed truck arrives, carrying a blue portable toilet. Sylvain watches from his camping chair.*

**Sylvain:** "Is that...?"

**The Guy (emerging from under his truck):** "It is."

*The driver lowers the toilet onto the sidewalk. He hands Sylvain a clipboard.*

**Driver:** "Sign here."

**Sylvain:** "Who ordered this?"

**Driver:** "City of Vancouver. Parks Department. Someone named Chen."

*Sylvain signs. The driver leaves. The toilet sits on the sidewalk, blue and gleaming.*

**Serena (walking over with Gimpy):** "It's beautiful."

**The Guy:** "It's a toilet."

**Serena:** "It's *our* toilet."

*They stand in a circle, admiring it. Randall's van door opens. Randall steps out. He removes his headphones. He looks at the toilet. He nods.*

**Randall:** "Good."

*He puts his headphones back on and retreats into his van.*

**Sylvain:** "He said 'good.' That's practically a speech."

**Serena:** "We should have a ceremony."

**The Guy:** "For a toilet?"

**Serena:** "For dignity."

*They do not have a ceremony. But that night, they all use the toilet. It is the best toilet any of them has ever used.*
