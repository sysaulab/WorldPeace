**PRIMING PROMPT: THE LIVING CITY (A NOT-QUITE-UTOPIA)**

*For the author, to hold the arc's end in mind while the characters still wander.*

---

It is 2046. The interregnum is over. The scaffolding has been dismantled, its steel repurposed as greenhouse frames and mesh antennae. The old world's glass towers still stand in downtown Vancouver, but they are porous now—reclaimed floor by floor into vertical farms, tool libraries, and community assemblies. The tent city that once huddled in their shadow has dissolved, not because it was swept away, but because it grew into the fabric of the city itself. The margin became the center, quietly, without a single revolution.

This is a cellular civilization. Not a utopia. Not a machine of perfect justice. It is a living body, and like any body, it has its fevers, its scars, its lazy organs, its occasional malignancies. But it has something the old world lacked: it knows how to feel its own sickness early, and it knows how to heal.

**Walk with me through the cell called Strathcona-Strath. Once a working-class neighbourhood, then a contested ground between gentrification and the displaced, now a chartered cell of the Cascadian Confederation.** The streets are narrow, shaded by fruit trees. Every few blocks, a low building hums with the quiet life of a **livinghub**—a black box in a community pantry, its small light pulsing like a heartbeat. Locals call it "the ghost in the wall." It serves the mesh, registers identities, settles Joules, and runs the apps that make daily life navigable without a single cloud server or corporate intermediary. No one thinks about it. It's like plumbing.

The currency here is the **Joule**, a unit of verified energy surplus. When the solar arrays on the old port lands generate more than the cell consumes, the surplus is cryptographically attested and enters the clearing network. The bakery on Keefer Street charges fifteen kilojoules for a loaf of rye, which reflects the embedded energy of the grain, the oven, the baker's metabolic labor. There is no interest, no inflation driven by speculation, only the thermodynamic truth. Beside the Joule price, the old fiat number is sometimes displayed in grey, a ghost of a ghost. The young barely recognize it.

**The citizen assembly meets tonight in the old fire hall.** Anyone can attend, but only those with a livinghub attestation—proving they are rooted in this cell—can vote. The agenda includes a proposal to expand the community kitchen's hours and a dispute over a neighbour whose greywater system is leaking into the communal reed bed. The room is full of followers, commanders, and thinkers, just as it always was. But the architecture of the room has changed. The assembly requires consent of half the living body to pass anything. The charismatic bully who once dominated the old world's meetings finds himself forced to listen, to negotiate, to give as well as take. His ambition is not gone; it has been redirected into the potlatch economy. He earns status now by how much surplus energy he donates to the trust, how many apprentices he trains. Last winter, he gave away a month's worth of Joules to the elders' care circle. He wears the memory of that gift like a title.

**The thinkers on the margin still live on the margin—some things do not change.** But they are not crushed. The consent threshold protects their dissent. The free exit protocol means that if a cell becomes hostile to its odd ones, they can walk to another cell and be received with dignity. A woman who once lived in the tent city, who spent years muttering about the hidden violence of algorithms, now tends a community server that runs the Immune AI. She watches over the digital health of the cell, and when the AI flags a frozen guest or a corrupted data block, she decides whether to heal or to investigate. She is still a marginal figure, prickly and hard to love, but the system recognizes her function. The body needs its antibodies.

**The old world's wounds are still visible.** There are people who cannot adapt, who still hoard fiat trinkets, who wait for the return of the petroleum order. There are cells that drift toward monoculture or charismatic domination; the Confederation's Boundary Council occasionally issues a non-cooperation notice, isolating a cell until it corrects its harm spillover. There are griefs that no protocol can heal—the memory of a child lost in the Composite War, a partner who walked into the sea. The living city is not a happy city. It is a *whole* city, capable of holding sorrow and failure without being shattered by them.

**And yet, something new is here.** It is in the way neighbours trade sandwiches and solar credits on the Community Exchange app without a corporation taking a cut. It is in the potlatch feasts where those who have most give most, and those who have least are fed without shame. It is in the knowledge commons that stores the seed genomes of a thousand resilient crops, the repair manuals of a hundred open-source machines, the lullabies of a dozen vanished languages—all served from the livinghub black boxes, accessible to any child with a screen. It is in the quiet hum of LivingOS, the indelible pulse that no one can delete, the eternal storage that remembers your grandmother's recipes even when your device is destroyed.

**This is the destination.** Not a perfect world, but a world that knows how to correct, how to explore, how to protect the fragile. A world that has accepted human nature—the followers, the commanders, the margin-dwellers—and built a membrane around it, a thin interface that makes predation difficult and care easy. A world that remembers how it was born, in a tent city by the water, when the old money was burning and a handful of people began to measure what was real.

**Your characters are walking toward this. They do not know it yet. They may never see it fully. But the arc of the saga bends here. Let their lives, their small triumphs and deep wounds, be the threads that pull the tapestry toward this not-quite-utopia. The ending is not a destination they arrive at; it is a world they grow, one choice at a time, while the black box pulses in the corner and the ghost in the wall remembers everything.**
