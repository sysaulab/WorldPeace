## Chapter 6: The SROs

The buildings were rotting. That was the first thing Leo noticed. The second thing was the smell—a thick soup of cigarette smoke, cat urine, despair, and something else, something sweet and chemical that he later learned was fentanyl residue cooked into the walls.

The city had sold them three adjacent SROs on Powell Street for one dollar each. The price was not a gift. It was an acknowledgment of liability. The buildings had been condemned twice, cited for code violations seventeen times, and abandoned by three different owners in the past decade. They were worth less than nothing.

To the trust, they were a palace.

"This one is the least bad," Kiah said, pointing to the middle building. "It still has a roof. Mostly."

"Mostly?" Leo asked.

"Two leaks. One in the stairwell, one in the fourth floor hallway. We can fix them."

"How?"

"Dev knows a guy who knows roofing. He'll trade for a month of soup."

Leo looked at the building. The brick facade was crumbling. The windows were boarded. The fire escape had rusted into a sculpture. But the bones were there—the walls were straight, the floors were level, the pipes still held water when the city bothered to turn it on.

"We're going to live here?" he asked.

"We're going to live here," Kiah said. "And we're going to fix it. And then we're going to buy the next one. And the next. Until the whole neighborhood is ours."

### The Work

The first week was demolition.

They stripped out the rotten drywall, the stained carpets, the broken fixtures. They filled three dumpsters with debris. They wore masks against the dust and gloves against the sharp edges. They worked from dawn until dusk, stopping only for Oso's soup and the occasional cigarette break.

Leo was not good at demolition. He had spent his life in libraries and seminar rooms. His hands were soft. His back was weak. His coordination was, as Dev put it, "philosophical."

But he learned. He learned to swing a sledgehammer without hitting himself. He learned to carry a bucket of rubble without spilling it. He learned to patch a hole in drywall, to snake a drain, to replace a light fixture without electrocuting himself.

"Not bad," Dev said on the third day, watching Leo successfully install a toilet. "You're becoming useful."

"I'm becoming tired," Leo said.

"That's the same thing."

### The Residents

The SROs had not been empty. A handful of holdouts had refused to leave when the previous owner abandoned the buildings. They were the ones who had nowhere else to go—the ones who had been forgotten by every system designed to help them.

There was **Old Mike**, who had lived in the same room for twenty-three years. He was blind in one eye and deaf in one ear, but his remaining senses were sharp. He knew every sound in the building: the creak of the stairs, the rattle of the pipes, the footsteps of every resident.

"You're not the police," he said when Leo first introduced himself.

"No," Leo said. "I'm a philosopher."

"Worse," Old Mike said. But he smiled.

There was **Debra**, a woman in her fifties who had been a nurse before her back gave out. She kept to herself, but she watched everything. Within a week, she had become the unofficial safety officer, pointing out loose railings, exposed wires, and trip hazards.

"There's a reason I'm still alive," she said. "I pay attention."

There was **Little Tony**, a man in his thirties who had been in and out of prison for most of his adult life. He was tattooed, muscle-bound, and surprisingly gentle. He helped with the demolition without being asked. He carried the heaviest loads. He never complained.

"I'm not a good person," he said to Leo one evening. "But I'm trying."

"That's all anyone can do," Leo said.

"Tell that to the parole board."

### The Toilet

The toilet that Leo installed was on the third floor, Room 307. It was a small room, barely larger than a closet, with a single window facing the alley. The previous tenant had left behind a collection of empty bottles, a sleeping bag, and a Bible open to Job.

Leo cleaned the room. He patched the holes in the wall. He painted it a color that Oso called "landlord beige" and Leo called "the color of resignation." And then he installed the toilet.

It was not a complicated job. Dev had shown him how. The wax ring, the bolts, the supply line, the caulk. But when Leo flushed the toilet for the first time—and the water went down, and the bowl refilled, and nothing leaked—he felt something he had not felt in years.

Pride. Not intellectual pride, the kind he had felt when his dissertation was accepted. Something humbler. Something realer.

He had fixed something. He had made something work. He had contributed.

He stood in the doorway of Room 307, looking at the small, clean, functioning bathroom. Old Mike shuffled past.

"Nice toilet," Old Mike said.

"Thanks."

"You cry?"

"No."

"Good. No crying over plumbing."

Old Mike shuffled away. Leo closed the door. He cried a little. Just a little. For the toilet. For the room. For himself.

### The First Night

A month after they started, the first residents moved in.

They were not the trust members—the core group was still living in tents and cars. They were the holdouts, the ones who had never left. Old Mike. Debra. Little Tony. A few others.

Kiah gave them keys. Real keys, not the cheap copies from the hardware store. Keys that turned in locks that worked.

"This is yours now," she said. "You live here. You belong here. No one can take that away."

Debra cried. Old Mike pretended not to. Little Tony held his key like it was made of gold.

That night, the loading bay was quiet. The fire burned low. Leo sat with Kiah, watching the smoke curl toward the ceiling.

"One building," Kiah said. "Three more to go."

"It feels like nothing," Leo said. "Compared to what we need."

"It feels like everything," Kiah said. "Compared to what we had."

She was right. Leo knew she was right. But he also knew that the work was just beginning. The buildings were rotting. The people were broken. The system was still there, waiting to crush them.

But tonight, they had keys. And keys, he was learning, were a kind of hope.

### Side Sketch 8: The Oppenheimer Suburbans – The City Councillor

*Cut to: The quiet side street. A Wednesday afternoon. A woman in a business suit gets out of a city-owned sedan. She carries a clipboard. She looks uncomfortable.*

**Councillor Chen:** "I'm looking for the person in charge of this encampment."

*Sylvain looks up from his camping chair. Serena looks up from her laptop. The Guy looks up from under his truck.*

**Sylvain:** "There's no person in charge."

**Councillor Chen:** "Someone must be responsible."

**Serena:** "We're all responsible. That's the point."

*Councillor Chen writes something on her clipboard. She looks at the vehicles—the Yaris, the van, the Jeep, the pickup. She looks at the camping chairs, the curtains, the rooftop bins.*

**Councillor Chen:** "I've received complaints about unauthorized camping."

**The Guy (from under the truck):** "We're not camping. We're residing."

**Councillor Chen:** "There's no legal distinction."

**The Guy:** "There's a moral one."

*She sighs. She has been doing this job for twelve years. She has seen everything. She is tired.*

**Councillor Chen:** "Look, I don't want to evict anyone. But I need to show that I've done my due diligence. Can you tell me what you need?"

*Sylvain stands up. He walks to his car and returns with a piece of paper. It is a list, handwritten, with checkboxes.*

**Sylvain:** "Portable toilet. Trash pickup. A mailing address. Someone to shovel the sidewalk when it snows. That's it."

*Councillor Chen reads the list. She looks at Sylvain. She looks at the parking lot.*

**Councillor Chen:** "I can do the mailing address. I can't promise the rest."

**Sylvain:** "That's a start."

*She leaves. The sedan drives away. The parking lot is quiet.*

**Serena:** "She seemed nice."

**The Guy:** "They always seem nice. Then nothing happens."

**Sylvain:** "Something happened. We got a mailing address."

*He pins the business card the councillor gave him to the bulletin board. It sits next to Serena's pet care flyer and the hand-drawn map of bathrooms.*
