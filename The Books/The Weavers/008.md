## Chapter 8: The Garden

The vacant lot behind the SROs had been a dumping ground for as long as anyone could remember. Old Mike said he had seen refrigerators, mattresses, a car engine, and once—he swore—a stuffed moose head. The soil was gray, compacted, and laced with broken glass. Nothing grew there except bindweed and shame.

“This is where we plant the garden,” Kiah announced.

Leo looked at the lot. He looked at Kiah. He looked back at the lot.

“You’re joking.”

“I never joke about food.”

“There’s glass in the soil. And oil. And who knows what else.”

“Then we clean it. We amend it. We build raised beds. We bring in compost.” Kiah was already walking the perimeter, measuring with her feet. “This lot gets sun from noon until six. The building blocks the wind. It’s perfect.”

“It’s a Superfund site.”

“It’s a garden,” Kiah said. “It just doesn’t know it yet.”

### The Dig

The first week was excavation. They cleared the debris—the refrigerators, the mattresses, the mysterious car engine. They hauled it to the curb in wheelbarrows and shopping carts. The city, to its credit, actually picked it up.

Then came the soil. Leo had never thought about soil. Dirt was just dirt, the stuff under your feet, the reason you washed your hands. But Kiah talked about soil like a poet. She talked about tilth and texture, about microbial communities and mycelial networks, about the invisible web of life that turned death into food.

“Soil is not dirt,” she said. “Dirt is what you wash off. Soil is what you build.”

They brought in compost from the community garden behind the church—six pickup truck loads, traded for a promise of future vegetables. They mixed it with the existing soil, which was still gray but now flecked with black. They added sand for drainage, lime to balance the pH, and a secret ingredient that Oso refused to name.

“What is that?” Leo asked, watching Oso sprinkle a brown powder over the beds.

“My grandmother’s recipe,” Oso said. “Eggshells, coffee grounds, and crushed oyster shells. Also hope. Mostly hope.”

### The Seeds

The seeds came from a seed library in Strathcona. A woman named Margaret—not the retired teacher, a different Margaret—ran it out of her basement. She had hundreds of varieties: tomatoes, peppers, beans, peas, kale, chard, carrots, radishes, squash, sunflowers, herbs.

“You can have anything,” she said, “as long as you save the seeds and bring them back next year.”

Kiah chose carefully. She picked varieties that were hardy, that could handle neglect, that produced a lot for a little. She picked beans that fixed nitrogen, marigolds that repelled pests, sunflowers that broke up compacted soil with their taproots.

“These are not just plants,” she said. “They’re workers. They’re medicine. They’re the beginning of a food system that doesn’t depend on trucks and grocery stores and money.”

Leo planted his first seed—a bean, small and brown, dropped into a hole the depth of his thumbnail. He covered it with soil. He watered it. He felt ridiculous.

“Now we wait,” Kiah said.

“How long?”

“For the bean? A week. For the garden? A season. For the food system? A lifetime.”

### The Sprouts

The bean sprouted on the sixth day.

Leo saw it when he came to water. A tiny green curl pushing through the soil, still folded, still tentative. He knelt down. He touched it lightly with his finger.

“Hello,” he said.

He was talking to a plant. He was a PhD in philosophy, talking to a plant. He did not care.

The other seeds followed. The tomatoes came up in clusters. The peas twined around their trellises. The sunflowers grew an inch a day, as if racing toward something only they could see.

Kiah taught him to thin the seedlings—to pull the weaker ones so the stronger ones could thrive.

“It feels wrong,” Leo said, holding a handful of uprooted sprouts.

“It feels wrong because you’re a philosopher,” Kiah said. “You think every life is sacred. But a gardener knows that some lives are sacrifices for others. That’s not cruelty. That’s abundance.”

Leo thought about this. He thought about the tip economy, about forced circulation, about the people who had to be pulled up so the community could thrive. He did not like the metaphor. But he understood it.

### The Watering

Watering became Leo’s job. He came every morning, before the loading bay meeting, with a hose and a nozzle. He watered each bed carefully, at the base of the plants, avoiding the leaves.

“Why not the leaves?” he asked Kiah.

“Wet leaves get diseases. Wet soil grows roots. Water what you want to grow.”

Leo filed this away. He was filing away a lot of things.

One morning, Sylvain appeared at the edge of the lot. He was holding a watering can—a cheap plastic one, bright green.

“I saw you out here every day,” Sylvain said. “Thought you could use some help.”

Leo handed him the hose. “Water the tomatoes. At the base.”

Sylvain nodded. He watered the tomatoes. He did not ask questions. He just worked.

After that, Sylvain came every morning. Then Serena came, with Gimpy on a leash. Then The Guy came, still wiping grease from his hands. Even Randall stood at the edge of the lot, watching, his headphones on, not speaking, but present.

The parking lot people and the trust people, watering together.

### The Pests

The aphids came in June. They were small, green, almost invisible—until you looked closely and saw that they were everywhere, sucking the life out of the tomato plants.

Kiah was unperturbed. “Ladybugs,” she said.

“What?”

“Ladybugs eat aphids. We need ladybugs.”

They found a woman in Burnaby who sold ladybugs by the thousand. She kept them in mesh bags in her refrigerator, waiting for gardeners to call.

Oso traded a week of soup for two thousand ladybugs. They released them at dusk, sprinkling the red bugs over the tomato plants. The ladybugs went to work immediately, crawling along the stems, devouring the aphids.

“That’s the most violent thing I’ve ever seen,” Leo said, watching a ladybug tear into an aphid.

“That’s nature,” Kiah said. “Violence and beauty, together.”

### The Harvest

The first harvest was in August. Beans, peas, lettuce, radishes. A small basket of food, barely enough for a single meal.

Oso made a salad. He dressed it with oil and vinegar and a sprinkle of salt. He served it in the loading bay, on a paper plate, to anyone who wanted some.

Leo took a bite. The bean was crisp. The lettuce was sweet. The radish was sharp.

“This is the best salad I’ve ever had,” he said.

“It’s just a salad,” Oso said.

“It’s a salad from a vacant lot that was full of broken glass and a stuffed moose head. That’s not nothing.”

Oso smiled. “No. It’s not nothing.”

The tomatoes came in September. The squash in October. The sunflowers stood tall, their heads heavy with seeds, nodding in the wind.

Leo stood at the edge of the garden, looking at what they had built. A year ago, this lot had been dead. Now it was alive. Now it was feeding people. Now it was a place where neighbours came to water, to weed, to talk.

He thought about the bean. The first seed he had planted, the first sprout he had seen. It had grown into a plant that had produced dozens of beans, each one a seed for next year.

*The first gift is always ugly,* he had written. *The second gift is easier. The third gift becomes habit.*

He looked at the garden. It was not ugly. It was beautiful. But it had started ugly—in broken glass and compacted soil and the stubborn refusal to give up.

He pulled out his notebook. He wrote:

*“A garden is not a metaphor. It is a fact. But it is also a metaphor. And the metaphor is this: you can grow food anywhere if you are willing to dig.”*

He closed the notebook. He went to water the tomatoes.

### Side Sketch 10: The Oppenheimer Suburbans – The Tomato

*Cut to: The quiet side street. A warm evening. Sylvain is sitting in his camping chair, holding a single tomato. It is small, imperfect, slightly cracked. He is staring at it.*

**Serena:** “What is that?”

**Sylvain:** “A tomato.”

**The Guy:** “I can see that. Why are you staring at it?”

**Sylvain:** “I grew it.”

*Silence.*

**Serena:** “You? You grew a tomato?”

**Sylvain:** “From a seed. I put it in the ground. I watered it. I kept the aphids off. Well, Kiah kept the aphids off. But I watered it.”

*He holds up the tomato. It catches the evening light.*

**The Guy:** “Are you going to eat it?”

**Sylvain:** “I’m going to look at it for a while. Then I’m going to eat it.”

**Serena:** “That’s weird.”

**Sylvain:** “That’s gardening.”

*He looks at the tomato for another minute. Then he bites into it. Juice runs down his chin. He closes his eyes.*

**Sylvain:** “That’s the best tomato I’ve ever had.”

**The Guy:** “It’s the only tomato you’ve ever grown.”

**Sylvain:** “That’s why it’s the best.”

*He finishes the tomato. He wipes his chin. He sits back in his chair, content.*

**Serena:** “Are you going to grow more?”

**Sylvain:** “Next year. More tomatoes. Maybe peppers. Maybe cucumbers.”

**The Guy:** “You’re becoming a farmer.”

**Sylvain:** “I’m becoming a person who grows food. There’s a difference.”

*He doesn’t explain the difference. He doesn’t have to.*

*End of Side Sketch 10.*
