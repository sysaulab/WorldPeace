## Chapter 9: The School

The children appeared in the loading bay one evening in late September. There were seven of them, aged four to twelve, trailing behind a woman named Jackie who had been living in the park for three months. She had fled an abusive relationship with nothing but her kids and a duffel bag. The shelter had turned her away—too full, too dangerous, too something. She had ended up in Oppenheimer Park, in a tent next to the playground.

“They need to learn,” Jackie said. “They’ve missed so much school. I can’t teach them. I never finished high school myself.”

Kiah looked at the children. They looked back. The youngest was sucking her thumb. The oldest was trying to look tough, but his eyes were scared.

“We don’t have a school,” Kiah said.

“Then build one,” Jackie said.

That was how the trust started a school.

### The Classroom

The second SRO had a basement similar to the first—concrete floor, water damage, exposed pipes. But it had one thing the other basement lacked: a row of windows along the north wall, just above ground level. They let in a thin, grey light that felt like hope.

Dev cleaned the space. Oso painted the walls a pale yellow—leftover paint from a job site, donated by a contractor who owed the trust a favour. Serena brought a rug from her Jeep, a faded thing with a pattern of geometric shapes. Someone else brought a chalkboard, rescued from a school that had closed.

“It’s not much,” Kiah said, standing in the middle of the room.

“It’s more than they had,” Jackie said.

The children came the next day. They sat on mismatched chairs and upturned milk crates. They had no books, no pencils, no paper. They had their hands and their minds and their hunger for something to do.

Leo was the only one with any teaching experience. He had been a teaching assistant for five years, leading seminars on Kant and Hegel to bored undergraduates. He had thought he knew something about education.

He knew nothing.

### The First Lesson

“What do you want to learn?” Leo asked the children.

They stared at him.

“Anything,” he said. “Math. Reading. History. Science. Art.”

The oldest boy, Marcus, age twelve, raised his hand.

“Yes?”

“I want to learn how to fix things. Like Dev.”

Leo nodded. “Okay. We can do that.”

The youngest girl, Lily, age four, tugged on his sleeve.

“I want to learn how to draw a cat.”

“Okay. We can do that too.”

The others chimed in: reading, writing, multiplication, the names of the planets, how to make bread, why the sky is blue, where Canada is, what happened to their dad.

Leo wrote it all down. The list was long. The list was impossible. The list was a curriculum.

### The Volunteers

They didn’t need to hire teachers. The trust was full of teachers.

Mira taught reading. She had been a professor, after all, before the crash. She sat with the younger children, sounding out words, pointing at letters. Her patience was infinite.

Dev taught fixing. He brought broken appliances from the dumpster—a toaster, a radio, a lamp—and showed the children how to take them apart, how to find the problem, how to put them back together. Marcus was a natural.

Serena taught art. She brought paper and crayons from the dollar store. The children drew cats, dogs, houses, trees, their families. Lily drew a cat with three legs, because Gimpy had three legs, and that was the only cat she knew.

Oso taught cooking. He set up a small station in the corner of the basement, with a hot plate and a skillet. The children learned to scramble eggs, to chop vegetables, to measure flour. They learned that food was not just something that appeared on a plate. It was something you made, with your hands, for people you loved.

Kiah taught gardening. She took them to the vacant lot, now full of tomatoes and beans and sunflowers. They learned to weed, to water, to harvest. They learned that seeds were promises, and that promises took time.

Even The Guy came once, to teach about engines. He brought a lawnmower engine and showed the children how a spark plug worked. The younger ones were bored. The older ones were fascinated. Marcus asked if he could keep the engine. The Guy said yes.

### The Philosopher

Leo taught what he knew: how to think.

He did not teach philosophy—not the kind with names and dates and footnotes. He taught questions. Why is the sky blue? (Because of scattering, but also because of beauty.) Why do people fight? (Because they forget they are the same.) Why do we have to share? (Because sharing is how we survive.)

The children asked better questions than his undergraduates ever had.

“If we share everything,” Marcus asked one day, “what happens if someone takes more than they need?”

“That’s a good question,” Leo said. “What do you think?”

“I think we should talk to them. Find out why.”

“And if they keep taking?”

“Then we stop sharing with them. Until they’re ready to share again.”

Leo wrote this down. It was not Kant. It was better than Kant.

### The Library

The school needed books. The trust had none.

Serena found a Little Free Library on a quiet street in Kitsilano. It was stuffed with romance novels and old thrillers, but at the bottom, buried under a stack of paperback westerns, was a children’s encyclopedia. She took it.

Sylvain found a box of books on a curb in Mount Pleasant. Someone had put them out for recycling. He grabbed the whole box: picture books, chapter books, a worn copy of *The Hobbit*. He brought it to the school and left it on the table, without saying a word.

Randall, of all people, contributed a complete set of *The Chronicles of Narnia*. He had kept them in his van for years, reading them over and over. He placed them on the shelf one morning, before anyone arrived. No one knew where they came from. The children read them anyway.

### The Recital

At the end of October, the school held its first recital.

The children performed for the trust—songs, poems, a short play about a tomato that wanted to be a sunflower. Lily showed her drawing of Gimpy. Marcus fixed a toaster in under three minutes.

Jackie cried. She had been crying a lot lately, but these were good tears.

“They’re learning,” she said to Leo. “They’re actually learning.”

“They’re teaching,” Leo said. “There’s a difference.”

He looked at the children—their faces lit by the basement’s thin light, their hands stained with soil and paint and flour. They were not the children who had arrived in September. They were braver, louder, fuller.

They were becoming themselves.

### The Night

After the recital, Leo walked back to his tent. He passed the quiet side street. Sylvain was in his camping chair, reading *The Hobbit* by flashlight.

“That’s a good book,” Leo said.

“I know,” Sylvain said. “I’m the one who found it.”

Leo sat on the curb. Gimpy came over and sat on his feet.

“The school is going well,” Leo said.

“I heard. The kids are happy.”

“They’re more than happy. They’re alive.”

Sylvain nodded. He turned a page.

“You know,” he said, “my father used to say that education is what survives when everything else is taken away.”

“What did he mean?”

“I don’t know. He died when I was twelve. But I think he meant that you can lose your house, your money, your job. But you can’t lose what you’ve learned. Not unless you forget it.”

Leo thought about this. He thought about his dissertation—four hundred pages, locked in a PDF, unread. He thought about the children, learning to fix toasters and grow tomatoes.

“I think your father was right,” Leo said.

“He usually was,” Sylvain said. “That’s why it hurt so much when he died.”

They sat in silence. The city hummed. The stars turned.

Leo went to his tent. He wrote in his notebook:

*“The school is not a building. It is a promise that every child belongs to someone. That no one is too broken to teach. That learning is not a product to be purchased, but a relationship to be lived.”*

He closed the notebook. He slept.

### Side Sketch 11: The Oppenheimer Suburbans – The Library Book

*Cut to: The quiet side street. Morning. Sylvain is sitting in his camping chair, reading. Randall’s van door opens. Randall emerges. He is holding a book.*

**Randall:** “I saw you took *The Hobbit*.”

**Sylvain:** “I found it. In a box. On a curb.”

**Randall:** “It was mine.”

*Sylvain stares at him.*

**Sylvain:** “You put your books on the curb?”

**Randall:** “I put them in the school. For the children. But you took one home.”

**Sylvain:** “I’m reading it.”

**Randall:** “I know. That’s why I’m here.”

*He holds out his hand. Sylvain hesitates, then hands him the book. Randall opens it. He turns to the title page. Inscribed in pencil, in a child’s handwriting: “Property of Randall. Do not steal.”*

**Randall:** “I wrote this when I was nine.”

*He closes the book. He hands it back.*

**Randall:** “Keep it. Read it. Then give it to a child.”

*He retreats into his van. Sylvain looks at the book. He looks at the inscription. He reads the first page again.*

**Sylvain (to himself):** “In a hole in the ground there lived a hobbit.”

*He smiles. He keeps reading.*

*End of Side Sketch 11.*
