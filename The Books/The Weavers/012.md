## Chapter 12: The First Winter

The cold came early that year. The first frost hit in mid‑November, earlier than anyone could remember. Leo woke to find his tent sagging under a crust of ice. His sleeping bag, which had been merely inadequate in October, was now a joke. His breath hung in the air like a ghost.

He crawled out into a world turned white. The park was silent—not the peaceful silence of a snow day, but the ominous silence of people huddled in their tents, waiting for the sun.

The woman in the orange raincoat—Mira—was already sweeping the sidewalk. She had cleared a path from the park entrance to the loading bay. Her broom made a scraping sound that echoed off the frozen ground.

“You’re still alive,” she said when she saw him.

“Barely.”

“Good. We need you.”

She didn't say what for. She didn't have to.

### The Scramble

The loading bay was chaos. Oso was handing out bowls of soup before anyone had even asked. Dev was testing a propane heater he had scavenged from a construction site. Kiah was on her laptop, typing furiously.

“We have fifty‑three people in tents,” she said. “The SROs are full. The shelters are overflowing. The city has declared a cold weather emergency, but that just means they open a few extra cots in a church basement.”

“What can we do?” Leo asked.

“We can keep people from freezing.” Kiah looked at him. “We have three empty rooms in the SROs. The ones we were saving for repairs. They're not finished—no heat, no windows, but they have walls and a door.”

“That's better than a tent.”

“Barely. But we need more. We need blankets. We need heaters. We need sleeping bags that aren't full of holes.”

Leo looked around the loading bay. The trust had some supplies—the scarves, the wool, a few donated blankets. But not enough. Not nearly enough.

“We need help,” he said.

“We need a miracle,” Kiah said.

### The Parking Lot

Leo walked to the quiet side street. The cars were covered in frost. Sylvain's Yaris had a thin layer of ice on the inside of the windows—a sign that someone had been breathing in there all night.

He knocked on the window. Sylvain emerged, wrapped in a sleeping bag, his hair wild.

“It's cold,” Sylvain said.

“It's freezing. We need blankets. Sleeping bags. Anything.”

Sylvain looked at his car. He looked at the other vehicles. He looked at the park.

“I have an extra sleeping bag,” he said. “It's old. It's thin. But it's something.”

“It's something.”

Sylvain pulled it from his trunk. It was olive green, worn, patched with duct tape. He handed it to Leo.

“I'll ask the others.”

Serena gave two blankets and a space blanket she had been saving for an emergency. The Guy gave a thermal tarp he used to cover his truck. Randall, through his van door, handed out three wool blankets that smelled faintly of him. No one commented.

Leo carried the pile back to the loading bay. It was not enough. But it was a start.

### The Church

That afternoon, Kiah went to see the pastor of the church behind the garden—Reverend Thompson, the one who had given them the prep table. He was a thin man with a kind face and tired eyes.

“We need blankets,” she said. “And sleeping bags. And heaters. And food. And anything else you have.”

Reverend Thompson looked at her. He looked at the empty pews behind him.

“The congregation is gone,” he said. “But the storage room is full. Take what you need.”

The storage room was a treasure trove. Boxes of blankets, sleeping bags, winter coats, boots, hats, gloves. Cases of canned goods. A kerosene heater that had never been used.

“Why do you have all this?” Kiah asked.

“We used to have a homeless ministry. Then the money ran out. Then the volunteers ran out. Then the hope ran out.” He shrugged. “But the stuff stayed.”

They loaded everything into The Guy's truck. Bertha groaned under the weight, but she didn't quit.

“Thank you,” Kiah said.

“Thank God,” Reverend Thompson said. “Or thank the people who donated. One of them is responsible.”

### The Distribution

That night, they distributed supplies. The loading bay became a distribution center. People came from the park, from the SROs, from the quiet side street. They came with empty hands and left with armfuls of warmth.

Leo handed out blankets. He didn't ask questions. He didn't check IDs. He didn't means‑test. If someone said they were cold, he gave them a blanket.

“This is chaos,” he said to Kiah.

“This is community,” she said. “Chaos is what happens when you don't have community. This is what happens when you do.”

By midnight, the supplies were gone. The loading bay was empty. The fire had burned down to embers.

Leo stood in the cold, looking at the park. The tents were dark. The people were inside them, wrapped in blankets and sleeping bags, warmer than they had been the night before.

Not warm. Just warmer.

“It's not enough,” he said.

“It's never enough,” Mira said, appearing beside him. “But it's more than they had. And tomorrow, we'll get more.”

“How?”

“We'll ask. We'll trade. We'll scavenge. We'll do what we always do.”

She walked away. Leo watched her go. He wondered how she stayed so calm. He wondered if she was calm, or if she had just learned to hide her fear.

### The Frozen Pipe

The next morning, a pipe burst in the third SRO. Water poured from the ceiling of the workshop, flooding the floor where Dev kept his tools.

Dev arrived at six to find six inches of water.

“No,” he said. “No, no, no.”

He waded in. The water was cold—just above freezing. His boots filled. His jeans soaked through. He found the shutoff valve and turned it, but the damage was done.

The tools were wet. Some were ruined. The drill press, the band saw, the table saw—all standing in water, their motors submerged.

Leo arrived minutes later. He saw Dev standing in the water, surrounded by his ruined workshop.

“Dev...”

“Don't.” Dev's voice was flat. “Just don't.”

They worked for hours. They bailed water with buckets and pots. They wiped down the tools with rags. They disassembled the motors, dried them with a heat gun, sprayed them with lubricant.

Some of the tools survived. The drill press, which had been on a pallet, was mostly dry. The band saw, which had been on the floor, was not.

“It's dead,” Dev said, staring at the band saw.

“Can you fix it?”

“The motor is fried. The bearings are rusted. It's cheaper to buy a new one than to fix this one.”

“We don't have money for a new one.”

“I know.”

Dev sat on a milk crate. He put his head in his hands.

“This is my fault,” he said. “I should have known the pipes would freeze. I should have drained them. I should have—"

“You should have been psychic?” Leo sat down beside him. “You can't predict everything.”

“I can predict winter. It happens every year.”

“This winter came early. Harder. No one expected it.”

Dev said nothing.

“We'll get you a new saw,” Leo said. “Somehow. We'll trade. We'll scavenge. We'll do what we always do.”

Dev looked at him. “That's what Mira said.”

“Mira is wise.”

“Mira is stubborn.”

“Same thing.”

Dev almost smiled. Not quite. But almost.

### The Community Meeting

That night, they held an emergency meeting in the loading bay. The topic: how to survive the winter.

People spoke for hours. They argued about priorities—heat vs. food vs. shelter. They argued about resources—who should get the new sleeping bags, who should make do with the old ones. They argued about the future—how to prevent another pipe burst, how to raise money, how to keep everyone alive.

Leo took notes. His hand cramped. His mind wandered.

Then Old Mike stood up.

“I've lived in this neighborhood for twenty‑three years,” he said. “I've seen a lot of winters. I've seen a lot of people die. And I've seen a lot of people survive.”

He looked around the loading bay.

“The ones who survive are the ones who share. Not because they're good. Because they have to. Because when your neighbour freezes, you freeze too. When your neighbour starves, you starve too. We're not separate. We never were.”

He sat down.

The room was silent.

Then Mira spoke.

“Old Mike is right. We're not separate. So let's act like it. From now on, no one sleeps alone. We pair up. Two to a tent. Three to a room. Body heat is free. Use it.”

People nodded.

“And no one eats alone. Oso will cook twice a day—morning and evening. If you miss a meal, someone will bring it to you. That's not charity. That's survival.”

More nods.

“And no one fixes alone. Dev, you need help with the workshop. Who can help?”

Hands went up. Sylvain. The Guy. Serena. Even Randall raised his hand, slowly, tentatively.

“Good,” Mira said. “Now let's get to work.”

### The Night

Leo paired with Sylvain. They would share Sylvain's Yaris—not ideal, but better than a tent.

Sylvain had cleared the back seat, laying down blankets and sleeping bags. It was cramped, dark, and smelled of old coffee. But it was warm.

“This is weird,” Leo said, lying across the back seat.

“This is my life,” Sylvain said, reclining the passenger seat. “You get used to it.”

They lay in the dark. The car windows were frosted. The city was quiet.

“Sylvain?”

“Yeah?”

“Why do you stay? You could leave. You could go somewhere warm. Somewhere safe.”

Sylvain was quiet for a long time.

“I don't know where that would be,” he said finally. “I've been running for years. From landlords. From jobs. From myself. I'm tired of running.”

He turned over, facing the window.

“This place is a mess. It's cold. It's dangerous. But it's mine. And the people here... they see me. They don't look away.”

Leo thought about this. He thought about the loading bay, the garden, the school, the workshop. He thought about the people who had given him blankets and soup and a reason to get up.

“I know what you mean,” he said.

They slept. The car idled. The city hummed.

### The Notebook

In the morning, Leo wrote:

*“The first winter is the hardest. Not because of the cold—though the cold is hard. Because of the fear. The fear that you won't make it. That the pipes will burst, the supplies will run out, the people will give up.*

*But they don't give up. They share. They adapt. They survive.*

*This is not philosophy. This is not economics. This is something older. Something that remembers how to live when everything else is stripped away.*

*The first winter is the teacher. The rest of us are the students.”*

He closed the notebook. He went to help Dev clean the workshop.

### Side Sketch 14: The Oppenheimer Suburbans – The Frozen Battery

*Cut to: The quiet side street. Morning. The Guy is trying to start his truck. The engine cranks slowly, then stops. He tries again. Nothing.*

**The Guy:** “No. No, no, no.”

*Sylvain emerges from his Yaris, wrapped in a sleeping bag.*

**Sylvain:** “Battery?”

**The Guy:** “Battery.”

**Sylvain:** “I have a booster pack.”

*He fetches the booster pack—the one he bought after the marathon. He connects it to the truck's battery. The truck starts on the first try.*

**The Guy:** “You saved me.”

**Sylvain:** “That's what neighbours do.”

*The Guy sits in his truck, letting it warm up. Sylvain sits in his camping chair, drinking cold coffee.*

**Serena (appearing with Gimpy):** “Is everyone alive?”

**Sylvain:** “Barely.”

**Serena:** “That's the goal. Barely alive.”

*She sits down. Gimpy curls at her feet.*

**Serena:** “Randall didn't come out this morning.”

**Sylvain:** “He's probably sleeping. The cold is hard on him.”

**The Guy:** “The cold is hard on everyone.”

*They sit in silence. The sun rises, pale and weak.*

**Sylvain:** “We're going to make it.”

**Serena:** “How do you know?”

**Sylvain:** “Because we have to.”

*End of Side Sketch 14.*
