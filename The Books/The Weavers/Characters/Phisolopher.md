
Imagine her. Let's call her Mariam.

She is in her fifties, though she looks older—weather has a way of erasing the difference between forty and seventy. She sleeps in a tent in a ravine in Toronto's Don Valley, one of those hidden encampments the city periodically tries to clear. She has a sleeping bag, a tarp, a small library of books salvaged from street sales and Little Free Libraries: Ivan Illich, Ursula Le Guin, a worn copy of *The Gift* by Lewis Hyde, a Bible missing its cover, and a collection of essays by Indigenous scholars on the potlatch and the commons. She reads by a headlamp. She writes in the margins. She talks to the river.

Mariam was not always homeless. She was a professor once, teaching political philosophy at York University. She walked away. Not in a dramatic, public breakdown—she simply stopped. Stopped grading papers, stopped attending faculty meetings, stopped paying rent. The trigger was a realization that came to her during a lecture on Rawls and distributive justice. She was explaining the difference principle to a room of tired undergraduates, and she heard herself say, "The problem isn't distribution. The problem is that we have consented to a system in which the means of life are distributed at all, as if they were prizes, as if existence itself were a competition." She paused. The students stared. She walked out of the lecture hall, and she never went back.

Her homelessness, she insists when anyone asks, is not a tragedy but an experiment. "I am testing the hypothesis," she says, "that it is possible to live on nothing and still be a person. If I fail, the system is correct. If I live, the system is a lie." She lives. She begs sometimes, but mostly she receives. People in the neighborhood know her. They bring her coffee, bread, socks. She accepts everything with a deep, unsettling gratitude—unsettling because she looks at the giver as if they have just revealed a fundamental truth about the universe. She says "thank you" the way a monk says "amen."

She started talking in parks. At first it was just muttering, then conversations with anyone who sat down nearby. Then small gatherings—three, four people listening to her speak about money as a neurotoxin, about the potlatch, about a society where no one would need to sleep in a ravine. Someone recorded her on a phone. The video, titled "The Homeless Professor Explains Why Money Is an Addiction," circulated on the margins of the internet: Reddit, niche podcasts, a Discord server for disillusioned economists. She was invited to speak at a community center, then a church basement, then a small conference on alternative economics organized by some graduate students who had heard the legend.

She never prepares notes. She sits cross-legged, her hair in a grey braid, her coat still smelling faintly of woodsmoke, and she speaks in parables.

"Imagine," she says, "that tomorrow, the government decided to guarantee housing and food for everyone. Not with vouchers. Not with credits. Actual homes, actual meals. You would still want things, of course. You would want books, music, travel, beautiful shoes. But you would not need money to survive. What would happen to the fear?"

She waits. The audience is silent.

"The fear would drain away. And with it, the addiction. Because addiction is not about the substance. It is about the void the substance fills. The void is terror. When you are no longer terrified, you can use money like a tool. You can use it to say 'I see you, I value your work, I love your art.' But you cannot do that now. Right now, money screams too loudly. It screams 'rent, rent, rent, food, food, food.' It drowns out the human voice."

She talks about the potlatch. She explains that the Indigenous nations of the Pacific Coast understood something we forgot: that wealth is not a thing you hold, but a current you channel. She describes the digital potlatch mechanism—half the surplus must be given away, in person, before the other half unlocks. She describes it not as a technical proposal but as a spiritual practice. "It is a discipline of the heart," she says. "It forces you to find someone to bless. It forces you to look into their eyes. Can you imagine a society where everyone, rich and poor, is constantly looking for people to bless?"

After one of these talks, a young software engineer approaches her. His name is Dev, and he works for a fintech startup. He is burned out, morally exhausted, making money from money. He has been thinking about blockchain for social good, but every project he encounters feels like a thin veneer over the same old extraction. Mariam's vision of a digital potlatch that requires in-person verification captivates him. "Can we build it?" he asks.

"I don't know," she says. "Can you build something that serves love? Most technology serves efficiency. Love is inefficient. Can you build an inefficient technology?"

Dev goes home and starts sketching. He calls his project *Potlatch*, and he open-sources the protocol. The core is simple: a smart contract that locks surplus into a dual-signature wallet. Half can only be released when the other half is sent to a verified recipient, with a geolocation tag confirming proximity and a mutually signed attestation. It's not perfect. It's a prototype. But it's a beginning.

Around the same time, a coalition of housing activists in Vancouver, who have been fighting for community land trusts for years, hear Mariam speak and realize that her universal floor concept—Universal Basic Outcome—provides the philosophical framework their movement has been missing. They rebrand their campaign. They stop talking about "affordable housing" and start talking about "decommodified life." They cite Mariam's parable of the hostage. They build alliances with food justice organizers, with Indigenous land defenders, with healthcare workers advocating for universal pharmacare.

The movement doesn't have a name yet. Some call it "The Floor." Some call it "The Ravine School," after where Mariam still sleeps. She refuses to lead it. "I'm not a leader," she says. "I'm a symptom. I'm the illness speaking its own diagnosis." But she keeps speaking. Keeps writing in the margins of her books. Keeps receiving bread and coffee and returning it as parables.

A journalist from *The Walrus* does a long profile on her: "The Homeless Woman Who Wants to Abolish Money (But Not Really)." It goes viral in a quiet, Canadian way—shared on CBC, discussed on *The Current*, debated in op-eds in the *Globe and Mail*. Conservatives call her a naive utopian. Liberals call her interesting but impractical. The left splits: some embrace her, some accuse her of appropriating Indigenous concepts. She responds to the latter with genuine humility. "You're right," she says publicly, inviting Indigenous elders to speak alongside her. "The potlatch is not mine. I am a student. I am here to listen." Several elders, cautiously, begin to engage. The conversation deepens.

Years pass. The movement grows, not fast, but steadily. Community land trusts expand. A pilot universal school food program launches in three provinces. Dev's *Potlatch* protocol, after a messy, contentious trial in a small community in Nova Scotia, shows that people *want* to give if the structure invites it. The first Potlatch Feast is held in Stanley Park in Vancouver—a thousand people gathering to give away surplus in person, with food, music, and a ceremony led by Musqueam, Squamish, and Tsleil-Waututh elders who bless the gathering as a step toward reclaiming the spirit of their own traditions. Mariam attends. She doesn't speak. She sits at the edge, watching, and when a young woman approaches her with a gift—a hand-knit scarf—she receives it with tears streaming down her weathered face.

She is still homeless, technically. But by now, everyone understands that her homelessness is a choice, a continuing experiment, a reminder of what the movement is trying to render impossible. She is the wound that won't heal until the system changes. And she is the oracle who saw, from the bottom of the ravine, what the top of the tower could never see: that the cure for addiction is not more willpower, but a new design. That money can be reformed, from dope to tool, if we are brave enough to guarantee the basics, to give away the surplus, and to look one another in the eye while doing it.

The movement has no single founder, no centralized leadership, no manifesto beyond the ideas Mariam scattered like seeds in soil she did not own. But when historians of the future ask, "Where did it begin?" they will point to a tent in a ravine and a woman who stopped believing in the necessity of her own misery, and in doing so, helped a civilization stop believing in its own.

*This is how it starts. Not with power, but with someone who has nothing left to lose except the lie.*
