Here’s Lenora, and how I ended up among them.

I can’t pinpoint the exact hour I joined the tribe, but I can tell you Lenora was the elder, and that made it official in a way no paperwork could. You don’t sign a document. You just start showing up, and someone shows you where to sit, and one day you realize you’re inside something that has its own laws and its own lineage. She was that lineage for me.

She had a home, technically. Four walls and a door somewhere. But she slept in the park. I’m not sure why, entirely—maybe the bottle, maybe the pull of the open sky, maybe the fact that home had never felt like safety and the tribe did. She walked with a half-wolf, half-dog at her side, a creature as in-between as the rest of us, and she enjoyed magic mushrooms, a different kind of medicine, one that opens instead of numbing. She was an alcoholic. I name that plainly because she would, and because it wasn’t a moral condition; it was a chronic consequence of a life lived in survival. The kind of life that had long stopped asking permission.

I remember Suzie Q asking me to help bring her stuff into her home. I imagined the room wasn’t available yet, and maybe it wasn’t. Maybe Lenora wasn’t ready to be indoors. Her things stayed in the park, and over time, they got stolen. The park has its own economy: during the day, we camped together and looked out for each other—some would go eat while others watched the stuff—but at night, whatever wasn’t secured in a tent was open game for the random druggies walking and scavenging in the dark. It was a constant rhythm of care and loss, like breathing.

The encampment itself was a kind of organism. Every night, many hands put up Lenora’s tent. She was the elder, so you didn’t let her do it herself. Every morning, those same hands collapsed it. It was ritual, repeated and reliable. Outsiders saw a group of drunks. I saw something else: people living their traditional life in the open, disturbing the conformists just by existing. The chronic alcoholism wasn’t a breakdown; it was almost expected, woven into the history of what they’d survived. It wasn’t a failure of character. It was a long, slow response to a world that gave them benches and called it shelter.

The first night I truly belonged, I brought beer because I’d heard it was Lenora’s birthday. I didn’t have a plan. I just handed it to her so she could spread it around. I thought I was being polite. I didn’t know I was stepping into the potlatch, the old tradition of redistribution, where a person’s standing is measured by what they give away, not what they keep. She received the gift and distributed it, and I watched the beer move through hands around the park, and I understood, without anyone explaining, that this was ceremony. This was how it had always been done.

Later, someone donated a whole keg of beer, and we kept it hidden in a rolling garbage can filled with ice. It was ridiculous and brilliant. The tribe knew exactly what to do: keep it cold, keep it hidden, share it. That was our wealth. Not the alcohol itself, but the act of circulating it, of making sure no one was empty-handed and everyone was seen. Lenora presided over that economy, half-wolf at her feet, tent going up and coming down around her like the sun.

I ended up with them because I didn’t walk away. I sat on a bench, I said hello back, I brought beer to a birthday, I helped carry things I couldn’t always save from theft, and I learned to see a potlatch where others saw disorder. They became my tribe, and Lenora became my elder. Not because she was perfect—she was complicated and drunk and stubborn and sacred—but because she held a place in a lineage I didn’t know I’d been looking for. And in that encampment, under the open sky, I found a traditional life still breathing, still being lived, still giving the finger to anyone who called it anything less.
