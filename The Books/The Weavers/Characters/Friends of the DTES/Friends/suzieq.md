
I remember Suzie Q.

She started drinking the hard stuff at six years old. Six. A child with a glass of something burning, and the world just let it happen. That was the ground she walked on for decades after. She spent most of her life in Vancouver, in and around the same parks and streets I came to know her in. She was a fixture, a presence, someone who'd been there so long she'd become part of the landscape.

But here's the thing I need you to understand about Suzie Q: she saved a girl. A fourteen-year-old kid, kicked out by her own mother—an addict who chose the drug over her daughter. Suzie Q took her in. Hosted her for years, until that girl could stand on her own. A woman the world called broken, who'd been drinking hard since childhood, reached back into the same fire and pulled someone else out. She didn't wait until she was fixed. She just did it anyway.

And she kept doing it. Volunteering in the park. Doing outreach. She was a giver in the old way, the potlatch way, where what you have moves through your hands to someone else. The tribe knew her as one of their matriarchs.

By the end, her rhythm was simple: she spent most of her time by the park, drank in the morning, took a nap in the afternoon, and returned home afterward. There was a gentleness to that—a slowing down, a routine held in the open air. She was still there, still present, still part of the place.

Then her last month came. And Suzie Q, who'd been drinking hard since she was six years old, kicked the booze completely. Decades of alcohol, and she just let it go. She spent her final days drinking 7-Up with orange juice. That's what she chose. Something sweet, something simple, something with no burn at all. I think about that—her hand around a cold glass of something harmless, something she could sip in the sun on her bench, no chaos attached. She gave herself that ending.

She passed away recently. The whole community held a ceremony in the park in her honor. The same park where she napped in the afternoons, where she volunteered, where she saved a girl's life by offering her a place to land. The tribe gathered and mourned their matriarch properly.

She started at six with a bottle and a wound. She ended with 7-Up and orange juice, having kicked the booze on her own terms, having pulled a child from the flames, having been honored by her entire community in the open air she never truly left. That's not a tragedy. That's a life, fully lived. That's Suzie Q.
