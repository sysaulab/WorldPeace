Here’s Brian, the way I remember him.

I met him on a bench. I was just sitting there one morning, and he sat down next to me and said hi. I said hi back, and we started talking. That was it. That was how I got to know him. The benches became our place after that. We’d sit there and talk, sometimes about nothing, sometimes about everything.

He was goofy. Genuinely, weirdly goofy, in a way that could make you forget for a minute where you were and what he was carrying. I remember one afternoon I took him to a patio. He showed up and immediately put his crack torch right there on the table, like it was a set of keys or a wallet, nothing to hide. And then he started telling me all about how he was clean, how he was doing great, how the sobriety was sticking. It felt like he was trying to convince himself way more than he was trying to convince me. I didn’t call him on it. I just watched. I watched his goofy, proud display of his drug paraphernalia right next to his speech about being clean, and I saw people around us react, and weirdly, I really enjoyed that night. It was so completely him. He was always two things at once, both of them true and neither of them cancelling the other out.

He claimed disability, but he took so much pride in working. His version of work was cleaning up old stuff he found in back alleys and trying to sell it. Discarded things, things no one else wanted. He’d talk about his finds like they were treasure. He was always broke, but he had this dream of going picking in the valley, making a real go of it, maybe getting out to Penticton like he always said he wanted to. The money was never there. He could never leave the Downtown Eastside. That place had its teeth in him.

He lived in shelters, and his stuff got stolen every single day—or at least that’s what he said. I started to suspect some of it was just his memory going, things lost inside his own head rather than taken. He blamed a lot of his condition on not having a father figure. He told me that on one of our bench talks, plain and simple, like he’d figured out the root cause and could name it even if he couldn’t fix it. I think about that a lot. He was always trying to clean things up, you know? The junk in the alleys, himself—he wanted to be "mister clean" so badly. Maybe he was always reaching for an order that never came.

One day I found him on a bench, passed out. He’d been there the whole day, in the sun, and no one had done a thing. Nobody intervened. When I got to him he was severely dehydrated and in shock. That image is burned into me. A whole day on a public bench, a person right there, and the world just walked past. I saw him invisible, and I saw him dying, and I saw the exact same man who’d sat there saying hi to me like it was the easiest thing in the world.

The last time I saw him, he had a crack pipe in his hand and he looked terrible. That’s my final snapshot of Brian. I don’t know if he’s dead now. I don’t know if he ever somehow made it back to Penticton like he always said he wanted to do. I miss him. I miss the goofy, proud, heartbreaking, contradictory man who sat with me on benches, who displayed his torch while reciting his sobriety, who scavenged beauty from back alleys and couldn’t manage to save any for himself. That was my friend. I’m just saying his name now, so he’s not just someone nobody noticed.
