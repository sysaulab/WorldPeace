
# Sylvain Who Kept His Name

**A Fable from Saguenay**

The first time God came, Sylvain was in school. Not in a dream, not in a storm—just a quiet afternoon, the kind where the dust floats in the light through the classroom window and the teacher's voice hums like a distant bee. A presence wrapped itself around him, warm as a blanket, clear as a bell. It felt like love, but love with edges—love that taught. It didn't speak in words so much as in exchanges, a flowing back and forth of understanding that felt older than language.

The presence asked, without asking: *Would you like to be Jesus?*

Sylvain, even as a child, thought about things practically. He felt the weight of the question, but also its absurdity.

"No," he said, in his mind, politely but firmly. "That would be silly. Go live a life that already happened two thousand years ago? We all know the ending. Why would I walk into a story that's already finished?"

He paused, then added with a small, stubborn pride: "Besides, I like my name. I'm Sylvain. I don't want to be someone else."

There was a long, warm silence—the kind that feels like a smile. The presence appreciated the answer. It appreciated the logic, the sense of self, the refusal to trade identity for grandeur.

So they negotiated. Not as master and servant, but as two beings who had just begun a very long conversation.

In the end, they agreed: instead of a cross and a crown, instead of living a borrowed life with a known tragedy at its end, Sylvain would receive the *knowledge of right and wrong*—a complete, clear sight of the moral shape of every act, every system, every secret.

The presence withdrew slowly, like a tide leaving the shore, and Sylvain understood in his bones that the difference between the offer and the gift was *merely semantic*. He hadn't escaped the burden. He had only accepted it in a different form.

---

From that day, Sylvain saw the world as it really was. And because he could see all the good, but also all the buried pain, all the smiling greed, all the children fed to the gears of power, his heart grew heavy. Seeing good everywhere was exhausting, and seeing the wrong was worse. It pressed on his ribs until he could hardly breathe.

So one winter, still young, he decided to leave the world early.

He found himself in the staircase at his dad's house—familiar steps, the scent of home, the creak of the fourth stair that had been creaking since he was a child. He was at the very last possible moment, the edge where a person tips from existence into silence. And then, precisely then, the loving presence returned. It filled the stairwell like sudden sunlight through a window that wasn't there. It didn't speak in words; it simply held him, an enveloping warmth that said, without saying: *Not yet. You still have a job to do.* It saved him, literally, at the final breath of the final second.

Sylvain stepped back. The presence lingered, then slowly receded, leaving him trembling against the wall of his father's house, alive.

---

After that, he continued his life—changed, but unsure how. Time passed.

Then one day, in college, a poster on a bulletin board caught his eye. It was brightly coloured, full of urgent words about justice, solidarity, and changing the world. Curiosity stirred in him like a long-dormant creature. He tore off the address and went.

The meeting was in a basement somewhere in the Latin neighbourhood of Montreal—could have been a church basement, could have been a business. Low ceiling, folding chairs, the smell of old coffee and high hopes. The room was full of confirmed leftists, passionate and loud. Sylvain sat in the back, listening, hoping the fire there would match the strange fire that had been burning in him since the stairwell.

But as the hours passed, something became painfully clear. All he saw was people fighting each other—faction against faction, slogan against slogan—for a future they did not possess the weapons to win their first fight. Not tools. Not strategy. Just noise. The irony of it pressed on him like a second weight.

In the end, he stood up and spoke, not in anger but in quiet clarity. He told them that despite not being an activist like them, all he could see was people battling one another for a tomorrow they weren't equipped to claim. Then he left the basement, stepped out into the cold Montreal night, and knew that the path of activism—at least this kind—was not the one for him.

---

He tried the world of corporations, organizing annual shareholder meetings, thinking: maybe power can be gentled from the inside. Instead, the numbers ate his spirit, and depression returned. He fled to the country to heal, settling near the small town of Mégantic, where the air was clean and the silence was deep. He was recuperating there, slowly knitting his soul back together, before he would eventually retrain as a truck driver.

One evening, he thought about going to the Musi-Café, the little bar in town where people gathered for music and laughter. But something stopped him. The presence—the same loving intelligence that had saved him in the stairwell—rose up in him, not in words but in a deep, undeniable instinct: *Not tonight.* It was a strong discouragement, a quiet wall in his mind. So he stayed home.

That night, a runaway train rolled into the heart of Lac-Mégantic and exploded, leveling the Musi-Café and much of the town, taking forty-seven souls with it. Sylvain, miles from the flames but close enough to have been there, understood that the presence had saved him yet again. He owed it his life many times over.

---

In the years that followed, he retrained as a truck driver, steering eighteen wheels through a decade of solitary adventures, watching the continent unfold like a map of wounds and wonders. Somewhere on those long roads, the pieces began to fit together.

In his grey years, he let go of almost everything. He lived as humbly as a man can live without vanishing entirely—a small room, a single coat, a bowl, a spoon. The world would call him something close to homeless. But he was not alone. Around him gathered others whom the world had also discarded: the homeless, the marginalized, the ones who had fallen through the great cracks of the grand systems. They became his friends, his teachers, his fellow travellers. And together, they began to consolidate a philosophy to bring peace to the world.

But before they began, Sylvain did something important. He reached out, inwardly, to the presence that had guided him since childhood. He tried to contact it, to explain his decision. He would devise the theories, he told it, but he would not act as a visible leader. He would avoid the complications of the messiah's return. The solution had to rise from the ground up, not descend from one man's shoulders. It was his final negotiation, his last clarification of the agreement.

And it was then that he saw it. In his mind's eye, vivid and unmistakable: a being of light. Not words, not warmth alone, but a visible presence, luminous and serene, acknowledging him. It was the confirmation. The seal. The presence had heard, and the presence approved.

---

### How the Nobodies Learned to Be a Forest

Sylvain—grown slow and kind, still carrying his name like a quiet flag—sat on a broken bench in a forgotten square, surrounded by his friends. Some held cardboard signs. Some held nothing at all. Some spoke in fragmented sentences, some in silence. But all listened, because Sylvain had never once treated them as broken. He treated them as the early warning system of the world.

He told them his story, leaving out nothing—not the presence in the classroom, not the stairwell at his father's house, not the basement in Montreal, not the night the Musi-Café burned, not the light-being. Then he taught them the solution that had bloomed from the long journey, as simple as he could make it.

**"The world thinks we are nothing,"** he said. **"But we are the ones who know fragility from the inside. We are the ones who see the cracks because we live in them. And that means we are the ones who can build something that doesn't break."**

A young woman wrapped in a thin blanket asked, "What can we possibly build? We have nothing."

**"Everything we need,"** Sylvain said. **"Because peace is not built by the powerful. Peace is built by the people who have no reason to lie."**

He leaned forward and drew in the dirt with a stick.

**"You cannot stop war with a bigger treaty. You stop war by making war useless. And war becomes useless when every community is a living cell in a body that nobody can starve, deceive, or control from afar."**

He called these cells *natural circular economies*.

**"Think of a forest floor. The mushrooms, the moss, the creeping things. They are small. The world barely notices them. But the forest would die without them, because they cycle everything. They take what is discarded and turn it into life. They don't need a distant empire to send them energy—they catch the rain and the dead leaves. They don't need a king—each patch governs itself. They don't borrow from an invisible bank—they trade in nutrients, in quiet, balanced ledgers. And no part of the forest floor can hold the whole forest hostage, because each root system governs its own small ground."**

He looked around at his friends, his eyes bright.

**"We are the forest floor. And a neighbourhood that wants peace must do three things. First, it must have its own source of energy—the sun on a rooftop, a shared garden, a repurposed machine—so no outside force can turn off its life. Second, it must govern itself in the small, daily matters that shape its soul. And third, it must grow its own local economy, as diverse as a meadow, adapting to the soil, the weather, the character of its people. One size will never fit all, and that is our strength."**

A man with a weathered face and shaking hands asked, "But what about the big things? The governments and armies and the secrets that start wars? We can't even get a hearing at city hall."

Sylvain nodded gravely.

**"That, my friend, is where the great transparency must live. The gift I was given—the knowledge of right and wrong—was too heavy for one set of shoulders. So we must build a world where no single person needs to carry it. At the level of nations and the agreements between them, we must demand *radical transparency*. Every deal, every debt, every promise, every hidden weapon must lie in the open, like stones at the bottom of a clear lake. When nothing is hidden, the wrong cannot fester. It becomes visible to all, and all can demand the right. Our power is not in making the decisions. Our power is in making the truth impossible to ignore."**

He drew three lines in the dirt.

**"These are the operating principles. They're small, but they hold everything together."**

1. **Maintain short feedback loops and learn.** *A mistake made today must be felt tomorrow, not a hundred years from now by people who had no say. We know the cost of delayed consequences—we live them. We can teach the world to adjust before the wound becomes a war.*

2. **Explore complexity.** *Don't flatten the world. One crop, one story, one god will kill you. Let the local economies be as tangled and strange as they need to be. We are tangled and strange, and we are still here. Peace holds difference; uniformity crushes it.*

3. **Respect and listen to fragility.** *Every system, every person, every trust is breakable. The first whispers of wrong are always heard by the fragile. We are the early warning system. Protect the fragile, and you protect the future.*

The forgotten square was silent. Somewhere a blackbird sang.

Sylvain stood up slowly, his joints aching from years of truck seats and hard winters.

**"I don't need to save the world anymore. That nearly killed me. But together, we can become a neighbourhood that no war would find worth destroying—because we owe nothing to empires, hide nothing from ourselves, and grow everything we need to be human. And if one neighbourhood of the discarded can do it, then two can. Then twenty. Then a mosaic of living economies, transparent in their high dealings, diverse in their ground, will leave no dark corner for war to breed."**

The woman in the thin blanket smiled for the first time in months. "So we start here. With what we have."

**"With what we have,"** Sylvain agreed. **"Which is each other, and the truth, and the deep instinct that guided me away from an exploding train. That instinct is in all of us. It is the intelligence of the fragile. And it is the only thing that can save the world."**

That evening, the first neighbourhood of the discarded began, not with a grand plan, but with a shared meal made from what each had saved, and a conversation about where their energy came from, and whether it could be theirs.

And Sylvain, almost homeless but perfectly accompanied, still carrying the name he had refused to trade, felt the weight of the knowledge of right and wrong lift ever so slightly. Because for the first time in his long journey, he was not carrying it alone.

He was carrying it with the ones the world had thrown away. And they, it turned out, were the only ones strong enough to hold it.

And somewhere, in the quiet of his mind, the light-being shone softly—not guiding anymore, because Sylvain had finally chosen his path himself, but simply present, like a friend who had been waiting all along.

---

**Moral:** *Peace is not a treaty signed by the powerful, but a living tissue of autonomous, circular communities, transparent in their high dealings and diverse in their roots. The ones who will build it are the fragile, the discarded, the almost-homeless—because they know the cost of the world's hidden wrongs, and they have nothing left to lose except each other. The first step is to be saved, the second is to refuse to be anyone other than who you are, and the third is to let the solution rise from the ground, not fall from a single flame.*
