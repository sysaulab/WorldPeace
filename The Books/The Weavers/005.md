## Chapter 5: The Potlatch

The elders arrived on a Sunday morning.

There were three of them: one from the Musqueam Nation, one from the Squamish, one from the Tsleil‑Waututh. They came in a weathered minivan driven by a granddaughter who stayed behind the wheel, reading a paperback, while the elders walked slowly across the park.

Kiah saw them first. She had been expecting them—had been waiting for weeks, making calls, sending messages through the network of Indigenous women who kept the old ways alive when no one was watching.

She stood up from her laptop. She walked to the edge of the loading bay. She bowed her head.

"Welcome," she said. "Thank you for coming."

The eldest of the three—a woman named Auntie Helen, who had been a residential school survivor, a language keeper, a witness to things no child should see—took Kiah's hands.

"You are building something," Auntie Helen said. "We heard. We came to see if it is true."

"It's true," Kiah said. "But we need your blessing. And your guidance."

Auntie Helen looked around the loading bay—the fire, the knitting machine, the soup pot, the people huddled in sleeping bags and second‑hand coats. Her face was unreadable.

"You are living like my grandparents lived," she said. "Before the ban. Before the *Indian Act*. Before they tried to make us forget."

She turned to the other elders. They nodded.

"We will stay," she said. "We will teach. And we will learn."

### The Teaching

That afternoon, the loading bay became a classroom.

Auntie Helen sat by the fire. The others gathered around her—not just the trust members, but people from the park, from the SROs, from the quiet side street. Sylvain came, sitting on the edge of the crowd with his camping chair. Serena came, with Gimpy on a leash. Even The Guy came, wiping grease from his hands.

Auntie Helen spoke for two hours without stopping.

She spoke about the potlatch—the great giving ceremonies that had been the heart of Coast Salish life for thousands of years. How wealth was measured not by what you kept, but by what you gave away. How the chiefs competed in generosity, not accumulation. How the whole community was bound together by a web of gifts, obligations, and songs.

"The potlatch was law," she said. "It was economy. It was religion. It was everything. When you gave a gift, you were not just giving an object. You were giving a piece of your story. And the person who received it became part of that story."

She paused. Her voice was steady, but her hands trembled.

"They banned it in 1885. The *Indian Act*. They said it was wasteful. They said it was primitive. They said it kept us from becoming civilized." She looked around the loading bay. "What they really meant was that it kept us from becoming poor. It kept us connected. It kept us strong. And they could not have that."

Kiah was crying. She wiped her eyes with the back of her hand.

"They took our potlatches," Auntie Helen continued. "They took our land. They took our children. But they could not take our memory. And now you are building something that looks like a potlatch. A community. A giving economy. A place where no one is left behind."

She looked at Leo.

"You, the philosopher. You wrote the rules. Did you know you were writing a potlatch?"

Leo shook his head. "I was just trying to solve a problem."

"The problem of hoarding," Auntie Helen said. "The problem of isolation. The problem of forgetting that we belong to each other." She smiled. "That is the same problem the potlatch solved. You found the same answer. That is not coincidence. That is memory."

### The Ceremony

That evening, the elders held a ceremony.

They sang songs that had not been sung in public for decades—songs of giving, songs of gratitude, songs of return. The trust members did not know the words, but they hummed along, learning as they went.

Auntie Helen took the first scarf—the ugly one, the one with the dropped stitch, the one that had made Mary weep—and held it to the fire.

"This is not a potlatch blanket," she said. "It is too small. Too rough. Too new. But it is the beginning. And every beginning is sacred."

She wrapped the scarf around Kiah's shoulders.

"You are the weaver now," she said. "Not just of wool. Of people. Of community. Of the future."

Kiah bowed her head. The scarf was warm. It smelled of smoke and wool and something older—something that remembered being given before.

The crowd was silent. Then someone clapped. Then everyone clapped. Then Oso started passing around bowls of stew, and the ceremony became a feast.

### The Promise

Late that night, after the elders had left, after the fire had burned down to embers, Leo sat with Kiah in the loading bay.

"What did it mean?" he asked. "The potlatch. What did it really mean?"

Kiah was quiet for a long time.

"It meant that you could not be rich alone," she said finally. "You could only be rich together. The chief who gave away the most blankets was the richest, not the one who kept them. Because giving created obligation. Obligation created relationship. Relationship created community. And community was the only real wealth there ever was."

Leo wrote this down in his notebook. Then he crossed it out. He didn't need to write it. He would remember.

### The Side Street

He walked back to his tent past the quiet side street. Sylvain was sitting in his camping chair, even though it was midnight. Randall's van was dark. Serena's Jeep had a soft glow.

"Sit down," Sylvain said.

Leo sat on the curb.

"You look like you've seen a ghost," Sylvain said.

"I've seen something," Leo said. "I'm not sure what."

Sylvain nodded. He didn't ask for details. He just sat, drinking cold coffee, watching the stars.

After a while, he said, "You know, my grandmother used to tell me stories. About the old country. About the village where everyone shared everything. I thought she was making it up."

"She wasn't."

"No," Sylvain said. "I don't think she was."

They sat in silence. The city hummed. The stars turned. Somewhere, a dog barked.

Leo thought about Auntie Helen's words. *Every beginning is sacred.*

He thought about the scarf—the ugly one, the one with the dropped stitch, the one that had started everything.

He thought about the potlatch—the giving, the obligation, the web of relationship that held people together when everything else fell apart.

He had spent six years studying philosophy. He had learned to deconstruct arguments, to analyze texts, to question every assumption. He had learned nothing about how to live.

The people in the loading bay were teaching him. The elders were teaching him. Even Sylvain, with his camping chair and his cold coffee, was teaching him.

*You cannot be rich alone.*

He pulled out his notebook. He wrote:

*"The first gift is always ugly. The second gift is easier. The third gift becomes habit. By the tenth gift, you have forgotten that you ever thought hoarding was a good idea. This is not economics. This is grace."*

He closed the notebook. He went to sleep.

### Side Sketch 7: The Oppenheimer Suburbans – Animal Control Returns

*Cut to: The quiet side street. Two weeks later. A white SPCA van pulls up. Officer #1 and Officer #2 get out. They look tired. They have been here before.*

**Officer #1:** "Ma'am, we have a report—"

**Serena (holding up a laminated card):** "I have a business card."

**Officer #2:** "That's not a license."

**Serena:** "It's a promise."

*She shows them the animals. Beans is sleeping. Toast is glaring. Gimpy is wagging his tail. The roof bins are organized. The litter box is clean. The water bowls are full.*

**Officer #1:** "The condition seems adequate."

**Officer #2:** "But we have to do a full inspection. New policy."

*Two hours later, they have inspected everything. They have interviewed Serena. They have interviewed Sylvain ("He's a good neighbour"). They have interviewed Randall (who nodded silently). They have interviewed The Guy ("She saved my truck once. I don't know how. She just did.").*

**Officer #1:** "We are closing the file."

**Officer #2:** "Again."

**Serena:** "See you in thirty days?"

*The officers look at each other. They sigh.*

**Officer #1:** "We'll put you on the annual review list. That means once a year."

**Serena:** "That's progress."

*They drive away. Serena collapses into her camping chair. Gimpy licks her face.*

**Sylvain:** "Annual review. That's practically a permit."

**Serena:** "I'm going to frame the letter. If they ever send one."

*She doesn't frame it. But she keeps it in the glove compartment, next to Gimpy's leash.*
