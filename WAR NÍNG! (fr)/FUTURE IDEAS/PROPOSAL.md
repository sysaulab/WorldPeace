# The Səlilwət Community Trust: A Proposal for Economic Sovereignty in Vancouver's Downtown Eastside

**A Ten-Year Pilot Project Combining Housing, Mutual Recognition, and Ecological Responsibility**

## Executive Summary

This proposal outlines a ten-year pilot project to house approximately 20,000 residents of Vancouver's Downtown Eastside in community-owned housing, organized around a single radical principle: **integrated members act as a single economic unit**. Members contribute their labor and production to the community; the community provides for all members' needs in return.

The model draws directly from the potlatch traditions of Pacific Northwest Indigenous nations—sophisticated economic systems that increased collective wealth through ceremonial distribution rather than private accumulation. Three tiers of distribution translate this principle into practice:

1. **Tier 1 – Unconditional Security:** All integrated members receive housing, food, clothing, education, and transportation without condition, fee, or means test.

2. **Tier 2 – The Tip Economy:** Each month, members receive their discretionary allocation split 50/50—half as immediately spendable cash, half as "liftable tips" that must be gifted to other members. When gifted, tips become immediately spendable for the recipient. This creates a pure social signal: generosity is rewarded with purchasing power for others, and hoarding is structurally impossible.

3. **Tier 3 – Ecological Ceiling:** Total discretionary funds (both cash and tips) are capped at a democratically determined limit, ensuring the community operates within sustainable consumption boundaries.

Critically, all transactions occur in **Canadian dollars** through conventional banking infrastructure. No separate currency is introduced. A custom banking application visualizes the community return on internal spending, creating feedback loops that encourage localization without coercion.

The project is designed as a participatory action research study, testing the hypothesis that unconditional basic provision combined with a dynamic recognition economy can prevent the social dissolution observed in Calhoun's "behavioral sink" experiments—where abundant resources without social function led to population collapse.

**At full scale (20,000 residents), the model is projected to generate $150-200 million in annual government savings through reduced administrative overhead, retained economic leakage, and prevention dividends, while building $500 million to $1 billion in community-owned assets over ten years.**

## 1. The Problem: Ideological Mismatch as Structural Violence

### 1.1 Who Lives in the DTES

The Downtown Eastside concentrates individuals whose cognitive styles, trauma histories, neurodiversity, and cultural values are poorly matched to the demands of neoliberal ideology—deferred gratification, entrepreneurial self-branding, and conformity to behavioral norms as conditions of access to resources.

Recent data from the DTES SRO Collaborative's comprehensive tenant survey reveals:

- **70%** of tenants would be homeless without SROs
- **39%** were homeless before securing their unit
- **31%** of tenants are Indigenous (compared to 2% citywide)
- Market SRO rents increased nearly **50% between 2013-2023**
- The 55-64 age demographic grew from 15% in 2008 to **29% in 2023**

These are not individuals who have failed to navigate the system. They are individuals for whom the system was not designed.

### 1.2 The Limits of Individualized Intervention

Current approaches rely on individual, means-tested interventions: case workers, income assistance, rent supplements, treatment referrals. These methods assume that problems are located in individuals and can be solved through individualized attention.

The evidence suggests otherwise. As Melissa Giles from BC Rent Bank presented at Housing Central 2025, rent arrears are the main eviction driver, with a **34% rise** in applicants facing 70% income-to-shelter ratios between 2023 and 2024. Prevention is consistently demonstrated to be more effective and less costly than crisis intervention—yet the system remains structured around crisis response.

### 1.3 The Distorted Economic Field

The DTES exhibits what physicists would call a "distorted field"—abnormal amplitudes of economic inequality, housing precarity, and social disconnection. These distortions are not accidental; they are the predictable outcome of treating housing as a financial asset and residents as consumers rather than co-owners.

**The Leakage Problem:** Every dollar of income assistance or disability payment that enters the DTES through an individual recipient quickly leaves the community via:
- Private landlords (rent increases up 50% in a decade)
- External retailers (no local ownership of basic goods)
- Predatory lenders and check-cashing services
- The unregulated drug market

A less distorted field requires **re-embedding the economy in social relationships**—creating a bounded system where resources circulate internally before interacting with external markets.

## 2. Theoretical Framework: The Potlatch Economy and the Behavioral Sink

### 2.1 Potlatch as Economic Institution

The potlatch ceremonies of Pacific Northwest Indigenous nations were systematically suppressed by Canadian government and church officials in the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, who dismissed them as "wasteful, uneconomic, and irrational." Contemporary economic analysis reveals the opposite: potlatches functioned as sophisticated systems of decentralized exchange and social memory.

In the potlatch model:

- Wealth is accumulated not for private hoarding but for ceremonial distribution
- Gift-giving creates obligations that bind communities across time
- Social memory—preserved through song, dance, and storytelling—serves as informal recordkeeping
- The system **increases total wealth and social welfare** by facilitating distribution

The Səlilwət model extends potlatch logic to the entire residential community. Integrated members function as a single economic unit, contributing all production to the community, with the community sharing everything with everyone. This is not charity—it is **community ownership of the means of subsistence and production**.

### 2.2 The Behavioral Sink: Lessons from Calhoun's Universe 25

John B. Calhoun's famous experiments with Norway rats (1958-1962) and later with mice ("Universe 25," 1968-1972) demonstrated a puzzling phenomenon: in environments with unlimited food, water, and protection from predators—ostensibly "utopias"—populations initially grew, then plateaued, and eventually collapsed toward extinction despite abundant resources.

Calhoun coined the term **"behavioral sink"** to describe the collapse in social behavior that preceded population decline. Key observations included:

- **Social withdrawal**—individuals who would emerge only when others slept ("the beautiful ones")
- **Breakdown of maternal behavior**—females abandoning or attacking their young
- **Loss of social roles and hierarchies**
- **Violence despite adequate nutrition**

Recent theoretical work by Mantzaris (2025) proposes that the behavioral sink resulted from **"hierarchy entropy degeneration"** —the progressive loss of uncertainty in perceived social ranks over generations. When the enclosure's design enabled full visibility of the pecking order, and this hierarchy became completely known, "the lack of uncertainty removed any attempts to engage in activities that touch upon that hierarchy." The payoff matrix for social engagement became flat; there was nothing left to compete for, nothing left to signal.

**The profound insight:** Unconditional resource provision without meaningful social differentiation may lead to the very collapse it aims to prevent. Social structures require **gradients**—uncertainty about status, opportunities for signaling, mechanisms for recognition—to maintain engagement and reproduction of social norms.

### 2.3 The Tip Economy as Social Signaling

The Səlilwət model addresses this challenge through its three-tier distribution system, with particular attention to Tier 2: the tip economy.

**The Mechanism:**

Each month, members receive their discretionary allocation split **50/50**:
- **Half as immediately spendable cash** (Tier 3)
- **Half as "liftable tips"** —funds that cannot be spent directly but must be gifted to other members

When a member gifts a tip to another member:
- **It becomes immediately spendable in the recipient's account**
- No waiting period, no chain of obligations, no complex tracking

The entire allocation—both cash and tip funds—counts toward an **ecological responsibility cap**, democratically determined by the community.

**Why This Works:**

| Element | Function |
|---------|----------|
| 50/50 split | Ensures everyone has both spending money and tipping capacity |
| Tips become spendable immediately upon gifting | Creates pure social signal: "I value your contribution enough to give you purchasing power right now" |
| Hoarding impossible | If you don't gift your tips, you don't access that half of your allocation |
| No hierarchy accumulation | Tips become money immediately for recipients; no permanent status markers accumulate |
| Maintained uncertainty | Today's tipper is tomorrow's potential recipient; hierarchy never settles |

This creates a **dynamic recognition economy** where generosity is the only path to full resource access, and where every act of recognition translates directly into another member's purchasing power.

## 3. The Economic Model: Three Tiers of Distribution

### 3.1 Tier 1 – Unconditional Security

All fully integrated members receive their absolute necessities without condition, fee, or means test:

| Category | Provision |
|----------|-----------|
| **Housing** | Adequate shelter in community-owned towers, organized by thematic floor clusters. Members may accumulate tips and spending money toward an extra room. |
| **Food** | Basic nutrition through community cafeterias and bulk distribution |
| **Clothing** | Basic garments produced internally |
| **Education** | Access to community learning programs |
| **Transportation** | Basic mobility within the city |

**Condition for Receiving:** Full integration—defined as presence in the community, participation in governance, and recognition by peers as a member in good standing. This is not employment conditionality; it is **belonging conditionality**. One must be *of* the community, not merely housed *in* it.

It remains possible to rent at market rates. The goal is to create a porous economic zone, not an enclave. Socioeconomic reintegration, not segregation.

### 3.2 Tier 2 – The Tip Economy (Social Recognition)

Each month, members receive their discretionary allocation split 50/50. The tip portion operates as follows:

**Source of Tips:** Members grant tips to other members for contributions not captured by formal work shifts:

- Emotional support and peer counseling
- Teaching skills (traditional knowledge, crafts, literacy)
- Unscheduled repairs or assistance
- Elders' guidance and cultural teaching
- Mediating disputes or supporting someone in crisis
- Any act of kindness or contribution that a member wishes to recognize

**The Critical Rule:** Tips cannot be spent by the person who receives them—**until they gift them**. When Member A tips Member B, those funds sit in B's account as "pending tips." When B gifts those funds to Member C, they become immediately spendable for C.

**What This Creates:**

1. **Immediate generosity incentive:** I can only access my full allocation if I give away the tip portion. Hoarding is impossible—either I give or I lose.

2. **No hierarchy accumulation:** Tips become money immediately for recipients, so no permanent status markers accumulate. Recognition flows, it does not settle.

3. **Pure social signal:** When I tip you, you get real money right now. The signal is unambiguous: "I value your contribution enough to give you purchasing power."

4. **Maintained uncertainty:** Because tips convert immediately upon gifting, the system never settles into fixed hierarchies. Today's generous tipper is tomorrow's potential tip recipient.

5. **Visibility for invisible work:** Caregiving, emotional labor, elder support, cultural teaching—all the contributions that markets ignore—become visible and valued.

### 3.3 Tier 3 – Ecological Ceiling

Total discretionary funds distributed to members (both cash and tip portions) are subject to a **globally capped limit**, democratically determined by the community through the Community Congress.

This implements the "sustainable consumption corridor" (SCC) framework, which defines both a **social foundation** (minimum needed for human flourishing) and an **ecological ceiling** (maximum the planet can tolerate). Drawing inspiration from the 2000-watt society concept originating in Switzerland, the community commits to living within per-capita consumption limits that could be sustained globally.

**Democratic Determination:** Each year, the Community Congress debates and sets the ecological ceiling for the coming year, based on:

- Community surplus from production and external sales
- Scientific data on sustainable consumption
- Competing priorities for investment (new housing, production equipment, community programs)

### 3.4 No Local Currency: The Canadian Dollar Interface

The Səlilwət model does **not** introduce a separate local currency. All financial transactions occur in Canadian dollars through conventional banking infrastructure. Members use standard bank cards that function anywhere in the world.

**The Innovation is Informational, Not Monetary:**

A custom banking application (to be developed) provides members with real-time visualization of the economic impact of their choices:

| Feature | Function |
|---------|----------|
| **Standard banking** | Balance viewing (Tier 3 cash), transaction history |
| **Tip interface** | Easy mechanism for granting tips; tracking of tips given, received, and pending |
| **Community Return Indicator** | When shopping internally, members see percentage returning to community pot |
| **Collective Dashboard** | Real-time display: internal circulation rate, surplus available, ecological ceiling status |

This creates **feedback loops that encourage localization and generosity without coercion**. Members are free to spend anywhere, but the information architecture makes visible the collective consequences of individual choices.

## 4. Physical Design: The Pencil Towers and the Park

### 4.1 Vertical Villages

Housing is provided in tall, slender towers (pencil towers) oriented to avoid casting shadows on the park to the north. Each tower houses approximately 2,000-2,400 residents across 30 floors.

**Sectional Organization:**
- **Level -2:** Heavy production (furniture, canning, bulk storage)
- **Level -1:** Public marketplace (shops, cafes, grocery)
- **Level 0:** Public park (playground, benches, stair pavilions to market)
- **Levels 1-30:** Housing

### 4.2 Thematic Floor Clusters

Floors are grouped to create communities of affinity, reducing forced integration while maintaining cross-contact:

| Floors | Theme | Design Features |
|--------|-------|-----------------|
| 1-5 | Welcome Floors (Transitional) | Higher support staff, orientation to community |
| 6-10 | Active Use Floors | Harm reduction based, nursing station |
| 11-15 | Healing Floors (Sober/Recovering) | Quiet hours, meditation room |
| 16-20 | Family Floors | Larger units, play areas |
| 21-25 | Elders & Knowledge Keepers | Accessible, Indigenous elders prioritized |
| 26-30 | Artisan Floors | Live-work, balconies for gardens |

**The Elevator as Social Infrastructure:** Residents move between clusters for work, learning, and relationship. The artisan teaches on the healing floors; the elder visits the family floors. The physical plant enables **gradients of connection** without forcing conformity.

## 5. Governance: Collective Self-Determination

### 5.1 Nested Governance Structure

| Body | Composition | Frequency | Responsibilities |
|------|-------------|-----------|------------------|
| **Floor Meetings** | All residents of a thematic floor | Weekly | Immediate concerns, recognition of contributions, nominate reps |
| **Tower Councils** | Floor representatives | Monthly | Coordinate across clusters, allocate resources |
| **Community Congress** | All members | Quarterly | Review finances, debate investment priorities, set ecological ceiling |
| **Elders Advisory Committee** | Indigenous elders | Ongoing | Guide Indigenous governance protocols |
| **Tenant Advisory Committee** | Elected SRO tenants | Ongoing | Represent SRO tenants in each building |

### 5.2 Monthly Development Brainstorms

Each month, the community convenes open sessions where **any member with ideas can debate how to invest surplus wealth**. These sessions:
- Democratize investment decisions
- Educate members about trade-offs and systems thinking
- Generate ownership over community direction
- Adapt collectively to changing conditions

## 6. The People's Investment Bank: Economic Sovereignty

### 6.1 Structure

The People's Investment Bank is a community-owned financial institution that:
- Holds title to all community land and housing (removed from speculative market)
- Receives bulk government transfers on behalf of integrated members
- Provides micro-loans to member-owned production shops
- Retains and reinvests all revenue internally

### 6.2 Initial Capitalization

The bank requires seed capitalization of **$50-100 million** for property acquisition and infrastructure development. This is framed as one-time infrastructure investment, not ongoing subsidy. At full scale, the project is projected to generate $150-200 million in annual government savings.

### 6.3 The Bulk Transfer Mechanism

Current system:
```
Government → Individual payments → Landlords, vendors, extractors (leakage)
                         → Case managers (administrative overhead)
                         → Individual consumption (mostly external)
```

Proposed system:
```
Government → Səlilwət Community Trust (bulk transfer) → Internal distribution (Tiers 1-3)
                                                     → Community-owned production
                                                     → Reinvestment in housing/assets
                                                     → Reduced leakage (estimated 40-60%)
```

### 6.4 Estimated Efficiency Gains

At full scale (20,000 residents):

| Metric | Current System | Proposed System (Mature) | Annual Difference |
|--------|----------------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| Total government expenditure | $900 million - $1 billion | $700-800 million | $150-200 million savings |
| Administrative overhead | $180-220 million | $80-100 million | $80-120 million |
| Leakage to external economy | $500-600 million | $200-250 million | $300-350 million retained |
| Capital available for reinvestment | $0 | $100-150 million | New asset creation |

**Cumulative 10-year impact:**
- $1.5-2 billion in government savings
- $3-4 billion retained in community (versus leaked)
- $500 million - $1 billion in community-owned assets

## 7. Research Framework: Testing the Hypothesis

### 7.1 Primary Hypothesis

> The prevalence of harmful behaviors in the DTES is not primarily a function of individual pathology, but of exposure to an economic environment that demands cognitive and behavioral patterns incompatible with residents' neurodiversity, trauma histories, and cultural values. By creating a bounded economic system that guarantees survival while preserving dynamic social signaling mechanisms through a tip economy, we can reduce harm, increase wellbeing, and generate longitudinal data on alternative economic structures—without requiring residents to conform to neoliberal ideology as a condition of belonging.

### 7.2 Secondary Hypotheses

**H1:** The three-tier system will generate higher rates of self-reported wellbeing and community belonging than conventional SRO housing with individual case management.

**H2:** The tip economy will produce measurable networks of reciprocity that cross-cut thematic floor clusters, with tipping patterns revealing the value of previously invisible work (caregiving, emotional support, cultural teaching).

**H3:** Per-capita government expenditure will decrease by 25-40% over ten years due to administrative savings, leakage reduction, and prevention dividends.

**H4:** Overdose rates will decline by at least 50% among integrated members compared to baseline DTES averages, due to reduced isolation and regulated internal supply.

**H5:** Social network analysis will reveal sustained uncertainty in hierarchy perception—tips will circulate continuously without settling into permanent status hierarchies, preventing the entropy degeneration observed in Calhoun's experiments.

### 7.3 Data Collection Methods

| Domain | Instrument | Frequency |
|--------|------------|-----------|
| **Economic** | Transaction records; government expenditure data | Continuous |
| **Tip Economy** | Complete directed graph of all tip transactions | Continuous |
| **Health** | Overdose events; emergency room visits; primary care | Quarterly |
| **Social Networks** | Network analysis metrics (density, reciprocity, clustering) | Monthly |
| **Wellbeing** | WHO-5 Well-Being Index; Indigenous wellbeing measures | Annually |
| **Qualitative** | Life history interviews; ethnography; photovoice | Years 1,5,10 |

### 7.4 Social Network Analysis: The Tip Dataset

The tip economy generates a complete record of community recognition—a dataset unprecedented in social science. Every act of kindness, every moment of support, every recognition of invisible work becomes visible and measurable.

**Key questions the data can answer:**

- What does a healthy recognition economy look like?
- How does tipping behavior differ across floor clusters?
- Do certain members act as "recognition hubs"—receiving and redistributing many tips?
- Does the 50/50 split produce different network structures than a purely voluntary system?
- What network characteristics predict individual wellbeing and community resilience?

## 9. Risk Assessment and Mitigation

### 9.1 Political Risk: Perceived as Cult or Communist

**Mitigation:** Language discipline and transparent data. Frame as:
- "Community wealth building" (not communism)
- "Vertical urbanism" (not compound)
- "Patient capital" (not anti-capital)
- "Indigenous economic reconciliation" (not separatism)
- "Fiscal responsibility" (reducing per-capita social costs)

All non-identifying data published openly to invite scrutiny and replication—the best defense against accusations of secrecy.

### 9.2 Social Risk: Cluster Segregation

**Mitigation:** Thematic floors create affinity without isolation. The elevator system, shared production spaces, marketplace, and monthly brainstorms ensure cross-contact. The tip economy specifically rewards recognition across clusters.

### 9.3 Economic Risk: External Dependency

**Mitigation:** The goal is eventual self-sufficiency through internal production and external sales. Initial government capitalization is framed as one-time infrastructure investment, not ongoing subsidy. The People's Investment Bank owns assets; revenue stays internal.

## 10. Conclusion: An Invitation

The Səlilwət Community Trust seeks partners who recognize that:

- The current system is not failing; it is functioning exactly as designed—to extract wealth from vulnerable populations
- The residents of the DTES are not broken individuals requiring repair; they are people whose cognitive and cultural differences have been pathologized by a system that demands conformity
- The potlatch tradition is not a historical artifact; it is a living economic logic that can guide the future
- Calhoun's rats warn us that material abundance without social function leads to collapse—but **abundance with meaningful recognition** can sustain vibrant communities

We invite government partners to seed an experiment in economic sovereignty. We invite First Nations to guide the governance of Elders' floors and the application of traditional law. We invite researchers to document what happens when a community is permitted to organize itself around belonging rather than buying.

**At best:** We have solved for housing, addiction, and discrimination in one of Canada's most complex neighborhoods—without displacement, without coercion, without requiring anyone to abandon their identity. We have generated a generation of research on human social organization and contributed to the revitalization of Indigenous economic traditions suppressed for over a century.

**At worst:** We have built beautiful housing, productive businesses, and a decade of research on alternative economics—infrastructure that serves the community regardless of whether the full vision is realized.

**The only serious risk** is exposing the inefficiency of profit-driven institutions. That risk we accept, with care taken to frame this work as complementary, not oppositional—an expansion of the possible, not an attack on the present.
