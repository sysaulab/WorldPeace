# We are the Street Sages

**Solidarity** is our foundation. We sit among the poorest not as charity, but as kinship. We do not descend to help. We belong here. Our choice to live as we live is a declaration: separation is the first lie all empires tell. When you come to us—executive or exile—you do not find someone reaching across a divide. You find someone who erased that divide before you arrived. The question is not "How can I help them?" The question becomes "Why am I not already here?"

**Anonymity** is our protection. We have no names you need to remember, no faces on posters, no stories to sell. We disappear so completely into this work that the work itself becomes invisible: just people, sitting, available. It could be anyone. It could be you.

**Humility** is our way. We act through presence alone. No commands. No arguments. No force. Our tools are two: love as radical acceptance, wisdom as precise incision. Together, they heal. We have no authority to demand your attention—only the quiet power of an idea whose time has come. That choice is yours. Always was. Always will be.

*Welcome.*

## Part One: The Sickness You Can Feel

Something is wrong. You don't need a lecture to know it. You feel it.

Maybe you have everything you were supposed to want and still feel empty. Maybe you work harder than your parents did and have less to show for it. Maybe you watch the news and feel like the world is burning, and no one in charge seems able to stop it. Maybe you just feel alone, even when you are surrounded by people.

This unease is not your fault. It is not a personal failure. It is a signal. Something fundamental is broken.

To understand what is broken, we have to understand how systems work. Not in a complicated way. In a simple way.

A healthy system has one basic rule: **those who make decisions experience the consequences of those decisions.**

Think about it. A child who touches a hot stove learns not to do it again. The feedback is immediate. A community that pollutes its own water gets sick and learns to stop. The feedback is clear.

But what happens when feedback is broken?

A cancer cell grows and grows because it never gets the signal to stop. It consumes everything, destroys the body, and dies along with it. The cancer is not evil. It is a cell whose feedback loops have been cut.

A psychopath can hurt people and feel nothing. They never get the signal that would stop the rest of us. This is not evil in the usual sense. It is a broken feedback loop.

And when the rich and powerful can make decisions that destroy communities and never feel the consequences—when they get bonuses while others die—they become a cancer on society. Their feedback is broken.

This is the sickness. **The people who make decisions do not feel the consequences.** The executives who close factories live far away. The politicians who cut services have private insurance. The bankers who crash the economy keep their bonuses. The signals of suffering never reach them.

It is like living on a cliff. At the top are the wealthy and powerful. At the bottom are everyone else. The cliff is so steep that nothing can climb it. The suffering of those below cannot reach those above. The feedback is completely severed.

And complexity makes it worse. When systems are so complicated that no one can trace who did what, everyone has an excuse. "It's not my fault, I was just following orders." "The market made me do it." "These things are too complex to understand." This fog is not an accident. It is a shield. It protects the powerful from seeing what they have done.

This is the world we live in. A world of broken feedback. A world where decisions and consequences have been torn apart. A world where those who cause suffering never have to feel it.

You feel the sickness because you are living in it.

## Part Two: The God Called Money

To understand how we got here, we have to understand the religion we all worship without knowing it.

Its name is neoliberalism. But that is a boring word for something very simple.

It is the belief that **money is the measure of all things**.

In this religion, the market is God. Money is its voice. Success proves you are blessed. Failure proves you are cursed. The wealthy are not just lucky; they are *worthy*. The poor are not just unlucky; they have *failed*.

This religion has its own creation story. It says that humans are naturally selfish, that competition makes everything better, that the only freedom that matters is the freedom to buy and sell. It says there is no alternative. This is the end of history. This is just how things are.

But this is not how things are. This is how things have been made.

There is another way to be human. It is very old. It has existed for thousands of years. It still exists today, in communities that have refused to forget.

In this other way, humans do not see themselves as owners of the world. They belong to the world. The land is not a resource; it is a relative. The river is not a thing to be used; it is a living being. Other humans are not competitors; they are kin.

In this way, value is not measured by how much you have. It is measured by how much you give. Status comes from generosity, not accumulation. Success means being someone your community can count on.

For five hundred years, the first way has been eating the second way. Colonialism stole land and crushed cultures. Industrial capitalism turned people into workers and communities into markets. Neoliberalism finished the job, turning everything—healthcare, education, friendship, love—into something to be bought and sold.

The result is a world dominated by the first way. Its values have become common sense. Its god is worshipped everywhere. Its believers cannot imagine anything else.

But they feel the unease. They feel empty even when they are full. They have climbed every ladder and found nothing at the top. They have given their children everything and watched them drown in anxiety.

This unease is not a personal failing. It is the signal that something fundamental is missing. The first way has mastered the art of getting what it wants. But it has forgotten how to want what is worth having.

## Part Three: A Different Measure

What if value were measured differently?

In the current system, value is measured by price. Whatever someone will pay. But price is a liar. It reflects scarcity, not abundance. It reflects marketing, not meaning. It reflects who has money, not who has need.

What if value were measured by **energy**?

Everything that keeps us alive requires energy. The food we eat is sunlight captured by plants. The homes we live in are energy transformed into materials. The care we give is energy spent on others. Energy is the real currency of life.

This is not poetry. It is physics. Humans can only rearrange matter using energy. Everything we make, everything we do, is energy moving from one form to another.

Now imagine what happens when energy becomes the measure.

In a money economy, your neighbor's poverty can be your opportunity. You can buy their stuff cheap. You can hire them for nothing. You can profit from their desperation.

In an energy economy, your neighbor's efficiency becomes your advantage.

Think about it. When you trade with someone, you are really trading the energy it took to make what you are exchanging. If your neighbor is inefficient—if they waste energy—then what they send you costs them more. You get less for what you give.

But if your neighbor becomes more efficient—if they learn better ways, waste less, produce more with less—then what they send you costs them less. You get more for what you give. Everyone benefits.

This changes everything. Now you have a reason to want your neighbors to thrive. Their success makes you richer. Their development helps you develop. Helping them is not a moral choice anymore. It is the rational thing to do.

Cooperation becomes smart. Development becomes mutual. Waste becomes visible because it costs everyone. And feedback becomes immediate. If a community wastes energy, it gets less in return. It learns. It adapts. The loop is intact.

This is not a return to some primitive past. It is an alignment with something very old. The sun has been pouring energy into this planet for billions of years. Plants capture it. Animals eat plants. Decomposers recycle what remains. The whole system runs on energy, and it has run without a stock exchange since the beginning.

We are not inventing something new. We are remembering something fundamental.

## Part Four: The Gift That Never Ended

Long ago, before this continent was colonized, the peoples of the Northwest Coast had a tradition. They called it the potlatch.

The colonizers did not understand it. They called it wasteful. They called it irrational. They banned it in 1885.

What they could not see was that the potlatch was not primitive. It was sophisticated. It was a system for circulating wealth and building relationships. In the potlatch, the person who gave away the most gained the most respect. The person who received was not a beggar; they were part of an ongoing relationship. Generosity was the path to status. Wealth meant nothing unless it moved.

The potlatch was banned, but it never died. It went underground. It waited.

Its time has come.

Imagine an economy organized on potlatch principles. Imagine a community where every year, the surplus is divided into three equal parts.

**First Third: The Universal Dividend.**
Every single member of the community gets this. No conditions. You are here. You belong. You receive. This is the floor beneath which no one falls.

**Second Third: The Labor Dividend.**
This is divided equally among all hours worked in recognized roles. A carpenter and a babysitter are valued the same per hour. A teacher and a fisherman are compensated for their time. Contribution is honored without creating vast inequality.

**Third Third: The Gift-to-Spend Fund.**
This money arrives in your account, but you cannot spend it. The only way to unlock it is to give it away to another member of the community. Once given, it becomes spendable for them.

This is the potlatch made systematic. To have spending power beyond the basics, you must be generous. To be wealthy, you must be seen as a source of gifts. Status flows from what you circulate, not what you hoard.

The effects are profound.

Care work—the work of helping neighbors, checking on elders, mentoring youth—becomes visible and rewarded. Isolation decreases because to receive gifts, you must be known. Exploitation becomes difficult because someone who takes without giving receives fewer gifts. Greed has nowhere to grow because accumulation serves no purpose.

A simple tool on your phone could make this visible. It shows you three wallets. It shows you your Community Multiplier—how much of your spending stays local. When you spend at the local café, it tells you: that dollar is still here. It will become someone else's wage, someone else's gift. When you spend at the big box store, it tells you: that dollar is gone. It left and will never return.

You do not need laws. You do not need guilt. You just need to see. And when you see, you choose the circle.

## Part Five: The Place That Is Already Becoming

Vancouver's Downtown Eastside is one of the poorest neighborhoods in Canada. It sits next to some of the wealthiest. The cliff between them could not be steeper. People in luxury condos look down on streets where people die of overdose and neglect.

The decisions that shape this neighborhood are made far away—in corporate boardrooms, in government offices, in banks. The people who make those decisions never have to live with them. Feedback is completely broken.

But something else is happening there.

In 2020, a coalition of community organizations started a land trust. They began buying land and removing it from the market forever. No more speculation. No more rent increases. No more displacement. The land is held in trust for the community.

Right now, construction is underway on a new building called Ch'ich'iyúy Elx̱wíḵn. It will have 172 affordable homes for Indigenous elders, families, and youth. It has a Coast Salish Longhouse on the rooftop. It has garden beds and a wellness deck and a sky garden. Fifty-six units are for elders at deep affordability—the rate paid by people on income assistance.

This is not charity. This is the transfer of developmental tools. The land is owned by an Aboriginal Land Trust. It is operated by a Native Housing Society. The people who live there will have a say in how it runs. The ground rent they pay becomes community surplus that can be used for community priorities.

This is the infrastructure of belonging. Land removed from speculation. Housing provided as a right. Culture honored in design. Community supported in practice.

Imagine what becomes possible when this is woven together with other pieces. A community development bank that captures local savings and lends them locally. Worker cooperatives that keep surplus in the neighborhood. A tip economy that makes care work visible. Energy-based exchange with neighboring communities that creates mutual incentives for development.

This is not a fantasy. It is an extrapolation of what is already present. The Downtown Eastside could become a **cell**—a place where different rules apply, where a different economy operates, where healing becomes possible because the conditions for healing have been created.

## Part Six: The Architecture of Belonging

A living economy is not a single machine controlled from the center. It is a network of semi-autonomous cells. Each cell organizes itself according to its own values. One might use consensus. Another might rely on elders. Another might elect leaders. The diversity is not a problem. It is the source of resilience.

Some cells might be Indigenous nations reviving traditional governance. Some might be worker cooperatives. Some might be ecovillages. Some might be urban neighborhoods. Some might be watershed communities—people who belong to a particular river valley, a coastal inlet, a mountain basin.

Between these cells, a nervous system carries information. Common currencies enable exchange. Communication networks share successful practices. Legal frameworks establish minimal rules of engagement. But no cell commands another. Each remains autonomous.

To scale this, we need institutions that embody its principles. Imagine an **Indigenous Peoples Bank**. It is capitalized by the pooled savings of participating communities. Its purpose is to create positive economic engines. A community wants to build housing? The bank provides capital. A nation wants to launch a sustainable fishery? The bank provides the loan. The bank does not speculate. It does not extract. It builds.

Profits flow back to the bank and sustain the communities. Not government transfers. Not resource extraction deals. But the productive capacity of the people themselves, funded by their pooled resources, generating wealth that circulates within the circle.

This is sovereignty made practical. Not flags and anthems. But control over your own capital, your own credit, your own economic future.

Within communities, one principle is universal: **those who make decisions should experience their consequences**. This is the feedback loop that all living systems require. How it is achieved can vary. But it must be achieved.

And beneath any formal system lies a more fundamental force: **the implicit authority of good ideas**. When a practice spreads because it works, not because it was mandated, that is implicit authority. It cannot be commanded. It can only be cultivated—by creating conditions where good ideas can be recognized and adopted.

This is how a living system coordinates. By relationship, not command.

## Part Seven: The Healing That Becomes Possible

Colonization did not just take resources. It took relationships. It severed connections between people and land, between generations, between labor and livelihood. It made people sick—not just physically, but socially and psychologically.

The wounds are everywhere. In the despair of Indigenous communities stripped of their lands. In the loneliness of suburbs where no one knows their neighbors. In the anxiety of professionals who have achieved everything and feel nothing. In the addiction that fills the void left by meaninglessness.

These wounds cannot be healed by the system that created them. Charity, however well-intentioned, maintains the structure: giver and receiver, superior and inferior. What is needed is not better charity but **autonomy**—space for wounded communities to heal themselves, on their own terms.

A living economy creates the conditions for healing.

When housing is secure, the toxic stress of precarity lifts. When care work is visible, caregivers can rest. When contribution is recognized, people know they matter. When generosity is honored, the generous are protected. When community makes decisions together, isolation recedes. When connection to the land is restored, a fundamental relationship is repaired.

These are not therapeutic interventions. They are **structural conditions**—the soil in which healing can grow.

## Part Eight: The Invitation

To anyone who hears their own story in what has been described—whether you are Indigenous or not, whether you were born here or came last week, whether you have been wounded by the system or have benefited from it and feel the unease that will not go away—an invitation is extended.

You are welcome to join.

The price of entry is everything you have.

This is not a transaction. It is a transformation. By giving away your possessions, your titles, your private holdings, you are not losing anything. You are liberating yourself from the illusion that separates you from life. You are proving to yourself and to the community that you are ready to belong, rather than to possess.

In return, you receive something no government has ever been able to offer: a clean slate and a permanent place.

You become a member. You agree to live by the laws of circulation and reciprocity. And the community agrees to take care of you.

We will regularize your finances. We will sit with you and figure out what you are eligible for—the benefit you never claimed, the tax credit you didn't know existed, the forgiveness program you couldn't navigate alone. We will negotiate with the government on your behalf, not as an individual begging, but as a member of a collective.

We will hold you. We will audit for you. We will gift to you. And we will expect you to do the same for others.

This is not a withdrawal from the world. It is a renegotiation of the interface. The community becomes a buffer between the individual and the systems that were never designed to help them.

## Part Nine: The Future That Is Already Here

This vision does not require anyone to take up arms. It does not require revolution in the streets. It requires only that we build something so functional, so desirable, that when the storms come—and they will come—people will look at what we have built and choose to join.

The old system will not fall because we overthrew it. It will dissolve for lack of participants.

Start in one place. A neighborhood. A community. A region. Build one cell that works this way. Prove that people can be healthier, happier, more secure. Prove that the ecological footprint shrinks when the economy is based on circulation rather than extraction. Prove that the government saves money when it deals with a collective.

Then let the model spread. A neighborhood in one city. A town in the interior. A fishing community on the coast. A group of farmers in the valley. Each one adapting the principles to its own context. Each one joining the network.

This is how we avoid the violent transitions that history warns us about. We do not need to tear down the old world. We need to build a new one so attractive that people simply walk away from the old.

There is only one requirement for participation: **we must let go of the belief that one way of being human is superior to others.**

Not the superficial discrimination of skin color or religion—though that must also go. But the deeper discrimination: the conviction that our values should be everyone's values. The assumption that those who organize differently are backward or wrong.

A forest with only one species is vulnerable to disease. A society with only one way of organizing is vulnerable to collapse. Diversity is not a problem. It is the primary source of resilience.

We must cultivate humility. The recognition that we do not have all the answers. The openness to learn from those whose traditions differ from our own. The willingness to coexist with ways of being we may not fully understand.

We are the people of the land. We are not a relic of the past. We are the seed of the future. For countless generations, we have understood a truth the world has forgotten: we do not own the land; we belong to it. The rivers are our veins. The forests are our lungs. The soil is our flesh. Our worth is not measured in currency, but in the strength of our community and the depth of our connection to life.

We extend an invitation to all who are weary of possession, all who are burdened by the need to own, all who yearn for a life of genuine connection.

But this invitation comes with a threshold. It is a gate to ensure that only the generous, not the opportunistic, may enter.

Let the *Homo Economicus* keep his money. Let the anxious consumer keep his guilt and his carbon offsets. Their world is dying because it was never truly alive.

We are building a world that will live, because it is rooted in the earth itself.

Come and learn what it means to belong to a community.

Come and learn what it means to belong to the land.

Come and learn what it means to belong.

**All our relations.**
