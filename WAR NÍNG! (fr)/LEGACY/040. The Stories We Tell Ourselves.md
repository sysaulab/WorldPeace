# The Stories We Tell Ourselves

***An Essay on Systems, Sanity, and Seeing Clearly***

## Introduction: The Comfort of Blame

Here's something strange about human beings: when something goes wrong, our first instinct is almost never to ask "what caused this?" but rather "who did this?" It's automatic, reflexive, almost irresistible. When a driver cuts us off, they're not someone whose brakes might have failed or who might be rushing to a hospital—they're just an inconsiderate person . When students fail to bring devices to school, they're not struggling with sharing limited resources at home—they're just forgetful . When someone commits a terrible act, we reach for words like "monster" or "evil" as if we've explained something.

This tendency has a name. Psychologists call it the **fundamental attribution error**—our hardwired habit of overemphasizing people's character and underestimating the power of their situation . It's not that we're stupid. It's that we're human, and humans evolved to make quick judgments about others because those judgments once kept us alive.

But here's the problem: this error doesn't just make us unfair to individuals. It makes us blind to how the world actually works. It prevents us from solving problems because we're always firing the messenger instead of redesigning the system that sent them. And in its most extreme form, it leads us to pathologize entire generations rather than ask whether their distress might be a rational response to an irrational world.

This essay invites you to see differently. To ask harder questions. To consider that maybe—just maybe—there are no bad people, only bad situations that produce predictable patterns of behavior. And if that sounds naive, I invite you to examine the evidence.

## The Situationist Challenge to Character

In 1961, a young psychologist named Stanley Milgram placed an advertisement in a New Haven newspaper. He was looking for men to participate in a study about memory and learning at Yale University. The pay was $4.50. The participants who showed up included postal clerks, high school teachers, engineers, and laborers—ordinary people from all walks of life .

What happened next has become one of the most famous and disturbing experiments in psychology's history. Each participant was assigned the role of "teacher" through what they believed was a random drawing. In another room, they could hear a pleasant middle-aged man—the "learner"—strapped into what looked like an electric chair. The teacher's job was to read pairs of words and then test the learner's memory. Each wrong answer required delivering an electric shock, starting at 15 volts and increasing by 15-volt increments all the way to 450 volts, where the shock generator bore only the ominous label "XXX."

The learner wasn't actually receiving shocks. He was an actor following a script. At 150 volts, he would yell that he wanted out. At 180, he'd cry that he couldn't stand the pain. At 300, he'd pound on the wall and then fall silent. Throughout, the experimenter sat nearby in a gray lab coat, offering calm prods when the teacher hesitated: "Please continue," "The experiment requires that you continue," "You have no other choice; you must go on" .

Before the experiment, Milgram asked psychiatrists, college students, and ordinary adults to predict how far they thought participants would go. The estimates averaged around 1-3% administering the maximum voltage. Most people assumed they would stop early, and that anyone who continued must be a sadist.

The actual result: **65% of participants administered the full 450 volts** .

These were not monsters. These were ordinary people—people like your neighbors, your parents, you—who found themselves in a situation that systematically dismantled their moral resistance. The shocks increased gradually, so each step was only slightly worse than the last. The authority figure seemed legitimate and took responsibility. The institutional setting of Yale University lent credibility. And the physical and emotional distance from the victim made it easier to continue .

Milgram's experiments have been replicated in variations across decades and cultures. The results are remarkably stable. When the learner is in the same room, obedience drops. When the teacher must physically force the learner's hand onto a shock plate, obedience drops further. When other teachers refuse to comply, obedience nearly vanishes. The behavior isn't driven by stable character traits—it's driven by situational features .

This is the **situationist challenge** to our ordinary understanding of morality. If character were robust and cross-situationally consistent, the same person should behave roughly the same way across different contexts. But the evidence suggests that minor tweaks to situations produce massive shifts in behavior. As one researcher put it, "ordinary people readily obey destructive orders from legitimate authorities" and "situational forces overpower individual personality in determining behaviour" .

Does this mean character doesn't exist? Not exactly. But it means character is far more context-dependent than we imagine. The cognitive-affective personality system theory, developed by Walter Mischel and Yuichi Shoda, suggests that personality consists not of static traits but of stable patterns of *if-then* relationships. You might be honest *if* the stakes are low, but less honest *if* the pressure is extreme. The consistency is in the pattern across situations, not in the behavior itself .

The implications are profound. When we see someone behaving badly—cheating on taxes, falsifying records, following unjust orders—our instinct is to attribute it to their character. But the statistical regularities across populations suggest something else: certain situations reliably produce certain behaviors in *most people*. The question isn't "what kind of person would do that?" but "what kind of situation makes that behavior predictable?"

## The Fundamental Attribution Error and Why It Matters

So if human behavior is so powerfully shaped by situations, why do we keep insisting it's all about character?

The answer lies in the **fundamental attribution error**, a concept first identified by social psychologists Edward Jones and Richard Nisbett in the 1960s . They observed a systematic asymmetry in how actors and observers explain behavior. Actors tend to attribute their own actions to situational factors: "I cut him off because my brakes failed." Observers tend to attribute others' actions to dispositional factors: "He cut me off because he's inconsiderate."

This bias isn't random. It serves psychological functions. Seeing others as stable, predictable characters makes the world feel manageable. If bad things happen because of bad people, we can protect ourselves by avoiding or punishing those people. But if bad things happen because of complex systems we can't easily change, we're faced with a much more uncomfortable reality .

The bias also gets culturally reinforced. Legal systems are built on individual responsibility. Moral discourse emphasizes personal virtue. Organizational accountability structures focus on hiring and firing individuals rather than redesigning workflows. We've built entire civilizations on the assumption that behavior reflects character, and that assumption becomes self-perpetuating .

Consider a real-world example from education. In 2014, two school district leaders faced a crisis: their "bring your own device" program was failing. Teachers were frustrated because students weren't bringing devices. Many assumed students were forgetful or unmotivated. The natural response would have been to crack down—punish students, pressure parents, blame teachers for not enforcing rules.

Instead, these leaders asked different questions. They collected data on the system rather than judging individuals. What they found surprised them: although 95% of students reported having a device at home, nearly half were sharing those devices with at least three other family members. The devices simply weren't available to bring to school. The problem wasn't forgetful students—it was a system that assumed device access without verifying it .

When the leaders shifted resources to provide additional devices, teacher frustration vanished and technology use increased. The same people, in a redesigned system, behaved completely differently.

This story illustrates something crucial: **systemic problems require systemic solutions**. Blaming and replacing individuals feels satisfying, but it rarely solves anything. If you don't change the system that produced the behavior, the next person will behave the same way .

## The Inversion—Predatory Systems and the Question of Complicity

Now let's perform a thought experiment. It's uncomfortable, but that's the point.

Imagine a serial killer strapped into the electric chair. As the current flows, every cell in his body dies—the neurons that might have loved, the muscles that committed murder, the healthy tissue alongside the cancerous. We don't ask which cells were guilty. We judge the system. We observe that the organism, as a whole, has developed a fatal and coherent logic of destruction, and we stop it.

Now look at the West—the United States, Europe, the global North—through the eyes of those it has acted upon for centuries. What do you see?

You see a system that has sustained itself by consuming the bodies, lands, and futures of others. It began with outright theft and enslavement. When that became unfashionable, it adapted: structural adjustment programs that strangle nations' ability to feed themselves, drone strikes that incinerate wedding parties, trade agreements that lock the Global South into providing cheap labor while the West enjoys cheap goods, sanctions that deny medicine to children until governments submit.

From the outside, this is not a collection of disconnected events. It is a **system**. It is coherent. It is self-perpetuating. It is a machine for the extraction of wealth and the maintenance of power .

And here's the uncomfortable question: if the logic of the electric chair is sound—if it's moral to permanently deactivate a coherent system of predictable, ongoing harm—why doesn't that logic apply here?

Why should those on the receiving end care about the "innocent" cells within this predatory organism? The comfortable liberal who tweets disapproval but continues to vote for the party maintaining the system, who buys the products funding the corporations, whose tax dollars paid for the bombs? That person is the beneficiary of the crime, the enabler by consent. Their personal morality doesn't alter the machine's output.

This is not an argument for violence. It's an argument for **systemic honesty**—for seeing clearly what we're part of. And it leads to a crucial distinction: a society is not a body. Its people are not cells.

When a serial killer dies, all his cells die. But when apartheid died in South Africa, white South Africans didn't die with it. When the Berlin Wall fell, East Germans didn't cease to exist. When the Soviet Union dissolved, Russians, Ukrainians, Georgians kept breathing. The society can die while its people live .

This is the distinction that saves us from the fire. The goal is not to kill the cells. The goal is to kill the organizing principle—the structures of extraction and justification that turn a collection of humans into a predatory machine. The people can be healed, or at least allowed to live on without the malignancy directing their energies toward harm.

## Cognitive Warfare—How Language Prevents Systemic Perception

If systemic perception is so important, why is it so rare? Why do we keep mistaking the menu for the meal?

The answer, in part, is that language itself has been weaponized to prevent clear seeing. We're living through what researchers call **cognitive warfare**—a sustained campaign of semantic manipulation designed to short-circuit critical thought .

A semantic weapon isn't a bullet or a bomb. It's a word or phrase deliberately loaded with emotional and political meaning to frame debate on terrain that guarantees one side's defeat. In cognitive warfare, the victor isn't the one with the stronger argument—it's the one who controls the dictionary.

Consider the 20th century's linguistic arsenals. In the West, "communism" was transformed from a description of an economic theory into a catch-all term for ultimate evil. This semantic loading was so successful that for decades, any policy suggesting collective action—a union drive, public healthcare, environmental regulation—could be defeated simply by attaching the suffix. The word did the work of a thousand arguments .

Simultaneously, the East deployed its own arsenal. "Bourgeois individualism" and "cosmopolitanism" were weaponized to erase the person. To speak of artistic freedom or the right to dissent was to be branded an enemy of the people.

Now step back and look at the symmetry. Both linguistic frameworks functioned identically as **obscurants**. Both were designed to hide the existence of a monopolist class. In the American model, "free markets" and "individual choice" hide the reality of an economy increasingly planned by a handful of investment firms. In the Soviet model, "the proletariat" and "people's ownership" hid the reality of a state bureaucracy controlling every lever of power .

Today's cognitive warfare is more sophisticated. It weaponizes social media through bot networks, opinion manipulation, and algorithmically amplified division. NATO describes it as "a deliberate effort to influence, disrupt, or protect the cognitive processes of individuals and societies" . During the Russia-Ukraine conflict, researchers documented how narratives invoking nuclear threats were synchronized with NATO summits and military aid announcements—manipulative messaging timed to maximize psychological impact .

The antidote is what might be called **systemic honesty**: a ruthless commitment to asking empirical questions regardless of labels. Who controls the surplus wealth? Who makes decisions affecting millions? Can ordinary people influence these decisions? What happens to dissenters? When these questions are asked without semantic fog, the differences between systems often blur .

## The European Laboratory—Crisis as Transformation Mechanism

Now apply this lens to recent European history. The conventional narrative tells a story of progress through cooperation: from the ruins of world wars emerged the Coal and Steel Community, then the Common Market, then the European Union—an ever-closer union delivering peace and prosperity.

This narrative contains truths. Interstate war has become unthinkable within Western Europe. Living standards have risen. These achievements are real.

But the narrative also obscures. Look at the pattern across crises:

**World wars** produced not a return to pre-1914 Europe but the first steps toward supranational institutions. **End of Cold War** produced not a Europe of sovereign nations but monetary union and eastern enlargement. **Financial crisis** produced not reform of monetary union's structural flaws but centralized fiscal authority through emergency measures. **Migration crisis** produced not border reinforcement but accelerated demographic transformation. **Pandemic** produced not a return to normal but digital surveillance infrastructure and common EU borrowing. **Ukraine war** produces not a negotiated settlement but military integration and further eastern expansion .

The pattern, once noticed, is striking: crisis after crisis, the response deepens integration and transfers authority upward. And crucially, the measures enacted often bear little relationship to resolving the stated causes of the crises. The financial crisis was caused by unsustainable debt and monetary union's structural flaws. The response didn't address these causes—it deepened monetary integration. The pandemic required public health measures. The response included those, but also created surveillance infrastructure and fiscal integration that long outlasted the emergency.

What explains this disjuncture? Not necessarily conspiracy. More likely, it's what systems theorists call **emergent direction**—a logic embedded in institutions, assumptions, and interests that produces coherent outcomes without requiring a central planner . Once created, institutions seek to expand their mandate. Once empowered, elites use crises to further empower themselves. The trajectory acquires momentum independent of anyone's intentions.

The consequence is a growing gap between elite and popular experience. From above, the European project looks like success: war eliminated, prosperity spread, rights expanded. From below, it looks like being acted upon: jobs lost to deindustrialization, communities transformed by rapid demographic change, identities delegitimized by official culture, decisions made in Brussels that national electorates cannot reverse .

This gap generates what political scientists call "constraining dissensus"—populations increasingly unwilling to accept transformations they didn't choose . The 2005 French and Dutch rejection of the EU constitution was a watershed. Voters in two founding members said no to further integration. The elite response? Enact the constitution anyway through the Lisbon Treaty, requiring no popular vote. The message was unmistakable: your vote doesn't matter.

When elites respond to resistance by further insulating themselves from popular control, alienation deepens. The spiral continues.

## The Young as Moral Barometer

Perhaps nowhere is this alienation more visible—and more tragic—than in the rising distress of young people.

Consider the statistics: adolescent depression and suicide have climbed steadily across the Western world. And in the most extreme cases, we see school shootings—the quiet catastrophe exploding into public view with terrifying force.

Our instinct is to diagnose this as illness of the individual brain. Chemical imbalances. Treatable mental health disorders. Pure evil. We medicate the pain and incarcerate the perpetrators, hoping to restore order .

But what if we have fundamentally misread the symptom? What if these young people are not ill, but are instead acting as the **moral barometer** of a profoundly unhealthy world—their anguish a rational response, and their most extreme actions a desperate attempt to be heard?

Recent research on school shooters reveals a consistent pattern: they "typically experience loss, trauma, bullying, abandonment, and undiagnosed psychiatric illness. They are often unwanted and marginalized children living in abusive environments" . They are identified as "troubled" by families, schools, and law enforcement—and then ignored.

When a teenager learns of distant wars, of civilians bombarded in their homes, of children their age displaced and killed, their emerging sense of justice is violently shaken. When they scroll through feeds saturated with ecological destruction and political turmoil, their minds aren't misfiring—they're absorbing the brutal reality of a system in crisis. Their depression isn't a glitch; it's a logical output from processing the violent data of our times .

In a warmongering society, those who refuse to normalize horror are labeled maladjusted. They're the canaries in the coal mine, whose silent collapse is the first warning of toxic atmosphere. Their grief for strangers, their anxiety about the future, their overwhelming sense of powerlessness—these are not symptoms of disorder. They're the appropriate emotional responses of a healthy moral compass spinning in a world where north has become south.

A healthy system relies on feedback loops. Pain tells us a hand is too close to a flame. Fever signals infection. The distress of youth is precisely this: a critical feedback signal telling us, in no uncertain terms, that the system is sick .

Our response has been to medicate the messenger. By reaching for prescription pads as first-line defense, we're not treating disease—we're silencing the alarm. We're chemically muffling the dissenting voices that provide necessary feedback for a society to correct its course.

And for a rare and tragic few, the pressure builds until it finds a new outlet. The school shooter is not a separate phenomenon from the depressed teen—he is the depressed teen who has been rendered invisible by a society that refuses to listen. Finding his quiet despair met with indifference, he makes a grotesque calculation: if the system won't hear my pain, it will hear my echo. He reproduces the violence he sees in the world to bypass filters that only register the language of force.

This is not to excuse the act. It is to understand it. To call it senseless misses the point entirely—it has a terrible, twisted sense. It is the logical endpoint of a system that refuses to hear its own critics.

## Metamorphosis, Not Annihilation

So where does this leave us? If the system is sick, if our institutions are unresponsive, if our youth are suffering—what do we do?

The temptation is to reach for the electric chair. To want to destroy the system entirely. This temptation is understandable. It offers clarity, satisfaction, the feeling of making the punishment fit the crime.

But remember the distinction that saves us from the fire. A society is not a body. Its people are not cells. When apartheid died, white South Africans didn't die with it. When the Berlin Wall fell, East Germans didn't cease to exist. The society can die while its people live .

This is the model: **metamorphosis, not annihilation**. The caterpillar doesn't die so the butterfly may live. The caterpillar *becomes* the butterfly. Its cells reorganize. Its form transforms. The continuity of life is preserved through radical change.

Applied to our sick systems, this means:

**Disable the extraction machinery.** Interrupt the flows of capital and resources that sustain predation. Debt cancellation, resource nationalism, parallel economies—these starve the beast without killing its people .

**Withdraw legitimacy.** Systems run on belief as much as violence. When the rest of the world stops pretending—when Western democracy is named as vehicle for extraction rather than moral cover—internal cohesion frays. Citizens who once felt proud begin to feel uneasy .

**Build alternatives that make the system irrelevant.** If the Global South builds its own financial institutions, media, supply chains—if it simply doesn't *need* the Western system anymore—the predator starves by becoming superfluous .

**Support internal resistance.** Every society contains dissidents, whistleblowers, activists. The world can amplify them, protect them, make their voices impossible to ignore.

**Make unsustainability visible.** The Western system is already dying—climate collapsing, democracies hollowing, populations sick with loneliness, inequality unmanageable, wars unwinnable. The task isn't to kill it, but to ensure it can't drag everyone down with it .

## Conclusion: The Stories We Choose

The fundamental attribution error is not just a cognitive quirk. It's a story we tell ourselves—a story that protects us from uncomfortable truths. If problems are caused by bad people, we can punish them and feel righteous. If problems are caused by systems we're part of, we might have to change.

The evidence suggests the second story is truer. Milgram's participants weren't monsters—they were ordinary people in extraordinary situations. The VA employees who falsified records weren't simply dishonest—they faced impossible targets with no alternative pathways. The school shooters aren't born evil—they're marginalized children whose distress signals were ignored until they exploded.

Seeing systemically doesn't mean abandoning moral judgment. It means making judgment more intelligent—more attentive to context, more humble about its own limitations, more focused on prevention than punishment. It means asking not just "who did this?" but "what produced this?" and "how can we redesign it?"

The cognitive warfare will continue. New semantic weapons will be deployed. The temptation to blame individuals will always be with us—it's human. But the human capacity for systemic honesty is resilient. It's the quiet voice that asks "who benefits?" when rhetoric gets loud. It's the stubborn refusal to mistake the menu for the meal.

The question facing your generation is whether you'll accept the stories you've been handed or demand the tools to see for yourselves. The world is not divided into good people and bad people. It's divided into those who benefit from current arrangements and those who don't, those who see clearly and those who can't, those who've been heard and those who've been silenced into screaming.

The system can change. It must change. But whether it changes through fire or metamorphosis depends on whether enough of us can learn to see—and to act on what we see—before it's too late.

The canaries are singing. The question is whether we'll listen.
