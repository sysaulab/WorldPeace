# Strategic Communication Plan
## The Səlilwət Community Trust: Gathering Support Across Diverse Constituencies

---

## Executive Summary

This strategic communication plan provides a framework for introducing the Səlilwət Community Trust to diverse stakeholders. The project's complexity—combining Indigenous economic traditions, behavioral science, housing innovation, and ecological limits—requires **tailored messaging** for each audience while maintaining core consistency.

**The Core Narrative (Universal):**

> "The Səlilwət Community Trust is a ten-year pilot project that will house 20,000 DTES residents in community-owned housing. Members contribute what they can; the community provides what they need. A simple tip system ensures that generosity and mutual support are recognized and rewarded. The project saves government money, builds community wealth, and restores dignity—without requiring anyone to abandon who they are."

**The One-Line Elevator Pitch (Choose based on audience):**

- **For government:** "A bulk-transfer model that saves $150 million annually while improving outcomes."
- **For Indigenous partners:** "Economic reconciliation through the return of potlatch principles."
- **For business:** "A stable local workforce, a new marketplace, and reduced demand on crisis services."
- **For community members:** "Housing you can't be evicted from, food you can always access, and a way to say thank you that actually matters."
- **For academics:** "A controlled test of hierarchy entropy theory with complete social network data."

## 2. Core Messaging by Stakeholder

### 2.1 DTES Residents (Current SRO Tenants, Homeless Individuals)

**Their Reality:** Trauma, extraction by landlords, constant insecurity, judgment by service providers, exhaustion with programs that promise much and deliver little.

**Their Question:** *"Is this just another program that will fail me, judge me, or disappear?"*

**Core Message:**

> "This is not a program. This is a home you can't be evicted from. You show up, you're part of the community, and everything basic is covered—no applications, no case workers, no judgment. The buildings are owned by all of us together. When someone helps you, you can tip them—and that tip becomes real money they can spend. When you help someone, they can tip you. No one tells you how to live. The only rule is that we're in this together."

**Key Language:**
- "Home," not "housing"
- "Belonging," not "integration"
- "No evictions," not "tenure security"
- "Tips for help," not "incentives for compliance"
- "Owned by us," not "community land trust"

**Language to Avoid:**
- "Intervention," "treatment," "case management," "clients"
- "Means-tested," "eligibility," "qualifying"
- "Neoliberal ideology," "behavioral sink" (academic jargon)
- "Ecological ceiling" (frame as "fair limits" instead)

**Channels:** SRO Collaborative outreach, existing tenant networks, word-of-mouth, trusted peer leaders, simple flyers with visuals.

---

### 2.2 First Nations Partners (Musqueam, Squamish, Tsleil-Waututh)

**Their Reality:** Centuries of dispossession, systematic suppression of potlatch, ongoing jurisdictional battles, overrepresentation in DTES, suspicion of yet another "partnership" that extracts Indigenous knowledge without returning power.

**Their Question:** *"Is this actually returning sovereignty to us, or just using our traditions to make a non-Indigenous project look good?"*

**Core Message:**

> "The potlatch was illegal for generations. Our laws were called 'wasteful' and 'irrational' by the same people who took our land and our children. Now we have an opportunity to rebuild an economic system based on those laws—not as a museum piece, but as a living institution. The tip economy is potlatch logic: you gain status by giving, wealth circulates rather than hoards, and the community grows stronger through generosity. We invite your elders to guide how this works. The Elders Floors in the towers are yours to govern. This is not consultation; this is leadership."

**Key Language:**
- "Potlatch," not "gift economy" (name it)
- "Our laws," not "traditions" (emphasizes sovereignty)
- "Suppression," not "history" (acknowledges ongoing impact)
- "Governance," not "participation"
- "Elders guide," not "Elders advise"

**Language to Avoid:**
- "Stakeholders" (implies equal footing with colonial institutions)
- "Cultural appropriation" (defensive; focus on leadership instead)
- "Reconciliation" (overused; specify what is being returned)
- "Pilot" (implies temporary; frame as permanent return)

**Channels:** Nation-to-nation protocols, Elder gatherings, land claims infrastructure, AHMA relationships, sitting fees for consultation time.

---

### 2.3 Urban Indigenous Organizations (AHMA, Friendship Centres, VAFCS)

**Their Reality:** Serving Indigenous populations with chronic underfunding, navigating between band councils and urban populations not always served by their home nations, competing for scarce resources.

**Their Question:** *"Does this help our people, or does it compete with us for funding and attention?"*

**Core Message:**

> "Thirty-one percent of DTES residents are Indigenous—triple the city average. They are our relatives. This project puts Indigenous governance at the center: Elders guide the tip economy, the Elders Floors follow Indigenous protocols, and the People's Investment Bank holds assets for future generations. We're not replacing your services; we're providing the housing and economic base that makes your services more effective. When our people are stable and housed, your addiction counselors can actually counsel, your cultural programs can actually teach, and your friendship centre can be a place of gathering rather than crisis response."

**Key Language:**
- "Our relatives," not "the Indigenous population"
- "Governance at the center," not "Indigenous representation"
- "Housing and economic base," not "wrap-around services"
- "Future generations," not "sustainability"

**Language to Avoid:**
- "Partner organizations" (implies equal; they are frontline)
- "Stakeholder engagement" (bureaucratic)
- "Service delivery" (clinical)

**Channels:** AHMA network, urban Indigenous gatherings, one-on-one meetings with executive directors, joint funding proposals.

### 2.4 BC Government (Housing, Mental Health, Social Development)

**Their Reality:** Escalating costs, public pressure on DTES conditions, limited new housing supply, unionized workforce, fear of setting precedents that create new entitlements.

**Their Question:** *"Will this save us money without creating political liability or union opposition?"*

**Core Message:**

> "The current system costs $45,000-70,000 per person annually and produces the same outcomes year after year. This model replaces individual case management with community-based internal audit. By transferring funds in bulk to the community trust, you eliminate 25-40% of administrative overhead immediately. The tip economy activates natural support networks, reducing demand on crisis services. Within five years, the community owns assets that remove people from the housing waitlist permanently. This is not a new program—it's a new funding mechanism that pays for outcomes rather than inputs. The savings are measurable, the assets are public-good, and the political risk is managed through Indigenous leadership and transparent data."

**Key Language:**
- "Bulk transfer," not "block grant" (avoids 1990s connotations)
- "Outcomes-based funding," not "experimental"
- "Administrative efficiency," not "cuts"
- "Community assets," not "public-private partnership"
- "Measurable savings," not "cost avoidance"

**Language to Avoid:**
- "Anti-capitalist," "post-neoliberal," "alternative economics" (triggering)
- "Unconditional" (use "streamlined" or "simplified" instead)
- "Decolonization" (use "Indigenous-led governance" for this audience)
- "Behavioral sink" (save for academics)

**Channels:** Deputy minister briefings, Treasury Board presentations, BC Housing research division, pilot program infrastructure (already exists for CLT demonstration projects).

### 2.5 Vancouver City Council

**Their Reality:** Constituent complaints about DTES conditions, NIMBY opposition to any new development, desire for visible "solutions" before next election, limited direct funding but significant permitting power.

**Their Question:** *"Will my constituents support this? Will it make the DTES less visible and contentious?"*

**Core Message:**

> "This project houses 20,000 people in towers that cast no shadows on surrounding neighborhoods—the park at ground level is a gift to the whole city. The underground marketplace becomes a destination, bringing customers to local businesses. The tip economy means residents have spending power and reason to support each other. The alternative is continued SROs, continued street disorder, continued emergency response. This is the first proposal that addresses root causes rather than symptoms, and it does so without displacing anyone. The towers are designed to integrate with the neighborhood, not wall it off."

**Key Language:**
- "No shadows" (specific, visual, addresses NIMBY)
- "Destination marketplace" (economic development frame)
- "Root causes," not "symptoms"
- "Without displacement," not "new housing" (addresses gentrification fear)
- "Integrated," not "dense"

**Language to Avoid:**
- "High-density" (triggers NIMBY; use "vertical villages")
- "Harm reduction" (use "health-focused" for public audiences)
- "Ecological ceiling" (use "sustainable limits")

**Channels:** One-on-one councillor briefings, planning department technical reviews, public open houses with visual renderings, Chinatown/Gastown/Strathcona community center meetings.

### 2.6 Federal Government (CMHC, Indigenous Services Canada)

**Their Reality:** National housing strategy commitments, reconciliation framework, desire for "scalable" innovations, jurisdictional caution about provincial responsibilities.

**Their Question:** *"Is this replicable? Does it advance reconciliation? Is this federal turf or provincial?"*

**Core Message:**

> "CMHC's own research has documented the DTES Community Land Trust as a promising model. This project scales that model to an entire neighborhood, adds an Indigenous governance structure rooted in potlatch law, and creates a research framework that will produce replicable findings for other urban Indigenous and marginalized populations. The federal role is seed capital for the People's Investment Bank—a one-time infrastructure investment that leverages ongoing provincial operational funding. This directly advances the National Housing Strategy's Indigenous Housing Fund and the reconciliation framework. It's a demonstration project with national implications."

**Key Language:**
- "Scalable," not "pilot" (implies broader applicability)
- "Leverages provincial funding," not "requires federal ongoing"
- "National implications," not "local solution"
- "Indigenous Housing Fund alignment," not "new request"

**Language to Avoid:**
- "Jurisdictional issues" (don't highlight; frame as partnership)
- "Experimental" (use "demonstration")
- "Provincial responsibility" (don't create turf battles)

**Channels:** CMHC Innovation Fund, Indigenous Services Canada urban programming, Minister's office briefings (through Indigenous partners), existing CLT research networks.

### 2.8 Health Authorities (Vancouver Coastal Health, PHSA)

**Their Reality:** Overwhelmed emergency departments, toxic drug crisis consuming resources, desire for upstream interventions, professional culture requiring evidence.

**Their Question:** *"Does this reduce acute care utilization? Is there evidence it works?"*

**Core Message:**

> "This project is designed as a randomized controlled trial with complete data linkage. We will measure emergency visits, overdose events, and primary care utilization against matched controls. The intervention—stable housing, unconditional belonging, and a recognition economy—targets the social determinants of health that drive 80% of your acute care costs. The tip economy activates peer support networks, which research shows are more effective than professional intervention for many populations. Within five years, we expect 50% reduction in overdose events and 30% reduction in emergency visits among integrated members. The research design will produce the evidence you need."

**Key Language:**
- "Data linkage," "matched controls," "measured outcomes" (evidence frame)
- "Social determinants," "upstream intervention" (public health frame)
- "Peer support networks," not "informal care"
- "Acute care utilization," not "hospital costs"

**Language to Avoid:**
- "Behavioral sink" (too esoteric)
- "Unconditional" (use "low-barrier" in health contexts)
- "Potlatch" (use "recognition economy" for this audience)

**Channels:** VCH Research Institute, Population Health division, Chief Medical Health Officer briefings, academic partnerships with UBC Faculty of Medicine.

### 2.9 Academic Institutions (UBC, SFU)

**Their Reality:** Need for research funding, publication pipelines, community partnerships for grant competitiveness, ethical oversight requirements.

**Their Question:** *"What's the research question? Is the data accessible? Is it publishable?"*

**Core Message:**

> "This is the first empirical test of hierarchy entropy theory in a human population. The complete tip transaction graph will be an unprecedented dataset—every act of recognition, every moment of generosity, made visible and measurable. Social network analysis can track the emergence of Simmelian ties, core-periphery structure, and the maintenance of uncertainty gradients over a decade. The three-tier design allows causal inference about the effects of unconditional provision versus recognition-based distribution. Multiple disciplines—sociology, economics, network science, public health, Indigenous studies—have intersecting interests. The data will be open (anonymized) and the research framework is participatory, ensuring community relevance and ethical rigor."

**Key Language:**
- "Hierarchy entropy," "Simmelian ties," "network analysis" (disciplinary hooks)
- "Unprecedented dataset," not "case study"
- "Causal inference," not "correlation"
- "Multiple disciplines," not "interdisciplinary" (implies funding potential)

**Language to Avoid:**
- "Advocacy research" (threatens objectivity frame)
- "Community-controlled data" (use "participatory governance")
- "Ten-year timeline" (emphasize interim publications)

**Channels:** UBC School of Community and Regional Planning, Department of Sociology, Institute for Resources Environment and Sustainability, SFU Urban Studies, tri-council funding networks.

### 2.10 Advocacy Groups (Carnegie Community Action Project, Pivot Legal Society, VANDU)

**Their Reality:** Deep distrust of "solutions" imposed from outside, hard-won gains in tenant protections, suspicion of anything that might enable displacement or reduce advocacy power.

**Their Question:** *"Is this co-opting our movement? Will it weaken our ability to fight evictions and demand rights?"*

**Core Message:**

> "You've fought for decades to protect tenants from extraction. This project takes that fight to its logical conclusion: the tenants own the building. There are no landlords. There are no evictions for rent arrears because there is no rent. The People's Investment Bank holds the land in trust forever—it can never be sold. The tip economy means the person who sits with someone in withdrawal gets recognized the same way the person who makes furniture does. This isn't a program designed by outsiders; it's a structure that gives residents economic sovereignty. We need your scrutiny, your skepticism, and your members' voices in the governance. If it doesn't work for the most marginalized, it doesn't work."

**Key Language:**
- "No landlords," "no evictions," "no rent" (core demands)
- "Economic sovereignty," not "social housing"
- "Your scrutiny," not "your support" (invites critical engagement)
- "Most marginalized," not "vulnerable" (respects agency)

**Language to Avoid:**
- "Pilot" (implies temporary; use "permanent structure")
- "Service provider" (you are not providing services; you are enabling ownership)
- "Harm reduction" (use "non-judgment" instead)

**Channels:** One-on-one with trusted leaders, small group briefings at Carnegie or VANDU office, written commitments to tenant governance, patience—trust takes years.

### 2.11 Media

**Their Reality:** Need for compelling narrative, visual elements, human interest, conflict or resolution angles, short attention spans.

**Their Question:** *"What's the story? Who are the characters? Why should readers care?"*

**Core Message:**

> "For decades, we've tried to fix the DTES with programs and case workers. Nothing has worked. This project tries something different: what if the community owned everything? What if everyone's basics were covered, no questions asked? And what if there was a simple way to say thank you—with actual money—to the person who helped you through a hard night? It's housing. It's economics. It's Indigenous tradition. And it might just work. Meet the first 100 residents who will move in next year, and the Elders who are guiding how the tip system works."

**Key Angles:**
- **Human interest:** Profiles of founding members, Elders, the first tip transaction
- **Visual:** Tower renderings, underground market, park, banking app interface
- **Conflict:** The fight against predatory landlords, the inefficiency of current system
- **Innovation:** First project of its kind in Canada, potlatch economics revived
- **Controversy (manage carefully):** "Some say it's communism. Supporters say it's common sense."

**Key Language:**
- "Groundbreaking," not "experimental"
- "Community-owned," not "collective"
- "Indigenous tradition revived," not "cultural appropriation"
- "No rent, no evictions, no judgment" (memorable)

**Language to Avoid:**
- Jargon (behavioral sink, hierarchy entropy, ecological ceiling)
- Lengthy explanations of mechanism (show the app, don't explain the code)
- Defensive posturing (anticipate criticism, don't pre-emptively argue)

**Channels:** Exclusive launch with one trusted journalist, visual media first (renderings, video), resident spokespeople trained, open house for media with access to site and members.

## 4. Phased Engagement Strategy

### Phase 1: Foundation (Months 1-6) – Trust Building

**Objective:** Secure core Indigenous partners and first 100 founding members.

**Activities:**
- Nation-to-nation protocol meetings with Musqueam, Squamish, Tsleil-Waututh
- Elder gatherings to design tip economy governance
- One-on-one with advocacy group leaders (Carnegie, Pivot, VANDU)
- Outreach through SRO Collaborative to identify first 100 founding members
- No media outreach yet

**Success Indicators:**
- Written support from at least two of three host nations
- 50 founding members committed
- Elder Advisory Committee formed with terms of reference

### Phase 2: Design (Months 6-12) – Co-Creation

**Objective:** Refine governance, design banking app, secure initial government buy-in.

**Activities:**
- Community design sessions for tip economy rules
- Banking app prototyping with founding members
- Deputy minister briefings (off-record)
- City planning department pre-application consultations
- Academic partnership MOUs drafted

**Success Indicators:**
- App prototype tested with 50 members
- Written expressions of interest from BC Housing and VCH
- Pre-application accepted by City planning

### Phase 3: Announcement (Months 12-18) – Public Launch

**Objective:** Controlled public announcement, seed funding secured.

**Activities:**
- Exclusive media launch with trusted journalist
- Open house with renderings at Carnegie
- Funding announcements coordinated with partners
- First building acquisition (existing SRO)

**Success Indicators:**
- Positive media coverage in at least two major outlets
- Seed funding committed ($50M+)
- First building purchased

### Phase 4: Implementation (Months 18-60) – Demonstration

**Objective:** First 500 members housed, production launched, research underway.

**Activities:**
- Regular community updates through floor meetings
- Quarterly research briefings for partners
- Annual public reports
- Continuous media engagement through resident stories

**Success Indicators:**
- 500 members housed
- First production shop profitable
- Tip economy generating measurable network data
- Interim research findings published

## 5. Anticipated Objections and Responses

### Objection 1: "This is communism / a cult / separatist."

**Response:** "This is community-owned housing, like a strata but without individual ownership—exactly the model CMHC has been funding through the Community Land Trust demonstration program. The tip system is a modern version of how extended families have always supported each other. The difference is we're making it visible and fair. The buildings are in the city, open to the public, with a park and marketplace anyone can use. This isn't separation; it's integration with dignity."

### Objection 2: "You're enabling addiction by not requiring sobriety."

**Response:** "The current system requires sobriety for housing, and people die on the street. We're trying something different: housing first, without conditions, and letting the community and environment do the healing. The tip economy means the person who sits with someone through withdrawal gets recognized and rewarded. That's happening now, invisibly. We're just making it visible and sustainable. If the theory is right, the environment itself promotes healthier choices. We're measuring that to find out."

### Objection 3: "This takes money away from existing services."

**Response:** "This doesn't redirect existing service funding—it creates a new funding stream for housing and economic development that reduces demand on crisis services. Your addiction counselors will still have jobs; they'll just have clients who are housed and stable instead of clients who are in survival mode. The savings to the health and justice systems are where the efficiency gains come from."

### Objection 4: "You're gentrifying the DTES."

**Response:** "Gentrification displaces people. This project displaces no one. Every resident comes from the DTES streets or SROs. The land is held in trust forever—it can never be sold. The buildings are owned by the community, not developers. This is the opposite of gentrification: it's returning land and wealth to the people who live there."

### Objection 5: "Why should government fund this when we already fund housing?"

**Response:** "You're already spending $45,000-70,000 per person on the current system. This model saves 25-40% of that through administrative efficiency and keeps the rest circulating in the community instead of leaking to landlords and external vendors. It's not new spending—it's smarter spending. The one-time seed funding for the bank is an investment that generates ongoing savings and builds public assets."

### Objection 6: "This is cultural appropriation of potlatch."

**Response:** "That's why the project is Indigenous-led from the start. The Elders Advisory Committee governs how potlatch principles are applied. The host nations are partners, not consultants. If they say it's appropriation, we stop. If they say it's revitalization, we follow their lead. This is their call, not ours."

## 6. Communication Principles

1. **Start with relationship, not presentation.** Meet people where they are. Listen more than you talk. Trust precedes funding.

2. **Use plain language first, theory second.** The behavioral sink is fascinating; lead with home and belonging. Academics can dig deeper later.

3. **Visuals matter more than words.** Renderings of towers and park, screenshots of the banking app, photos of founding members. Show, don't tell.

4. **Resident voices are the only voices that matter.** Train members to speak for themselves. Staff and academics stay in the background.

5. **Transparency disarms criticism.** Open data, open meetings, open books. If you have nothing to hide, you have nothing to fear.

6. **Patience is strategy.** The DTES has seen a hundred "solutions." Trust takes years. Move at the speed of trust, not the speed of funding cycles.

7. **Frame as expansion, not attack.** You're not saying current services are bad; you're saying this adds something new. Complementary, not oppositional.

8. **Name what you're against indirectly.** Instead of "anti-capitalist," say "community-owned." Instead of "anti-eviction," say "no landlords." The positive vision carries the critique.
