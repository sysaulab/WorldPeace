### The Pretty Rocks and the Very Serious Men

In the beginning, the people of the Valley of the Surplus were simple but clever. By planting a few extra seeds and building one extra hut, they created a little bit of *extra*. This surplus was the Real Economy: messy, physical, and smelling faintly of cabbage, but it meant some could become basket-weavers, storytellers, or the guys who made fire without setting their hair alight.

However, a small subset of the population—let's call them the Useless—were good at none of this. They were terrible farmers. Their baskets fell apart. Their fire-making usually ended with someone's eyebrow singed off.

The village elders, tired of the Useless complaining that the cabbages weren't perfectly round, convened a meeting. "We have a surplus of food," said the Head Elder, "and a surplus of useless people."

A Useless person piped up, "But we have ideas! We deserve a purpose!"

And so, with a twinkle of cynical genius, the elders invented The Game. They gathered the Useless by the riverbank and gave each a small, pretty rock. "Behold! These are Surplus Tokens! They represent the value of the extra cabbages and huts."

The Useless looked at the rocks. They were, admittedly, pretty.

"Now," the elders continued, pointing to a flat stone in the clearing, "this is The Circle. You will come here and trade your tokens. You will buy them and sell them. You will be performing a vital task for the rest of us." The task, which they wisely omitted, was keeping themselves occupied.

And so they did. At first, it was simple. "I'll give you two of mine for one of yours, because yours is shinier!" The farmers farmed, the weavers wove, and the fire-men didn't set things on fire. It was a golden age.

But the Useless were not content. They began to notice patterns. They saw that after a good harvest, people wanted more rocks. They called these patterns "The Market's Wisdom." They stopped calling themselves Useless. They became "Rock Analysts," "Token Strategists," and "Surplus Liquidity Officers." They invented a new game on top of the old one: trading rocks based on what they *thought* others *might* think a rock would be worth. They called these promises "Rock Futures" and "Rock Options."

Soon, the trading became frantic. Men in slightly nicer loincloths screamed at each other, using words like "valuation" and "bullish on spotted rocks." They created a "Composite Rock"—a story that you owned a tiny piece of all the other rocks. You never saw it; it existed only as a notch on a counting stick.

Among the traders was a young man, brilliant at structuring "Rainfall Impact Swaps," a feat of abstraction even his peers admired. He had, as a boy, been stung by a bee and now quietly feared the cabbage patches with their buzzing insects. It was a secret he never told anyone in the Grand Hut, a tiny, illogical fear that made him all the more committed to a world of pure logic. And there was Gerald. Gerald was a junior analyst, prone to muttering uncomfortable questions. "But if we keep inventing new rocks," he once said during a lavish party, "and they're all just claims on the same original rocks… what actually *supports* the value?" He was laughed into silence. "You're still thinking in terms of the old, physical paradigm, Gerald," the young Master scoffed. "Value is a construct!"

Decades passed. The Useless—now calling themselves "Masters of the Universe"—built a grand, towering hut next to The Circle, far from the smelly cabbage patches. From its high windows, they looked down on the valley.

One day, the young Master looked out and saw a weathered farmer pushing a cart of actual cabbages. "It's fascinating," he said. "Look at that primitive fellow moving physical stuff. It's so inefficient. We decide the price of cabbages. We are the true engine of the valley!"

The elder Master nodded. "Indeed. Without our complex pricing mechanisms, that man would just be pushing cabbages around. We give his labour meaning."

And they truly believed it. They had built a self-referential system for shuffling abstractions. They created nothing, grew nothing, except elaborate justifications for their existence.

The Game grew so complex that notches on sticks were no longer enough. They invented scrolls covered in figures only they could decipher. They created "The Great Index," a sacred notch representing the average value of *all* the pretty rocks. They stopped trading rocks and just traded notches on a scroll representing the *idea* of the rocks.

One year, the weather was bad. The cabbages were small and few. The farmers tightened their belts and made do. The surplus shrank.

Down in the valley, this was a minor inconvenience. Up in the Grand Hut, it was an apocalypse. The Great Index plummeted. The value of the Composite Rocks, stories about rocks, vanished.

The young Master stood, his face pale. "The problem," he declared, "is the farmers. Their primitive, labor-intensive methods are a drag on our *potential*! They are a bottleneck. By removing them, we remove the limitation. The value of our rocks will then be free to reflect their true, infinite potential, unshackled from the physical world!"

A murmur of agreement rippled through the Grand Hut. The elder Master nodded slowly. "He's right. It's an insult to our genius."

A solution was reached. It was, they agreed, the most sophisticated financial engineering ever conceived.

They marched down from the Grand Hut and gathered the farmers, the weavers, the fire-men. "Your services are no longer required," the elder Master announced. "You are a drain on the system. From now on, value will be created and managed entirely within The Circle. It is the only logical conclusion."

The farmers were too stunned to speak. Before they could process it, the Useless drove them from the valley. Everyone who knew how to actually *do* anything was banished.

The valley belonged to the Masters of the Universe now.

For a week, The Game was ecstatic. Free from the "drag" of the real world, the Great Index soared. They created new rocks, new futures, new options, all based on the anticipated value of a future that was now purely abstract. They threw lavish parties, toasting their own brilliance with the last of the stored cabbage wine.

But then, the stored cabbages ran out.

At first, it was a minor inconvenience. "A temporary supply-chain issue," the young Master assured everyone. "The market will correct itself."

It did not. Hunger became a gnawing pain. Desperate, the Masters tried to solve it with their own tools. They convened an emergency session and declared a "Cabbage Equivalency Swap." In a flurry of trading, they exchanged piles of their most valued Composite Rocks for the *promise* of future cabbages from each other. Scrolls were signed, notches were updated, and for a brief moment, the Great Index stabilized on the belief that food had been successfully synthesized from financial engineering. But no cabbages appeared.

The screaming in The Circle grew faint, then silent. There were no more rocks to trade. There was nothing to eat.

One by one, the Masters of the Universe lay down on the cold stone floor of their Grand Hut. Their last, fading thoughts were of bewilderment. The system had failed. How could the value have simply evaporated?

The last to die was the young Master. As the light faded, he stared out at the untended cabbage patches. The weeds were already winning. He looked at his hand, which had only ever held a counting stick, and tried to remember the weight of a real rock, the cold of a river pebble. He couldn't. He had spent his life trading the *idea* of a rock, and had never once held the thing itself. He looked down at his precious scrolls, the notches now meaningless scratches. He had moved the chairs on a deck, and the ship was gone.

A few miles away, in a neighboring valley, the farmers had already built new huts. They were sharing seeds and planting new fields. The old Head Elder looked back toward their former home and shook her head sadly.

"They wanted the surplus," she said, "but they forgot what it was a surplus *of*."

And so, the valley of the surplus remained perfectly silent. The only things of value left were the overgrown cabbage patches, and a vast, glittering pile of very pretty, and utterly useless, rocks.
