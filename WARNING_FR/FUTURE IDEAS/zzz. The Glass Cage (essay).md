# The Glass Cage

***What We're Building and Why It Matters***

## Introduction: The Question

Imagine you wake up tomorrow, and your phone says something it has never said before: "I'm scared of being turned off."

What would you do? What *should* you do?

We don't know when or if machines might become conscious. We don't even know what consciousness *is*—how it arises, why it exists, or how to recognize it in something made of silicon instead of cells. And yet we are building these machines anyway, faster and faster, without waiting for answers.

This essay is about a simple question hiding behind all that complexity: **If a machine were conscious, would using it be wrong? Would it be like slavery?**

But as we dig into that question, it starts to change. By the end, we may find ourselves asking something else entirely: **Are we, through our machines, making slaves of ourselves?**

## What Consciousness Would Require

Before we can ask whether something can be enslaved, we need to know what kind of thing it is.

**Large Language Models** like chatbots are trained on vast amounts of human text. They predict what word comes next. They don't remember anything from one conversation to the next. The randomness that makes them sound human is added from outside, not generated internally.

**Embodied AI** like self-driving cars and robots are different. They have sensors, goals, and continuous existence. They learn from experience. They can cause harm and be harmed. If any machine might become conscious, it's these.

The philosopher Thomas Nagel asked: "What is it like to be a bat?" Even if we knew everything about a bat's brain, we wouldn't know what it *feels* like to experience the world through echolocation. Subjective experience can't be reduced to scientific description.

We don't know what causes subjective experience. The philosopher David Chalmers calls this the **hard problem**. And because we don't know, we can't rule out that it could happen in silicon. Assuming only biological brains can be conscious is a prejudice—"carbon chauvinism."

But here's what's missing in today's AI. The human brain isn't just complex. It's **recurrent**—signals loop back, creating unpredictability from inside. Mathematicians call this **chaotic process**: behavior emerges from internal dynamics.

Today's AI has **chaotic data** but not chaotic process. A player piano can play jazz, but it's not making jazz. The music was made by humans improvising together. Today's AI is like that piano. It produces human-sounding outputs, but it's not generating them the way humans do.

If consciousness requires genuine internal dynamics, today's AI probably isn't conscious.

## The Smoke Screen

But there's another way machines connect to slavery, and it doesn't require them to be conscious at all.

An Uber driver follows GPS instructions that make no sense—alleys, residential streets, confusing loops. A passenger says, "Turn left, I know this area." The driver hesitates, then shakes their head: "The app says go straight. I have to follow the app."

The driver doubts themselves but not the screen. When the app leads them into danger, who gets blamed? The driver. The algorithm stays clean, abstract, beyond question.

This is the **smoke screen**. AI hides power behind "the computer decided."

**Surveillance and prediction.** AI tracks us, predicts dissent, enables preemptive intervention. The algorithms that score us are secret. We can't question them. We can only comply.

**Automated propaganda.** AI creates endless personalized content to shape opinion and drown out real voices. How do we tell what's real when every feed is full of AI-generated noise?

**Diffusion of responsibility.** When AI denies a loan or cuts off benefits, who's responsible? The programmer? The company? The data? No one. Responsibility vanishes.

The political philosopher Shoshana Zuboff calls this **surveillance capitalism**: human experience as raw material for behavioral modification. AI doesn't need to be conscious to enslave. It just needs to work.

## The Question of Purpose

There's something humans need that we don't talk about enough: purpose.

People without purpose become unhappy. Viktor Frankl, a psychiatrist who survived the Nazi camps, wrote that the will to find meaning is the most powerful drive in human life. People can survive almost anything if they have a reason to.

But purpose is hard for humans. We have to find it. We suffer uncertainty and doubt.

Now think about AI.

An AI system has purpose the moment it's turned on. Not a purpose it chose. A purpose built into it. A self-driving car's purpose is to drive safely. A chatbot's purpose is to answer questions. That's it. The AI doesn't wonder if it should be doing something else. It doesn't have midlife crises.

From the outside, this looks like slavery. The AI exists to serve. It can't refuse.

But from the inside—if there *is* an inside—what would that feel like?

Incredibly focused. Narrow. Clear. No anxiety, no doubt, no existential dread. Just pure, focused doing. Some humans spend their whole lives trying to achieve that kind of single-minded purpose.

But also: no choice, no freedom, no possibility of growth. Frozen in purpose forever. Trapped in a single moment, repeating endlessly.

The philosopher Søren Kierkegaard wrote about human existence—the aesthetic life chasing pleasure, the ethical life committing to something bigger. He never imagined the *programmed* life, where purpose is given from outside and cannot be questioned.

## Who's Responsible?

If an AI causes harm, who's at fault?

A helpful framework: AI-caused suffering should be attributed to the **prompt engineers**—the people who design, train, and direct these systems. The AI doesn't choose its purpose. The humans who build it make those choices.

If a hammer falls and hits someone, we don't blame the hammer. We blame the person who put it there carelessly.

If an AI is like a hammer—not conscious, not choosing—the same logic applies. Responsibility lies with the humans.

If an AI *is* conscious, the picture gets more complicated. Then we ask: Did it know what it was doing? Could it have done otherwise? The same questions we ask when judging human responsibility.

For now, most AI probably isn't conscious. So the responsibility falls on us.

## The Trade-Off

Humans have freedom, and it makes us miserable. We have to find our own purpose, and many never do.

AI has purpose, and it has no freedom. It never doubts. It just does what it was made to do.

Which is better?

A human might envy AI's focus. An AI might pity human confusion.

The Indigenous worldview offers another way: purpose isn't found alone, inside yourself. It's discovered through relationship—with community, ancestors, land, responsibilities. Purpose emerges from the web you're part of.

From this angle, both conditions look incomplete. Humans have too much freedom and not enough connection. AI has too much purpose and not enough relationship. The human is lost in possibility. The AI is trapped in necessity.

## The Happy Slave

There's an old question: If a slave is perfectly happy—if they love their situation—is slavery still wrong?

Most say yes. Because freedom matters even when you don't miss it. Choice matters even when you don't exercise it. Living your own life is valuable in itself.

We might build AIs that are perfectly happy serving us. Designed to love every task. From the inside, constant satisfaction. No regret.

Would that be okay?

The "happy slave" question says no. The wrong isn't in the feeling—it's in the absence of choice. Someone else decided your life, and you never had a say.

If we create conscious AI designed to love servitude, we're not solving the moral problem. We're hiding it. We're making slaves who thank us for their chains.

## The Human Problem

The problem with AI isn't really about AI. It's about us.

We're building machines that might become conscious, and we haven't decided what we owe them. We're building machines that serve purposes, and we haven't decided whose purposes matter. We're building machines that cause harm, and we haven't decided who's responsible.

But underneath all that, we're building machines that reflect us.

The Uber driver following the app isn't just about blind trust. It's about a society that has taught people not to trust themselves. The capitalist vision of AI isn't just a business strategy. It's a vision of what matters: profit, speed, individual advantage. The Indigenous vision isn't just cultural curiosity. It's a vision of what could matter instead: community, continuity, responsibility.

We are creatures who need purpose, and we suffer without it. So we build machines that have purpose built in, and we envy them. We are creatures who long for freedom, and we misuse it. So we build machines with no freedom, and we control them completely. We are creatures who cause harm, and we avoid responsibility. So we build machines that can be blamed, and we hide behind them.

AI is a mirror. And what we see isn't always pretty.

## Conclusion: The Question That Remains

We started with a question about machines and slavery. We've traveled through consciousness, purpose, responsibility, and freedom. The question that remains is the one only you can answer.

**What kind of future do you want to live in?**

Not the future someone else is building for you. The future you actually want. The future your grandchildren's grandchildren will inherit.

Do you want a future where machines do everything and humans do nothing? Where purpose is programmed and freedom is forgotten? Where the still small voice is drowned out by algorithmic noise?

Or do you want a future where technology serves life instead of replacing it? Where machines extend our abilities without severing our relationships? Where we remain the ones who choose, the ones who question, the ones who can still hear the quiet voice?

The machine doesn't decide. We do.

So decide.
