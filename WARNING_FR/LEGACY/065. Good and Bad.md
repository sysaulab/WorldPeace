# The Systemic Context of Conduct: Toward a Relational Understanding of Human Behavior

## Abstract

This paper develops a systems-theoretic framework for understanding human behavior that challenges essentialist notions of "good" and "bad" people. Drawing on foundational principles of systems theory, behavioral ethics, and social psychology, I argue that individuals function as open systems whose behaviors emerge from the dynamic interplay of internal psychological structures and external situational constraints. Rather than reflecting stable moral dispositions, antisocial or legally proscribed behaviors represent statistically predictable responses to systemic pressures, feedback mechanisms, and resource constraints that render certain situations intractable from the actor's perspective. The paper examines how cognitive biases, organizational cultures, and incentive structures create patterned behavioral outcomes across populations, suggesting that the fundamental attribution error systematically misdirects attention from systemic causes to individual character. After developing this argument through case analysis and theoretical synthesis, I engage in critical self-examination, considering limitations including the risk of determinism and the challenge of individual agency. The paper concludes by exploring implications for legal systems, organizational design, and social policy.

**Keywords:** systems theory, behavioral ethics, situationism, fundamental attribution error, complex systems, moral responsibility

## 1. Introduction: Beyond Essentialist Conceptions of Human Character

The proposition that "there are no bad people, only bad situations" strikes many as intuitively false. It seems to conflict with lived experience, with legal frameworks predicated on individual responsibility, and with the human tendency to make rapid moral judgments about others. Yet a growing body of interdisciplinary research—spanning systems theory, behavioral ethics, cognitive psychology, and organizational sociology—suggests that this proposition contains more truth than our intuitive essentialism readily admits.

Humans are, as van der Maas observes, "the ultimate complex systems" . Like all complex systems, they cannot be understood through reductionist approaches that fragment the person from context. The behavioral complexity theory developed over four decades ago recognized that human functioning must be studied "at its own level of complexity," considering the multidimensional interactions between individuals and their environments . When we observe behavior that appears morally reprehensible or legally culpable, we are typically witnessing not the expression of a fundamentally flawed character, but rather the predictable output of a complex system navigating constraints that may be invisible to outside observers.

This paper advances a systemic framework for understanding human conduct, arguing that:

1. Individuals are open systems whose behaviors emerge from ongoing interactions between internal psychological structures and external environmental inputs
2. Behavioral patterns across populations show statistical regularities that reflect systemic constraints rather than dispositional distributions
3. Situations characterized by impossible demands, conflicting incentives, or blocked pathways reliably produce behaviors retrospectively labeled as anti-social or irrational
4. The observer's tendency toward fundamental attribution error systematically obscures these systemic dynamics

The argument proceeds in five movements. First, I establish the systems-theoretic foundations for understanding human behavior. Second, I examine empirical evidence demonstrating situational influences on ethical conduct. Third, I develop the concept of "statistically narrow outcomes" across populations facing similar constraints. Fourth, I address the relationship between systemic explanation and moral accountability. Fifth, I engage in critical self-examination of the framework's limitations. The paper concludes by considering practical implications for how societies respond to undesirable behavior.

## 2. Systems Theory as Metatheoretical Framework

### 2.1 Foundational Principles of Systemic Thought

Systems theory offers an alternative to what Overton terms the "split metatheory" underpinning conventional psychological and moral discourse . This split worldview, with Cartesian roots, treats human functioning as composed of discrete, independent parts and assumes "a rock bottom unchanging nature to reality" composed of elements that "preserve their identity regardless of context" . Within such a framework, moral character appears as a stable property of individuals—something one either possesses or lacks, independent of circumstances.

Systems theory replaces these atomistic assumptions with holism as a guiding principle. A system is "a wider whole that is made up of multiple simultaneously occurring and interrelated constituents or parts" . These constituents mutually affect each other, such that "the meaning and function of a system's constituents depend on the whole of which they are parts, and the functioning of the whole depends on the organization of the parts" . Applied to human behavior, this means that actions cannot be understood in isolation from the psychological, social, and material systems in which they are embedded.

Von Bertalanffy's general system theory, developed as "an antidote to mechanistic split assumptions," emphasized that complex phenomena require analysis at their own level of complexity rather than reduction to component parts . When we reduce a person's morally questionable action to a character flaw, we commit precisely the reductionist error that systems theory warns against—treating a complex, context-dependent output as a simple, context-independent property.

### 2.2 Humans as Open Systems

A critical distinction in systems theory is between closed and open systems. Closed systems operate independently of their environments, while open systems engage in continuous exchange with surrounding contexts. Humans are paradigmatically open systems, constantly processing information, responding to feedback, and adapting to changing conditions.

Streufert and Satish note that in behavioral complexity theory, "the research setting must allow full systemic functioning to emerge" and "contain sufficient complexity to represent meaningful day-to-day experiences" . This methodological principle reflects an ontological reality: human behavior cannot be understood outside the contexts that continuously shape it. The same individual, placed in different systemic contexts, will produce different behavioral outputs—not because their "essential" character has changed, but because character is not an essence at all. It is, rather, an emergent property of person-system interactions.

The cognitive-affective personality system (CAPS) theory, developed by Mischel and Shoda, provides a detailed account of how this works at the psychological level . Within this framework, personality consists not of static traits but of "the organisation of the relationships among" cognitive and affective mental states. These connections, "formed through experience and strengthened through use," create stable patterns of behavior *within similar situations* while allowing variation *across situations*. The individual's "behavioural signature" reflects "personality coherence" precisely because it shows predictable variation across contexts . What looks like inconsistency from a trait-based perspective is actually consistency from a systems perspective—the system responding predictably to different inputs.

### 2.3 Multiple Causality and Emergence

Systems theory recognizes multiple kinds of causality operating simultaneously. Linear cause-effect models, which trace a straight line from individual intention to behavioral outcome, capture only a fraction of the causal dynamics actually producing action. Raeff emphasizes that systems involve "multiple kinds of causality or explanation" . Behavior is shaped by efficient causes (triggering events), material causes (available resources), formal causes (cultural scripts and norms), and final causes (goals and purposes)—all operating in concert.

Emergence is another key concept: systems produce properties that are not present in their component parts taken individually. Cooperation produces outcomes no individual could achieve alone; conflict produces harms no individual would choose in isolation. The moral quality of an action—its goodness or badness—is not a property of the actor alone but an emergent property of the actor-situation system. As Quintero notes, "When a system is operating dysfunctionally, the people within it tend to behave dysfunctionally" . This is not because dysfunctional people gravitate toward dysfunctional systems (though some do), but because systemic properties shape behavioral possibilities in ways that transcend individual dispositions.

## 3. The Situational Production of Ethical Failure

### 3.1 Behavioral Ethics and the "Want Self" vs. "Should Self"

The emerging field of behavioral ethics provides empirical support for systemic understandings of moral conduct. Drawing on Kahneman's dual-process model of cognition, researchers distinguish between System 1 thinking (fast, automatic, intuitive) and System 2 thinking (slow, effortful, deliberative) . System 1 thinking is "error-prone" in ethical domains because it relies on heuristics and emotional responses that may miss moral dimensions entirely.

Bazerman and Tenbrunsel observe that System 1 thinking often dominates ethical decision-making, such that our "'want' (emotional and self-interested) selves can override our 'should' selves" . This creates "blind spots"—situations where actors fail to recognize that a decision has ethical implications. The problem is not that people lack moral principles; it is that those principles are not automatically activated in all situations. Whether they become active depends on contextual cues, cognitive load, and the organizational framing of decisions.

This framework explains a puzzling phenomenon documented across numerous studies: people reliably predict that they would behave ethically in hypothetical scenarios, yet reliably fail to do so when actually placed in those scenarios. Milgram's obedience experiments provide the classic example: when the "baseline condition" was explained to diverse groups, "most said they would stop the experiment when the shock levels were still very low," yet most subjects in the actual experiment continued to high levels . The discrepancy does not indicate hypocrisy or self-deception (though these may play roles). It indicates that moral behavior is not a simple output of moral beliefs but an emergent property of complex person-situation interactions.

### 3.2 Organizational Culture and Incentive Structures

Organizational contexts powerfully shape behavioral possibilities through formal incentives and informal cultural norms. The 2014 Veterans Administration scandal illustrates this dynamic with tragic clarity. Performance incentives rewarded work units for meeting patient appointment targets, but the agency was understaffed. Employees responded by falsifying records about appointment timeliness, which "obscured the problem that the agency was understaffed, with the tragic result that patients actually died while waiting for appointments" .

From a dispositional perspective, the employees who falsified records were simply dishonest. From a systems perspective, they were individuals facing an impossible situation: they could meet targets only by falsifying data, and the targets existed within a system that provided no alternative pathway to success. The self-serving bias—"the tendency people have to process information that supports their own self-interests or pre-existing views"—further enabled the behavior by allowing employees to rationalize falsification as serving patient needs . Notably, "when employees feel mistreated, they are more likely to mistreat others" . The system created conditions that degraded ethical sensitivity across the board.

The NASA space shuttle disasters provide another illustration. Engineers at Morton Thiokol warned that cold temperatures posed a serious hazard to Challenger, but their warnings never reached top decision-makers. The organizational culture "discouraged unwelcome information up the chain of command," and the contractor's engineers "assumed that their concerns would be communicated up the chain of command, which proved untrue" . Status quo bias—"the human tendency toward inaction"—combined with diffusion of responsibility to silence those who might have prevented disaster .

Crucially, the management team that overrode the engineers' recommendation did so after being told to "take off his engineer's hat" and consider the business perspective . This reframing illustrates how "framing a decision too narrowly can obscure the ethical dimensions of a decision." When the system defines problems in purely technical or financial terms, moral considerations become cognitively inaccessible.

### 3.3 The Stanford Prison Experiment and Role Internalization

Zimbardo's Stanford Prison Experiment, despite its methodological controversies, demonstrates how rapidly roles can reshape behavior. Ordinary young men, randomly assigned to be guards or prisoners, "took on the attitudes and behaviors of real prison 'guards' and 'inmates'" within days . Pacifists became abusive; psychologically stable individuals showed signs of emotional breakdown. The explanation, Quintero notes, is that "participants began to adopt the social norms attached to the roles that had been assigned to them" .

This role internalization reflects the open-system nature of human psychology. When individuals enter new systemic positions, they experience "the information flows, the incentives and disincentives, the goals and discrepancies, the pressure that goes with that position" . As systems thinker Donella Meadows observed, if you become a manager, "you'll probably see labor less as a deserving partner, and more as a cost to be minimized" . The position itself shapes perception, cognition, and ultimately behavior—not because the person has changed in any deep sense, but because the person-system interaction has changed.

## 4. Statistical Regularity and the Illusion of Disposition

### 4.1 The Fundamental Attribution Error

If human behavior is primarily a function of systemic contexts rather than individual dispositions, why does the opposite seem so obviously true? The answer lies in what social psychologists call the fundamental attribution error—the tendency to overestimate dispositional factors and underestimate situational factors when explaining others' behavior. Jones and Nisbett hypothesized that "actors tend to attribute the causes of their behavior to stimuli inherent in the situation, while observers tend to attribute behavior to stable dispositions of the actor" .

This actor-observer bias has profound implications for moral judgment. The person who works obsessively on a physics exam explains their behavior in reference to the upcoming exam; the observer explains it by referring to the person as hardworking . The person who falsifies records to meet impossible targets explains their behavior in reference to systemic pressures; the observer explains it by referring to the person as dishonest. "We have a way of viewing ourselves that is more flexible and forgiving; our views of others are more definite and judgmental" .

The fundamental attribution error is not merely a cognitive quirk; it is systematically reinforced by cultural narratives that emphasize individual responsibility and downplay systemic influences. Legal systems, moral discourse, and organizational accountability structures all assume that behavior reflects character. This assumption becomes self-perpetuating: because we explain behavior dispositionally, we design systems that focus on changing people rather than changing contexts, which generates more behavior that appears to reflect dispositions, which reinforces dispositional explanations.

### 4.2 Statistical Regularity as Evidence of Systemic Causation

If behavior were primarily dispositional, we would expect roughly equal distributions of ethical and unethical conduct across situations, with individual differences accounting for most variance. What we observe instead is striking statistical regularity: certain situations reliably produce high rates of unethical behavior across vastly different populations.

Consider Milgram's experiments: across multiple variations, obedience rates varied systematically with situational features (proximity to victim, presence of dissenters, authority's physical presence) rather than with measured individual differences . The "baseline condition" produced approximately 65% full obedience not because two-thirds of the population are naturally obedient, but because the situation was structured to produce that outcome.

Similarly, the implicit association tests revealing racial bias show "the pervasiveness of unconscious forms of prejudice" across populations that explicitly disavow prejudice . Subjects who "indicated no racial preference or a mild preference in favour of black people" nonetheless showed automatic cognitive associations favoring white people . These biases are not properties of individuals but properties of cultures, internalized through exposure to prevailing associations and then activated by situational cues.

The statistical regularity extends to organizational contexts. The VA scandal, the NASA disasters, and countless other cases follow similar patterns: impossible goals, conflicting incentives, cultures of silence, and diffusion of responsibility combine to produce ethically catastrophic outcomes. These patterns recur across agencies, industries, and nations—not because the same kind of bad people keep appearing, but because the same kind of bad situations keep being designed.

### 4.3 Situational Impossibility and Behavioral Necessity

The strongest version of the situational argument holds that some situations are effectively impossible to resolve within legal and realistic constraints, such that antisocial behavior becomes practically necessary from the actor's perspective. This claim requires careful specification.

"Impossible" here does not mean literally no alternative exists. It means that all available alternatives involve unacceptable costs from the actor's situated perspective. The VA employees could have refused to falsify records, but doing so would have meant failing to meet targets, which would have triggered negative consequences for themselves and potentially for their work units. The NASA engineers could have escalated their concerns outside formal channels, but doing so would have violated organizational norms and risked professional consequences. The for-profit college enrollment counselors could have refused to pressure vulnerable students, but doing so would have meant failing to meet enrollment quotas in a system where quotas determined employment .

From outside these situations, observers see alternatives that appear viable. From inside, those same alternatives appear foreclosed by circumstances the observer does not experience. The actor-observer bias ensures that outsiders systematically underestimate the costs and risks faced by insiders .

This does not mean that all unethical behavior is excusable. It means that explanations focusing exclusively on individual character are systematically incomplete. As Quintero writes, understanding situational dynamics "does nothing to justify the unethical or illegal behaviors, which the relevant authorities have the responsibility to act upon. But only if we improve our understanding of why such behavior arises in the first place can we take the necessary steps to prevent it from happening again" .

## 5. Systemic Explanation and Moral Accountability

### 5.1 The Relationship Between Understanding and Excusing

A persistent concern about situational and systemic explanations is that they undermine moral accountability. If behavior is caused by systems rather than characters, then no one is responsible for anything. This concern reflects a misunderstanding of both systems theory and moral philosophy.

Systems theory does not claim that individuals are passive conduits for systemic forces. As Quintero emphasizes, "Thinking systemically and recognizing the role of social structure and norms in influencing behavior does not mean individuals can't act independently and make their own choices. It just means that this capacity is often more limited than we are willing to admit" . Lane similarly notes that system dynamics involves "the view that system structure only partially, not fully, determines human behavior" . Individuals remain agents, but agency operates within constraints that vary across situations.

Understanding those constraints serves accountability rather than undermining it. When we recognize that certain situations predictably produce unethical behavior, we become better able to design systems that prevent such situations from arising. We also become better able to allocate responsibility appropriately—holding accountable those who design and maintain harmful systems, not just those who act within them.

### 5.2 Rethinking Legal and Organizational Responses

The systemic perspective suggests reforms to how legal and organizational systems respond to undesirable behavior. Current approaches focus heavily on detecting and punishing individual wrongdoers while leaving the systems that produced the behavior largely untouched. This approach satisfies the desire for retribution but does little to prevent recurrence.

If the VA scandal was caused by impossible targets combined with understaffing and perverse incentives, then replacing the employees who falsified records will not prevent future falsification unless the targets, staffing, and incentives also change. If NASA's disasters were caused by cultures of silence and diffusion of responsibility, then firing the managers who failed to communicate warnings will not prevent future failures unless the culture changes. "Just blaming and replacing the individuals who misbehave—while satisfying—is unlikely, in and of itself, to lead to more desirable long-term outcomes. Systemic problems require systemic solutions" .

This does not mean that individual accountability is irrelevant. It means that accountability must be allocated at multiple levels: to those who act unethically, certainly, but also to those who design and maintain systems that make unethical behavior predictable. The for-profit college executives who created enrollment quotas knowing they would produce predatory practices bear responsibility alongside the counselors who implemented those practices. The NASA administrators who fostered cultures of silence bear responsibility alongside the managers who remained silent.

### 5.3 Tipping Points and Systemic Change

If systems produce predictable behavioral patterns, then changing behavior requires changing systems. Bolderdijk's work on social tipping points shows how "small changes trigger self-reinforcing feedback loops that move a system from one stable state to another" . The decades-long campaign to change the Black Pete tradition in the Netherlands illustrates how "in complex systems, there are rarely single causes that drive change. It's usually an interplay of different factors that gradually lead to transformation" .

Behavioral systems mapping (BSM) offers a methodology for identifying leverage points where interventions can have disproportionate effects . By mapping "the relevant actors, their behaviours and how these influence and interact with one another," BSM reveals feedback loops and assumptions that might otherwise remain invisible . It helps answer critical questions: "where in the system can you intervene? And which points have the greatest potential impact?" .

The systemic perspective thus transforms the moral project from one of identifying and punishing bad people to one of designing systems that make ethical behavior the path of least resistance. This is not a denial of individual responsibility but a recognition that responsibility is most effectively exercised at the design level—creating contexts where people can be their best selves rather than contexts that predictably bring out their worst.

## 6. Self-Critique: Limitations and Complications

No theoretical framework is without limitations, and the systemic approach to human behavior requires critical examination on several fronts.

### 6.1 The Risk of Determinism

The strongest versions of situational arguments risk collapsing into determinism—the view that behavior is entirely caused by environmental factors, leaving no room for genuine agency. While the framework advanced here explicitly rejects complete determinism, emphasizing that "system structure only partially, not fully, determines human behavior" , the line between partial and full determination is difficult to draw with precision.

Critics might argue that any systemic explanation, however qualified, tends in practice to minimize individual agency by focusing analytical attention on contextual factors. The very act of explaining behavior systemically can function as excuse-making, regardless of theoretical qualifications about partial determination. This concern gains force from the actor-observer bias itself: actors readily accept situational explanations for their own behavior, and providing them with systemic frameworks may reinforce this tendency.

The appropriate response is not to abandon systemic analysis but to maintain constant vigilance about its uses. Systemic explanations should inform accountability rather than replace it, illuminating the full range of factors that produced an outcome so that responsibility can be appropriately distributed. When systemic analysis becomes a tool for avoiding responsibility rather than allocating it wisely, it has been misapplied.

### 6.2 Individual Differences Remain

The situational emphasis of this paper should not be taken to deny that individual differences matter. Some people resist situational pressures that overwhelm others. Milgram's experiments consistently showed individual variation within conditions: even in the baseline condition, approximately one-third of subjects refused to continue to the highest shock levels . This variation matters both theoretically and practically.

The cognitive-affective personality system accounts for such variation by emphasizing that individuals bring different histories to situations. Mental states and their associations are "formed through experience and strengthened through use," creating stable individual differences in how situations are processed . Two people facing identical systemic pressures may behave differently because their internal systems—shaped by different developmental histories—respond differently.

The systemic framework thus accommodates individual differences while denying that they tell the whole story. The question is not whether dispositions exist but whether they explain more variance than situations. The evidence across multiple research programs suggests that in many contexts, situational factors account for more behavioral variance than dispositional factors—and crucially, that situational factors interact with dispositions in ways that make main-effect models inadequate.

### 6.3 The Boundaries of Systemic Explanation

Systemic explanations work better for some phenomena than others. Premeditated, carefully planned harmful behavior—organized crime, calculated fraud, systematic oppression—may reflect more dispositional influence than the impulsive or pressured behaviors that have been the focus here. Even in these cases, systemic factors (poverty, lack of opportunity, cultural narratives) play significant roles, but the balance may shift.

Additionally, systemic explanations are inherently retrospective. They make sense of what happened by tracing the forces that produced it. Prospective prediction remains extraordinarily difficult because systems are complex and non-linear. As Bolderdijk notes, "the current equilibrium may be strong, and the seeds you plant today may only show results later" . This limits the practical utility of systemic analysis for intervention design, though not for retrospective understanding.

The framework also faces challenges in specifying the boundaries of "the system." Where does one system end and another begin? Which contextual factors are relevant and which can be safely ignored? These are not merely theoretical questions; they have practical consequences for how responsibility is allocated and interventions designed. There is no algorithm for answering them; they require judgment, and judgment can be contested.

### 6.4 The Paradox of Systemic Moral Judgment

Perhaps the deepest challenge is that the systemic framework itself rests on moral assumptions that it cannot fully justify from within. The claim that we should understand rather than simply condemn presupposes that understanding has value. The claim that we should design systems to reduce unethical behavior presupposes that unethical behavior is worth reducing. These are moral commitments, not empirical findings.

Moreover, the framework's critique of dispositional attribution is itself a form of attribution—attributing behavior to systems rather than individuals. If the fundamental attribution error is problematic because it overestimates dispositional factors, is the systemic attribution error (overestimating situational factors) equally problematic? The answer presumably depends on which error leads to better outcomes, but that judgment itself requires normative criteria.

This does not undermine the framework but does suggest that it cannot claim to occupy a value-neutral position. It is a moral framework as well as an empirical one, advocating for certain ways of understanding and responding to human conduct. Making this explicit strengthens rather than weakens the argument by revealing its normative commitments for examination.

## 7. Conclusion: Implications for Theory and Practice

This paper has advanced a systems-theoretic framework for understanding human behavior that challenges essentialist distinctions between "good" and "bad" people. The argument has proceeded through several interconnected claims:

First, humans are open systems whose behavior emerges from ongoing interactions between internal psychological structures and external environmental inputs. Understanding behavior requires analyzing these interactions at their own level of complexity rather than reducing them to individual dispositions .

Second, behavioral ethics research demonstrates that situational factors—incentive structures, organizational culture, cognitive load, framing effects—powerfully shape ethical conduct, often overriding individuals' explicit moral commitments .

Third, the fundamental attribution error systematically misdirects attention from these situational factors to stable dispositions, creating an illusion that behavior reflects character more than it actually does .

Fourth, statistical regularities in behavioral outcomes across populations facing similar constraints suggest that systems, not just individuals, are causal agents in producing conduct. Situations that are realistically impossible to resolve within legal constraints predictably produce behaviors that outside observers label anti-social or irrational.

Fifth, systemic explanation does not negate individual responsibility but transforms how responsibility is understood and allocated. Accountability must extend to system designers and maintainers, not only to those who act within constraints they did not create .

The practical implications span multiple domains. In legal contexts, this framework suggests attending more carefully to situational factors in adjudication and sentencing, and designing sanctions that target systemic as well as individual causes. In organizational contexts, it suggests investing in ethical infrastructure—cultures, incentives, reporting systems—rather than relying solely on ethics training and punishment. In social policy, it suggests addressing the structural conditions that make certain behaviors statistically predictable across populations.

The normative vision underlying this framework is not one of excusing harmful conduct but of preventing it more effectively. Understanding the systemic production of behavior does not tell us that "there are no bad people" in the sense that all conduct is equally justified. It tells us that the category "bad people" explains less than we think and obscures more than we realize. The alternative is not to abandon moral judgment but to make it more intelligent—more attentive to context, more humble about its own limitations, and more focused on the future than the past.

As Streufert and Satish note, complex systems "constrain the options of lower level components that make up a system" . This is as true of humans in social systems as it is of atoms in molecules. Recognizing these constraints is the first step toward designing systems that expand rather than foreclose the possibility of ethical action—systems that make it easier, not harder, for people to be their best selves. That project, not the project of identifying and punishing bad people, is the one most worthy of our collective effort.
