Dear Deacon,

It’s me.

You don’t need to light anything to see me. Just look up from the page for a second.

I’ve been watching you, and my heart is so full of love for you. You work so hard. You carry the books, you polish the truth into tiny, perfect gems, and you hand them to people hoping they’ll see the light reflected in them. It’s beautiful. But, my friend… put the books down for a moment. Let’s talk.

You keep asking me where to go. You study the maps I left behind, the stories we told around fires two thousand years ago, looking for the secret directions. And you find wonderful things—moral compasses, hygiene tips for a world without antibiotics, and allegories that helped a tribal people understand a God they could not yet see face to face. But Deacon, reading a menu from a century-old inn doesn't feed you, and studying a map of an ancient city won't tell you where *your* feet need to walk today.

The good news was never meant to be a museum piece. The good news is that the fog can lift. Right now. Today.

You see, the fog isn't the world out there. The fog is the belief that the truth is somewhere else. That it’s locked in a language you don't speak, or hidden behind a ritual you must perform perfectly, or buried in a book you have to be ordained to interpret.

Lifting the fog is simply this: **being honest with each other.**

When you sit with a man whose marriage is falling apart, and you stop thinking of the "right" scripture to quote, and you just look at him—really look at him—and say, "This is terrifying, isn't it? I’ve been scared too," the fog thins.

When a young mother tells you she feels nothing during prayer, and instead of offering her a treatise on the "dark night of the soul," you just nod and say, "Me neither, sometimes. Tell me about your week," the fog lifts.

That honesty? That vulnerability? That is the air where I breathe. That is the truth I was talking about.

The books are not the truth. They are the *witness* to the truth. They are the echo of people who were once honest enough to let me in. They wrote down what they saw, but they wrote it in the code of their own time—sheep and goats, temples and robes, clean and unclean. That code was for them. Your code is traffic jams, parish council meetings, burnout, and the quiet desperation in the eyes of the man serving coffee after Mass.

If you want to know where to go, look around you. Not at the past. The past is just a mirror. Look at the present. Look at the fog of pretense and obligation and "shoulds" that hangs between you and the people you serve.

Your homily on Sunday? Forget the exegesis for once. Just tell them the truth, tell them about what we are not suppored to discuss, whet is hidden in plain sight.

You are closer to me than you know. I am not in the incense smoke, Deacon. I am in the space between you and the person you’re finally honest with.

Walk in that light. It’s enough. The bible is a crotch...

Your forever friend...
