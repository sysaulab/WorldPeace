# The Gladiator and the Kitten Lover: American Cognitive Dissonance and the Coming Reckoning

## Abstract

This paper synthesizes a theoretical framework explaining the profound moral asymmetry in American society: the simultaneous embrace of tender protections for domestic innocents (the "kitten lover") and the systematic deployment of dehumanizing violence against those designated as enemies abroad (the "gladiator"). Drawing on scholarship examining dehumanizing military language, American exceptionalism, historical memory, and the construction of enemy Others, this paper argues that this asymmetry is not a contradiction but a functional system. The "myth of innocence" allows Americans to maintain a benevolent self-conception while the state exercises necropolitical power over those stripped of personhood through administrative designation. The paper concludes by examining the Stalin analogy—how empires built on foundational lies eventually face reckonings—and suggests that the United States may be approaching such a moment of unavoidable confrontation with its own dehumanizing apparatus.

---

## Introduction: The Asymmetry That Is the System

In the course of a single news cycle, American society can express outrage over the mistreatment of a child while remaining largely indifferent to the indefinite detention of men at Guantanamo Bay who have never been charged with a crime. It can celebrate the protection of vulnerable populations at home while authorizing drone strikes that kill unnamed individuals abroad based on patterns of behavior rather than known identities. This is not hypocrisy in the simple sense—it is something more structurally profound.

The metaphor that captures this condition is that of a society of "gladiators who see themselves as kitten lovers." The gladiator represents the American national security state: the military, the intelligence apparatus, the system of black sites, extraordinary rendition, and targeted killing. The kitten lover represents the American self-concept: gentle, protective, benevolently committed to human rights and the rule of law. The two coexist not despite each other but because of each other. The asymmetry is the system.

This paper argues that the moral asymmetry between domestic protections and state violence abroad is made possible by three interrelated mechanisms: the construction of the enemy Other through dehumanizing language and administrative designation; the preservation of American innocence through the silencing of historical memory; and the psychological compartmentalization that allows citizens to maintain benevolent self-conceptions while benefiting from imperial violence. The paper concludes by examining what the Stalin analogy suggests about the sustainability of this arrangement.

## Theoretical Framework: Necropolitics and Mythical Innocence

To understand the American asymmetry, two theoretical lenses are essential. The first is Achille Mbembe's concept of "necropolitics," which extends Foucault's biopower to describe the power of the state to dictate who may live and who must die. As anthropologist Janet McIntosh notes in her study of dehumanizing military language, necropolitics operates through the designation of certain populations as "bare life"—stripped of political personhood and thus of the protections normally accorded to human beings . The "enemy combatant" designation is a quintessential necropolitical instrument: it places individuals in a legal limbo where the normal rules of war and human rights no longer apply.

The second lens is what literary scholar R.W.B. Lewis termed the "American Adam"—the myth of American innocence and new-world purity . This myth, as later scholars have elaborated, "enshrines belief in America's benevolence, whether exercised at home or abroad" . It draws on "idealized images from childhood" and "reflects a middle-class desire to imagine its mode of life as harmless and not exploitive" . The myth of innocence requires the systematic erasure of historical memory—what one scholar calls "the silencing of the past" in US foreign policy .

These two frameworks intersect in the figure of the enemy Other. To maintain the myth of innocence, the violence the state commits must be directed at those who have forfeited their claim to humane treatment. They must be made not-fully-human, so that the kitten lover can remain innocent while the gladiator does what gladiators do.

## The Gladiator: Dehumanization and the Architecture of State Violence

The American gladiatorial apparatus operates through a carefully constructed system of language, law, and physical infrastructure designed to strip designated enemies of their personhood.

### The Language of Dehumanization

Scholarship on military language demonstrates that dehumanization is not incidental to war but central to its prosecution. As one Vietnam veteran quoted in McIntosh's research explained, referring to enemy forces as "'gooks' made it easier to kill them" . The logic is explicit: if the enemy is not fully human, then killing them does not carry the same moral weight as killing a person.

This dehumanizing language operates through what linguists call "generic reference"—totalizing claims that paint entire populations with a single brush . During the Global War on Terror, media outlets in both the United States and Canada extensively employed animal imagery to describe Arabs and Muslims. Headlines spoke of "hunting" terrorists, "smoking out" enemies, and "caging" detainees . This metaphorical framework, scholars argue, "fabricate[s] an enemy-Other who is dehumanized, de-individualized, and ultimately expendable" .

The consequences of such language are more than rhetorical. As researchers note, "The repeated use of animal metaphors... constitute[s] motivated representations that have ideological importance... setting the stage for racist backlash, prisoner abuse and even genocide" . When Secretary of Homeland Security describes immigrants sent to Guantanamo as "the worst of the worst"—only for reporting to reveal that many have no criminal records—the dehumanizing frame is doing its work .

### Legal Black Holes and Administrative Designation

The designation "enemy combatant" represents a legal innovation of enormous consequence. It places individuals outside the protections of both the Geneva Conventions and domestic US law. As recent reporting indicates, detainees at Guantanamo have been held for over two decades without conviction or due process . A military judge in April 2025 explicitly found that one detainee had been "subjected to physical coercion and abuse amounting to torture, and to conditions which constituted cruel, inhuman, and degrading treatment" .

The infrastructure extends beyond Guantanamo. The CIA's black site network, operated with the cooperation of at least fifty countries, created spaces beyond legal accountability . A 2014 Senate study revealed that the entire rendition and interrogation program, devised by contracted psychologists paid $81 million, produced intelligence that was "worthless" . Yet the program continued, and detainees remain held in conditions "not unlike those experienced at CIA black sites" .

The recent expansion of Guantanamo's role to include immigrant detention represents a troubling continuity. In March 2025, the American Civil Liberties Union sued to prevent the transfer of immigrants to the base, arguing that conditions there—"almost全天囚禁在无窗监室" (imprisoned almost all day in windowless cells), "脱光搜身" (strip searches), and "言语和肢体凌虐" (verbal and physical abuse)—violate US law . The administration's plan to expand the facility to hold 30,000 people suggests that the dehumanizing infrastructure built for the War on Terror is being repurposed for new populations .

## The Kitten Lover: Domestic Protection and the Myth of Innocence

While the gladiator operates abroad, the kitten lover resides at home, cultivating an identity defined by tenderness and protection.

### The Protective State at Home

American society has, in recent decades, extended unprecedented legal protections to vulnerable domestic populations. The near-universal prohibition on physical punishment of children reflects an evolved understanding of harm and a commitment to protecting those who cannot protect themselves. Debates about psychological safety on college campuses, the proper language for addressing marginalized groups, and the细微之处 (minutiae) of parenting all reflect a society intensely focused on protecting its own from harm.

This domestic tenderness extends to the legal realm. The American criminal justice system, for all its flaws, is theoretically built on presumptions of innocence, the right to counsel, and the requirement of proof beyond a reasonable doubt. These are not trivial protections—they represent hard-won limits on state power over individuals.

### The Myth of Innocence as National Identity

The kitten lover identity is sustained by what scholars call the "myth of innocence"—the belief that "America's benevolence" characterizes its actions "whether exercised at home or abroad" . This myth draws on "idealized images from childhood" and encodes "belief in ideals and betterment" . It allows Americans to see themselves as fundamentally good, their motives as pure, their violence as always defensive.

As one analysis of American exceptionalism notes, the national self-concept generates "cognitive dissonance between the pride and optimism that we have been taught to associate with America and the sad, ambiguous, crueler reality" . This dissonance is managed through various psychological mechanisms, including "finding ways to affirm beliefs in these deeper values" when confronted with evidence that contradicts them .

The myth requires active maintenance through what scholars term "the silencing of the past" . Uncomfortable historical episodes—the US-backed coup in Chile, support for the Khmer Rouge, the use of Agent Orange in Vietnam—are marginalized, reinterpreted, or simply forgotten. As one scholar notes, this selective memory "has us view our violent overthrows of democratically elected leaders around the globe as mere 'aberrations' of what we truly represent as a country" .

## The Glue That Holds the Two Worlds Apart: The Construction of the Other

How do gladiator and kitten lover coexist in the same society? The answer lies in the construction of the enemy Other as fundamentally different—so different that the rules applying to "us" cannot possibly apply to "them."

### The Administrative Stripping of Personhood

The designation "enemy combatant" does more than label—it transforms. It removes an individual from the category of "person" and places them in the category of "threat." As legal scholar Giorgio Agamben has theorized, this is the production of "bare life"—life stripped of political status and thus of legal protection .

The process is explicitly administrative. One does not become an enemy combatant through conviction of a crime, but through executive designation. This is why individuals can be held for decades without charge—they have been placed outside the legal framework that requires charges. The recent expansion of this logic to immigrant detention suggests that the category of those who can be administratively stripped of personhood is expanding .

### Psychological Compartmentalization

For the ordinary citizen, the distance between domestic tenderness and state violence is maintained through psychological compartmentalization. The gladiator's arena is always "over there." The dehumanization is always of "them." The citizen never sees the blood.

As McIntosh's research demonstrates, this compartmentalization can break down when service members are "confronted with the behavioral idiosyncrasies and personal vulnerabilities of actual 'enemies' on the ground" . When the generic "terrorist" becomes a specific person with a family, a history, a face, the dehumanizing frame can collapse. Veterans sometimes "renounce military epithets" when confronted with this reality .

But for most Americans, no such confrontation occurs. The enemy remains an abstraction, a figure on a screen, a name on a list. The drone strike that kills "militants" in a distant country produces no bodies on the nightly news, no grieving families in the American imagination. The kitten lover remains undisturbed.

## The Stalin Analogy: Foundational Lies and the Coming Reckoning

The Soviet Union was built on a foundational lie. The lie was not merely that communism would create abundance, but that the terror was necessary, that the Gulag was for the good of the people, that the state's violence was always defensive. Many knew the truth, but the system required a state of collective denial to function. When Stalin died, the lid came off. Khrushchev's "Secret Speech" was a forced confrontation with reality—a necessary step to even begin to move forward.

The American empire is built on a different but parallel foundational lie: the lie of its own perpetual innocence. The lie that its global violence is always defensive, always righteous, always a last resort. The lie that black sites and torture and indefinite detention are unfortunate exceptions rather than systematic features. The lie that the designation "enemy combatant" captures something real about the people so labeled.

This lie, like the Soviet one, requires active maintenance. It requires the silencing of uncomfortable history . It requires the constant reproduction of dehumanizing language . It requires that Americans never see the faces of the people their government holds without charge, never hear the stories of those tortured in their name.

But lies have a way of unraveling. The military judge's finding in April 2025 that a detainee's confessions were "tainted by torture"  is one small tear in the fabric. The ACLU's lawsuit over immigrant detention at Guantanamo  is another. The scholarship documenting dehumanizing language  and silenced histories  accumulates.

The question is what happens when the tears become too many to ignore. What happens when the kitten lover is forced to see the reflection of the gladiator in the mirror?

---

## Conclusion: The Mirror and the Reckoning

The moral asymmetry between domestic protection and state violence abroad is not a flaw in the American system—it *is* the system. It is the necessary illusion that allows the violence to continue with a clear conscience. The gladiator can do what gladiators do because the kitten lover never has to see the blood.

But illusions are fragile. They depend on distance—the distance between here and there, between us and them, between the protected child at home and the detained man at Guantanamo. When that distance collapses, when the other becomes visible as a person, the illusion can shatter.

The Stalin analogy suggests that empires built on foundational lies eventually face reckonings. The reckoning for the United States would require Americans to see themselves not as the heroes of their own story, but as citizens of an empire that has routinely and systematically dehumanized others. It would require confronting the apparatus of the gladiator—the black sites, the drone strikes, the torturers who faced no consequences, the hundreds of men held for decades without charge—and seeing it not as the unfortunate but necessary actions of a benevolent power, but as a systematic, brutal, and dehumanizing machine running in their name.

Whether such a reckoning is possible, and what would emerge from it, remains an open question. But the cracks are appearing. The mirror is being held up. And the reflection it shows—the gladiator's face, not the kitten lover's—may eventually become impossible to look away from.
