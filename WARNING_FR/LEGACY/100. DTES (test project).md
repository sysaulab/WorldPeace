# The Vision

I came to the Downtown Eastside because I was looking for a place where I could breathe.

For most of my life, I felt like a stranger in my own land. Not because of the color of my skin—there are people of every color in the colonial machine. But because of what I believed. Because of what I valued. Because of the constant, grinding pressure to abandon the teachings of my ancestors and become something else: a consumer, a competitor, a solitary unit in a machine designed to extract and discard.

I believed in solidarity. I believed that we are not alone, that we are meant to share, that the health of the one is tied to the health of the whole. And everywhere I went, I found that these beliefs were treated as quaint at best, dangerous at worst. Smiling faces, polite conversations, and beneath it all, a steady pressure to conform.

Then I found this place. The Downtown Eastside.

It is not what the newspapers say. It is not just a problem to be solved. It is a gathering place for people who never bought the lie. For people who, despite everything, still share their last cigarette. Still watch out for each other in the alley. Still give when they have nothing.

I remember camping with my circle, outside the city, away from the noise. We shared everything. Food, blankets, stories. We took care of each other's children. We made decisions together. And in that circle, for the first time, I felt what I can only call **ideological comfort**. A bubble where my deepest beliefs—that we are responsible for each other, that wealth is meant to circulate, that the land is not a resource but a relative—were not strange. They were normal. They were the air we breathed.

Coming back to the city was like surfacing from warm water into cold wind.

## The Uniformizing Machine

Let me name something that we do not talk about enough.

There is a machine in this society. It is not visible in the way a factory is visible. But it is everywhere. It is a machine that grinds down anything that does not fit its shape.

We have words for discrimination based on skin, based on religion, based on gender. And those are real. But there is another discrimination, deeper and less named. It is **ideological discrimination**.

The machine only allows one ideology to exist: the ideology of the market. Of competition. Of individual accumulation. Of extraction without end. It presents itself as neutral, as common sense, as simply "how the world works." But it is not neutral. It is a fundamentalism as rigid as any that has ever existed.

The bankers who deny our loans, the government representatives who design our policies, the corporate executives who extract from our territories—they are not just rich. They are **neoliberal fundamentalists**. They have drunk the Kool-Aid so deeply they cannot imagine any other way. They believe, with the fervor of true believers, that their ideology is not an ideology at all, but simply reality.

And because they control the institutions, there is no one at the table who represents us. No one who represents the people who believe that solidarity is a better path. No one who speaks for the ones who think the purpose of an economy is to take care of everyone, not to make a few people rich.

The machine allows diversity of skin, of food, of music. It loves multicultural festivals. It celebrates cultural symbols worn as accessories. But underneath, it demands uniformity of behavior. You can wear your Indigenous necklace, your African print, your turban, your hijab—as long as you behave like a domesticated consumer. As long as you participate in the machine.

To us, to those of us who see the society for what it is, the people who have fully internalized this ideology look like something strange. They look like automata. They move through their days, chasing wages, accumulating things, competing with neighbors, and they seem genuinely confused about why they are so exhausted, so lonely, so empty.

We see them. And we see what the machine has done to them. And we know there is another way.

## The Need for Space

Every major spiritual tradition understands this. The Catholics have monasteries. The Buddhists have nunneries. The monastics withdraw from the dominant society to live according to their deepest beliefs. They create spaces where a different set of values can be practiced, protected, and passed on.

We need the same.

We need spaces where the ideology of solidarity is not a fringe belief, but the operating system. Where the Potlatch is not a museum exhibit, but the way we handle money. Where giving away is the path to status, not hoarding.

The Downtown Eastside can be such a space. It already is, in fragments. The solidarity economy is already here, in the sharing, the watching out, the mutual aid. But it is fragile. It is under constant assault from the machine. The police, the developers, the bureaucrats, the fundamentalists who cannot understand why we won't just "integrate" and become good consumers like everyone else.

We need to build walls around this space. Not physical walls—we are not retreating from the world. But institutional walls. Economic walls. Legal walls. Walls that protect our ability to live according to our traditions, not just wear our symbols while behaving like everyone else.

## The Indigenous Peoples Bank

This is why we propose the **Indigenous Peoples Bank**.

It is capitalized by the pooled savings of all participating Indigenous communities. Every nation that chooses to join contributes its collective resources. Every nation can apply for economic development funding.

The bank has one purpose only: **to create positive economic engines**.

A community wants to build housing? The bank provides the capital. A nation wants to launch a sustainable fishery, a renewable energy cooperative, a language revitalization center? The bank provides the loans. The bank does not speculate. It does not extract. It builds.

The profits from these enterprises flow back to the bank. And those profits are what sustain the communities. Not government transfers. Not resource extraction deals negotiated by people who do not share our values. The productive capacity of the people themselves, funded by their own pooled resources, generating wealth that circulates within the circle.

This creates logical, healthy economic cells. Each community has its own tools for self-development. Each nation governs its own affairs according to its own traditions. And yet, each remains linked—through the bank, through shared principles, through continent-scale coordination with central governments on macroeconomics, infrastructure, and ecological planning.

We are not withdrawing. We are building a base from which we can engage the world on our own terms.

## The Price of Entry: Total Surrender

Now, here is the offer to anyone who hears their own story in what I have said. Whether you are Indigenous or not. Whether you were born here or came last week. If you believe that solidarity is a better path, that sharing is more natural than hoarding, that the land is a relative and not a resource—you are welcome.

The price of entry is everything you have.

Your assets, if you have any. Your future income. Your debts. You bring us the whole mess. The tribe takes it all.

In return, we give you something no government has ever been able to offer: a clean slate and a permanent place.

You become a member. You agree to live by the laws of the Potlatch, which are the laws of circulation and reciprocity. And we, the tribe, agree to take care of you.

We will regularize your finances. We will sit with you, one human being to another, and figure out what you're eligible for—the disability benefit you never claimed, the tax credit you didn't know existed, the forgiveness program you couldn't navigate alone. We will negotiate with the government on your behalf, not as an individual begging, but as a member of a collective.

## The Bulk Mechanism

This is where we speak to the governments, the bureaucrats, the people who are currently overwhelmed by the complexity they've created.

Instead of dealing with ten thousand individual cases from the Downtown Eastside, you deal with one entity: the tribe.

We do the audits internally. We employ human beings—trained, accountable, connected to the community—to sit with each member and understand their situation. We verify eligibility through relationship. We bundle the claims into a single, verified package.

You reduce your overhead. You don't need the army of bureaucrats. You don't need the risky algorithms that will inevitably harm the most vulnerable. You get the political benefit of supporting an Indigenous-led solution to the most intractable social problems in the country.

And we get what we are owed, efficiently and respectfully, without having to fight the machine one exhausting case at a time.

This is not a withdrawal from the state. It is a renegotiation of the interface. The tribe becomes a buffer, a translator, a collective bargaining agent between the individual and the systems that were never designed to help them.

## The Three-Thirds Model

Each year, after debt service, after building reserves, after ensuring our consumption stays within the limits the land can sustain—no more than the equivalent of 2,000 watts per person, a physical cap that respects the ecosystem—the distributable surplus is divided into three equal parts.

**First Third: The Universal Dividend.**
Every member receives this, unconditionally. You are here. You belong. You receive. This is the floor of dignity.

**Second Third: The Labor Dividend.**
Distributed equally among all hours worked in traditional jobs with responsibilities. A carpenter and a community babysitter are valued by the hour, not by the market's distorted wages. Contribution is honored without creating vast inequality.

**Third Third: The Gift-To-Spend Fund.**
This money arrives in your account, but you cannot spend it. The only way to unlock it is to give it away to another member of the community. Once given, it becomes spendable for the recipient.

This is the Potlatch mechanism made digital. To have spending power, you must be generous. To be wealthy, you must be seen as a source of gifts. Status flows from what you circulate, not from what you hoard.

## Making the Circle Visible

Finally, we make all of this visible through a simple tool on your phone.

An app shows you your three wallets. It shows your **Community Multiplier**—how much of your spending stays local, circulating through the community rather than draining away. It shows you who around you might need a gift, or whose work you admire.

When you spend at the local café, the app shows you: that dollar is still here. It will become the baker's wage, the babysitter's fee, the new bench in the park. When you spend at the big box store, the app shows you: that dollar is gone. It left and will never return.

This creates a natural incentive. You don't need laws. You don't need guilt. You just need to see. And when you see, you choose the circle.

## The Long Transition

Now, here is the part that matters for the future of the whole society.

This model scales. Not through conquest, not through crisis, but through demonstration.

Start here, in the DTES. Build one community that works this way. Prove that people can be healthier, happier, more secure. Prove that the ecological footprint shrinks when the economy is based on circulation rather than extraction. Prove that the government saves money when it deals with a collective.

And then, let the model spread.

A neighborhood in Vancouver. A town in the Interior. A fishing community on the coast. A group of farmers in the Fraser Valley. Each one adapting the principles to its own context. Each one joining the network.

This is how we avoid the violent transitions that history warns us about. The pattern is old: create a crisis, soften the social fabric through shock, and then impose a new order wall to wall. That path leads to war, to algorithm, to control without consent.

We offer a different path. Gradual. Visible. Consensual. It does not require anyone to take up arms. It requires only that we build something so functional, so desirable, that when the storms come—and they will come—people will look at what we've built and choose to join.

The old system will not fall because we overthrew it. It will dissolve for lack of participants.

## The Global Horizon

And then, perhaps, we look further.

The Indigenous Peoples Bank, once established, need not stop at the borders of this land. It could spread. It could offer its model to other communities, other nations, other regions struggling with the same questions.

Imagine a world where the bank helps bridge the gaps between the rising powers of the BRICS nations and our friends to the south, after they are done fighting their bloody cultural disputes. Imagine a financial institution whose currency is trust, whose collateral is relationship, whose profit is measured in human well-being.

The twentieth century was defined by competing visions of global order: Pax Britannica, Pax Americana, and others that remain more rumored than real. Each required the domination of one culture over all others. Each required constant conflict at the edges.

We are proposing something different. Not a new empire. Not a new center. But a network of cells, each rooted in its place, each connected through principles of reciprocity and circulation. Global coherence without global domination. Coordination without conquest.

This is not a fantasy. It is the logical extension of the Potlatch. It is what happens when you stop trying to build one big machine and start tending a garden of many flourishing things.

## The Restoration

We are not inventing something new. We are restoring something very old.

The Potlatch is our oldest tradition. It is older than the machine, older than the colonies, older than the fundamentalism of the market. It is the way our ancestors kept balance for millennia. It is the way wealth becomes relationship, and relationship becomes wealth.

We believe we can make the world better by restoring this tradition. Not by forcing it on anyone, but by living it so well that others want to join.

The price of entry is everything you have. Your assets. Your debts. Your future. Your loyalty.

What you get in return is a place. A people. A circle that will hold you, audit for you, gift to you, and expect you to do the same for others.

We are not asking you to believe in a theory. We are asking you to believe in the person next to you. And to prove it by surrendering the one thing the old system taught you to guard above all else: your isolated, anxious, insufficient self.

Bring us everything. We will give you back what matters.

Thank you.
