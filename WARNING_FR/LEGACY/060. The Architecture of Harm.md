### The Architecture of Harm: How We Learn to Look Away

We like to think that evil has a face. We give it a uniform, a symbol, a name—the swastika. It becomes a kind of warning system: when the symbol is present, we know atrocity is happening; when it's absent, we assume we are safe. This allows us to condemn the horrors of the past while remaining blind to the ones unfolding in the present.

But what if the methods used to enable suffering are more like a technology than a symbol? What if the same basic operating system—the same architecture—can be installed in any society, justified by any ideology? This is the question at the heart of a comparative analysis of three very different conflicts: the Holocaust, Israel's military campaign in Gaza (specifically 2023-2024), and the United States' global War on Terror.

The goal is not to argue that these events are the same. The scale of the Holocaust was unique in its industrial ambition to exterminate an entire people. The contexts, the legal frameworks, and the specific justifications are all vastly different. But if we look beneath the surface, we find four structural mechanisms that operate in almost exactly the same way across all three cases. They form an invisible architecture of exclusion that makes mass civilian suffering possible.

#### Mechanism One: The Creation of the "Other"

Before a person can be killed without public outcry, they must first be removed from the category of "people like us." They must be stripped of their humanity. This is not a philosophical accident; it is a deliberate process of **semantic recategorization**. Scholars studying the Gaza conflict have documented how analytical categories like "genocide," "self-defense," and "terrorism" have become "loyalty tests" that determine what language can legitimately be used to describe suffering.

In Nazi Germany, this was done with the language of disease and infestation. Jews were depicted in films and posters not as human beings, but as rats, vermin, and parasites. The 1935 Nuremberg Laws stripped them of citizenship, legally cementing their status as non-persons. Once a group is seen as a threat to public hygiene, their elimination is no longer murder; it is a sanitary measure.

In the American War on Terror, a new vocabulary was invented to achieve the same result. Civilians killed by drone strikes were not "murdered"; they were "collateral damage"—a term that transforms dead people into an accounting error. Detainees were not "prisoners of war"; they were "enemy combatants," a legal category created from scratch to strip them of the protections of the Geneva Convention. Young men in a strike zone became "military-age males," their individuality erased by a statistic that made them killable.

In the discourse surrounding Gaza, we see a parallel operation documented extensively by researchers. Israeli Defense Minister Yoav Gallant announced a complete siege on Gaza in October 2023 by declaring Israel was fighting "human animals." This followed a long historical pattern—similar to how colonial powers described indigenous populations as "fauna" in Australia or "subhuman" in the Congo, and how Tutsis were called "cockroaches" before the Rwandan Genocide. A population of over two million—half of them children—is rarely described simply as "civilians." Instead, they are "human shields" exploited by Hamas. The term "terrorist infrastructure" has been stretched so far that it can be used to justify strikes on hospitals, schools, and residential buildings. When a military spokesperson suggests "there are no innocent civilians in Gaza," they are performing the same function as a Nazi propagandist: they are emptying the category of "civilian" of its meaning, making it possible to discuss mass death without acknowledging the humanity of the dead.

#### Mechanism Two: Reframing Violence as Virtue

Once the target population has been dehumanized, the violence against them must be justified. This is the role of **legitimating discourses**, which perform a neat trick of inversion: the perpetrator becomes the defender, the victim becomes the threat. Critical discourse analysis of political speeches during the Gaza War reveals how leaders deploy linguistic strategies including "authority, numerical framing, disclaimers, national glorification, hyperbole, and lexical choices that portray the in-group as moral saviors while silencing the suffering of the out-group."

For the Nazis, this was framed as "racial hygiene" and national survival. The elimination of Jews was not an act of aggression, but a necessary defense of the German *volk* against a corrupting influence. It was framed as a difficult but necessary surgery to save the patient.

For the United States, the War on Terror was framed as a mission to spread freedom and democracy. The invasions of Afghanistan and Iraq were not conquests, but liberations. The torture and indefinite detention were not crimes, but necessary tools to protect the homeland from another 9/11.

In Israel, the framing is one of security and self-defense. Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu explicitly invoked the biblical story of Amalek—a passage that commands followers to "slay both man and woman, infant and suckling"—while announcing the full-scale invasion of Gaza. Other Israeli leaders have made statements that international bodies have condemned as incitement to genocide: Heritage Minister Amihai Eliyahu suggested a nuclear bomb was "one of the possibilities"; a rabbi declared that killing children was justified by Jewish law because "today he is a baby; tomorrow he is a fighter"; Finance Minister Bezalel Smotrich suggested starving Gazans could be "justified and moral." In each case, the discourse does the same work. It allows the soldiers and pilots to see themselves as protectors. It allows the public to support the war as a righteous cause. It turns the victims into the authors of their own destruction.

#### Mechanism Three: The Machinery of Looking Away

For these justifications to hold, institutions—universities, media outlets, professional organizations—must fall in line. They must learn to filter information through the official frame and to sanction those who refuse. This is the mechanism of **institutional conformity**. Philosopher Jason Stanley has documented how mainstream media spent a decade fueling "moral panic" about leftists on campus while failing to report on coordinated right-wing campaigns targeting academics, including the "Professor Watchlist" and Canary Mission, which specifically targets professors for advocating the Palestinian cause.

In Nazi Germany, this meant that universities quietly expelled their Jewish faculty while continuing their research. Churches continued their services while their Jewish neighbors were deported. Businesses profited from "Aryanization" while their former Jewish owners were sent to camps. It was a silent, efficient system of looking away.

In the United States during the War on Terror, the media became "embedded" with the military, producing coverage that was intimate with American soldiers but distant from the Iraqi and Afghan civilians dying by the thousands. Journalists who reported extensively on civilian casualties were marginalized. Think tanks with ties to defense contractors churned out reports that supported the war. Academia produced a "terrorism studies" industry that rarely questioned the counter-terrorism framework itself.

Today, regarding Gaza, we see the same patterns. Western universities continue to operate while Gaza's universities are systematically destroyed. Journalists are killed in record numbers, and the media outlets they worked for often repeat military press releases without challenge. Academics who criticize Israeli policy face professional sanctions—tenure denied, funding cut, invitations rescinded. Students who organize protests face disciplinary hearings. And any comparison to the mechanisms of the past is shut down with a single, powerful accusation: antisemitism. This accusation acts as a "closure device," ending the conversation before it can begin, and ensuring that the institutional machinery of looking away keeps running smoothly.

#### Mechanism Four: The Bureaucracy of Harm

Finally, for mass suffering to be sustainable, no single person can feel responsible for it. The work must be broken into small, seemingly innocent tasks. This is the function of **bureaucratic procedure**. As legal scholar Shealyn Millay explains in her expansion of Hannah Arendt's work, "banal evil is hyper-rational, yet thoughtlessly intertwined within bureaucratic systems. It occurs when a system is working toward an evil end, and the people within the system don't see themselves as responsible for evil because they are only doing their job." The Nuremberg archives reveal how "murder was converted into administrative language. Deportations became transfers. Extermination became special treatment. Confiscation became reallocation."

The Holocaust was an administrative triumph. It required census data to identify victims, railway schedules to transport them, and camp procedures to kill them. The man who scheduled the train to Auschwitz could say he was just a railway clerk. The company that supplied the Zyklon B could say it was just selling pesticide. Responsibility was divided so finely that it seemed to disappear.

The American drone program operates on the same principle. Targets are selected through "disposition matrices"—spreadsheets. The process involves multiple agencies, multiple reviews, and multiple signatures. By the time a missile hits a wedding party, the decision has passed through so many hands that no single hand feels the weight of it. The lawyers who signed off on torture techniques at black sites could point to the memos that authorized them. The officials who wrote the memos could point to the requests from the field. The chain of responsibility was a circle.

In Gaza, the bureaucracy of harm is visible in the permit systems that control the flow of food and water, in the targeting approvals that turn a school into a legitimate military target, and in the "operational inquiries" where the military investigates itself and always finds no wrongdoing. Recent scholarship has reopened the conversation between Hannah Arendt and historian Christopher Browning, showing that "ordinary men" participate in mass murder not because they are monsters, but because they "adapted themselves to the Nazi order the same way they adapted themselves to the pre- and post-Nazi order." The system is designed to absorb the horror. The legal advisor who certifies a target does not see the children. The pilot who drops the bomb is following a procedure. The official who dismisses the casualty figures from Gaza's health ministry is just doing his job. Everyone can point elsewhere.

#### Conclusion: The Architecture Remains

The swastika is not the only warning sign. The architecture of exclusion operates through ordinary language, ordinary institutions, and ordinary procedures. It is a human technology, and it is portable. It can be deployed by any society, for any ideology, at any time.

The ignorance that makes this possible is not an absence of information; it is the presence of propaganda so pervasive that it has become invisible—the very air we breathe. To prevent future atrocities, we must learn to see this architecture in its early stages: when a group is first being recategorized, when violence is first being reframed as defense, when institutions are first learning to look away, when the first bureaucratic procedures are being put in place.

The architecture remains. The question is whether we will learn to see it before it is used again.
