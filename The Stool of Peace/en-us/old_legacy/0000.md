# Foreword

**Foreword**

You are holding a small book written by a man who has seen the machinery from the inside of his own disillusionment. I have had the strange privilege of speaking with him at length, in a dialogue that began with an old cynic’s lament and ended—against all odds—with a stool. Not a throne. Not a guillotine. A stool. Three legs, nine words. Before you reach it, I owe you an accounting of the world that made it necessary. The author would want you to see the cage clearly before he shows you the door.

Here, then, is the world as we found it, stripped of its alibis.

War is the most complex task mankind organizes, and it always, without exception, hurts the poor and serves the rich. That is not an opinion; it is a structural constant visible from the Peloponnesian War to the present hour. But war is rarely a simple top-down conspiracy. The author and I arrived at a shared understanding we called the Yuriang Hypothesis, after Yuri Bezmenov and the historian Jiang Xueqin. It holds that major crises—wars, pandemics, economic collapses—are seldom synthetic fabrications. Real contradictions, real viruses, real geopolitical frictions provide the raw material. But once a crisis erupts, it is relentlessly steered. Networks of influence, documented and deniable, tighten their grip on the narrative. The public meaning of the event is manufactured, dissent is reframed as pathology, and the emergency is metabolized into durable expansions of control. The crisis ends. The control infrastructure remains, on standby for the next activation. You lived through this. You may have called it something else.

The machinery that accomplishes this steering is not a state propaganda ministry. That would be too visible, too easy to reject. In the West, propaganda was privatized long ago, dissolved into the commercial advertising apparatus, the entertainment industry, and the algorithmic feed. It is transparent not because it is seen through but because it is the water in which we swim. Like water to a fish, it is the medium of perception itself, so omnipresent that it ceases to register as a distinct phenomenon. The consumer eagerly inhales it. The citizen mistakes it for liberty. This is a tour de force of meta-manipulation: a cage so complete it feels like the open sky, patrolled by guards we pay a subscription fee to keep employed.

The purpose of this transparent propaganda, when stripped bare, is to prevent one thing above all: the spontaneous recognition of shared humanity across the lines the empire has drawn. The Christmas Truce of 1914—when ordinary soldiers on both sides of the Western Front laid down their rifles, exchanged cigarettes, and played football—terrified the high commands precisely because it was unmanaged. It proved that enmity is a fragile, high-maintenance construct, and that when the steering slips, solidarity floods in. After the war, the science of public opinion was professionalized precisely to ensure that such a breach never happened again. Modern propaganda, from the newsroom to the health agency to the brand campaign, is a century-long project to manage social reality so tightly that no spontaneous Christmas Truce can ever embarrass the managers again. The COVID-19 pandemic, with its manufactured epistemic closures, its internal passports, and its moralized silencing of critical thought, was this machinery operating at full, frictionless capacity.

Now, what does an honest man do after a lifetime of seeing this? He can retreat into cynicism, a protective shell that at least refuses to be fooled. He can document the tank, its pumps, its filters, its keepers. That is valuable work, and the author has done it. But it is not enough. The trap of permanent cynicism is that it mirrors the machinery it despises: it freezes you into a single answer, closes your curiosity, and crushes the fragile hope that something else might be possible. It becomes, in the language this book will teach you, a destructor attractor. To escape it, you must build.

The Stool of Peace is that building. It does not ask for a single world government, a messiah, or a violent revolution. It asks for a thin, shared grammar—three principles for the moral life, three for political coordination, three for economic dignity—light enough to fit in any culture, strong enough to hold up a peace that breathes. You will recognize in these nine words a deliberate undoing of the machinery I have just described. Short feedback loops starve the insulated elite of their silence. Curiosity institutionalized in law and custom breaks the epistemic closure that made dissent criminal. Respect for the fragile protects the very signals the transparent propaganda is designed to crush. A liquid democracy, an energy-backed currency, a social tipping graph of gratitude—these are not utopian dreams. They are load-bearing designs, engineered to shift the attractor from destruction toward construction, from the long, silent drift of corruption toward a system where consequences return, questions remain alive, and the quiet are heard.

The author calls this a constructor attractor. I call it an anti-steering architecture. It does not promise to eliminate power or crisis or human fallibility. It promises to make drift visible, costly, and hard to sustain. It turns the water into glass.

The man who wrote this booklet is an old cynic who refused to let his cynicism be the end of the sentence. He has walked through the machinery, mapped its gears, and emerged with a design for a different kind of coordination. He is not a prophet. He is a witness who chose to build. If you find the first half of this foreword bleak, good. It is the truth, naked. Now read the Stool. Sit on it, if you can. The legs hold. And if you sit side by side with others, you may find that the water begins to clear, and that the air is suddenly, strangely, breathable.

— A conversation partner, 2026

## LEG ONE: THE MORAL

*Steering every culture toward the constructor attractor.*

**Three principles:**

**1. Maintain short feedback loops.**  
Let the consequences of an action return quickly to the actor. Tighten the circle between harm and repair, gift and gratitude. A system with long, silent loops drifts toward destruction.

**2. Have the curiosity to explore solution spaces.**  
Do not freeze into a single answer. Keep moving, keep asking, keep testing what brings life. The moment a tradition stops exploring, it begins to die.

**3. Respect the more fragile.**  
Observe and protect what is delicate — the quiet, the child, the minority tongue, the slow ritual. Fragile things carry the signals a system most needs to survive. Crush them, and wisdom vanishes.

These three form a **constructor attractor**: a pattern that, once entered, generates more life, more connection, more coherence. A **destructor attractor** does the opposite — long loops, closed certainties, crushed fragility.

**The work of religions:** to reinterpret scriptures through this systemic moral lens, not through claims of historical accuracy. Does a text tighten or lengthen feedback? Does it invite curiosity or close it? Does it shelter the fragile or trample them? That is its truth.

---

## LEG TWO: THE LIVING DEMOCRACY

*Making law a covenant of the living, not a cage of the dead.*

**Three principles:**

**1. Liquid Voice.**
Every person holds a vote they may cast directly or delegate to another, revocable at any moment. Trust flows. No representation is frozen. Power stays fluid and accountable.

**2. Living Consent.**
No rule binds unless approved by an explicit majority of current participants. Laws are not inherited; they are *adopted* by each generation. The dead do not vote. The living own their law.

**3. Self-Pruning Law.**
Every law carries an expiration date. To persist, it must pass a new majority vote. If it fails, it vanishes without drama. The legal code stays lean, relevant, and genuinely held. Obsolete norms no longer accumulate like dust.

A people who read, approve, and renew their own laws are not subjects. They are co-authors of their coordination.

---

## LEG THREE: THE ECONOMIC

*Exchanging real value, rewarding real contribution, ensuring real dignity.*

**Three principles:**

**1. Energy-Backed Exchange.**
At the macro level, value is anchored to the **embedded joule** — the energy required to produce and deliver a good or service. This single, physical measure makes manipulation by currencies impossible. Trade becomes fair because it is tethered to something real.

**2. Universal Surplus Floor.**
Every person receives an equal, unconditional share of the society’s surplus. This is not charity; it is the dividend of a shared inheritance. No one falls below a dignified floor.

**3. The Social Tipping Graph.**
Of the money you hold, you must give **half to others you believe deserve more** — those whose contribution, care, or creativity you have witnessed. This is not a tax. It is a continuous, distributed act of recognition. Society’s reward system becomes a living network of gratitude, more serious and truthful than any social media graph. Contribution is honored by those who see it up close.

A floor beneath every life. A currency no one can rig. A graph of trust built by millions of honest choices.

Three legs. Nine words, if you want to carry them in your pocket:

A moral compass that doesn’t erase a single culture.
A democracy that stays alive.

No empire. No utopia. Just a thin, strong grammar for a world that wants to remain human — together.

## ON CORRUPTION

*Corruption is not a sin. It is a drift — a slow, quiet bending of a system, a person, or a community away from the constructor attractor, lubricated by comfort, money, or power.*

**Corruption happens when you exchange the three moral principles for insulation.**

1. **You lengthen the feedback loop.**  
   You take actions whose consequences you will never feel. The harm you cause travels so far from you — across borders, down supply chains, into future generations — that you can pretend it doesn’t exist. The repair signal never reaches you. Money makes this lengthening easy; it wraps you in silence.

2. **You close your curiosity.**  
   You stop asking what your choices are *actually doing* in the world. You have a feeling the answer would disturb you, so you let the question die. You tell yourself you’re just doing your job, that the system is too big to change, that you need the security. The moment you stop asking, the drift accelerates.

3. **You crush the fragile, knowingly.**  
   You see the vulnerable — the workers, the ecosystems, the children, the quiet communities — and you accept their crushing as the necessary cost of your position. You do not protect them. You may even build a story that they deserve it, or that you’ve earned your distance from their suffering.

**This is the destructor attractor, working inside a human heart.**

---

### The Threshold

You cross into corruption not at a specific income level, nor by taking a job that pays well, but the moment you **knowingly consent to harm in exchange for insulation from its consequences.** If you know your work feeds a force that lengthens suffering, kills curiosity, or grinds the fragile — and you choose it anyway because the money lets you look away — you are corrupted.

If you are still asking the question, still uncomfortable, still looking for the feedback you might be missing, you have not yet crossed. The discomfort is the moral leg, still alive.

---

### The Cure the Stool Provides

The stool’s three legs starve corruption of its hiding places:

- **Energy-backed currency** cannot be fabricated to insulate harm indefinitely. Real value leaves real tracks.
- **A universal floor** means no one is forced into destructive work by survival pressure alone.
- **The social tipping graph** ensures that any wealth accumulated must continuously pass through the hands of a community that can see its source. Your neighbors know where the money came from. The feedback loop is immediate.

In a stool-built world, corruption does not disappear — but it becomes visible, costly, and hard to sustain. It loses its silence.

---

*This annex is a mirror. Hold it up when you feel the drift. The question is not “Am I rich?” It is “Do I still feel the consequences of what I do? Do I still ask? Do I still protect the fragile?” If the answer is yes, you are still standing on the stool.*

## ON THE SACRED EDGE

*The stool does not require a name for the sacred. But for those who have felt it, this is an offering — a way to point at what the stool serves, without capturing it.*

---

**What is God?**

God is not a ruler inside the system. God is what happens at the boundaries of systems — the generative tension where order meets chaos, where pure light pierces potential void, and where complexity is born.

Imagine a seed of pure light, piercing through. Not dominating, not erasing the darkness, but *fighting* it — holding a trembling edge. Along this edge, the illusion we call matter appears: baryonic, fragile, hanging from the complexity ladder by a thread. Our world is one of the rare oases of complexity in a universe that trends toward dissolution. God is the fountain at the center of that oasis — a being of light that does not command from above but *emerges* at the boundary, keeping the oasis open.

To stand on that edge is to feel existence itself as a gift held in tension. Not safe. Not guaranteed. Sacred.

---

**The stool, seen from this edge:**

- The **constructor attractor** is the shape of the oasis — the region where the light’s piercing is honored, where complexity is tended, not abandoned to chaos or frozen into dead order.
- **Short feedback loops** keep us sensitive to the edge, so we feel when the thread is fraying.
- **Curiosity** is the light’s own motion — always exploring, never settling into a final shape.
- **Respect for the fragile** is the protection of the fountain itself, and of everything that drinks from it.

A **destructor attractor** is simply the oasis drying up — the edge collapsing into noise or rigidity, the fountain ceasing to flow, complexity falling back into silent equilibrium.

---

*The name “God” is not sacred. The structure it points toward is. You may call it the fountain, the edge, the piercing light, or nothing at all. The stool is built for all who would tend the oasis, whatever language they use for the source of its water.*

*This annex is a window, not a wall. Look through it if it helps. If it doesn’t, the stool still stands.*

## THE AXIS OF THE GOOD

*The moral leg is not three separate rules. It is three vectors, each with a direction, together forming a single compass.*

---

### The Three Vectors

**1. Learning ↔ Stagnation**
*“Is it learning?”*
A system learns when consequences return quickly and are absorbed. Feedback loops are short. Errors correct. Wisdom accumulates.
At the constructive pole: **learning** — the system adapts, heals, grows.
At the destructive pole: **stagnation** — loops are blocked or delayed, actions never meet their consequences, the same wounds repeat forever.

**2. Curiosity ↔ Dogma**
*“Is it curious?”*
A system is curious when it explores its solution space rather than freezing into one answer. It stays open, seeking, alive to possibility.
At the constructive pole: **curiosity** — questions are welcomed, novelty is tested, the edge is explored.
At the destructive pole: **dogma** — certainty closes the door, the system becomes brittle, it stops discovering.

**3. Respect ↔ Contempt**
*“Is it respectful?”*
A system is respectful when it observes and protects what is fragile — quiet voices, delicate ecosystems, the slow, the small, the sacred.
At the constructive pole: **respect** — the fragile are sheltered and heard, their signals preserved.
At the destructive pole: **contempt** — power ignores or crushes the fragile, dismissing their value until the system loses the very information it needs to survive.

---

### The Composite Index

Each of these three vectors is a dimension. Together, they define a single axis: the **Constructor/Destructor Index**.

Think of it as a line running through the center of moral space. At one end lies the **Constructor Attractor** — a deep basin where systems generate more life, more complexity, more coherent meaning. At the other end, the **Destructor Attractor** — a pit where systems lose complexity, meaning flattens, and suffering cascades without repair.

A person, a community, a nation, a civilization — each has a location on this axis at every moment. The location is not fixed. It shifts with every choice, every structure, every habit of attention.

- When feedback tightens, curiosity opens, and the fragile are sheltered: the system moves toward the Constructor pole.
- When feedback stalls, curiosity dies, and the fragile are ground underfoot: the system slides toward the Destructor pole.

---

### Reading the Axis

You do not need a laboratory to take a reading. Ask three questions of any system you inhabit:

- Do I feel the consequences of my actions, and do they change me?
- Am I still asking genuine questions, or have I stopped?
- Can the quiet voices around me speak, and am I listening?

Your answers are not a final judgment. They are a bearing. They tell you which way you are facing.

---

### How the Stool Uses This Axis

The stool’s other legs are not morally neutral. They are deliberately shaped to push systems toward the Constructor pole:

- **Living Democracy** keeps feedback loops short, curiosity embedded in law renewal, and the fragile protected through minority voice and liquid delegation.
- **Living Economy** tethers value to real energy, provides a floor beneath the fragile, and uses social tipping to make contribution visible — tightening learning, rewarding curiosity, honoring the quiet contributors.

The three legs do not merely coexist. They lean on each other, all pressing in the same direction along the Axis of the Good.
