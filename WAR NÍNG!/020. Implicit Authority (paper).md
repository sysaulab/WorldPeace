# The Spectrum of Authority

***From Explicit Enforcement to Implicit Virtue***

**Abstract**

This paper extends the existing economic framework of organizational authority by proposing a conceptual spectrum that ranges from pure explicit authority (enforced through monitoring) to implicit authority (where an idea's intrinsic virtue commands compliance without promotion). Building upon van den Brink and Gilles' foundational distinction between explicit and latent authority, and Aghion and Tirole's work on formal and real authority, this paper introduces a third category—implicit authority—that operates outside formal hierarchical structures entirely. The analysis suggests that authority flows not only from position and enforcement capacity but also from the perceived truth or utility of ideas themselves, with implications for understanding innovation, knowledge dissemination, and organizational design.

## 1. Introduction

The study of authority within organizations has long occupied a central place in economic and organizational theory. Traditional approaches have conceptualized authority as flowing from hierarchical position, property rights, or contractual arrangements. Yet anyone who has participated in organizational life recognizes that authority operates in more subtle ways: a junior team member's brilliant insight may carry greater weight than a senior manager's suggestion; a well-reasoned argument may prevail over an ill-considered directive from above; and some ideas propagate through organizations without any formal backing whatsoever.

This paper seeks to develop a more nuanced understanding of authority by mapping it along a spectrum. At one pole lies what van den Brink and Gilles term "explicit authority"—the exercise of power through monitoring and enforcement . At the opposite pole lies what I term "implicit authority"—the compliance that arises not from threat of sanction but from recognition of an idea's intrinsic merit. Between these poles lies the territory van den Brink and Gilles have already charted as "latent authority," where subordinates submit to authority even when it is not explicitly enforced .

The central contribution of this paper is to argue that implicit authority represents a qualitatively distinct phenomenon requiring separate theoretical treatment. Unlike latent authority, which still operates within hierarchical relationships, implicit authority transcends hierarchy entirely. It is the authority of the good idea that propagates because of its goodness, not because of its source.

## 2. The Established Framework: Explicit and Latent Authority

**2.1 Explicit Authority**

Van den Brink and Gilles define explicit authority as the "myopic exercise of authority" that occurs when decision makers actively enforce their directives . In their game-theoretic model, explicit authority emerges when monitoring costs are sufficiently low—when superiors can observe subordinate behavior at reasonable cost and impose sanctions for non-compliance .

This conception aligns with traditional understandings of authority in organizational economics. Williamson's work on hierarchical control emphasizes the role of monitoring in ensuring compliance . Grossman and Hart's property rights approach similarly grounds authority in the ability to exclude others from valuable assets . Explicit authority is, in essence, authority backed by the capacity to punish.

**2.2 Latent Authority**

More innovative is van den Brink and Gilles's concept of latent authority. They describe this as situations where "subordinates submit themselves to authority even though such authority is not enforced explicitly" . Latent authority represents a form of voluntary compliance that nonetheless remains embedded within hierarchical relationships.

Crucially, van den Brink and Gilles demonstrate that latent authority can be sustained as an equilibrium under certain conditions. When enforcement costs are low enough, both explicit and latent authority may coexist; when enforcement costs rise, latent authority may persist even after explicit authority becomes infeasible . This suggests that authority relationships can survive the removal of enforcement mechanisms, provided they have been internalized by subordinates.

The key insight here is that authority can operate without active exercise. A manager whose directives are followed without any need for monitoring possesses latent authority over their subordinates. The authority remains latent because it need not be activated to be effective.

**2.3 Formal and Real Authority**

A complementary framework comes from Aghion and Tirole, who distinguish between formal authority (the right to decide) and real authority (effective control over decisions) . Real authority, in their analysis, is determined by the structure of information: subordinates gain real authority when they possess information that principals lack, and when formal delegation gives them scope to act on that information .

Aghion and Tirole's framework helps explain how authority can diverge from formal position. A formally subordinate employee may exercise considerable real authority if they control critical information. Conversely, a formal superior may find their authority hollow if they lack the information needed to make meaningful decisions.

## 3. Beyond Latent Authority: The Case for Implicit Authority

**3.1 The Limits of the Existing Framework**

Both van den Brink and Gilles's latent authority and Aghion and Tirole's real authority remain fundamentally hierarchical concepts. Latent authority still presupposes a superior-subordinate relationship; it simply posits that this relationship can function without active enforcement. Real authority still flows from position, albeit mediated by information asymmetries. In both cases, authority attaches to persons occupying roles within organizational structures.

Yet there exists a form of authority that attaches not to persons but to ideas. When a compelling argument persuades listeners regardless of its source, when a brilliant insight spreads through an organization without sponsorship, when a practice is adopted because it works rather than because someone mandated it—these phenomena reflect a different kind of authority altogether.

**3.2 Defining Implicit Authority**

I propose the following definition: **implicit authority** is the capacity of an idea, argument, or practice to command acceptance or compliance based solely on its perceived intrinsic merit, independent of the hierarchical position of its proponent or the presence of enforcement mechanisms.

Several features distinguish implicit authority from the forms previously discussed:

First, implicit authority inheres in propositions rather than persons. It is the authority of the claim itself, not the claimant.

Second, implicit authority operates outside formal hierarchies. It can flow upward, downward, or laterally without regard to organizational structure.

Third, implicit authority requires no enforcement—indeed, enforcement would be irrelevant to its operation. A proposition accepted on its merits needs no backing by sanctions.

Fourth, implicit authority often operates without conscious recognition. We follow good ideas without necessarily reflecting that we are doing so.

**3.3 The Mechanism of Implicit Authority**

What explains the operation of implicit authority? Several complementary mechanisms suggest themselves:

**Epistemic deference.** Humans are reasoning creatures who seek true beliefs and effective practices. When we encounter an argument that appears sound or an idea that seems useful, we adopt it because doing so serves our epistemic and practical interests. The authority of the idea derives from its apparent truth or utility.

**Consensual validation.** Ideas that command widespread assent acquire authority through social proof. The fact that many have accepted an idea becomes itself a reason to accept it—not because of any enforcement mechanism, but because consensus signals credibility.

**Practical success.** Ideas that work acquire authority through demonstrated effectiveness. A manufacturing technique that reduces defects, a management practice that improves morale, a strategic insight that generates profits—these propagate because they produce results, not because they are mandated.

**Intrinsic persuasiveness.** Some arguments simply convince. Their logical structure, evidential support, and rhetorical force command assent regardless of who offers them.

**3.4 Implicit Authority Distinguished from Latent Authority**

The distinction between latent and implicit authority merits careful attention. Both involve compliance without enforcement, but they differ fundamentally in their underlying structure:

| Dimension | Latent Authority | Implicit Authority |
|-----------|------------------|---------------------|
| **Locus** | Inheres in persons (superiors) | Inheres in ideas/propositions |
| **Structure** | Presupposes hierarchy | Independent of hierarchy |
| **Basis** | Internalized acceptance of role | Perceived merit of content |
| **Direction** | Typically downward | Omni-directional |
| **Mechanism** | Socialization, habituation | Reason, evidence, utility |
| **Example** | Following manager's unenforced directive | Adopting colleague's brilliant idea |

Latent authority, in van den Brink and Gilles's conception, represents the internalization of hierarchical relationships. Subordinates follow because they accept the superior's right to direct, even when that right is not actively exercised. The authority remains attached to the person and role.

Implicit authority, by contrast, represents the operation of reason and evidence independent of hierarchy. We follow an idea not because of who proposed it but because of what it is. The authority attaches to the idea itself.

**3.5 The Spectrum of Authority**

These distinctions suggest a continuous spectrum of authority forms:

**Pure explicit authority.** Authority exercised through direct monitoring and enforcement. Compliance occurs because non-compliance would be punished.

**Enforced latent authority.** Authority that could be enforced but need not be, because subordinates have internalized hierarchical relationships. The possibility of enforcement remains in the background.

**Unenforced latent authority.** Authority sustained purely by internalized acceptance, without any realistic threat of enforcement. Subordinates comply because they accept the superior's authority, not because they fear sanctions.

**Formal but hollow authority.** Authority that exists on paper but is not exercised because the superior lacks information or capacity (Aghion and Tirole's formal authority without real authority).

**Real authority.** Effective control exercised by informed subordinates despite formal subordination (Aghion and Tirole's real authority without formal authority).

**Implicit authority.** Authority of ideas accepted on their merits, independent of hierarchical position.

At one end of this spectrum stands authority backed by force; at the other end stands authority backed by truth.

## 4. Implications and Applications

**4.1 Organizational Design**

Recognizing implicit authority has implications for how organizations structure themselves. Organizations that wish to harness implicit authority must create conditions under which good ideas can be recognized and adopted regardless of their source. This suggests:

- Flattening hierarchies to reduce status-based filtering of ideas
- Creating multiple channels for idea dissemination
- Rewarding those who recognize and adopt good ideas, not just those who originate them
- Developing shared criteria for evaluating proposals

**4.2 Innovation Diffusion**

The concept of implicit authority helps explain how innovations spread. An innovation succeeds not because someone with formal authority mandates it, but because its advantages become apparent to potential adopters. The authority of the innovation is implicit in its demonstrated superiority.

This perspective aligns with diffusion of innovations research, which emphasizes the role of perceived attributes (relative advantage, compatibility, complexity, trialability, observability) in adoption decisions. These attributes constitute the implicit authority of the innovation.

**4.3 Knowledge Management**

Implicit authority also illuminates knowledge management challenges. Explicit authority can mandate the use of particular knowledge management systems, but it cannot compel the sharing of valuable knowledge. Knowledge sharing occurs when participants recognize the implicit authority of good ideas and the value of contributing to collective understanding.

**4.4 Limits and Boundary Conditions**

Implicit authority is not unlimited. Several factors constrain its operation:

**Cognitive limitations.** Humans are imperfect reasoners. We may fail to recognize good ideas or may accept bad ones.

**Social pressures.** The desire for belonging and approval can override epistemic considerations.

**Path dependence.** Existing practices and investments create inertia that good ideas must overcome.

**Power asymmetries.** Those with explicit authority can suppress ideas that threaten their position.

**Information problems.** The merits of an idea may not be immediately apparent, requiring resources for evaluation.

## 5. Conclusion

This paper has argued for expanding our conception of authority beyond the hierarchical frameworks that have dominated organizational economics. Van den Brink and Gilles's distinction between explicit and latent authority, and Aghion and Tirole's distinction between formal and real authority, have significantly advanced our understanding of how authority operates within organizations. Yet these frameworks remain tethered to hierarchical premises.

Implicit authority—the authority of ideas accepted on their merits—represents a qualitatively distinct phenomenon requiring separate theoretical treatment. Unlike latent authority, which internalizes hierarchy, implicit authority transcends it. The good idea propagates because of its goodness, not because of its source.

Recognizing implicit authority has implications for how we understand organizations, innovation, and knowledge. It suggests that authority flows not only from position and enforcement capacity but also from truth and utility. It reminds us that organizations are not just structures of command but also arenas of reason, where the better argument can—and sometimes does—prevail.

The spectrum from explicit enforcement to implicit virtue captures the full range of ways in which compliance and acceptance are secured in organizational life. At one end stands the threat of sanction; at the other end stands the force of the better argument. Between them lies the rich territory where hierarchy, information, and reason interact to shape how we work together.
