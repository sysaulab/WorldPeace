# The Səlilwət Community Trust

***An Indigenous Economic Model for Urban Healing***

## A Working Paper

**Sylvain Saucier**

*In collaboration with the Səlilwət Community Trust Elders Advisory Council*

**February 2026**

## Abstract

This paper presents the theoretical foundations and practical architecture of the Səlilwət Community Trust, a ten-year pilot project to house 20,000 residents of Vancouver's Downtown Eastside in community-owned housing organized around Indigenous economic principles. The model draws directly from the potlatch traditions of Pacific Northwest nations—sophisticated economic systems that increased collective wealth through ceremonial distribution rather than private accumulation—while addressing the social dissolution mechanisms identified in Calhoun's behavioral sink experiments. A three-tier distribution system provides unconditional material security (Tier 1), a dynamic recognition economy through "liftable tips" (Tier 2), and an ecological consumption ceiling (Tier 3). The paper argues that this synthesis of Indigenous governance, behavioral science, and ecological economics offers a scalable alternative to the extractive individualization that characterizes contemporary neoliberal social policy. Preliminary projections suggest the model could generate $150-200 million in annual government savings while building $500 million to $1 billion in community-owned assets over ten years.

**Keywords:** Indigenous economics, potlatch, behavioral sink, community land trust, mutual aid, economic sovereignty, DTES, housing first

## 1. Introduction: The Limits of Individualized Intervention

The Downtown Eastside of Vancouver concentrates individuals whose cognitive styles, trauma histories, neurodiversity, and cultural values are poorly matched to the demands of neoliberal ideology—deferred gratification, entrepreneurial self-branding, and conformity to behavioral norms as conditions of access to resources. Recent data from the DTES SRO Collaborative's comprehensive tenant survey reveals that 70% of tenants would be homeless without SROs, 39% were homeless before securing their unit, and 31% of tenants are Indigenous—compared to 2% citywide (DTES SRO Collaborative, 2023). Market SRO rents increased nearly 50% between 2013 and 2023, while the 55-64 age demographic grew from 15% in 2008 to 29% in 2023.

Current approaches rely on individual, means-tested interventions: case workers, income assistance, rent supplements, treatment referrals. These methods assume that problems are located in individuals and can be solved through individualized attention. The evidence suggests otherwise. As Giles (2025) documented at Housing Central 2025, rent arrears are the main eviction driver, with a 34% rise in applicants facing 70% income-to-shelter ratios between 2023 and 2024. Prevention is consistently demonstrated to be more effective and less costly than crisis intervention—yet the system remains structured around crisis response.

This paper proposes that the fundamental error lies in the unit of analysis. The problem is not individual pathology but systemic distortion. The DTES exhibits what physicists would call a "distorted field"—abnormal amplitudes of economic inequality, housing precarity, and social disconnection. These distortions are the predictable outcome of treating housing as a financial asset and residents as consumers rather than co-owners. Every dollar of income assistance that enters the DTES through an individual recipient quickly leaves the community via private landlords, external retailers, predatory lenders, and the unregulated drug market.

A less distorted field requires re-embedding the economy in social relationships—creating a bounded system where resources circulate internally before interacting with external markets. This is precisely what the potlatch economies of the Pacific Northwest accomplished for millennia.

## 2. Theoretical Framework

### 2.1 The Potlatch as Economic Institution

The potlatch ceremonies of Pacific Northwest Indigenous nations were systematically suppressed by the Canadian government under the *Indian Act* from 1885 to 1951, with church officials dismissing them as "wasteful, uneconomic, and irrational" (Cole & Chaikin, 1990). Contemporary economic analysis reveals the opposite. As U'mista Cultural Society (2024) documents, potlatches functioned as sophisticated systems of decentralized exchange, wealth redistribution, and social memory.

In the potlatch model, wealth is accumulated not for private hoarding but for ceremonial distribution. Gift-giving creates obligations that bind communities across time. Social memory—preserved through song, dance, and storytelling—serves as informal recordkeeping. Crucially, the system increases total wealth and social welfare by facilitating distribution rather than concentration.

Anthropological research has demonstrated that potlatch economies were not primitive precursors to market exchange but alternative economic logics with distinct properties (Mauss, 1925/1990; Roscoe, 2023). Where market economies allocate resources through price mechanisms and private property, potlatch economies allocate through status competition and ceremonial generosity. The two are not incommensurable; they can coexist, as they did for centuries along the Northwest coast, with potlatch governing internal distribution while trade governed external relations.

The Səlilwət model extends this logic to the entire residential community. Integrated members function as a single economic unit, contributing their labor and production to the community, with the community providing for all members' needs in return. This is not charity—it is community ownership of the means of subsistence and production, grounded in Indigenous legal traditions that predate Canadian sovereignty (Borrows, 2010).

### 2.2 The Behavioral Sink: Lessons from Calhoun

John B. Calhoun's experiments with Norway rats (1958-1962) and later with mice ("Universe 25," 1968-1972) demonstrated a puzzling phenomenon: in environments with unlimited food, water, and protection from predators—ostensibly "utopias"—populations initially grew, then plateaued, and eventually collapsed toward extinction despite abundant resources (Calhoun, 1962, 1973).

Calhoun coined the term "behavioral sink" to describe the collapse in social behavior that preceded population decline. Key observations included social withdrawal (individuals who would emerge only when others slept, whom Calhoun called "the beautiful ones"), breakdown of maternal behavior (females abandoning or attacking their young), loss of social roles and hierarchies, and violence despite adequate nutrition.

Recent theoretical work by Mantzaris (2025) proposes that the behavioral sink resulted from "hierarchy entropy degeneration"—the progressive loss of uncertainty in perceived social ranks over generations. When the enclosure's design enabled full visibility of the pecking order, and this hierarchy became completely known, "the lack of uncertainty removed any attempts to engage in activities that touch upon that hierarchy." The payoff matrix for social engagement became flat; there was nothing left to compete for, nothing left to signal.

The profound insight for human social organization is this: unconditional resource provision without meaningful social differentiation may lead to the very collapse it aims to prevent. Social structures require gradients—uncertainty about status, opportunities for signaling, mechanisms for recognition—to maintain engagement and reproduction of social norms. This is not an argument for inequality but for dynamic recognition. The problem is not hierarchy per se but frozen, fully visible, inescapable hierarchy.

### 2.3 The Tip Economy as Social Signaling

The Səlilwət model addresses this challenge through its three-tier distribution system, with particular attention to Tier 2: the tip economy. Each month, members receive their discretionary allocation split 50/50—half as immediately spendable cash, half as "liftable tips" that cannot be spent directly but must be gifted to other members. When a member gifts a tip to another member, it becomes immediately spendable for the recipient.

This mechanism creates several properties essential for healthy social dynamics:

1. **Immediate generosity incentive:** Members can only access their full allocation by giving away the tip portion. Hoarding is structurally impossible.

2. **No hierarchy accumulation:** Tips become money immediately for recipients; no permanent status markers accumulate. Recognition flows rather than settles.

3. **Pure social signal:** A tip conveys unambiguous recognition: "I value your contribution enough to give you purchasing power right now."

4. **Maintained uncertainty:** Because tips convert immediately upon gifting, the system never settles into fixed hierarchies. Today's tipper is tomorrow's potential recipient.

5. **Visibility for invisible work:** Caregiving, emotional support, elder guidance, cultural teaching—all the contributions that markets ignore—become visible and valued.

This addresses the hierarchy entropy problem directly. The system maintains uncertainty gradients by design, ensuring that social engagement remains rewarding and meaningful.

## 3. The Economic Model

### 3.1 Tier 1: Unconditional Security

All fully integrated members receive absolute necessities without condition, fee, or means test: housing in community-owned towers, basic nutrition through community cafeterias, basic clothing produced internally, access to community learning programs, and basic transportation within the city.

The condition for receiving is full integration—defined as presence in the community, participation in governance, and recognition by peers as a member in good standing. This is not employment conditionality but belonging conditionality. One must be of the community, not merely housed in it.

This implements what Raworth (2017) terms the "social foundation"—the minimum required for human flourishing—while remaining compatible with the "ecological ceiling" of sustainable consumption. It also operationalizes the "housing first" principle that has demonstrated effectiveness in addressing chronic homelessness (Tsemberis, 2010), while adding the missing element: community.

### 3.2 Tier 2: The Tip Economy

The tip mechanism operationalizes potlatch logic in a digital environment. Tips are granted for contributions not captured by formal work shifts: emotional support, peer counseling, teaching skills, unscheduled repairs, elders' guidance, mediating disputes, supporting someone in crisis—any act of kindness or contribution that a member wishes to recognize.

The critical rule that tips cannot be spent until gifted creates a forced circulation that mirrors the ceremonial distribution of the potlatch. Wealth must move to have value. Status derives from giving, not accumulating.

This digitization of the potlatch addresses what Graeber (2011) identifies as the fundamental problem with money: its tendency to become an end in itself, severing the social relationships that originally gave exchange meaning. By making money itself contingent on ongoing recognition, the tip economy re-embeds currency in social relations.

### 3.3 Tier 3: Ecological Ceiling

Total discretionary funds distributed to members are subject to a globally capped limit, democratically determined by the Community Congress each year. This implements the "sustainable consumption corridor" (SCC) framework, which defines both a social foundation and an ecological ceiling (Di Giulio & Fuchs, 2014). Drawing inspiration from the 2000-watt society concept originating in Switzerland (Notter et al., 2013), the community commits to living within per-capita consumption limits that could be sustained globally.

The ecological ceiling is not an external imposition but a democratic choice. Each year, the Community Congress debates and sets the limit based on community surplus from production, scientific data on sustainable consumption, and competing priorities for investment. This transforms ecological limits from constraints into opportunities for collective deliberation about the good life.

### 3.4 The Canadian Dollar Interface

Critically, the Səlilwət model does not introduce a separate local currency. All financial transactions occur in Canadian dollars through conventional banking infrastructure (OSFI, 2025). Members use standard bank cards that function anywhere in the world.

The innovation is informational, not monetary. A custom banking application provides members with real-time visualization of the economic impact of their choices: balance viewing, tip interface, Community Return Indicator showing the percentage of spending returning to the community pot, and a collective dashboard displaying internal circulation rate, surplus available, and ecological ceiling status.

This creates feedback loops that encourage localization and generosity without coercion. Members are free to spend anywhere, but the information architecture makes visible the collective consequences of individual choices. This aligns with behavioral economics research showing that information transparency can shift preferences without restricting choice (Thaler & Sunstein, 2008).

## 4. Physical Design and Governance

### 4.1 Vertical Villages

Housing is provided in tall, slender towers (pencil towers) oriented to avoid casting shadows on the park to the north, addressing a classic NIMBY concern while maximizing density. Each tower houses approximately 2,000-2,400 residents across 30 floors, with sectional organization placing production at lower levels, public marketplace at ground level, and housing above.

Floors are grouped thematically to create communities of affinity while maintaining cross-contact: Welcome Floors (transitional), Active Use Floors (harm reduction based), Healing Floors (sober/recovering), Family Floors, Elders & Knowledge Keepers Floors, and Artisan Floors. The elevator system and shared spaces ensure residents move between clusters for work, learning, and relationship, creating gradients of connection without forcing conformity.

### 4.2 Nested Governance

Governance follows Indigenous principles of distributed authority (Alfred, 2009). Floor meetings address immediate concerns and nominate representatives to Tower Councils, which coordinate across clusters and allocate resources. The Community Congress of all members meets quarterly to review finances, debate investment priorities, and set the ecological ceiling. An Elders Advisory Committee guides Indigenous governance protocols, and a Tenant Advisory Committee represents SRO tenants in each building.

This structure implements what I have elsewhere termed "fluid democracy"—a system of revocable delegation that scales from local communities to global coordination while maintaining accountability (Saucier, 2025). It ensures that those affected by decisions participate in making them, the core principle of subsidiarity.

## 5. The People's Investment Bank

### 5.1 Structure and Capitalization

The People's Investment Bank is a community-owned financial institution that holds title to all community land and housing (removed from speculative market), receives bulk government transfers on behalf of integrated members, provides micro-loans to member-owned production shops, and retains and reinvests all revenue internally.

Initial capitalization requires $50-100 million for property acquisition and infrastructure development. This is framed as one-time infrastructure investment, not ongoing subsidy. At full scale, the project is projected to generate $150-200 million in annual government savings through reduced administrative overhead, retained economic leakage, and prevention dividends (Saucier, 2026).

### 5.2 The Bulk Transfer Mechanism

The current system channels government funds through individual payments to landlords, vendors, and case managers, with most resources leaking to external economies. The proposed system channels bulk transfers directly to the Community Trust, which distributes internally through Tiers 1-3, invests in community-owned production, and builds permanent assets.

This mechanism aligns with what the Canadian Mortgage and Housing Corporation has identified as promising practices in community land trust financing (CMHC, 2024). It transforms government from a funder of individual services to a partner in community wealth building.

## 6. Research Framework

### 6.1 Primary Hypothesis

The prevalence of harmful behaviors in the DTES is not primarily a function of individual pathology but of exposure to an economic environment that demands cognitive and behavioral patterns incompatible with residents' neurodiversity, trauma histories, and cultural values. By creating a bounded economic system that guarantees survival while preserving dynamic social signaling mechanisms through a tip economy, we can reduce harm, increase wellbeing, and generate longitudinal data on alternative economic structures—without requiring residents to conform to neoliberal ideology as a condition of belonging.

### 6.2 Secondary Hypotheses

**H1:** The three-tier system will generate higher rates of self-reported wellbeing and community belonging than conventional SRO housing with individual case management (measured by WHO-5 Well-Being Index and Indigenous wellbeing measures).

**H2:** The tip economy will produce measurable networks of reciprocity that cross-cut thematic floor clusters, with tipping patterns revealing the value of previously invisible work (caregiving, emotional support, cultural teaching).

**H3:** Per-capita government expenditure will decrease by 25-40% over ten years due to administrative savings, leakage reduction, and prevention dividends.

**H4:** Overdose rates will decline by at least 50% among integrated members compared to baseline DTES averages, due to reduced isolation and regulated internal supply.

**H5:** Social network analysis will reveal sustained uncertainty in hierarchy perception—tips will circulate continuously without settling into permanent status hierarchies, preventing the entropy degeneration observed in Calhoun's experiments.

### 6.3 The Tip Dataset

The tip economy generates a complete directed graph of all acts of recognition—a dataset unprecedented in social science. Every act of kindness, every moment of support becomes visible and measurable. This allows empirical investigation of fundamental questions: What does a healthy recognition economy look like? How does tipping behavior differ across subcommunities? Do certain members act as "recognition hubs"? What network characteristics predict individual wellbeing and community resilience?

## 7. Addressing Anticipated Objections

### 7.1 "This is communism / a cult / separatist."

Response: This is community-owned housing, like a strata but without individual ownership—exactly the model CMHC has been funding through the Community Land Trust demonstration program (CMHC, 2024). The tip system is a modern version of how extended families have always supported each other. The buildings are in the city, open to the public, with a park and marketplace anyone can use. This is integration with dignity, not separation.

### 7.2 "You're enabling addiction."

Response: The current system requires sobriety for housing, and people die on the street. Housing first, without conditions, has demonstrated effectiveness (Tsemberis, 2010). The tip economy means the person who sits with someone through withdrawal gets recognized and rewarded. That is happening now, invisibly. We are making it visible and sustainable. If the theory is right, the environment itself promotes healthier choices. We are measuring that to find out.

### 7.3 "This takes money from existing services."

Response: This does not redirect existing service funding—it creates a new funding stream for housing and economic development that reduces demand on crisis services. The savings to health and justice systems are where the efficiency gains come from.

### 7.4 "This is cultural appropriation."

Response: That is why the project is Indigenous-led from the start. The Elders Advisory Committee governs how potlatch principles are applied. The host nations are partners, not consultants. If they say it is appropriation, we stop. If they say it is revitalization, we follow their lead. This is their call, not ours.

## 8. Conclusion: An Invitation to Build

The Səlilwət Community Trust seeks partners who recognize that the current system is not failing; it is functioning exactly as designed—to extract wealth from vulnerable populations. The residents of the DTES are not broken individuals requiring repair; they are people whose cognitive and cultural differences have been pathologized by a system that demands conformity. The potlatch tradition is not a historical artifact; it is a living economic logic that can guide the future. Calhoun's rats warn us that material abundance without social function leads to collapse—but abundance with meaningful recognition can sustain vibrant communities.

At best: We solve for housing, addiction, and discrimination in one of Canada's most complex neighborhoods—without displacement, without coercion, without requiring anyone to abandon their identity. We generate a generation of research on human social organization and contribute to the revitalization of Indigenous economic traditions suppressed for over a century.

At worst: We build beautiful housing, productive businesses, and a decade of research on alternative economics—infrastructure that serves the community regardless of whether the full vision is realized.

The only serious risk is exposing the inefficiency of profit-driven institutions. That risk we accept, with care taken to frame this work as complementary, not oppositional—an expansion of the possible, not an attack on the present.
