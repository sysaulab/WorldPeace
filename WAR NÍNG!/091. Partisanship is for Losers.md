# Partisanship is for Losers

> "In war, when sides demand me to take for them, they are demanding of me to adopt their cult of hatred and death of an entire culture. I cannot take that position for I love everybody. They are both friends to me and I want both healthy and happy. In essence it is like if my lungs were in war against my heart, each demanding my hatred of the other, it is not possible for me to choose." —God

This is not a political statement. It is a biological and spiritual one. It reveals the core delusion of extreme partisanship: the belief that we are separate. When the heart and the lungs go to war, the body does not get a better circulatory system or improved respiration. It gets a corpse. The victory of one over the other is not a victory at all; it is the death of the whole.

In the modern era, partisanship is often celebrated as a sign of conviction and loyalty. To be a "good" Democrat or a "true" Republican is to hold the line, to demonize the opposition, and to cheer for your team’s victory as if it were your own. But if we step back from the emotional thrill of the tribal scrum and examine the outcome through the cold lens of social psychology—and the warm lens of holistic wisdom—a different picture emerges. Extreme partisanship is a profound cognitive, strategic, and spiritual failure. It is, quite simply, for losers.

This assertion is not about which party wins or loses an election. It is about the fundamental loss of objective reality, the forfeiture of critical thinking, the inevitable strategic blunders that arise from groupthink, and the ultimate destruction of the very body politic we claim to want to save.

## The Tribal Brain and the Cult of Hatred

The foundation of partisanship's failure lies in our evolutionary wiring, a mechanism that the quote identifies as the "cult of hatred." Social Identity Theory, pioneered by Henri Tajfel, demonstrates that humans have an innate tendency to categorize themselves into "in-groups" (us) and "out-groups" (them). We derive a significant portion of our self-esteem from the status and perceived superiority of our group. This is the psychological bedrock of partisanship.

Once this categorization is in place, a cascade of biases takes over. The **in-group bias** leads us to favor, trust, and overvalue the opinions and actions of our own party. Simultaneously, the **out-group homogeneity bias** causes us to see the opposition as a monolithic, unthinking mass. This is the first major loss: the loss of nuanced perception. A partisan doesn't see a complex opponent with a mix of good and bad ideas; they see a cartoon villain.

The quote pierces through this bias by refusing it entirely. It identifies the demand to choose as a demand to "adopt their cult of hatred." This is the poison. Partisanship, at its worst, is not just a disagreement about policy; it requires a total ideological surrender that mandates hatred of the other. It demands you see your fellow citizen not as a misguided friend, but as an enemy to be destroyed. From the perspective of a consciousness that loves the whole creation—that sees both the heart and the lungs as vital—this demand is impossible. To love one and hate the other is to wish for the death of the self.

## The Groupthink Machine

It is within this "us vs. them" framework, fueled by this "cult of hatred," that the most operationally destructive psychological force takes hold: **groupthink**. Coined by Irving Janis, groupthink describes the mode of thinking that people engage in when they are deeply involved in a cohesive in-group, when the members' striving for unanimity overrides their motivation to realistically appraise alternative courses of action.

For the hyper-partisan, political affiliation is not just a preference; it is a core part of their identity. Dissent is not just a disagreement; it is a betrayal. This pressure manifests in several classic symptoms of groupthink, all of which are recipes for losing:

1.  **Illusion of Invulnerability:** The group becomes excessively optimistic and willing to take extraordinary risks. This leads to overreach and strategic miscalculations. Parties push through unpopular legislation or nominate flawed candidates, insulated in their echo chambers, convinced of their own infallibility. They have forgotten that the heart cannot live without the lungs.

2.  **Collective Rationalization:** Members dismiss warnings that might shatter their shared assumptions. Data that contradicts the party line is explained away as fake, biased, or part of a conspiracy. This is the loss of reality. When a party collectively decides that economic indicators are irrelevant, they are building policy on a foundation of sand, ignoring the fact that the body is struggling to breathe.

3.  **Stereotyping of Out-Groups:** The opposition is caricatured as too evil or too stupid to negotiate with. This forecloses any possibility of compromise. The goal is no longer to find the best policy for the whole body, but to crush the inferior organ. This self-amputation ensures the body will be crippled, even if one faction briefly claims to be "on top."

4.  **Self-Censorship & Illusion of Unanimity:** Members who have doubts learn to keep quiet. The silence of dissent is mistaken for thunderous support, leading to decisions that are shockingly out of touch with reality. The loss here is of critical feedback, the single most important input for any healthy system. The heart cannot tell the lungs they are working too hard if the lungs are not allowed to speak.

5.  **Self-Appointed "Mindguards":** Certain members actively protect the group from dissenting information. They attack journalists or shun former allies who question the orthodoxy. This active suppression ensures the group remains blissfully ignorant until the entire organism flatlines.

## The Compound Loss

The ultimate outcome of these dynamics is a compound loss. First, there is the loss of **epistemic integrity**—the ability to know what is true. A mind consumed by partisanship cannot accurately assess the world, because all information is filtered through the need to protect the group's status.

From this distorted worldview flows the loss of **strategic effectiveness**. The partisan group, blinded by its own biases and silenced by groupthink, makes catastrophic errors. They misread the electorate, double down on unpopular policies, and attack straw men instead of addressing real concerns. They become brittle. When a plan inevitably fails, the blame is not placed on the flawed thinking of the group, but on the sinister machinations of the out-group, reinforcing the cycle and preventing any real learning.

This is the cycle that the divine voice in the quote laments. It is the spectacle of the lungs and heart each blaming the other for the body's fatigue, while refusing to see that only their coordinated effort can restore health.

## Conclusion: The Wisdom of the Whole

In the end, hyper-partisanship is a loser's game because it forfeits the very tools needed to win: critical thought, adaptability, and a clear perception of reality. It trades long-term problem-solving for short-term tribal gratification. It exchanges the possibility of genuine progress for the fleeting high of collective self-righteousness. But most profoundly, as the quote teaches us, it is a game built on a lie—the lie that we are separate.

The winners in society are not those who cling most tightly to a party line, but those who can see past it. They are the ones who understand that the ultimate victory is not for a party, but for the whole. They refuse the "cult of hatred." They maintain relationships across the divide. They hold the tension of opposites, saying, "I see the value in what the heart offers, and I see the value in what the lungs offer. Your war is not my war. My war is against the sickness of the body, which includes both of you."

To choose a side in the war between the heart and the lungs is to choose death. To refuse the choice, and to work for the health of the whole, is the only path to life. That is the ultimate victory, and it is one that partisanship, by its very nature, can never achieve.
