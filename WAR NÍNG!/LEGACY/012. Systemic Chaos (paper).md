# Systemic Chaos: Emergent Unpredictability from Deterministic Concurrent Topologies

## Abstract

This paper presents a theoretical framework for understanding a class of chaotic behavior that arises not from mathematical idealizations nor from stochastic noise, but from the **topological structure of interdependent deterministic subsystems** executing concurrently. We term this phenomenon **systemic chaos**. Through analysis of the Seedy generator—a minimal implementation using three threads in cyclic dependency—we demonstrate that deterministic components, when coupled cyclically and executed asynchronously, can produce behavior that is effectively unpredictable, computationally irreducible, and statistically indistinguishable from true randomness. This framework reframes the generator not primarily as an entropy harvester, but as a proof-of-concept for **liquid topologies**: systems where the interaction topology itself generates emergent complexity. We situate systemic chaos within the broader taxonomy of chaotic systems, distinguish it from mathematical, stochastic, and computational chaos, and explore its philosophical implications for determinism and predictability. Finally, we propose a systematic research program to embed this topology directly into hardware—from ultra-low-cost microcontrollers to custom ASICs—to determine whether the phenomenon is intrinsic to the topology or dependent on specific architectural noise mechanisms. The ultimate goal is a sovereign, open-source family of entropy sources grounded in the principles of systemic chaos.

## 1. Introduction

The generation of unpredictable sequences from deterministic computational substrates has long occupied a curious position in both computer science and physics. True random number generators (TRNGs) harvest entropy from physical processes; pseudo-random number generators (PRNGs) expand small seeds using deterministic algorithms. The Seedy generator, originally conceived as a software-based entropy harvester, revealed something more fundamental: a minimal system of three concurrent threads, each executing a fixed deterministic function and arranged in a cyclic dependency, produces output that passes rigorous statistical tests for randomness—**without any explicit entropy source** beyond the physical substrate on which it runs.

This observation raises a profound question: Is the unpredictability a property of the *topology itself*, or merely an artifact of the specific noise mechanisms in a general-purpose CPU (OS scheduling, cache contention, thermal variations)? More broadly: **Can deterministic components, when coupled in certain ways, generate emergent unpredictability that is irreducible to their individual behaviors?**

This paper proposes that the answer is yes. We introduce the concept of **systemic chaos**—chaos that arises from the *system as a whole*: the concurrent interplay of deterministic threads, their cyclic interdependency, and the asynchronous timing of their execution. Unlike mathematical chaos, which exists in continuous equations and is deterministic in principle; unlike stochastic chaos, which injects external noise; and unlike computational chaos, which is an artifact of finite precision, systemic chaos is a property of **interaction topology**. It suggests that even in a fully deterministic universe, certain configurations of processes may be fundamentally unpredictable because their evolution is computationally irreducible and their initial conditions include microscopic variations that are inaccessible and inseparable from the system's operation.

The Seedy generator serves as our canonical example—a minimal system that instantiates systemic chaos. We will describe its operation, analyze its behavior through the lens of liquid topologies, and then outline a research program to embed this topology directly into hardware. By systematically varying the physical implementation—from bare-metal multicore microcontrollers to three independent single-chip systems to custom ASICs—we aim to isolate the essential conditions for systemic chaos and to develop a sovereign, open-source family of entropy sources for applications ranging from low-cost IoT devices to high-assurance cryptographic modules.

## 2. The Seedy Generator: A Minimal Systemic Chaos System

### 2.1 Architecture

The Seedy generator consists of three threads arranged in a cyclic dependency:

- Thread 0 reads from node 0 and writes to node 1
- Thread 1 reads from node 1 and writes to node 2
- Thread 2 reads from node 2 and writes to node 0

Each thread executes a fixed deterministic transformation, `seed_modify_64`, which performs bit rotations, accumulator mixing, and multiplication by large primes. The choice of prime depends on the least significant bit of the source value, introducing data-dependent nonlinearity. The function is applied repeatedly in an infinite loop:

```c
void seed_modify_64(uint64_t *in, uint64_t *out) {
    uint64_t acc = 1;
    for (int x = 0; x < 64; x++) {
        *in = (*in << 7) | (*in >> (64 - 7));
        acc = (acc << x) | (acc >> (64 - x));
        acc *= primes[(2 * x) + (1 & *in)];
        *out += acc ^ *in;
    }
    *out ^= acc;
}
```

Crucially, there is no synchronization between threads. They read and write shared memory concurrently, creating **race conditions**—situations where the outcome depends on the precise microsecond-scale timing of memory access. The global state is the XOR of the three node values, sampled at regular intervals.

### 2.2 What Seedy Is—and Is Not

Seedy is **not** primarily an entropy harvester, though it can serve that role. It is a **demonstration system** for a more fundamental principle: three deterministic components, coupled cyclically and executing asynchronously, produce behavior that is:

- **Effectively unpredictable**: Statistical test suites (PractRand) find no patterns in long output streams when run on suitable hardware.
- **Computationally irreducible**: There is no shortcut to predicting future states beyond simulating the system itself.
- **Topologically complex**: Trajectories through the joint state space explore regions rapidly and decorrelate quickly from initial conditions.

The qxo64 generator, mentioned elsewhere, is a separate tool—an entropy amplifier used for testing and verification. It is not part of the core theoretical claim. Seedy alone embodies the principle of systemic chaos.

### 2.3 The Role of the Physical Substrate

When Seedy runs on a modern multicore processor, three physical cores execute the three threads truly concurrently. The race conditions depend on:

- Clock jitter between cores (thermal noise in oscillators)
- Memory bus arbitration timing
- Cache line states and contention
- Power supply fluctuations
- Scheduler decisions (if an OS is present)

These are **physical variations**—not part of the algorithm's specification. However, even if we treat the substrate as deterministic but incomprehensibly complex, the system's behavior remains practically unpredictable. The key insight is that the topology **amplifies** these microscopic variations into macroscopic unpredictability. Without the cyclic coupling and concurrency, the same variations would produce only tiny, ignorable effects.

## 3. Liquid Topologies: A Theoretical Framework

### 3.1 The Liquid Topology Hypothesis

Consider a system composed of *N* deterministic subsystems, each executing a fixed transformation function *f* on its internal state. When these subsystems are arranged in a **cyclic dependency graph**—where each subsystem's output feeds into another's input—and executed **asynchronously**, the global state space can exhibit properties qualitatively different from any individual subsystem.

We hypothesize that such configurations can produce:

1. **Topological mixing**: Trajectories through the joint state space explore regions in ways that rapidly erase memory of initial conditions.
2. **Effective high dimensionality**: The interdependency creates coupling that distributes information across all subsystems.
3. **Computational irreducibility**: No shortcut exists for predicting future states beyond simulating the system itself.

The term *liquid* captures the fluid, non‑rigid nature of these trajectories—states flow through the configuration space in ways that resist simple decomposition into independent components. Unlike classical chaos, which is typically low-dimensional and described by equations, liquid topology is inherently high-dimensional and described by interaction structure.

### 3.2 Essential Ingredients

From observations of the Seedy generator and related experiments, we identify three essential ingredients for systemic chaos:

1. **Deterministic components**: Each subsystem must be deterministic given its inputs. Stochasticity is not required; unpredictability emerges from the interaction.
2. **Cyclic interdependency**: The dependency graph must contain cycles. Acyclic graphs (feed-forward) produce predictable compositions.
3. **Asynchronous execution**: Subsystems must not be perfectly synchronized. Small timing variations—whether from physical noise or independent clocks—provide the "slack" that allows trajectories to diverge.

The cyclic topology creates **mutual causation**: each subsystem's future depends on another's output, which itself depends on the first's prior modifications. This circular causality, absent in sequential systems, is the engine of emergent unpredictability.

### 3.3 Contrast with Existing Frameworks

| Property | Mathematical Chaos | Stochastic Chaos | Computational Chaos | **Systemic Chaos (Liquid Topology)** |
|----------|-------------------|------------------|---------------------|--------------------------------------|
| Substrate | Continuous equations | Equations + noise | Finite-precision arithmetic | **Discrete deterministic functions** |
| Coupling | Usually low-dimensional | Variable | None specified | **Cyclic interdependency** |
| Source of unpredictability | Sensitivity to initial conditions | Noise injection | Rounding error | **Topological mixing + asynchronous coupling** |
| Deterministic components? | Yes | Mixed | Yes | **Yes** |
| Role of concurrency | None | Optional | None | **Essential** |
| Reproducibility | In principle, yes | No | No | **No—computationally irreducible** |

Systemic chaos occupies a unique niche: unpredictability emerges not from mathematical properties alone, nor from external noise, but from the *interaction topology* of deterministic components executing asynchronously.

### 3.4 Empirical Markers

To operationalize this concept, we propose measurable indicators:

- **State-space coverage**: Over time, trajectories should visit a large fraction of the accessible state space. In Seedy, the three 64-bit nodes define a 2^192-dimensional space; entropy estimation suggests effective exploration.
- **Autocorrelation decay**: The output sequence should decorrelate rapidly. Seedy's sampling captures values that differ from previous readings, indicating short memory.
- **Sensitivity to scheduler variations**: Small changes in thread interleaving should produce divergent trajectories—observable by running identical binaries on differently loaded systems.
- **Computational irreducibility**: There should be no faster-than-simulation prediction method. The failure of statistical tests to find patterns is consistent with irreducibility.

## 4. A Research Program: Embedding Systemic Chaos in Hardware

The software implementation of Seedy demonstrated that systemic chaos exists, but it left fundamental questions unanswered:

- Is the chaotic behavior intrinsic to the topology, or does it depend on the specific noise mechanisms of a CPU (OS scheduling, cache contention)?
- What is the minimal hardware required to produce systemic chaos?
- Can we build practical entropy sources based on this principle that are verifiable, sovereign, and suitable for cryptographic applications?

To answer these questions, we propose a systematic empirical investigation using four complementary hardware platforms.

### 4.1 Experimental Platforms

**Platform 1: Bare-Metal Quad-Core Microcontroller.** Three physical cores will each run a thread executing the Seedy mixing function in an infinite loop, communicating via shared memory with no synchronization. The fourth core handles output sampling. Code runs bare-metal (no OS, no interrupts) to eliminate extraneous variability. This platform tests whether intrinsic silicon noise—clock jitter, thermal fluctuations, power supply variations—is sufficient when the topology is implemented in truly concurrent hardware.

**Platform 2: Three Independent Single-Core Microcontrollers.** Three physically separate chips (e.g., Attiny85, STM32F0) will be interconnected via GPIO pins using a simple asynchronous protocol. Each has its own clock source—crystal, RC oscillator, or external generator. We will test synchronous (same crystal), plesiochronous (same nominal frequency but independent), and asynchronous (different RC oscillators) modes. This platform explores the minimal hardware complexity: if chaos emerges from three $1 microcontrollers, ultra-low-cost TRNGs become possible. It also enables physical security through separate packages and isolated power supplies.

**Platform 3: FPGA-Based Amplifier.** While the amplifier is not the focus of the theoretical claim, it is a necessary tool for testing and verification. We will implement the qxo64 construction in an FPGA as a high-speed peripheral that can be seeded by any entropy source (including the microcontroller harvesters) and generate output at multiple gigabits per second. Double-buffered entropy pools will enable seamless operation across the 2^64-step period.

**Platform 4: Custom ASIC.** As the culmination of this research, we will design a custom ASIC integrating three (or more) chaotic cores in a cyclic topology, along with double-buffered entropy pools for the amplifier section. The cores will rely on intrinsic silicon noise amplified by the topology, with no dedicated entropy sources unless experiments prove them necessary. Tamper-resistance features (shields, glitch detectors) will be included for high-assurance applications. This chip will serve as a reference design for a sovereign, open-source, high-performance TRNG.

### 4.2 Key Experimental Parameters

Across all platforms, we will systematically vary:

- **Physical concurrency**: One chip (multicore) vs. three separate chips
- **Communication medium**: Shared memory vs. explicit message passing
- **Communication latency**: 0 to milliseconds (via software delays or wire length)
- **Clock synchronization**: Synchronous, plesiochronous, asynchronous
- **Coupling granularity**: Full 64-bit state exchange vs. partial (32-bit, 16-bit, 1-bit)

We hypothesize there exists a **minimum slack threshold**—a combination of latency and clock drift—below which chaos collapses. Characterizing this threshold will guide the design of minimal TRNGs.

### 4.3 Predictions

- On the quad-core microcontroller, intrinsic silicon noise will be sufficient to produce output that passes PractRand.
- On three independent microcontrollers, asynchronous drift between independent clocks will provide sufficient slack; the quality may exceed that of a single chip due to uncorrelated noise sources.
- The FPGA amplifier will preserve seed quality and generate output at I/O-limited speeds.
- The ASIC will achieve the highest performance and security, with double-buffering eliminating periodic artifacts.

### 4.4 Toward a Sovereign Entropy Source Stack

The research will produce an open-source family of designs:

| Design | Platform | Target Application |
|--------|----------|---------------------|
| **Ultra-Low-Cost TRNG** | Three 8-bit microcontrollers | IoT devices, smart cards |
| **High-Performance Embedded TRNG** | Quad-core microcontroller (bare-metal) | Cryptographic seeding, general embedded use |
| **FPGA-Based Amplifier** | FPGA (open-source RTL) | Servers, HPC, testing |
| **Commercial-Grade ASIC TRNG** | Custom silicon | High-assurance cryptography, critical infrastructure |

All designs will be released under open-source licenses, enabling independent verification, manufacture, and integration. This sovereignty is essential where trust in commercial TRNGs is insufficient.

## 5. Philosophical Implications: Rethinking Determinism

Systemic chaos invites reflection on foundational questions about determinism and predictability. Laplace's demon—the hypothetical intelligence that could predict the entire future given complete knowledge of initial conditions and forces—has long served as a touchstone for debates about determinism [14]. Classical physics seemed to license such a view; quantum mechanics introduced fundamental indeterminacy, but debate continues over whether this indeterminacy is ontological or merely epistemic.

Systemic chaos offers a third perspective: **even in a fully deterministic substrate, concurrent interdependent systems can produce behavior that is effectively unpredictable because the relevant initial conditions include microscopic physical variations that are inaccessible and irreducible**. The Seedy generator's output depends not only on the algorithm but on the exact microsecond timing of memory contention—events influenced by thermal noise, quantum fluctuations, and cosmic rays. These are not "hidden variables" in the classical sense; they are genuine physical variations that interact with deterministic computation.

Now consider the universe itself. If the underlying substrate operates in a manner analogous to a systemic chaotic system—with concurrent, interdependent processes unfolding across all scales—then a theoretical "replay" of the universe from identical initial conditions might not yield identical outcomes. The physical noise inherent in every interaction would be amplified by chaotic dynamics, leading to divergent trajectories. This is not merely a limitation of our measurement precision; it is a fundamental feature of systems where deterministic rules are continually perturbed by irreducibly random events.

Thus, Laplace's demon must be reconsidered. Even if one could know every law and every particle's state with infinite precision, the presence of concurrent, interdependent processes infused with physical randomness would render the future unpredictable in principle. The demon would need to simulate the universe itself, including all its random fluctuations—a task that is computationally irreducible and, more importantly, ontologically impossible because the randomness is not pre‑computable.

This aligns with Wolfram's concept of **computational irreducibility** [13]: many systems cannot be sped up by shortcuts. It suggests that the universe may be inherently creative, producing novelty that cannot be foretold even by an infinite intelligence.

Systemic chaos provides a concrete computational model illustrating how deterministic algorithms and physical randomness intertwine to produce genuine unpredictability. It invites us to take seriously the possibility that the universe's concurrent, interdependent fabric may be the source of its inexhaustible richness.

## 6. Conclusion

The Seedy generator demonstrates a fundamental principle: **deterministic components, coupled cyclically and executed asynchronously, can produce emergent unpredictability**. This phenomenon—systemic chaos—arises from the interaction topology itself, not from mathematical idealizations or external noise. It occupies a unique position in the taxonomy of chaotic systems, with concurrency and interdependency as essential ingredients.

Systemic chaos challenges the classical vision of a fully predictable universe. Even in a deterministic substrate, concurrent interdependent systems may be computationally irreducible and fundamentally unpredictable. The microscopic variations that seed this unpredictability are not mere measurement limitations but intrinsic features of physical implementation.

The proposed research program will systematically explore the boundaries of systemic chaos by embedding the Seedy topology directly into hardware. By varying the physical implementation—from ultra-low-cost microcontrollers to custom tamper-resistant ASICs—we will isolate the essential conditions for systemic chaos and develop a sovereign, open-source family of entropy sources. These designs will empower developers with verifiable, trustworthy randomness for applications ranging from IoT devices to critical infrastructure.

As processor architectures become increasingly parallel, the opportunities for systemic chaos expand. The challenge for future research is to quantify, model, and ultimately certify the security of these systems for cryptographic applications, while also exploring their potential as a lens through which to view the nature of reality itself.
