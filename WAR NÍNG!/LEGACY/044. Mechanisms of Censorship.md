## Mechanisms of Censorship

Social taboos function as powerful mechanisms of social control that, in democratic societies, often prove more effective than formal censorship because they become internalized by citizens. When individuals fear social marginalization more than state punishment, they preemptively silence themselves, creating a paradox where democracies with strong free speech protections nevertheless experience significant constraints on public discourse. This comprehensive analysis explores how social taboos create internalized censorship in democratic contexts, drawing on theoretical frameworks and empirical research.

### Conceptual Framework: Understanding Taboo and Internalized Censorship

#### The Nature of Taboos in Democratic Societies
Taboos represent the strongest form of negative social sanction, operating through moral condemnation rather than legal prohibition. Unlike authoritarian systems where state repression enforces silence, democracies rely on what scholars call "self-imposed" censorship driven by social fear .

As Kimball Young noted in his foundational social psychology work, taboos function as "guide posts in associative thinking which keep [individuals] within the boundaries set by the moral codes" . When individuals encounter ideas that conflict with sacred group values, they experience psychological inhibition—a form of internalized censorship that operates before any external sanction occurs.

#### The Distinction Between External and Internalized Censorship
| Aspect | External Censorship | Internalized Censorship (via Taboos) |
|--------|---------------------|--------------------------------------|
| Source of control | State institutions, legal sanctions | Social groups, moral communities, peer networks |
| Mechanism | Formal prohibition, punishment threat | Anticipatory self-silencing, shame avoidance |
| Visibility | Overt, observable | Hidden, often unconscious |
| Democratic paradox | Obviously undemocratic | Can coexist with formal freedoms |
| Primary fear | Legal consequences | Social marginalization, identity threat |

*Information derived from analysis of Ejdus  and Young *

### Mechanisms of Internalization: How Social Taboos Create Self-Censorship

#### 1. **Social Influence and Diffusion Processes**
Recent empirical research from Hong Kong demonstrates how self-censorship spreads through social networks via two primary mechanisms :

- **Direct Persuasion**: When individuals receive cautionary reminders from family members and peers about the risks of expressing certain views, they adjust their behavior accordingly
- **Observational Learning**: Seeing acquaintances engage in self-censorship normalizes silence and signals which topics are unsafe

Both mechanisms operate through mediating factors including **risk perception** and **emotional reactions** such as fear and anxiety. This challenges the notion that self-censorship is merely an individual decision—it is fundamentally a social phenomenon reproduced through interpersonal influence .

#### 2. **The Spiral of Silence**
The Council of Europe's research on academic freedom identifies the "spiral of silence" as a key dynamic in self-censorship :

> "Individuals tend to remain silent if they perceive their views to be in the minority or at odds with the majority. This reduces the visibility of minority positions, while reinforcing the dominance of majority views, creating a self-perpetuating dynamic." 

This mechanism is particularly powerful because it operates without any formal coordination. People silence themselves based on **perceptions** of what others believe, which may or may not reflect actual public opinion.

#### 3. **Internalization Through Identity and Affect**
Contemporary analysis suggests that identity politics has transformed how taboos function in democratic societies . When identity becomes "a property to be defended," it legitimizes silencing those perceived as trespassing on "sacred grounds." Affect becomes "the watchdog—not only sounding the alarm but attacking everyone who allegedly climbs over the fence."

This creates a psychological dynamic where:
- Individuals internalize group identity boundaries
- Expressing taboo views feels like betraying one's community
- Self-censorship becomes automatic and often unconscious

#### 4. **Motivational Categories for Self-Censorship**
Research identifies four broad categories of motivations that drive internalized censorship :

| Motivation Category | Description | Example in Democratic Contexts |
|--------------------|-------------|-------------------------------|
| Fear of consequences | Dismissal, reputational damage, social isolation | Avoiding controversial opinions at work |
| Belief in futility | Speaking out won't change anything | Not engaging in political discussions |
| Prosocial motivations | Protecting others, preserving unity | Silence to avoid conflict in diverse groups |
| Individual gains | Avoiding extra work, maximizing benefit | Staying quiet to maintain professional relationships |

### Empirical Documentation: Evidence from Democratic Contexts

#### Case Study 1: Hong Kong Under Democratic Backsliding
Research on pro-democracy protesters following the 2020 National Security Law provides compelling evidence of how social influence fosters self-censorship . The study found that:

- Respondents who received cautionary reminders from family were significantly more likely to self-censor
- Having more acquaintances who self-censored increased the likelihood of similar behavior
- These effects were partially mediated by heightened risk perception and emotional reactions

This demonstrates how, even in a context transitioning away from full democracy, **horizontal social pressure**—not just vertical state repression—drives self-censorship.

#### Case Study 2: Cancel Culture and Self-Censorship in the United States
Analysis of "cancel culture" reveals how social taboos operate in highly polarized democracies :

> "A recent study found that 40% of Americans engage in significant amounts of self-censorship. Although political intolerance has decreased, they found that people hinder their speech based on fear of alienation from close friends or colleagues." 

This represents a shift from macro-level suppression (government censorship) to **micro-environmental factors**—the fear of being "canceled" by coworkers or peers. The dynamic is reinforced by increased political polarization and social media amplification .

#### Case Study 3: Academic Self-Censorship in Europe
The Council of Europe's investigation into academic freedom reveals that self-censorship threatens democratic discourse even in stable European democracies . Key findings include:

- **Early-career scholars and non-cis men** are more likely to self-censor
- In Western countries, **heterodoxy** (holding views contrary to dominant consensus) increases self-censorship likelihood
- Loosely formulated prohibitions subject to **discretionary enforcement** increase self-censorship
- Self-censorship can become **unconscious** through internalization of prohibitions

#### Case Study 4: Art and Religious Taboos
The controversy over Jani Leinonen's sculpture "McJesus" (a crucified Ronald McDonald) in Israel illustrates how taboos create censorship pressure even where legal protections exist . Although Israel's attorney general determined the work was protected speech, local political pressure led to its removal after "consultations with church leaders" and concerns about "physical injury and violence."

This case demonstrates how **social mobilization around taboos** can achieve censorship outcomes without formal legal prohibition—the "imagined vox populi" replacing courts as arbiters of acceptable speech .

### Theoretical Explanations for Taboo-Induced Internalized Censorship

#### Power Relations and Hegemonic Control
Critical discourse analysis suggests that power blocs actively work to maintain taboos because taboo discourses possess "inherent capacity to disrupt and destabilise existing hegemonic orders" . By positioning certain views as taboo—in opposition to widely accepted cultural, ethnic, religious, and social doctrines—dominant groups persuade citizens to disengage from potentially destabilizing discussions.

#### Psychological Foundations
Young's early analysis remains relevant: censorship has a psychological counterpart in individual experience. "We all censor or pass judgment of good or bad, like or dislike, on what we see and hear. This censorship depends naturally upon our temperament and upon our previous social-cultural conditioning" . When social taboos are internalized, "we inhibit our associations on a dangerous subject. We do not like it. We cast it out of our mind."

#### The Public Sphere Perspective
From Habermasian theory, self-censorship degrades the public sphere essential for democratic functioning . When citizens cannot openly debate ideas and form public opinion, their ability to mediate government activities is impaired. This represents a form of "bottom-up" democratic erosion that can be as harmful as top-down government suppression.

### Conclusion: Democratic Implications

The use of social taboos to create internalized censorship in democracies represents a profound paradox. Societies that pride themselves on free speech protections may nevertheless experience significant discursive constraints because citizens internalize social fears more effectively than any formal censor could impose.

Key implications include:

1. **Invisibility challenge**: Self-censorship is "a hidden phenomenon, often indistinguishable from conformity, obedience, lack of opinion, or strategic silence" . This makes it difficult to measure and address.

2. **Democratic erosion**: When self-censorship becomes widespread, it undermines the public sphere essential for democratic accountability and informed citizenship .

3. **Reinforcing dynamics**: As more people self-censor, the perceived risks of speaking increase, creating self-perpetuating cycles of silence .

4. **Pluralism under threat**: Academic and intellectual diversity suffers when heterodox views are silenced, impoverishing both scholarly inquiry and public debate .

The evidence suggests that addressing internalized censorship requires not just legal protections for speech, but conscious efforts to create environments where diverse ideas can be expressed without fear of social retaliation—what the Council of Europe calls "procedural justice" and institutional cultures that value pluralism .

---

**💡 Key Takeaway**: Social taboos create internalized censorship in democracies by leveraging human sociality itself—our deep need for belonging and fear of exclusion becomes the mechanism that limits what can be said. The result is a form of control that requires no censors, only citizens who have learned to censor themselves.
