# A Slave and a Pet on a Farm

## Three Ways of Being Dependent

Think about the relationship between humans and animals for a moment. A **slave** is forced to work through violence and fear, valued only for what they produce. A **pet** is cared for and loved, but their life is completely managed by their owner. **Livestock** on a farm are fed and sheltered, but only because they provide something valuable—meat, milk, or eggs.

Now here's an uncomfortable question: **Where do modern consumers fit in this picture?**

Most people today depend completely on what we might call the "corporate infrastructure." Your phone, your food, your electricity, your internet—all come from massive systems that you didn't build and can't easily live without. If those systems failed, your life would change dramatically within days .

This dependence has led some thinkers to wonder: Is our economy more like a traditional farm than we'd like to admit? Let's explore what this means.


## The Difference Between a Slave and a Pet

Before applying these ideas to humans, it helps to understand what makes slavery different from pet-keeping.

Philosopher Heather Kendrick has explored this question in academic work. She argues that the key difference lies in something called **autonomy** .

**Autonomy** means the ability to make your own choices and shape your own life. A person has autonomy. A dog? Not really.

Here's how Kendrick explains it:

- **Preference liberty** is the freedom to get what you want. Your dog wants a treat, and you give it one. The dog has preference liberty.
- **Autonomous liberty** is the freedom to choose what you might want in the first place—to question your desires, change your mind, and chart your own course in life.

The core wrong of slavery is that it **destroys autonomous liberty**. A slave is treated as a tool, not a person with their own life to live .

A pet, however, never had autonomous liberty to begin with. Dogs don't dream of careers or wonder if they should move to a different city. So while pet-keeping can be done badly (neglect, abuse), the relationship itself isn't slavery .

This distinction matters because it helps us think about different kinds of dependence.


## The Modern Consumer—Not a Slave, But Maybe Something Else

Are modern consumers slaves? Not in the historical sense. No one owns you legally. You're not forced to work at gunpoint.

But there are uncomfortable similarities. Your labor is used to create wealth for others. The threat of poverty and homelessness pushes you to keep working. If you stop participating, you quickly discover how few options exist outside the system .

A more accurate comparison might be the **pet**.

Think about how the system treats you:

- **Your basic needs are provided**—as long as you play by the rules. Food, shelter, entertainment, healthcare—all available through corporate channels.
- **Your choices are managed, not eliminated**. You can pick any brand of phone, any streaming service, any career path—as long as it fits within the existing system.
- **Your identity is shaped for you**. Companies spend billions telling you who you should be: the eco-conscious consumer, the tech enthusiast, the fitness guru. These identities come with products attached.
- **Leaving is nearly impossible**. Try living completely off-grid, without a job, without money, without corporate services. The barriers are enormous .

As one observer noted about modern India's economy, consumers are increasingly "locked into corporate interfaces that resemble those of a passport office more than a marketplace." When switching costs become too high, "loyalty is indistinguishable from captivity" .

This isn't slavery. It's more like being a well-cared-for pet—dependent, managed, and shaped by forces much larger than yourself.


## The Farm Economy—A Disturbing but Useful Metaphor

Now we come to the farm. A traditional farm has a clear structure:

- **The farmer** builds and maintains the system
- **The livestock** are fed, sheltered, and kept healthy
- **But the purpose** is production—milk, eggs, meat, wool

When you look at modern economic life through this lens, some patterns emerge.

**We are the livestock.**

This is uncomfortable to think about, but consider:

- **We are fed and sheltered** through jobs, wages, and social services. A healthy worker is a productive worker.
- **We are managed in groups**. Demographics (age, income, education) determine which "pen" you live in—which neighborhood, which schools, which opportunities.
- **We are cultivated for products**. Your labor is one product. But increasingly, your **data** is another. Every click, purchase, and scroll is harvested to make the system more efficient .
- **We are kept content**. Entertainment, social media, sports, shopping—these keep the herd calm and productive. An agitated herd is bad for business.

**The "farmers" are the corporation-state system.**

This isn't a conspiracy. It's an emergent system—the result of millions of decisions over decades. Massive corporations and governments build and maintain the fences (laws, regulations, infrastructure), provide the feed (jobs, wages, benefits), and manage the breeding (education, family policies) .

**The "slaughterhouse" is the edge of the safety net.**

The farm metaphor holds because not all livestock make it. Unemployment, bankruptcy, homelessness, addiction—these are where the system's care stops and its brutal logic becomes visible. You were valuable as long as you produced. When you stop producing, the system may discard you .


## What Does This Mean for Understanding Economics?

Economists and philosophers have long used metaphors to explain complex systems. One blog post exploring this topic told a simple story:

> Two farmers—one grows wheat, one raises cows. They trade with each other. One day, the dairy farmer decides to buy less wheat. The wheat farmer's income drops, so he buys less milk. Then the dairy farmer's income drops, so she buys even less wheat. Around and around it goes until both families have nothing .

This story illustrates a basic economic truth: **spending is income**. Your spending is someone else's paycheck, and their spending is yours.

Now apply the farm metaphor to this story. The farmers aren't independent—they're part of a larger system. Their choices are shaped by forces beyond their control. The same is true for modern consumers .

Another thinker, Yanis Varoufakis, has suggested that new technology is creating an economic model that pushes costs down to the "farmers"—the ordinary people actually producing value—while profits flow upward to those who control the infrastructure .


## The Psychology of Dependence

Beyond economics, there's a psychological dimension to all this.

When every option in the store comes in plastic packaging, your choice to buy plastic isn't really a choice—it's a **concession to the system** .

When every phone is designed to be unrepairable, your choice to buy a new one isn't really a choice—it's the **path of least resistance**.

Psychologists call this **status quo bias**—our preference for the current state of affairs. The system reinforces this bias constantly. The easiest option is the system-approved option. Choosing differently requires mental energy, research, and often extra cost .

Over time, this creates **decision fatigue**. You stop fighting the system because fighting is exhausting. You accept what's available. You become, in effect, domesticated.


## What Do We Do With This Idea?

None of this means we're literally slaves, pets, or livestock. Those are metaphors—tools for thinking, not literal descriptions.

But metaphors matter. They shape how we understand the world .

The farm metaphor helps reveal something real about modern life: **we are deeply dependent on systems we didn't create and can't easily change**. Those systems provide for us, but they also constrain us. They shape our choices, our identities, and our possibilities.

Understanding this dependence is the first step toward thinking clearly about it. If we're livestock, can we become something more? Can we build fences of our own? Can we create spaces of genuine autonomy within the larger system?

These aren't easy questions. But they're worth asking.
