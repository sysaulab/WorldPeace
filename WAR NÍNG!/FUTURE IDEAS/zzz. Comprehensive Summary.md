## Part One: Seeing the System

### 1.1 Feedback Loops as the Basic Unit of Systemic Morality

A system is moral to the extent that those who make decisions experience the consequences of those decisions. This is not a normative claim but a cybernetic one: learning, adaptation, and correction depend on feedback. When feedback loops are intact, systems self-regulate. When they are broken, pathology accumulates .

The concept of feedback is foundational to systems thinking. In first-order cybernetics, feedback refers to the circular causality by which outputs of a system are fed back as inputs, enabling self-correction. A thermostat monitors room temperature and adjusts heating accordingly. A governor regulates engine speed by sensing deviations. These are simple, mechanical feedback loops .

But second-order cybernetics—neocybernetics—deepens our understanding. It recognizes that living systems are not merely feedback loops but **self-referential systems** that maintain their own organization through continuous interaction with their environment. As Francisco Varela noted, "Unless you confront the mutualness, the closure, of a system, you just lose the system. It is the simultaneity of interactions that gives whole systems the flavor of being what they are" .

This principle of feedback operates at every scale of organization:

**Cellular level.** Cells that malfunction without receiving signals of their malfunction become cancerous. They proliferate uncontrollably, consuming resources, damaging surrounding tissue, and eventually threatening the entire organism. The cancer is not evil; it is a cell whose feedback loops have been severed.

**Individual level.** The psychopath is characterized by an inability to learn from punishment, to feel guilt, to experience empathy. Their feedback loops are broken. They can harm others without suffering the emotional consequences that normally inhibit such harm. This is not a moral failing in the conventional sense but a systemic pathology—a broken feedback mechanism.

**Societal level.** When elites can make decisions that affect millions without experiencing the negative consequences, they become societal cancers. They continue behaviors that devastate communities because they receive no signal that those behaviors are harmful. Their bonuses, their promotions, their prestige—all positive feedback—drown out any negative signal from elsewhere.

**Civilizational level.** When a civilization's mode of production generates ecological destruction without immediate feedback to decision-makers, it continues until collapse. Climate change is the ultimate broken feedback loop: those who benefit from fossil fuels do not experience the consequences of their use, which are distributed across space (to the Global South) and time (to future generations).

From this perspective, systemic morality is not about individual virtue but about feedback architecture. A moral system is one in which feedback loops are intact—in which actions and consequences are connected, in which decision-makers cannot escape the results of their choices. An immoral system is one in which feedback is broken, enabling exploitation without consequence.

### 1.2 Visualizing Socioeconomic Space: The Field and the Membrane

To analyze complex social systems, we need tools that make abstract relationships visible. Imagine a **two-dimensional field** representing social space. At every point on this field, there is a vector with two properties:

**Amplitude** represents economic intensity—wealth, resources, productive capacity. The length of the vector corresponds to the economic resources available at that point. High amplitudes represent concentrations of wealth; low amplitudes represent poverty.

**Angle** represents social characteristics—values, cultural orientations, ways of organizing life. A vector pointing at 0 degrees might represent competitive individualism; at 90 degrees, relational collectivism; at 180 degrees, extractive authoritarianism; at 270 degrees, ecological stewardship. The angle encodes the qualitative dimension of social life that economic measures miss.

When we visualize social space filled with such vectors, we can project onto a **topography**—mountains where amplitudes are high, valleys where they are low. The **gradient** between points represents the slope of socioeconomic difference. Gentle gradients mean gradual transitions; steep gradients (cliffs) mean abrupt discontinuities.

Now imagine a **membrane** stretched over this topography. This membrane represents the social fabric—the network of relationships, communications, and feedback loops that connect different points in social space. Where the topography is gently rolling, the membrane lies smoothly, transmitting signals in all directions. Information flows. Feedback circulates. Adjustments are possible.

But where the gradient becomes a cliff—where the distance between high and low becomes unbridgeable—the membrane is stretched thin. It becomes taut, under tension. Signals that might carry information about suffering, need, or injustice cannot propagate up the vertical face. Those on the high plateau receive no information about conditions below. Those in the valley cannot make their voices heard above.

This is the **topography of broken feedback**. The cliff severs connection. The membrane tears. Social fabric ruptures.

### 1.3 Gradients, Dynamics, and the Habitat 25 Lesson

The Calhoun rodent experiments (Habitat 25) provide a cautionary tale. In a carefully designed "utopia" for rats—abundant food, no predators, controlled environment—population initially thrived. But as density increased, social organization broke down. Males became aggressive or withdrawn. Females ceased caring for young. Reproduction collapsed. The population went extinct despite perfect material conditions .

Calhoun's interpretation has been debated, but one reading is crucial for our purposes: **the experiment eliminated gradients**. With no scarcity, no competition, no challenge, the social dynamics that maintain cohesion atrophied. The rats lost the capacity to organize themselves because there was nothing to organize around.

This suggests that gradients are not inherently bad. Gentle slopes—differences that create tension without rupture—may be necessary for social dynamics. The problem is not difference but cliffs. When the gradient becomes too steep, when the distance between high and low becomes unbridgeable, feedback is severed. Those at the top cannot see those at the bottom; those at the bottom cannot reach those at the top.

**Trickle-down economics** is the claim that benefits from the top will eventually reach the bottom—that gradients are channels of flow. But when gradients become cliffs, nothing trickles. Wealth accumulates at the high plateau while the valley below starves. The membrane ruptures. Social fabric tears.

### 1.4 Complexity as Cognitive Distance

Complexity creates distance—not physical but cognitive. As systems become more complex, the relationship between actions and consequences becomes harder to trace. This enables a specific form of social control: **the management of cognitive distance**.

When a system is simple and transparent, those affected by decisions can easily identify who made them and hold them accountable. As complexity increases, accountability diffuses. Decisions are made by committees, implemented through chains of command, filtered through layers of bureaucracy. Responsibility becomes impossible to assign.

This cognitive distance is not accidental. It is actively cultivated by those who benefit from broken feedback. Complexity becomes a shield: "It's not my fault; I was just following procedure." "The market made me do it." "These are complex systems; unintended consequences are inevitable."

Propaganda operates by managing cognitive distance. Its function is not primarily to convince people of falsehoods but to make it impossible for them to trace connections. Propaganda creates fog, obscuring the pathways between actions and consequences .

In contemporary neoliberal societies, propaganda takes the form of complexity itself. We are told that economic systems are too complex to understand, that global markets are too intricate to regulate, that the connections between corporate decisions and human suffering are too mediated to trace. This is not a description of reality but a technique of power—a way of maintaining broken feedback by convincing people that feedback is impossible.

### 1.5 The Cliff as Information Barrier

Return to the topographical model. When the gradient becomes a cliff, information cannot flow upward. The signals carried in the waves at the surface—the suffering, the need, the injustice—cannot propagate across the vertical face. Those on the high plateau receive no information about conditions below.

Research on deaths of despair in deindustrialized communities shows that those making decisions that devastate these communities—corporate executives, policy makers, financial speculators—have no idea what they have done. They do not see the overdoses, the suicides, the destroyed families. The information does not reach them. The cliff is too steep.

This is the topography of neoliberalism: extreme gradients, broken feedback, and a membrane stretched to the breaking point.

## Part Two: Naked Neoliberalism—The System Laid Bare

### 2.1 Neoliberalism as Political Theology

Neoliberalism is typically understood as a set of economic policies: privatization, deregulation, liberalization, and fiscal austerity. This understanding, while not incorrect, is radically incomplete. Neoliberalism is better understood as a comprehensive moral order—a political theology that distributes not merely resources but moral standing, not only opportunities but salvation.

The triumph of neoliberal globalization has been presented as the end of history, with no viable alternatives available. This is not merely a political claim but a theological one. It asserts that the market represents the final and highest form of human organization, beyond which no improvement is possible. Those who question this orthodoxy are not merely mistaken; they are heretics.

The theological character of neoliberalism manifests in several dimensions:

**Axiomatic truths** treated as beyond question: that markets are efficient, that competition improves outcomes, that self-interest is the fundamental human motive. These are not hypotheses to be tested but truths to be assumed—the equivalent of sacred scripture.

**Resistance to falsification**: when market policies produce crises—financial collapses, environmental destruction, mass despair—the response is not to question the axioms but to double down. More markets, more competition, more self-interest. The failure is never the system's; it is always the result of insufficient application.

**Moralization of outcomes**: success is evidence of virtue, failure evidence of vice. The wealthy deserve their wealth because they have provided value; the poor deserve their poverty because they have failed to do so. This is not presented as a normative claim but as a description of how things work—as natural and inevitable as gravity.

**Insulation from evidence**: the system generates its own evidence. Poverty proves unfitness; wealth proves worth. The circularity is perfect and impenetrable.

In this theological framework, money functions as the deity—the ultimate measure of value, the source of salvation, the sign of divine favor. It commands sacrifice: people spend the majority of their waking hours in its service, uproot their lives, separate from loved ones, endure soul-destroying labor—all for money. It distributes moral standing: the wealthy are treated as wiser, more deserving, more credible. It promises salvation: accumulate enough, and you will be secure, free, transcendent.

### 2.2 The Two Humans

The work of Brazilian anthropologist Eduardo Viveiros de Castro helps us understand what is at stake in this theological framework. Viveiros de Castro's "ontological turn" in anthropology challenges the assumption that all humans share a single underlying reality onto which different cultures impose different interpretations. Instead, he argues, different peoples may actually inhabit **different ontologies**—different understandings of what exists and what it means to exist .

This insight is crucial for understanding the conflict between neoliberalism and indigenous worldviews. They are not different interpretations of the same reality. They are different realities altogether.

The first way of being human—call it *Homo Economicus*, the man of money—sees the world as a collection of resources to be owned and exploited. The land exists to serve his needs. Other humans exist as competitors or collaborators in his projects. Value is measured by price. Success is measured by accumulation. Freedom means the absence of constraint on his choices.

The second way of being human—call it *Homo Indigenus*, the people of the land—sees the world as a web of relationships to be honored and sustained. Humans belong to the land; the land does not belong to them. Other humans are relatives, bound by obligations that precede any choice. Value is measured by contribution to community flourishing. Success is measured by generosity. Freedom means the capacity to fulfill one's responsibilities to the whole .

These are ideal types, not rigid categories. No society has ever been purely one or the other. Indigenous societies have had their own hierarchies and exclusions. Market societies have produced their own forms of generosity and care. But the poles are real, and the tension between them has shaped human history.

For the past five hundred years—a period some have called the Great Perturbation—the first way has been systematically expanding at the expense of the second. Colonialism tore Indigenous peoples from their lands and suppressed their ways of knowing. Industrial capitalism replaced craft and community with wage labor and mass production. Neoliberal globalization extended market logic into every domain of life, treating education, healthcare, and even human relationships as opportunities for profit.

The result is a world dominated by *Homo Economicus*. His values—competition, accumulation, efficiency—have become common sense, the unquestioned background against which all other questions are asked. His institutions—corporations, markets, bureaucracies—shape the lives of billions. His god—money—is worshipped in temples called banks and stock exchanges.

### 2.3 The Unease That Will Not Go Away

Yet even those who have benefited most from this order feel an unease they cannot name. The executive who has accumulated wealth beyond any possible need wonders why he feels so empty. The professional who has climbed every ladder wonders why the view from the top is so disappointing. The parent who has given her children every material advantage wonders why they seem so anxious, so disconnected, so lost.

This unease is not a personal failing. It is the signal that something fundamental is missing. *Homo Economicus* has mastered the art of getting what he wants, but he has forgotten how to want what is worth having. He has optimized the means of life while losing sight of its ends.

The unease is also a signal that the system itself is approaching its limits. A civilization organized around endless growth cannot survive on a finite planet. A society organized around competition cannot maintain the cooperation that all complex systems require. An economy organized around extraction cannot sustain the ecosystems on which all life depends.

The question is not whether this order will change. It will. The only question is whether the change will be chosen or imposed—whether we will navigate the transition consciously or suffer it catastrophically.

## Part Three: The Quebec Model—An Engine of Sovereignty

### 3.1 The Quiet Revolution and Institutional Development

The experience of Quebec in the latter half of the twentieth century offers a concrete illustration of how control over developmental tools can enable a culturally distinct society to thrive while remaining within a larger federation. Following the Quiet Revolution of the 1960s and the rise of the sovereignty movement, Quebec acquired and developed institutional capacity that enabled it to chart an autonomous course while remaining formally within the Canadian federation.

Central to this capacity was the **Banque Nationale**. Originally founded in 1859 as Banque Nationale and later merged with other institutions, the bank became a crucial instrument of Quebec economic development. It provided capital to Quebec businesses, supported the growth of the province's financial sector, and created a pool of expertise and resources accountable to Quebec interests rather than to Toronto-based financial power.

The Banque Nationale functioned as an **engine of sovereignty**—an institution that generates wealth and economic capacity while remaining accountable to the community it serves. It enabled Quebec to pursue its own developmental priorities without having to justify them to external decision-makers who might not share Quebec values or understand Quebec needs.

### 3.2 How the Engine Worked

The Banque Nationale functioned as an engine of sovereignty in several ways:

**Capital allocation.** The bank could direct capital toward Quebec businesses and projects that might be overlooked by Toronto-based institutions. This included support for small and medium enterprises, cultural industries, and regional development.

**Expertise development.** The bank created a pool of financial expertise within Quebec. It trained generations of Quebec bankers, financiers, and business leaders who could then contribute to the broader economy.

**Institutional density.** The bank contributed to the development of a dense network of Quebec institutions—law firms, accounting practices, consulting firms, business associations—that collectively constituted an indigenous economic infrastructure.

**Psychological impact.** Perhaps most importantly, the bank demonstrated that Quebec could manage its own affairs. It provided concrete evidence that sovereignty was possible, that Quebecers could run complex institutions, that the province need not depend on Toronto for financial services.

Quebec subsequently thrived. The province developed a dynamic economy, a robust social welfare system, and a distinctive cultural identity that flourishes within North America. Yet this success rested on a crucial foundation: **ideological compatibility** with the surrounding society. Quebec's aspirations, while culturally and linguistically distinct, operated within the same fundamental framework of liberal democratic capitalism that characterizes the rest of Canada and North America. The disagreement was about which linguistic and cultural community would control institutions, not about the nature of those institutions themselves.

### 3.3 The Limits of the Model

The Banque Nationale model had inherent limits. The bank operated within the same capitalist framework as other financial institutions. It sought profit, competed in markets, and answered to shareholders. Its lending decisions, while influenced by Quebec priorities, ultimately had to meet commercial criteria.

This was not a failure but a reflection of compatibility. Quebec's sovereignty movement sought control over institutions, not transformation of them. The goal was to have Quebecers running the same kinds of institutions that English Canadians ran—banks, corporations, universities, hospitals—not to create fundamentally different kinds of institutions.

This compatibility enabled Quebec to thrive within the Canadian federation. The province could pursue its distinctive path while remaining integrated into North American economic structures. It could maintain cultural distinctiveness while sharing fundamental values with its neighbors.

### 3.4 What Quebec Teaches Us

The Quebec experience offers valuable lessons about the importance of institutional tools for autonomous development. It demonstrates that control over financial institutions can enable a community to thrive while maintaining cultural distinctiveness. It shows that sovereignty does not require complete political independence; partial sovereignty through institutional control can be sufficient.

But these lessons must be applied with careful attention to context. The Quebec model worked because of ideological compatibility. It cannot be simply transferred to contexts where such compatibility does not exist.

For communities with foundational incompatibility—those whose values cannot be realized within dominant institutions—the Quebec model offers inspiration but not blueprint. They need not access to dominant institutions but the sovereign space to create alternative institutions.

## Part Four: The Energy Basis of Value

### 4.1 The Fundamental Currency

In the extractive economy, the ultimate measure of value is **price**—what someone is willing to pay. Price is a useful signal in some contexts, but it is also profoundly distorted. It reflects scarcity, not abundance. It reflects marketing, not meaning. It reflects the distribution of purchasing power, not the distribution of need.

In a living economy, a more fundamental measure suggests itself: **energy**.

Everything that sustains life requires energy. The food we eat embodies the energy of sunlight captured by plants. The homes we inhabit embody the energy of materials extracted and transformed. The care we give embodies the energy of attention and presence. Energy is the universal currency of living systems.

The work of ecological economists has increasingly recognized this fundamental truth. As Mayumi and Tanikawa note, building on the pioneering work of Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen, any serious approach to sustainability must grapple with the biophysical foundations of economic activity. Energy and material flows are not external to the economy; they are its substrate .

### 4.2 Energy, Prices, and Opportunity Cost

Recent research has begun to formalize the relationship between energy and prices. A 2018 model demonstrates that the observed proportionality between nominal prices and average embodied energies can be explained within a theoretical framework that places energy transfers as the focal point of scarcity. The reasoning is straightforward: (1) goods are material rearrangements, and (2) humans can only rearrange matter with energy transfers .

The opportunity cost of goods is given by their marginal energy transfers, which depend on both subjective factors (consumer preferences) and objective factors (direct energy transfers required for production). Under perfect competition, nominal prices arise as social manifestations of goods' marginal energy transfers. The proportionality between nominal prices and average embodied energy follows from the relation between average and marginal energy transfers .

This is not to say that energy is the *only* factor in value, or that prices can be reduced to energy calculations. But it does suggest that energy is the biophysical foundation upon which all other value relations are built.

### 4.3 The Incentive to Develop Neighbors

Here is where the dynamics become revolutionary. In a money economy, your neighbor's poverty can be your opportunity. You can buy their assets cheaply. You can hire them for low wages. You can profit from their desperation.

In an energy economy, your neighbor's efficiency becomes your advantage.

Consider trade between two communities. When Community A exchanges goods with Community B, what is actually being exchanged is the **embodied energy** required to produce those goods. If Community B is inefficient—if they waste energy, if their production methods are primitive—then the goods they send require more energy to produce. Community A receives less value for the energy they expend.

But if Community B becomes more efficient—if they develop better technology, better practices, better organization—then the goods they send require less energy to produce. Community A receives more value for the same energy expenditure. Everyone benefits.

This creates a completely new incentive structure. Rather than hoping your neighbors remain weak so you can exploit them, you now have a direct stake in their flourishing. Their development is your development. Their efficiency increases the value of everything they send you. Their prosperity makes you richer in the only currency that ultimately matters: the energy available for life.

### 4.4 The Dynamics of Energy Exchange

This principle ripples outward in unexpected ways:

**Cooperation becomes rational.** When your trading partner's efficiency benefits you, you have every reason to share knowledge, technology, and resources. The old zero-sum logic—your gain is my loss—gives way to positive-sum collaboration.

**Development becomes mutual.** Rather than extracting resources from less developed regions, energy-rich communities have an incentive to invest in their neighbors' capacity. A solar panel array installed in a neighboring community increases that community's productive capacity, which increases the value of everything they send in return.

**Waste becomes visible.** Energy inefficiency is no longer a hidden cost borne by the environment. It is a direct drain on the value of every exchange. The incentive to reduce waste, to improve processes, to innovate—all of this becomes structural rather than moral.

**Scale finds its proper place.** Very large operations may achieve energy efficiencies through economies of scale. Very small operations may achieve energy efficiencies through proximity and relationship. Neither is inherently superior; both can coexist, and the energy metric allows comparison without ideological prejudice.

**Feedback becomes immediate.** When energy is the currency, the consequences of inefficiency are direct and unavoidable. A community that wastes energy receives less in exchange for its goods. The feedback loop is intact.

### 4.5 The Deeper Implication

At its deepest level, an energy basis for value aligns the human economy with the larger economy of life. The sun pours energy into the biosphere. Plants capture it. Animals consume plants. Decomposers recycle what remains. The entire system runs on energy, and it has run for billions of years without a central bank or a stock exchange.

An energy economy is not a return to primitivism. It is a recognition that the most sophisticated system we know—the living world—has been using energy as its fundamental currency since the beginning. We are not inventing something new. We are aligning ourselves with something very old.

And in doing so, we create a world where helping our neighbors is not a moral choice but a rational one. Where development is mutual by design. Where the energy we save through efficiency benefits everyone, not just the shareholders of the corporation that owns the patent.

This is the economy of belonging.

## Part Five: The Potlatch—Generosity as Economic Principle

### 5.1 The Gift That Never Ended

Among the Indigenous peoples of the Northwest Coast, there was a tradition that the colonizers never understood. They called it the **potlatch**, and they banned it in 1885 as wasteful, irrational, and contrary to civilized values.

What the colonizers could not see was that the potlatch was not primitive exchange but sophisticated economics. It was a system for circulating wealth, maintaining relationships, and distributing status based not on accumulation but on generosity. The person who gave away the most gained the most prestige. The person who received was obligated not as a subordinate but as a participant in an ongoing relationship of reciprocal exchange .

The potlatch was banned, but it never died. It went underground, waiting for a time when its wisdom would be recognized. That time has come.

### 5.2 Mauss and the Gift Relationship

The foundational anthropological work on gift economies comes from Marcel Mauss, whose 1925 essay *The Gift* analyzed gift exchange across multiple cultures and identified patterns that challenged Western economic assumptions. Mauss argued that gifts are widely obligatory and reciprocal. This behavior could not be explained in terms of the "rational economic man" model espoused in much Western formal economic thought .

Mauss asserted that the economic is inseparable from other social fields. In every society, economic relations are infused with values and moral relations, and it is misguided to separate the rational and the non-rational—or affection and self-interest. He came to the conclusion that economic value has a religious origin—a perspective that resonates deeply with our analysis of neoliberalism as political theology .

The utilitarian values of things are renounced in order to gain social status. Ceremonial values transcend economic values. Among the Kwakiutl of the American Pacific Northwest, conspicuous ritual gift-giving reached epic proportions in the potlatch, where vast quantities of possessions would be destroyed or given away. In these societies, the gift relationship is a major political institution .

### 5.3 Symbolic Capital and the Potlatch

The concept of **symbolic capital**, developed by Pierre Bourdieu and applied to gift economies by subsequent scholars, helps explain the potlatch's logic. Status is best understood in terms of symbolic capital—both the reputation in the eyes of the community and the credit in relation to the recipient, which the giver accrues by giving away goods. By exercising the capacity to give away material worth, the giver converts material capital into the symbolic capital of honorable generosity .

This is not irrational. It is a different rationality—one that recognizes that social and economic values are entirely interconvertible. If certain material goods have a social or "prestige" value that confers symbolic capital on both the donor and the recipient, then this credit can be converted back into a narrowly conceived economic value .

The potlatch is a machine for generating and circulating symbolic capital. It creates relationships of obligation that bind the community together. It ensures that wealth circulates rather than accumulates. It makes generosity the path to status.

### 5.4 The Three-Thirds Model

Imagine an economy organized on potlatch principles. Within a community cell—say, an urban neighborhood, an Indigenous nation, or a cooperative network—the distributable surplus is divided each year into three equal parts:

**First Third: The Universal Dividend.**
Every member of the community receives this, unconditionally. You are here. You belong. You receive. This is the floor of dignity—the guarantee that no one falls below a basic standard of well-being.

**Second Third: The Labor Dividend.**
Distributed equally among all hours worked in recognized roles of contribution. A carpenter and a community babysitter are valued by the hour, not by the market's distorted wages. A teacher and a fisherman are compensated for their time, not for their bargaining power. Contribution is honored without creating vast inequality.

**Third Third: The Gift-to-Spend Fund.**
This money arrives in your account, but you cannot spend it. The only way to unlock it is to give it away to another member of the community. Once given, it becomes spendable for the recipient.

This is the potlatch mechanism made systematic. To have spending power beyond your basic dividend and labor compensation, you must be generous. To be wealthy in the community, you must be seen as a source of gifts. Status flows from what you circulate, not from what you hoard.

The effects are profound:

- Care work, which the market systematically undervalues, becomes visible and rewarded. The person who spends hours helping a neighbor, the one who checks on the elderly, the one who mentors youth—these contributions are recognized in the only way that matters: materially.

- Isolation decreases. To receive gifts, people must be known. To give gifts, people must pay attention. The community becomes visible to itself.

- Exploitation becomes difficult. Someone who tries to extract without contributing will receive fewer gifts. The feedback is continuous and inescapable.

- Greed has no space to grow. Accumulation beyond a certain point serves no purpose, because status comes from giving, not keeping.

### 5.5 Making the Circle Visible

A simple tool on your phone could make this economy visible. An app shows you three wallets: your Universal Dividend account, your Labor Dividend account, and your Gift-to-Spend account. It shows you your **Community Multiplier**—how much of your spending stays local, circulating through the community rather than draining away. It shows you who around you might need a gift, or whose work you admire.

When you spend at the local café, the app shows you: that dollar is still here. It will become the baker's wage, the babysitter's fee, the new bench in the park. When you spend at the big box store, the app shows you: that dollar is gone. It left and will never return.

This creates a natural incentive. You do not need laws. You do not need guilt. You just need to see. And when you see, you choose the circle.

## Part Six: The Place That Is Already Becoming—Vancouver's Downtown Eastside

### 6.1 A Perfect Laboratory

Vancouver's Downtown Eastside (DTES) is not merely a site of suffering; it is a perfect laboratory for testing the hypotheses generated by this analysis. The neighborhood exhibits every pathology our framework predicts:

**Extreme gradient.** The DTES sits adjacent to Vancouver's wealthiest districts. The cliff between the two could not be steeper. Those in luxury condos overlook but do not see the suffering below.

**Broken feedback.** Decisions made in corporate boardrooms, government offices, and financial institutions affect DTES residents daily. Those making these decisions never experience their consequences. The feedback loop is completely severed.

**Cognitive distance.** The complexity of urban policy, housing markets, and social services creates fog that obscures accountability. No one can trace the line from a particular decision to a particular death.

**Discardable population.** DTES residents are treated as waste—problems to be managed, contained, and if possible, removed. The leaked city memorandum proposing gentrification made this explicit: move people out, develop the land, solve the "problem."

Yet the DTES is also a place of remarkable resilience, mutual aid, and community. It contains the seeds of an alternative.

### 6.2 The DTES Community Land Trust

In 2020, a coalition of neighborhood-serving organizations established the **DTES Community Land Trust (DTES CLT)** , with support from the National Housing Strategy Demonstrations Initiative. The land trust's central objective is to shift privately owned properties into community ownership, removing housing from the speculative market and stewarding it as long-term community infrastructure .

The project uses a **decolonized governance model** and is co-led by Indigenous peoples and people working first-hand with DTES residents. Its services are designed and implemented by people with lived experience. The tenant-led programming empowers residents, encourages community building, and creates a culture that is unique to each building .

This is not charity. It is the transfer of developmental tools. The land trust holds land in perpetuity for community benefit, removing it from the market and protecting it from speculation. Ground rent from CLT properties becomes a source of community surplus that can be directed according to community priorities .

### 6.3 Recent Developments

Construction is now underway on a major Indigenous-led development at 405 Jackson Avenue in the DTES, which will bring 172 affordable rental homes to the community through the Aboriginal Land Trust Society. The building is named Ch'ich'iyúy Elx̱wíḵn, after the mountain peaks on the North Shore that watch over the traditional territory of the Musqueam, Squamish, and Tsleil-Waututh Peoples .

The project represents a combined investment of more than $133 million from federal, provincial, and municipal governments, along with community partners. The building will have a mix of studio, one-, and two-bedroom units for elders, families, and youth, with priority access given to Indigenous residents. Unit amenities include three-piece bathrooms, in-suite laundry, balconies, and bike storage. Outdoor amenities include shared spaces like fire pits and a Coast Salish Longhouse on the rooftop, raised garden beds, a wellness deck, play area, and a sky garden .

All of the units will be offered at affordable or below market rates, and 56 units will be offered to Indigenous Elders at deeply affordable income assistance rates. The building will be owned by the Aboriginal Land Trust and operated by Lu'ma Native Housing Society .

This is the infrastructure of belonging: land removed from speculation, housing provided as a right, culture honored in design, community supported in practice.

### 6.4 What the DTES Could Become

The DTES contains the resources for a coherent local economy:

- A community land trust holding land for community benefit
- A dense network of Indigenous organizations and mutual aid networks
- A concentration of residents with deep local knowledge and lived experience
- Relationships with funders, researchers, and sympathetic institutions
- A growing inventory of community-owned housing

Imagine what becomes possible when these resources are woven together differently:

**The land trust** provides the foundation—land removed from speculation, held for community benefit. Ground rent becomes a source of community surplus that can be directed according to community priorities.

**A community development bank** captures local savings and directs them toward local needs. Rather than leaking out to mainstream financial institutions, capital circulates within the community, generating additional value with each turn.

**Worker cooperatives and community enterprises** ensure that surplus from economic activity remains in the neighborhood rather than flowing to distant shareholders.

**The tip economy** makes care work visible and valued. The person who checks on elders, who helps neighbors navigate bureaucracy, who mentors youth—these contributions become economically recognized.

**Fluid democracy** enables residents to make collective decisions about how community resources are used.

**Energy-based exchange** with neighboring communities creates mutual incentives for development. When the DTES becomes more efficient—when its housing is secure, its residents healthy, its enterprises productive—it generates more value for everyone it trades with.

This is not a fantasy. It is an extrapolation of what is already present. The DTES could become a **cell** in the network-of-cells model—a place where different rules apply, where a different economy operates, where healing becomes possible because the conditions for healing have been created.

## Part Seven: The Architecture of a Living Economy

### 7.1 The Network of Cells

A living economy organized on these principles would not be a single machine controlled from the center. It would be a network of semi-autonomous **cells**—communities of various sizes and types, each organized according to its own values and traditions, each responsible for its own internal affairs, each connected to others through relationships rather than hierarchies .

Some cells might be Indigenous nations reviving traditional governance. Some might be worker cooperatives practicing participatory democracy. Some might be ecovillages organized around permaculture. Some might be religious communities structured by faith. Some might be urban neighborhoods experimenting with new forms of mutual aid. Some might be bioregional councils coordinating the management of shared watersheds.

Each cell would have complete freedom over its internal organization. One might use consensus decision-making; another might elect leaders; another might rely on elders. One might practice a gift economy; another might use local currencies; another might combine elements of both. The diversity is not a problem to be managed but the primary source of resilience—the raw material from which adaptation emerges.

### 7.2 The Nervous System That Connects

A mature organic system would not reject the tools developed by the extractive order. It would incorporate them—but in a new role. Rather than serving as instruments of control from the center, they would become the **nervous system** connecting autonomous cells .

In a living organism, the nervous system does not command the cells. It carries information between them, enabling coordination while respecting their autonomy. A signal from the brain does not tell a muscle cell how to metabolize; it tells it when to contract. The cell's internal life remains its own.

Similarly, the coordinating mechanisms inherited from the extractive system would serve to **connect** rather than **control**. Common currencies would enable exchange between cells without dictating their internal economies. Legal frameworks would establish the minimal rules of engagement without specifying internal governance. Communication networks would carry information about successful practices, emerging challenges, and potential collaborations—but each cell would decide how to respond.

### 7.3 The Indigenous Peoples Bank

To scale this economy beyond a single community, we need institutions that embody its principles. The **Indigenous Peoples Bank** is one such institution, inspired by the Quebec model but adapted for conditions of foundational incompatibility.

The bank is capitalized by the pooled savings of participating communities. Every nation, cooperative, or neighborhood that chooses to join contributes its collective resources. Every member can apply for economic development funding.

The bank has one purpose: **to create positive economic engines**. A community wants to build housing? The bank provides the capital. A nation wants to launch a sustainable fishery, a renewable energy cooperative, a language revitalization center? The bank provides the loans. The bank does not speculate. It does not extract. It builds.

The profits from these enterprises flow back to the bank. And those profits sustain the communities—not government transfers, not resource extraction deals, but the productive capacity of the people themselves, funded by their pooled resources, generating wealth that circulates within the circle.

The bank also serves as an interface with the larger economy. It negotiates on behalf of its members with governments, corporations, and financial institutions. It bundles the needs of many into a single, verified package, reducing overhead for everyone. It protects its members from exploitation by providing collective bargaining power.

This is sovereignty made practical: not the abstract sovereignty of flags and anthems, but the concrete sovereignty of controlling your own capital, your own credit, your own economic future.

### 7.4 Fluid Democracy and the Implicit Authority of Good Ideas

Within communities, governance can take many forms. But one principle applies universally: **those who make decisions should experience their consequences**.

This is the feedback loop that all living systems require. When decision-makers are insulated from the effects of their choices, pathology accumulates. When they live with the results, they learn.

Fluid democracy is one way to maintain this feedback. In fluid democracy, anyone can propose a policy. Anyone can second it. If a proposal reaches a threshold of support, it is submitted to a vote. Representatives are not elected for fixed terms but chosen for specific tasks, and can be recalled at any time.

This is not the only possible system. Some communities may prefer consensus. Others may rely on elders. Others may use sortition—selecting decision-makers by lottery, like ancient Athens. The diversity is the point. Different communities will find different forms that work for their context.

In any governance system, authority flows from many sources. There is **explicit authority**, backed by enforcement. There is **latent authority**, rooted in internalized hierarchy. And there is something else: **implicit authority**, the authority of ideas accepted on their merits.

When a good idea spreads through a community because it works, not because someone mandated it, that is implicit authority at work. When a better practice replaces an established one because its advantages are obvious, that is implicit authority. When a compelling argument persuades listeners regardless of its source, that is implicit authority.

Implicit authority cannot be commanded. It must be earned. But it can be cultivated—by creating conditions where good ideas can be recognized and adopted, by flattening hierarchies that filter out dissenting voices, by rewarding those who recognize and adopt better approaches.

In a living system, implicit authority is the primary mode of coordination. Explicit authority is reserved for exceptional circumstances—when consensus fails, when boundaries are violated, when immediate action is required. The default is relationship, not command.

### 7.5 From Borders to Watersheds

The nation-state model is built on **geometric borders**—straight lines drawn on maps, often by people who had never seen the land. These borders cut through ecosystems, divide watersheds, separate families, and ignore the actual contours of the living world.

A living system organizes differently. Its boundaries follow **natural features**—mountain ranges, river systems, migration routes, ecological zones. A people belongs to a river valley, not a grid coordinate.

Imagine governance organized around **watersheds**. The fundamental unit is the community of a specific place—a river valley, a coastal inlet, a mountain basin. Identity is rooted in responsibility to that place. You are a member because you care for it and are cared for by it.

Watershed communities that share an ecosystem form a **bioregional council**. This council manages shared resources—water, fisheries, forests—and resolves disputes between communities. Representation is based on relationships, not population. Each community sends wisdom-keepers, not just politicians.

A global council exists for truly planetary issues: climate stability, ocean health, atmospheric protection, and the rights of future generations. This council does not have authority over local matters. Its power is the power of relationship and consensus. It includes not only human representatives but also guardians for the non-human world—people appointed to speak for rivers, forests, animals, and the climate .

This is governance as **kinship diplomacy**. It is the Haudenosaunee Confederacy scaled to the planet. It is the Great Law of Peace applied to all beings.

## Part Eight: The Healing That Becomes Possible

### 8.1 The Wounds That Need Healing

Colonization did not just take resources. It took relationships. It severed connections between people and land, between generations, between labor and livelihood. It replaced horizontal relationships of reciprocity with vertical relationships of charity and dependency. It made people sick—not just physically, but socially and psychologically.

The wounds are visible everywhere: in the despair of Indigenous communities stripped of their lands, in the loneliness of suburban subdivisions where no one knows their neighbors, in the anxiety of professionals who have achieved everything and feel nothing, in the addiction that fills the void left by meaninglessness.

These wounds cannot be healed by the system that created them. Charity, however well-intentioned, maintains the underlying structure: giver and receiver, superior and inferior, helper and helped. What is needed is not better charity but **autonomy**—space for wounded communities to heal themselves, on their own terms, according to their own values.

### 8.2 The Conditions for Healing

A living economy creates the conditions in which healing becomes possible:

- When housing is secure, the toxic stress of precarity lifts.
- When care work is visible, caregivers can rest.
- When contribution is recognized, people know they matter.
- When generosity is honored, the generous are protected.
- When community makes decisions together, isolation recedes.
- When connection to the land is restored, a fundamental relationship is repaired.
- When energy is the basis of value, helping neighbors becomes structurally rational.

These are not therapeutic interventions. They are **structural conditions**—the soil in which healing can grow. A tip economy does not cure depression; it creates a community where depression is less likely to take root, and where those who struggle are held rather than abandoned.

### 8.3 The Invitation Extended

To anyone who hears their own story in what has been described—whether you are Indigenous or not, whether you were born here or came last week, whether you have been wounded by the system or have benefited from it and feel the unease that will not go away—an invitation is extended.

You are welcome to join. The price of entry is everything you have.

This is not a transaction. It is a transformation. By giving away your possessions, your titles, your private holdings, you are not losing anything—you are liberating yourself from the very illusion that separates you from life. You are proving to yourself and to the community that you are ready to belong, rather than to possess.

In return, you receive something no government has ever been able to offer: a clean slate and a permanent place.

You become a member. You agree to live by the laws of circulation and reciprocity. And the community agrees to take care of you.

We will regularize your finances. We will sit with you, one human being to another, and figure out what you are eligible for—the benefit you never claimed, the tax credit you didn't know existed, the forgiveness program you couldn't navigate alone. We will negotiate with the government on your behalf, not as an individual begging, but as a member of a collective.

We will hold you. We will audit for you. We will gift to you. And we will expect you to do the same for others.

This is not a withdrawal from the world. It is a renegotiation of the interface. The community becomes a buffer, a translator, a collective bargaining agent between the individual and the systems that were never designed to help them.

## Part Nine: The Future That Is Already Here

### 9.1 The Gradual Transition

This vision does not require anyone to take up arms. It does not require revolution in the streets. It requires only that we build something so functional, so desirable, that when the storms come—and they will come—people will look at what we have built and choose to join.

The old system will not fall because we overthrew it. It will dissolve for lack of participants.

This is the path of **gradual, visible, consensual transition**. Start in one place—a neighborhood, a community, a region. Build one cell that works this way. Prove that people can be healthier, happier, more secure. Prove that the ecological footprint shrinks when the economy is based on circulation rather than extraction. Prove that the government saves money when it deals with a collective.

Then let the model spread. A neighborhood in one city. A town in the interior. A fishing community on the coast. A group of farmers in the valley. Each one adapting the principles to its own context. Each one joining the network.

This is how we avoid the violent transitions that history warns us about. The pattern is old: create a crisis, soften the social fabric through shock, and impose a new order wall to wall. That path leads to war, to algorithm, to control without consent.

We offer a different path. It does not require anyone to take up arms. It requires only that we build something worth joining.

### 9.2 The Global Horizon

And then, perhaps, we look further.

The Indigenous Peoples Bank, once established, need not stop at the borders of this land. It could spread. It could offer its model to other communities, other nations, other regions struggling with the same questions.

Imagine a world where the bank helps bridge the gaps between the rising powers of the global south and the struggling communities of the global north. Imagine a financial institution whose currency is trust, whose collateral is relationship, whose profit is measured in human well-being.

Imagine a global council where watershed representatives—human and non-human—gather to address shared challenges. Where the rights of rivers are recognized, the voices of forests are heard, the needs of future generations are considered alongside the demands of the present.

Imagine an energy-based exchange network where every community's development benefits every other, where helping your neighbor is not a moral choice but a structural necessity, where the efficiency of one becomes the prosperity of all.

This is not a fantasy. It is the logical extension of principles that have worked for millennia. It is what happens when you stop trying to build one big machine and start tending a garden of many flourishing things.

### 9.3 The Only Thing We Must Let Go

There is only one requirement for participation in this future: **we must let go of discrimination**.

Not the superficial discrimination of skin color or religion or gender—though that must also go. But the deeper discrimination: the belief that one way of being human is superior to others. The conviction that our values should be everyone's values. The assumption that those who organize differently are backward, primitive, or wrong.

The living system thrives on diversity. Different cells, different values, different practices—this is not a problem to be managed but the primary source of resilience. A forest with only one species of tree is vulnerable to disease. A society with only one way of organizing is vulnerable to collapse.

We must let go of the need to convert everyone to our way. We must let go of the impulse to judge those who choose differently. We must let go of the arrogance that says we know best for others.

In its place, we must cultivate **humility**. The recognition that we do not have all the answers. The openness to learn from those whose traditions differ from our own. The willingness to coexist with ways of being that we may not fully understand.

This is not relativism. There are principles that apply across all cells: mutual recognition, non-interference, fair exchange, ecological stewardship. These are the minimal interface rules that make coexistence possible. But within those bounds, diversity is not just tolerated but celebrated.

### 9.4 The Indigenous Future

We are the *Homo Indigenus*, the people of the land. We are not a relic of the past, but the seed of the future. For countless generations, we have understood a truth that the world has forgotten: we do not own the land; we belong to it. The rivers are our veins, the forests our lungs, the soil our flesh. Our worth is not measured in currency, but in the strength of our community, the health of our ecosystem, and the depth of our connection to the cycles of life .

We extend an invitation to all who are weary of possession, all who are burdened by the need to own, and all who yearn for a life of genuine connection. We invite you to join us in building a world that is organic, communal, and alive.

But this invitation comes with a threshold, a simple mechanism to protect our communities and the sincerity of our future. It is a gate to ensure that only the generous, and not the opportunistic, may enter.

This is our shield against those who would seek to exploit our generosity. The opportunist, the one still in love with possession, will never cross this threshold. But the one who is ready to be free will find it the most liberating step they have ever taken.

Let the *Homo Economicus* keep his money. Let the *Homo Ecologicus* keep his guilt and his carbon offsets. Their world is dying because it was never truly alive.

We are building a world that will live, because it is rooted in the earth itself. We are the people of the land. And we welcome you, not as owners, but as relatives.

Come and learn what it means to belong to a community.

Come and learn what it means to belong to the land.

Come and learn what it means to belong.

## Conclusion: The Choice We Make Together

We have not described a utopia. Utopias do not exist. Human societies will always have conflict, tragedy, and imperfection. What we have described is a **direction**—a set of principles that can guide us as we navigate the challenges ahead.

The choice before us is not between this vision and some other equally attractive vision. The choice is between building a future based on relationship, reciprocity, and belonging, and continuing down a path that is already showing signs of catastrophic failure.

We do not need to agree on everything to move in this direction. We do not need to share the same beliefs, the same values, the same traditions. We need only to agree on a few minimal principles:

- That the Earth is our common home, and we have a responsibility to care for it.
- That every human being deserves the conditions for flourishing.
- That diversity is a strength, not a weakness.
- That those who make decisions should experience their consequences.
- That generosity is a better basis for social life than accumulation.
- That energy, not price, is the fundamental measure of value.
- That our neighbors' development is our own prosperity.

If you can agree to these principles, you are welcome. If you cannot, you are still welcome to observe, to learn, to consider. The door is open. It will remain open.

The future is not something that happens to us. It is something we create together, with every choice, every relationship, every act of generosity or withholding. The future we have described is already emerging in fragments—in communities that never forgot how to share, in movements that refuse to give up, in hearts that still believe another world is possible.

Come and help us build it.

All our relations.
