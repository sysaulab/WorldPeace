# The Indigenous Model

The Canadian government's assertion that it could define who is "Indian" is not an anomaly; it is the **operating system of the nation-state itself**. Every nation-state on earth is built on the same foundational arrogance: the claim that a central authority has the right to draw borders, assign identities, grant rights, and control the relationship between people and land.

Indigenous societies offer a different operating system. They are not perfect utopias—they are human, complex, and varied. But they contain within them the seeds of a **global governance model that is fluid, relational, and rooted in the actual living world.**

## 1. The Problem of Borders vs. The Reality of Watersheds

The nation-state model is built on **geometric borders**—straight lines drawn on maps, often by people who had never seen the land. These borders cut through ecosystems, divide watersheds, separate families, and ignore the actual contours of the living world.

**The Indigenous Model: Bioregional Governance**

Indigenous societies traditionally organized themselves around **natural boundaries**—mountain ranges, river systems, migration routes, and ecological zones. A people belonged to a river valley, not a grid coordinate.

**For global self-governance:**

- Political boundaries would be redrawn to follow **watersheds, ecosystems, and bioregions**.
- Governance would begin at the **local level**—the community of a river valley—and scale outward through relationships, not through hierarchical control.
- A person's identity would be tied to their **place of origin and responsibility**, not to a flag or a passport issued by a distant capital.

This is the antidote to the arrogance of the Indian Act. Instead of a government in Ottawa deciding who is Indigenous, the **land itself and the community of the land** would be the foundation of belonging.

## 2. The Problem of Sovereignty vs. The Reality of Interdependence

The nation-state model is built on the concept of **absolute sovereignty**—the idea that a state has supreme authority within its borders and owes nothing to those outside them. This leads to competition, resource wars, and the externalization of harm (pollution, poverty, conflict) across borders.

**The Indigenous Model: Kinship Diplomacy**

Indigenous nations did not have borders in the European sense. They had **territories**, but these were understood as areas of responsibility and relationship. When two peoples shared a watershed or a migration route, they didn't build walls; they built **treaties, protocols, and marriage alliances**. The Haudenosaunee Confederacy, for example, united five (later six) nations into a peaceful alliance based on the Great Law of Peace—a model that influenced thinkers like Benjamin Franklin.

**For global self-governance:**

- The foundational principle would be **interdependence, not independence**. No community, bioregion, or nation would be an island.
- Governance would be based on **consensus and relationship**, not majority rule that tramples minority rights.
- Disputes would be resolved through **dialogue, restoration, and the rebalancing of relationships**, not through war or economic coercion.

This is the opposite of the Canadian government's unilateral imposition of definition. It is governance as a **continuous conversation**.

## 3. The Problem of Hierarchy vs. The Reality of Distributed Authority

The nation-state model is a **pyramid**. Power flows from the top (the federal government, the monarch, the president) down to the bottom (the individual citizen). This creates a system where a small number of people control the lives of millions.

**The Indigenous Model: The Circle and the Council**

Many Indigenous societies operated through **councils, consensus, and distributed leadership**. Among the Anishinaabe, for example, leadership was often temporary and context-specific. A war chief was different from a peace chief. Women's councils held separate but equal authority. Decisions were made by those who would live with the consequences.

**For global self-governance:**

- Power would be **distributed and nested**. Local communities would handle local matters. Bioregional councils would handle shared resources (water, air, migratory species). A global council would handle truly global concerns (climate, oceans, atmospheric health).
- No level of governance would have **absolute authority** over the levels below it. Higher levels would exist to **coordinate and support**, not to command.
- Leadership would be **rotating, temporary, and accountable**. Leaders would be chosen for their wisdom, not their wealth or ambition.

This is the antidote to the Indian Act's bureaucratic control. Instead of a distant official defining identity, the **community itself**—in relationship with the land—would be the source of authority.

## 4. The Problem of Rights vs. The Reality of Responsibilities

The nation-state model is built on the language of **rights**. Individuals have rights against the state. States have rights against other states. This framework, while important, often becomes adversarial and abstract. It separates the right from the context in which it exists.

**The Indigenous Model: Responsibilities and Reciprocity**

In many Indigenous worldviews, rights are inseparable from **responsibilities**. A person has the right to clean water because they have the responsibility to care for the watershed. A community has the right to self-determination because it has the responsibility to maintain peace and balance within its territory.

**For global self-governance:**

- Governance would begin with the question: **"What is our responsibility here?"** not "What are our rights?"
- Rights would be understood as the **outcome of fulfilled responsibilities**.
- The land itself would have rights—not as an abstraction, but as a living entity with whom humans are in relationship. This is already happening in legal systems that recognize the rights of rivers (e.g., the Whanganui River in Aotearoa New Zealand).

This reframes the entire debate about Indigenous identity. The question would no longer be "Who has the right to be called Indigenous?" but rather **"Who is taking responsibility for this land, and who holds the memory of how to care for it?"**

## 5. The Problem of Universal Law vs. The Reality of Place-Based Wisdom

The nation-state model aspires to **universal law**—the same rules apply everywhere, regardless of local conditions. This leads to one-size-fits-all policies that often fail because they ignore the specificities of place.

**The Indigenous Model: Place-Based Jurisprudence**

Indigenous law is not a set of abstract principles; it is **law that comes from the land**. It emerges from generations of observing and living in a specific place. What works in a desert may not work in a rainforest. What works on the plains may not work on the coast.

**For global self-governance:**

- Law would be **plural and contextual**. There would be universal principles (e.g., "do not harm the living world"), but their application would vary according to place.
- The wisdom of long-term inhabitants—Indigenous peoples—would be recognized as **legal and scientific authority**. They would not be consulted as an afterthought; they would be at the center of governance for their territories.
- Colonial law's claim to supremacy would be **dissolved**. No external government would have the authority to define who belongs to a place or what is best for that place.

This directly addresses the irony of the Indian Act. The Canadian government's claim to define "Indian" was based on the assumption that **its law was superior to Indigenous law**. In a global Indigenous governance model, that assumption would be inverted. The local, place-based law would be primary.

## The Architecture of Global Indigenous Self-Governance

Imagine, then, a world organized on these principles:

**The Local: The Watershed Community**

- The fundamental unit of governance is the **community of a specific place**—a river valley, a coastal inlet, a mountain basin.
- Identity is rooted in **responsibility to that place**. You are a member because you care for it and are cared for by it.
- Decisions are made by **consensus** among those who live there.

**The Bioregional: The Council of Relatives**

- Watershed communities that share an ecosystem (e.g., a major river system, a mountain range) form a **bioregional council**.
- This council manages shared resources (water, fisheries, forests) and resolves disputes between communities.
- Representation is based on **relationships, not population**. Each community sends wisdom-keepers, not just politicians.

**The Global: The Circle of All Beings**

- A global council exists to address truly planetary issues: climate stability, ocean health, atmospheric protection, and the rights of future generations.
- This council does not have authority over local matters. Its power is the power of **relationship and consensus**.
- It includes not only human representatives but also **guardians for the non-human world**—people appointed to speak for rivers, forests, animals, and the climate.

**Conclusion: From Arrogance to Humility**

The Canadian government's decision to "assign" Native status was the ultimate expression of colonial arrogance: the belief that a foreign power could know better than the land and its people who belongs.

A global system of self-governance modeled on Indigenous principles would be the ultimate expression of **humility**. It would recognize that:

- Authority comes from the land, not from a capital.
- Identity is a matter of relationship, not paperwork.
- Governance is about responsibility, not control.
- The human is part of the ecosystem, not its master.

This is not a return to some mythical past. It is a **practical framework for survival**. As the climate crisis deepens and the nation-state system proves increasingly incapable of responding, the old ways become the new way forward.

The question is not whether Indigenous peoples have the right to define themselves. The question is whether the rest of humanity has the humility to learn from those who never forgot how to belong.

**All our relations.**
